# C25 · Crunch Founders — Zero to Launch · Syllabus

**10 weeks · ~10 hours/week · ~100 hours total · A validated startup concept by the end of the cycle.**

An opinionated founder course designed to take a learner from "I have an idea" to "I have evidence." It is problem-first, interview-driven, and allergic to vanity. The capstone is not a fundraise and not a launch — it is *proof that you talked to enough of the right people, built the smallest thing that tests the idea, and can defend the result in three minutes.*

---

## Program at a glance

| Phase | Weeks | Outcome |
|-------|-------|---------|
| **Phase 1 — Founder & Problem** | 01 – 03 | Founder-market fit, a triaged idea, and ten real discovery interviews |
| **Phase 2 — Team & Build** | 04 – 05 | A co-founder agreement draft and a lean MVP that tests one assumption |
| **Phase 3 — Model & Market** | 06 – 07 | Unit economics that hold up and a go-to-market motion with one traction metric |
| **Phase 4 — Money, Law & Pitch** | 08 – 10 | Fundraising literacy, an incorporation/ops plan, and a demo-day pitch |

## Weekly breakdown

### Phase 1 — Founder & Problem

**Week 1 — Founder mindset & founder-market fit.** What the founder's job actually is (deciding what is worth building, for whom, and surviving the people decisions) versus what people imagine it is. The control-vs-wealth tension: do you want to be rich or to be in charge — you usually cannot maximize both. Honest founder-market fit: are *you* the right person for *this* problem?

- *Mini-deliverable:* A one-page founder brief — your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, and a problem space you have real standing in.

**Week 2 — Idea generation & problem validation.** Idea generation as a problem-first discipline, not a solution-first brainstorm. Separating a problem worth solving from a solution you happen to like. Lightweight desk validation before you ever build: search demand, existing alternatives, "is this a vitamin or a painkiller."

- *Mini-deliverable:* An idea-triage log of 5–10 ideas scored against a problem-first filter, with one chosen to carry forward.

**Week 3 — Customer discovery & interviews.** Getting out of the building. How to run a discovery interview that uncovers problems instead of confirming your bias: ask about the past, not the future; never pitch; dig into the last time it actually happened. Synthesizing many conversations into a pattern.

- *Mini-deliverable:* Five completed discovery interviews (write-ups) plus a first pattern summary. (You will reach ten by the capstone.)

### Phase 2 — Team & Build

**Week 4 — Co-founders, roles, equity & vesting.** The founder's dilemmas: solo vs. co-founders, who decides what, and how to split equity *before* resentment makes it impossible. Why an equal split is a decision, not a default. Vesting with a cliff as protection for everyone, including the people who stay. The control-vs-wealth trade reappears here, concretely.

- *Mini-deliverable:* A co-founder agreement draft — roles, decision rights, an equity split with stated reasoning, and a vesting schedule with a cliff.

**Week 5 — Lean MVP & build-measure-learn.** The MVP as an experiment, not a tiny product. Picking your single riskiest assumption and the cheapest test of it: smoke test / fake door, concierge, Wizard of Oz, clickable prototype. Build → measure → learn, then decide: persevere or pivot.

- *Mini-deliverable:* A built MVP (any fidelity) wired to one clear metric, plus the result and your persevere/pivot decision.

### Phase 3 — Model & Market

**Week 6 — Business models & unit economics.** The business model canvas as a one-page hypothesis. Revenue models and pricing basics. The unit economics that decide whether scale helps or hurts: contribution margin, CAC, LTV, the LTV:CAC ratio, and payback period.

- *Mini-deliverable:* A lean canvas plus a unit-economics sheet (CAC, LTV, contribution margin, payback) with assumptions stated.

**Week 7 — Go-to-market & early traction.** Choosing a go-to-market motion that matches the model (founder-led sales, community, content, paid, partnerships). Defining the *one* metric that matters at your stage. Building a first funnel and reading it honestly; spotting vanity metrics.

- *Mini-deliverable:* A go-to-market one-pager naming the motion, the first channel to test, and the single traction metric you will hold yourself to.

### Phase 4 — Money, Law & Pitch

**Week 8 — Fundraising basics.** When *not* to raise. Bootstrapping vs. angels vs. pre-seed. How a cap table works and how a round dilutes founders. SAFEs and convertibles in plain terms, valuation caps and discounts conceptually, and what "post-money" does to your ownership. Modeling dilution across a round in a spreadsheet.

- *Mini-deliverable:* A cap-table model showing founder ownership before and after a hypothetical pre-seed round, with dilution computed.

**Week 9 — Legal, incorporation & ops.** Why and when to incorporate, common entity choices at a high level, founder stock and the role of vesting, IP assignment, and the basic agreements every early company needs. Lightweight ops: banking, bookkeeping, and the few records that matter. (Education, not legal advice — you leave able to brief a lawyer, not replace one.)

- *Mini-deliverable:* An incorporation & ops checklist tailored to your concept, with open questions flagged for a real advisor.

**Week 10 — The pitch + demo day (capstone).** Assembling everything into a deck and a 3-minute pitch: problem, evidence, solution, model, traction, ask. Recording it. Handling the obvious hard questions. Demo day.

- *Capstone:* A validated startup concept — ≥10 discovery interviews synthesized, a lean business model canvas, an MVP or clickable prototype, a pitch deck, and a 3-minute recorded pitch.

## How the weekly load adds up

| Component | hrs/wk |
|-----------|------:|
| Lecture / reading | 2 |
| Customer interviews / field work | 3 |
| Hands-on (model, MVP, spreadsheet, deck) | 3 |
| Exercises & quiz | 1 |
| Reflection / journaling | 1 |
| **Total** | **10** |

Field work spikes in Weeks 3–7 (interviews, MVP test, GTM) and eases in the framework weeks. Treat the table as a target, not a contract.

---

## Capstone rubric

| Criterion | Weight |
|-----------|------:|
| Discovery evidence: ≥10 real interviews, synthesized into a defensible pattern (not cherry-picked) | 25% |
| MVP or prototype actually tests the stated riskiest assumption, with a recorded result | 20% |
| Lean canvas + unit economics are internally consistent and assumption-stated | 15% |
| Pitch deck and 3-minute recording land the structure (problem → evidence → solution → model → traction → ask) | 20% |
| Co-founder agreement draft covers roles, decision rights, equity, and vesting with a cliff | 10% |
| The pitch survives the three most obvious hard questions | 10% |

## What you won't learn (intentionally)

- **How to build the product itself.** Engineering and design depth live in [C8](../../C8-CRUNCH-LABS-WEB-DEV/) and [C16](../../C16-CRUNCH-PRO-WEB-BACKEND/).
- **Shipping under deadline pressure as a team.** That muscle is [C4](../../C4-Crunch-Labs-Hackathons/) — and it pairs perfectly with Week 5's MVP build.
- **Actual legal or tax advice.** Week 9 makes you a better client for a real lawyer or accountant; it does not replace one.
- **Growth-stage operating.** This is a zero-to-validation course. Scaling is a different rhythm and a different course.
