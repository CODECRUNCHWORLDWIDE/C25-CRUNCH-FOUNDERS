# Week 4 — Co-founders, Roles, Equity & Vesting

This is the founder's-dilemmas week — the cluster of people decisions that quietly decide whether a company survives its first two years. Co-founder conflict is one of the most common reasons early startups fail, and almost all of it is avoidable with conversations most founders postpone until it is too late: who decides what, how the equity splits, and what happens when someone leaves. These are uncomfortable on day one and catastrophic on day three hundred. We have them now, in writing.

The deliverable is a co-founder agreement draft — roles, decision rights, an equity split with stated reasoning, and a vesting schedule with a cliff. (If you are solo, you draft the equivalent for your first key hire and your own founder vesting.)

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Decide** solo vs. co-founders deliberately, knowing the trade-offs.
- **Define roles and decision rights** so disagreements have a resolution path.
- **Reason about an equity split** as a decision, not a reflex — and explain why an automatic 50/50 can be either right or lazy.
- **Explain vesting and the cliff**, and why they protect *everyone*, including the people who stay.
- **Connect** the equity and control choices back to your Week 1 control-vs-wealth lean.
- **Draft** a plain-language co-founder agreement (a template, not legal advice).

## Topics covered

- Solo vs. co-founders — the first big people-and-equity decision
- Roles and decision rights: who has the final call on what
- Equity splits: equal vs. unequal, and the inputs that justify each (idea, time, capital, risk, skill, opportunity cost)
- The dangers of the handshake split and the "we'll sort it later" deferral
- Vesting with a cliff: standard four-year/one-year shape, and *why* it protects the company and the remaining founders
- Founder departures and the "what if someone leaves" clause
- How control-vs-wealth (Week 1) shows up concretely in the split

## In-class activities

- **The split debate.** Two founders argue a 50/50 vs. 60/40 split for the same scenario; the room scores which reasoning is sounder. The point is that *reasoning*, not the number, is what matters.
- **Decision-rights mapping.** List the ten decisions a startup faces in year one (hiring, pricing, fundraising, pivoting) and assign a final-call owner to each. Gaps are future fights.
- **The cliff thought experiment.** A co-founder leaves at month three with no vesting. What happens to the cap table? Now add a one-year cliff and re-run it.

## Worked example

Two founders, Ana and Ben. Ana had the idea, has been full-time for six months, and brings the customer access; Ben joins now, full-time, with the engineering skill they lack. A reflexive 50/50 ignores Ana's head start and risk. After honest discussion they choose 55/45 — close enough to feel fair, weighted for Ana's prior risk — *and* both put all founder equity on a four-year vest with a one-year cliff. Six months later Ben realizes it isn't for him and leaves. Because of the cliff, he vested nothing, the equity returns to the pool, and Ana can recruit a replacement without a dead-weight 45% on the cap table. The cliff protected the *company* and Ana — and had Ben been the one carrying the company, it would have protected him.
