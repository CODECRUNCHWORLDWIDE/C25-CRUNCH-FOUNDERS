# Week 4 — Co-founders, Roles, Equity & Vesting

Welcome to **C25 · Crunch Founders**, Week 4. You have spent three weeks on the problem: finding founder-market fit, triaging an idea, and getting out of the building to run real discovery interviews. This is the week the course turns from "the problem" to "the people who will attack it with you." It is also the week most founders are tempted to skip — to hand-wave the equity split, avoid the awkward decision-rights conversation, and "figure it out later." Later is where companies die. This week we do it now, on paper, while everyone still likes each other.

By Sunday you will have drafted a real co-founder agreement: roles, decision rights, an equity split you can *defend* line by line, and a vesting schedule with a one-year cliff that protects every founder — including the ones who stay. Solo founders do the equivalent: their own founder vesting plus the terms they would offer a first key hire, because the same instruments apply.

This is a **business** week. You will write zero code. You will, however, do something harder than most code: have an honest conversation about money and power with someone you want to build a company with, and then write down what you agreed so that future-you and future-them cannot quietly rewrite history.

The single sentence to internalize before we start:

> **An equal split is a decision, not a default — and vesting with a cliff is mutual protection, not distrust.** Founders who treat either as an afterthought are the single most common preventable failure in early-stage startups.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Decide** solo vs. co-founders deliberately — choosing co-founders for *specific gaps you cannot fill*, not for company or comfort.
- **Distinguish** titles from **decision rights**, and assign final-call ownership for every decision that actually causes founder fights (product, hiring, pricing, fundraising, spending).
- **Reason** about an equity split from its real inputs — idea/prior work, time commitment, capital, skill/replaceability, opportunity cost — and write the reasoning down, not just the number.
- **Reject** the "handshake split, figure it out later" trap and explain, concretely, why it is one of the most expensive sentences in startups.
- **Explain** founder vesting: a four-year vest, a one-year cliff, monthly thereafter — and why it protects the company *and* every individual founder.
- **Draft** a departure clause covering unvested equity (returns to the pool), vested equity (buyback rights), and IP/account handover, written while everyone is still happy.
- **Recognize** acceleration (single- vs. double-trigger) and the **83(b) election** as real terms a lawyer will raise — enough to brief a professional without pretending to be one.
- **Connect** every decision back to your Week 1 control-vs-wealth lean, so the split and the structure are *consistent* with the founder you said you are.
- **Produce** a co-founder agreement draft that an actual startup lawyer could read and immediately know what to paper.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed **C25 Weeks 1–3**, or have equivalent founder context. Specifically:

- You have a **one-page founder brief** from Week 1, including your control-vs-wealth lean and the unfair advantages you bring. You will reference it directly this week.
- You have a **chosen idea** carried through the Week 2 triage — the concept your future co-founder is joining.
- You have **at least five discovery interviews** from Week 3, so the company you are splitting equity in is grounded in real evidence, not a daydream.
- You are comfortable having an uncomfortable, direct conversation and writing the result down plainly.

You do **not** need any legal background. Nothing this week is legal advice; the entire point is to make you a *better client* for a real lawyer in Week 9, not to replace one. If you have formed a company before, you may already know some of this — use the week to pressure-test the assumptions you made last time.

## Topics covered

- Solo vs. co-founders: the real trade (load and accountability vs. control and equity), and the "would I make this person a 50/50 partner with my own money?" filter.
- Roles vs. decision rights: why titles are cheap and the tiebreak table is where fights are prevented.
- The five inputs to an equity split — idea/prior work, time, capital, skill/replaceability, opportunity cost — and how to weight them honestly.
- Why an equal split is a legitimate *decision* and when it is a cowardly *avoidance*.
- The handshake-split trap and the "dynamic vs. static split" debate (Slicing Pie and why most fundable companies still use a fixed, vested split).
- Founder vesting: the four-year / one-year-cliff standard, why it is founder-*friendly*, and why investors require it anyway.
- The departure clause: unvested return, vested buyback, leaver provisions (good leaver / bad leaver), IP and account handover.
- Acceleration: single-trigger and double-trigger, in plain terms, as something to flag — not master — for a lawyer.
- The **83(b) election**: what it is, the 30-day clock, and why missing it is an avoidable tax disaster (education, not advice).
- Restricted stock vs. options for founders, at a high level, and where vesting lives in each.
- Control instruments beyond percentages: board seats, voting agreements, and protective provisions — named, not deep-dived (that's Weeks 8–9).
- Writing the unhappy path while everyone is happy: the discipline that makes the whole agreement worth the paper.

## In-class activities

- **The split debate.** Two founders argue a 50/50 vs. 60/40 split for the same scenario; the room scores which *reasoning* is sounder. The point is that the reasoning, not the number, is what makes a split survive stress.
- **Decision-rights mapping.** List the ten decisions a startup actually faces in year one (hiring, pricing, fundraising, pivoting, the first real budget overrun) and assign a final-call owner to each. Gaps are future fights.
- **The cliff thought experiment.** A co-founder leaves at month three with no vesting. Watch what happens to the cap table. Now add a one-year cliff and re-run it.

## Worked example (the spine of the week)

Two founders, Ana and Ben. Ana had the idea, has been full-time for six months, and brings the customer access; Ben joins now, full-time, with the engineering skill they lack. A reflexive 50/50 ignores Ana's head start and risk. After honest discussion they choose **55/45** — close enough to feel fair, weighted for Ana's prior risk — *and* both put all founder equity on a **four-year vest with a one-year cliff**. Six months later Ben realizes it isn't for him and leaves. Because of the cliff, he vested nothing, the equity returns to the pool, and Ana can recruit a replacement without a dead-weight 45% on the cap table. The cliff protected the *company* and Ana — and had Ben been the one carrying the company while Ana drifted, it would have protected him just as cleanly. We return to Ana and Ben in every lecture, exercise, and the challenge.

## The "would you say it to their face?" promise

C25 uses a recurring test in every artifact about people: **every line of your agreement must be something you could read aloud, to your co-founder's face, without flinching.** If a clause only works because your co-founder didn't read it carefully — a quiet buyback at a punitive price, an equity split you can't actually justify, a decision right you grabbed because you could — it will not survive contact with reality. The point of writing it down *now*, while everyone is happy, is that the happy version is the only fair version. We will repeat this test all week.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Read the **Y Combinator documents** (Series Seed/SAFE) and the **Founder Institute FAST agreement** end to end, and note three clauses you do not understand: <https://www.ycombinator.com/documents/> and <https://fi.co/fast>.
- Read **Carta's "How startup equity works"** and trace a hypothetical 50/50 split through a future pre-seed round to see how *both* founders dilute equally: <https://carta.com/learn/>.
- Read about the **83(b) election** until you can explain the 30-day clock and the downside of missing it to a peer in two sentences: <https://www.cooleygo.com/what-is-a-section-83b-election/>.
- Watch or read a real co-founder-breakup postmortem (search "co-founder conflict postmortem" or the public r/startups threads) and write a paragraph on what a written agreement would have changed.
- Compare the **dynamic equity** philosophy (Mike Moyer's *Slicing Pie*) against the standard fixed-and-vested split, and write a short note on why most venture-backed companies still choose the latter.
