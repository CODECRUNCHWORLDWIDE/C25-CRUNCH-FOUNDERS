# Challenge 1 — The Departing Co-founder

**Time estimate:** ~75–90 minutes.

**Format:** A founder decision scenario. You read the situation, make a series of calls, and *defend* them in writing. There is no answer key with one right number — you're graded on the quality and consistency of your reasoning. Submit a Markdown write-up (600–1,000 words plus tables) to your Week 4 repo at `challenges/challenge-01-the-departing-cofounder.md`.

---

## The situation

You are **Ana**, CEO and co-founder of **Reweave**, a startup that helps independent textile workshops sell surplus inventory to designers. Eighteen months ago you brought on **Ben** as co-founder and CTO — he built the marketplace; you found the customers and ran the company. Here's what you agreed on day one (you did the Week 4 work — congratulations to past-you):

- **Equity split:** Ana 55% / Ben 45%, reasoning written down (your six-month head start, $5k, and customer access vs. Ben being the only one who could build it).
- **Vesting:** both founders on a **4-year vest, 1-year cliff, monthly thereafter.** Restricted stock, 83(b) elections filed on time by both.
- **Decision rights:** Ana owns fundraising, hiring, pricing; Ben owns architecture and the build roadmap; pivots require both.
- **Departure clause:** unvested equity returns to the pool at original cost. On *vested* equity, you wrote: *"the company has the option, but not the obligation, to repurchase a departing founder's vested shares at fair market value; good-leaver vs. bad-leaver treatment to be confirmed with counsel."* You never finished the good-leaver/bad-leaver definition. (This gap is deliberate — it's the crux.)

**Total founder shares:** Ana 5,500,000; Ben 4,500,000 (matching the 55/45 on a 10,000,000-share founder pool).

### What's happening now (month 14)

Reweave just hit real traction: 40 paying workshops, monthly revenue growing ~20% month-over-month, and a pre-seed investor circling with a verbal offer. The company is finally worth something.

Ben tells you, privately and amicably, that he's burned out and wants to leave. He's not angry, there's no scandal — he's been carrying the engineering alone, he's exhausted, and he's realized he wants to go work somewhere with a team and a salary. He'll stay 6 weeks to hand off. He says, reasonably: *"I built the whole product. I've been here 14 months. I'd like to keep my equity."*

You have a problem and a decision.

---

## Your calls

Work through these **in order**, showing your reasoning for each. Use the vesting and departure mechanics from Lecture 2.

### Call 1 — The vesting math (mechanical; get it exactly right)

Ben is leaving at **month 14** on a 4-year / 1-year-cliff schedule, 4,500,000 shares.

- How much has Ben **vested**? Show the arithmetic.
- How much is **unvested**, and what happens to it under your day-one clause?
- After the unvested return, what does the cap table look like (Ana's %, Ben's %, the returned pool)?

### Call 2 — The vested equity (the hard part)

This is where your unfinished clause comes due. Ben has a real, *earned* vested stake and he wants to keep it.

- Is Ben a **good leaver** or a **bad leaver**? Make the call and define your terms — burnout-and-amicable-departure is genuinely ambiguous, which is exactly why you must decide.
- Do you **exercise the company's repurchase right** on his vested shares, or let him keep them? Argue your choice. Consider: it's *his earned equity*; but also, the pre-seed investor will ask why a non-building ex-founder holds a meaningful chunk; and also, how you treat Ben signals to every future hire how you'll treat *them*.
- If you repurchase, at what **price**? "Fair market value" of an illiquid pre-seed startup is a real fight — how do you set it fairly?

### Call 3 — The investor and the cap table

The pre-seed investor will see this in diligence.

- What do you tell the investor about Ben's departure and remaining stake?
- Does an ex-founder holding equity help or hurt the raise, and how do you make the cap table *fundable* without being unfair to Ben?
- Note where, if you'd finished the good-leaver definition on day one, this would be a 10-minute conversation instead of a negotiation.

### Call 4 — The handover and the relationship

- What does the 6-week handover need to cover (IP, accounts, credentials, the build roadmap) so Ben leaving doesn't lock the team out? Tie this to the CIIA (Lecture 2 §7).
- Ben is still going to be a person you know, possibly a reference, possibly a future re-hire. How do your money decisions in Calls 2–3 protect or poison that?

---

## The objections your decision must survive

Before you submit, stress-test your Calls 2 and 3 against these three (a strong write-up addresses each):

1. **"You're punishing Ben for being honest about burnout."** If you repurchase his vested shares hard, are you teaching every future team member to hide problems and grind until they break? Defend your choice against this.
2. **"You're letting a non-builder keep a chunk of a company he's not building, which is unfair to you and the next CTO."** The opposite charge. Defend against this too.
3. **"You never defined good-leaver/bad-leaver, so you don't actually *have* the right you're trying to exercise."** Is your day-one clause even enforceable as written? What does that mean for what you can fairly do now?

You cannot fully satisfy all three — they pull against each other. The mark of a strong founder is showing you *see* the tension and choosing deliberately, not pretending it isn't there.

## Deliverable

A Markdown write-up with:

- [ ] **Call 1** — vesting math, exact, with the post-return cap table.
- [ ] **Call 2** — a good/bad-leaver determination with defined terms, a keep-vs-repurchase decision with reasoning, and a fair pricing approach if repurchasing.
- [ ] **Call 3** — what you tell the investor and how you make the cap table fundable without being unfair.
- [ ] **Call 4** — a concrete handover checklist tied to IP/CIIA, and a note on the relationship.
- [ ] **Objections** — each of the three addressed head-on.
- [ ] A closing paragraph: **what you would change in your day-one agreement** now that you've lived this. (This is the real lesson — feed it back into your mini-project.)

## Why this matters

A clean co-founder departure is one of the most valuable things a written agreement buys you — and a messy one is one of the most common ways early companies stall or die. This scenario is deliberately built so the *easy* part (unvested return — the cliff already solved it) is trivial and the *hard* part (vested equity, the relationship, the investor) is where you actually have to think. That's real founding. When you go finish your own mini-project's departure clause, you'll write it like someone who's already been through this once.

## Stretch

- Re-run the whole scenario assuming Ben left at **month 9** (before the cliff) instead. Notice how much simpler it is — and write one sentence on what that tells you about *why* the cliff exists.
- Re-run it assuming you'd given Ben **no vesting** on day one ("we trust each other"). Compute the disaster. This is the homework cliff-experiment made vivid.
- Draft the **good-leaver/bad-leaver definition** you wish past-Ana had written, in plain language, in under 150 words. Add it to your mini-project agreement.
