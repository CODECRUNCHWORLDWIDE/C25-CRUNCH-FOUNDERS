# Week 4 Homework

Five problems, about **6 hours** total. Work in your Week 4 repo so each problem produces at least one commit you can point to later. Keep adding discovery interviews this week toward your capstone target of ten.

Each problem has a **problem statement**, **acceptance criteria**, a **hint**, and an **estimated time**. The homework feeds directly into the mini-project (the co-founder agreement draft) — none of it is busywork. A grading rubric is at the bottom.

> **Education, not legal or tax advice.** These problems make you a sharper client for a real lawyer in Week 9. They don't replace one.

---

## Problem 1 — Decision-rights table for your venture

**Problem statement.** At `notes/decision-rights.md`, build the full decision-rights table from Lecture 1 §2 for *your* venture (real or planned). Cover at least the ten decisions in Exercise 2's worksheet. Assign a single final-call owner (the **A**) to every row, set a real dollar figure for the spend threshold, and write an explicit tiebreak rule for the genuinely shared rows (pivot / new direction).

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present; at least ten decisions, each with a single named final-call owner — **no blank cells**.
- The spend threshold is a real number with a sign-off rule.
- The two company-defining rows (pivot, new direction) have explicit tiebreak rules.
- The table is consistent with your Week 1 control-vs-wealth lean (or the contradiction is explained).
- Committed.

**Hint.** If you're solo, note who you'd *want* deciding each once you hire, and where you keep the final call. Divide domains by competence, then give each owner the final call inside their lane.

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

## Problem 2 — The cliff thought experiment

**Problem statement.** At `notes/cliff-experiment.md`, model what happens to a co-founder who leaves at **month three** under two regimes:

- **(a) No vesting** — they owned their full stake outright on day one.
- **(b) Four-year vest with a one-year cliff.**

Use a clean 60/40 split on a 10,000,000-share pool (so the departing 40% founder holds 4,000,000 shares). For each case, state: how much the departing founder keeps, what returns to the pool, what the remaining cap table looks like, and **who is protected (or unprotected)** and how. Then write two sentences on why this single comparison is the strongest argument for vesting.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present; both cases worked with the actual share numbers.
- The protection effect is explained explicitly for each case.
- A two-sentence takeaway ties it back to "vesting protects everyone, especially the founder who stays."
- Committed.

**Hint.** In case (a), the departing founder keeps all 4,000,000 shares forever — a dead-weight 40% on the cap table that makes the company hard to recruit into and hard to fund. In case (b), at month three the cliff hasn't hit, so they vested **zero** and all 4,000,000 return to the pool. The contrast is the whole point.

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

## Problem 3 — The unhappy-path (departure) clause

**Problem statement.** At `notes/departure-clause.md`, draft in plain language what your agreement says when a founder leaves. Cover all four:

1. **Unvested equity** — what happens to it and at what repurchase price.
2. **Vested equity** — does the company have a buyback right, at what price, and does it depend on *how* the person leaves?
3. **Good leaver / bad leaver** — define, in under 150 words, what makes someone each.
4. **IP and accounts** — how work product, credentials, and accounts transfer to the company (reference the CIIA).

Then flag every part you'd want a lawyer to review before signing.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present; all four elements addressed.
- A concrete good-leaver/bad-leaver definition is written (not hand-waved).
- Lawyer-review flags are noted explicitly.
- Every clause passes the "would you say it to their face?" test.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Write the unhappy path while everyone is happy — it's the only fair time, because today neither founder knows which one will be the leaver. Don't make the vested-equity buyback punitive; that's the clause most likely to fail the say-it-to-their-face test.

**Estimated time.** 1 hour.

## Problem 4 — Vesting schedule + 83(b) reminder for every founder

**Problem statement.** At `notes/vesting-and-83b.md` (or a linked spreadsheet), produce a four-year / one-year-cliff vesting schedule for **each** founder in your venture (including you), with cumulative vested shares marked at months 0, 12, 24, 36, and 48. Then add an **83(b) block**: each founder's grant date, the grant-date-plus-30-days deadline, and the named person responsible for making sure each election is filed.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- A vesting table per founder with correct numbers and the cliff marked at month 12.
- An 83(b) block with each founder's 30-day deadline and a single named owner.
- One sentence in your own words on *why* the 83(b) is almost always worth filing.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Per-month vest = total ÷ 48. At the cliff (month 12), 25% vests at once. The 83(b) lets you pay tax on near-worthless founder stock now instead of on its appreciating value as it vests — and the 30-day clock has no extensions, so a named owner per founder is the safeguard.

**Estimated time.** 1 hour 15 minutes.

## Problem 5 — Control-vs-wealth consistency reflection

**Problem statement.** Write a 350–500 word reflection at `notes/week-04-reflection.md` answering:

1. Pull up your Week 1 control-vs-wealth lean. Now look at the split, the decision-rights table, and the vesting you decided this week. Are they **consistent** with that lean? Where, specifically, do they agree, and where (if anywhere) do they pull against it?
2. Which conversation this week did you most want to avoid — the split, decision rights, or the departure clause? Why? What did writing it down change?
3. If a prospective co-founder read your whole agreement draft tonight, which single clause would you be most nervous about them reading carefully — and what does that nervousness tell you?
4. What is one thing about co-founders, equity, or vesting you believed before this week that turned out to be wrong or incomplete?

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present, 350–500 words, each numbered question addressed in its own paragraph.
- Question 1 explicitly references your Week 1 lean and your Week 4 decisions.
- Honest, specific, first-person (this is for future-you, not for show).
- Committed.

**Hint.** The nervous-clause question (3) is doing real work — the clause you'd be nervous about is usually the one that doesn't yet pass the "say it to their face" test, which means it's the one to fix before the mini-project.

**Estimated time.** 30 minutes.

## Grading rubric

Each problem is graded out of its weight. "Strong" requires the reasoning, not just a filled-in template.

| Problem | Weight | What "strong" looks like |
|--------:|-------:|--------------------------|
| 1 — Decision rights | 20% | No blank final-call cells; real spend threshold; explicit tiebreaks; consistent with the control/wealth lean |
| 2 — Cliff experiment | 20% | Both regimes computed with real share numbers; the protection effect explained for each; a sharp takeaway |
| 3 — Departure clause | 25% | All four elements; a concrete good/bad-leaver definition; lawyer flags; passes say-it-to-their-face |
| 4 — Vesting + 83(b) | 20% | Correct per-founder schedules with the cliff marked; an 83(b) block with deadlines and a named owner |
| 5 — Reflection | 15% | Honest, specific, explicitly ties Week 4 decisions back to the Week 1 lean |

**Passing bar:** 70% overall. Anything under, revise and resubmit — these artifacts are the spine of your mini-project, so weak homework makes the agreement weak.
