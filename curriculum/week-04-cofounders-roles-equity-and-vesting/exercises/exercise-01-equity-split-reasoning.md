# Exercise 1 — Equity-Split Reasoning

**Goal:** Practice arriving at an equity split from its real inputs, and *defending* it input by input — so that the split you commit to in the mini-project is a decision you can read aloud, not a reflex you fell into. The number you land on matters less than whether you can justify it under stress.

**Estimated time:** 45 minutes.

**You produce:** a Markdown file, `exercise-01-equity-split-reasoning.md`, in your Week 4 repo, with two scenarios worked.

---

## Before you start

Keep two things open:

1. Your **Week 1 founder brief** — specifically your control-vs-wealth lean.
2. **Lecture 1 §3** — the five inputs (idea/prior work, time, capital, skill/replaceability, opportunity cost).

The discipline of this exercise is: for every split, write the *why* next to the *number*, weighted across all five inputs. A split with no written reasoning is exactly the resentment bomb the lecture warns about.

---

## Scenario A (given) — Ana and Ben

This is the worked example from the lectures. Pin down the reasoning yourself before you peek at the lecture's answer.

**Facts:**
- **Ana** had the idea, has been full-time for six months, spent ~$5,000 of her own money on the first prototype and early outreach, and brings warm access to the exact customers the product serves.
- **Ben** joins now, full-time. He brings the engineering ability Ana lacks and cannot afford to hire — he's the reason the product can actually get built. No capital.

Fill in this table for Scenario A, then propose a split and justify it:

```markdown
## Scenario A — Ana / Ben

| Input | Ana | Ben | Who it favors | Weight (low/med/high) |
|-------|-----|-----|---------------|------------------------|
| Idea / prior work       | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Time (going forward)    | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Capital                 | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Skill / replaceability  | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Opportunity cost        | ... | ... | ... | ... |

**Proposed split:** Ana __ / Ben __
**Reasoning (2–4 sentences):** ...
```

Then answer two follow-ups in prose:

1. **What changes if Ben can only commit part-time** (keeps his job, nights and weekends, until you raise)? Re-propose the split and say why it moved.
2. **The generosity check:** the lecture notes that a demotivated co-founder is more expensive than a few equity points. Does that argument push your Scenario-A number? Should Ana go *more* generous than the five inputs strictly justify? State your view in one or two sentences.

## Scenario B (yours) — your real situation

Now run the same analysis on **your** company — the one you're carrying through C25. Use your closest-to-real situation: an actual prospective co-founder if you have one, or the most realistic version if you're still solo.

```markdown
## Scenario B — mine

Founders (roles/initials): ...

| Input | Founder 1 | Founder 2 | Who it favors | Weight |
|-------|-----------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| Idea / prior work       | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Time (going forward)    | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Capital                 | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Skill / replaceability  | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Opportunity cost        | ... | ... | ... | ... |

**Proposed split:** __ / __ (/ __ if three founders)
**Reasoning:** ...
**Control-vs-wealth tie-in:** my Week 1 lean was ____, and this split is consistent with it because ...
```

The **control-vs-wealth tie-in is mandatory.** If your split contradicts your stated lean (e.g., you said "control" and you're giving away 60%), you must either fix the split or explicitly note that your lean has changed and why. An unexamined contradiction here is the single most common mistake in this exercise.

### If you are solo

Treat Scenario B as two things stacked:
1. Your **own founder vesting** (yes, solo founders vest — Lecture 2 §1; investors will require it, and it protects a future co-founder relationship).
2. The **equity terms you would offer your first key hire** — the person filling the biggest gap in your skill set. Run the five-input analysis as if you were deciding *their* slice.

## Worked snippet (so you know the altitude)

Here's the *shape* of a good Scenario-A answer (don't copy it — derive your own):

> Idea/prior work favors Ana strongly: six months full-time plus a working prototype is real, de-risking value that already exists. Capital favors Ana (medium weight — $5k is meaningful at this stage but not decisive). Skill/replaceability is roughly even: Ana's customer access is rare, but Ben is currently *the only person who can build the thing*, which is at least as rare. Time and opportunity cost are even going forward. Net: a modest tilt to Ana. **Proposed: 55/45.** It credits Ana's head start and risk without making Ben feel like a hired hand — and both of us can explain it, which is the test that matters.

Notice it (a) touches every input, (b) weights them, (c) lands on a defensible number, and (d) names *why* not just *what*.

## Hints

<details>
<summary>What makes a split "fair"?</summary>

A fair split is one **both parties understand and accept under stress** — not necessarily 50/50, and not necessarily the output of a formula. The five inputs are a thinking tool to surface what each person actually brings; the conversation is where "fair" is decided. If you can't say your number out loud to your co-founder's face, it isn't fair yet.

</details>

<details>
<summary>How much should prior work really count?</summary>

It's the input founders most often distort. A useful frame: prior work matters to the extent it *de-risked the company* or *created value that still exists today* — a working prototype, real customer commitments, a key hire landed. "I've been thinking about this for two years" is not prior work. "I built the v0 that's getting users" is.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I'm solo and this feels artificial.</summary>

It isn't. The moment you bring on a key hire, a co-founder, or even a serious advisor, you'll run exactly this analysis under time pressure. Doing it now, calmly, builds the reflex. And your *own* founder vesting plus 83(b) (Exercise 3) is real and necessary regardless of headcount.

</details>
