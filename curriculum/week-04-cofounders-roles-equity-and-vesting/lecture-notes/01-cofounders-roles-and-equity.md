# Lecture 1 — Co-founders, Roles, and Equity

> **Duration:** ~2 hours of reading + the conversation it forces you to have.
> **Outcome:** You can decide solo vs. co-founders deliberately, assign decision rights for every decision that actually causes founder fights, and reason about an equity split from its real inputs — then defend your number out loud, input by input.

If you remember one sentence from this lecture, remember this:

> **Co-founder conflict is one of the most-cited reasons early startups die — and almost all of it is preventable by deciding roles, decision rights, and the equity split in writing, early, while the company is still worth nothing.**

The paperwork is the easy part. A lawyer or a template can paper a clean split in an afternoon. The hard part is the *conversation* — having it honestly, having it early, and writing down what you decided so neither of you can quietly rewrite the memory later. That is the entire job of this lecture: to make you brave enough, and equipped enough, to have the conversation now.

---

## 1. The trade you are actually making: solo vs. co-founders

The first decision is the biggest, and most founders make it by accident. They have a friend, the friend is around, the friend becomes a "co-founder," and nobody ever decided anything. Let's decide it on purpose.

**Solo founder.** You own everything, you decide everything, and there is no one to fight with about equity because there is no split. That is the upside. The downside is real: you carry the whole load, you have no built-in accountability partner, you have nobody to catch your blind spots at 2am, and some investors are warier of solo founders because there is no one to share the founder's brutal workload or to disagree with you when you're wrong.

**Co-founders.** You get complementary skills, resilience (when one of you is in a hole, the other can pull), shared load, and someone who is genuinely on the hook with you. The price is that you have just signed up for the hardest set of people-decisions in this entire course — equity, decision rights, what happens when one of you wants out — and you have signed up to make them with someone whose judgment you are betting the company on.

Here is the filter that cuts through the romance of "we'll build it together":

> **Would I make this person a 50/50 partner in a small business, with my own money on the line, today?**

If the honest answer is "no, but they're great and I'd love to work with them," you have just discovered that this person is probably an **early employee, an advisor, or a contractor — not a co-founder.** That is not an insult. It is a different (and often better) relationship, priced differently. The failure mode is giving someone 30% of the company because it felt awkward to offer them a salary and 1%.

**Take co-founders for specific gaps you cannot fill, not for company.** If you are a strong technical founder with no idea how to sell, a co-founder who can sell is filling a real gap. If you are a strong seller who can't build, a technical co-founder is filling a real gap. If you're taking on a co-founder because building alone is lonely and scary — that's a real feeling, but it's a reason to find a co-working space or a founder community, not to give away half your company.

### Co-founder vs. employee vs. advisor vs. contractor

| Relationship | Typical equity | Vesting? | Decision rights? | When it's right |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Co-founder** | 10–50% (a *real* slice) | Yes — 4yr / 1yr cliff | Yes — shared, mapped | Fills a gap you can't fill; in it with you from near-zero |
| **Early employee** | 0.5–2% (options) | Yes — 4yr / 1yr cliff | No (input, not final call) | Joins after some validation; gets a salary |
| **Advisor** | 0.1–1% (FAST-style) | Yes — often 1–2 yr | No | Periodic guidance, intros, credibility |
| **Contractor** | 0% (cash) | N/A | No | A defined deliverable for money |

The numbers move with stage and contribution; the *categories* are what matter. The most expensive mistake in this table is paying co-founder equity for an early-employee relationship.

## 2. Roles and decision rights — titles are cheap, the tiebreaker is everything

Founders love to assign titles on day one. "I'm CEO, you're CTO." Fine. Titles are cheap and mostly for the outside world. What actually prevents fights is **decision rights**: when the two of you genuinely disagree and the clock is ticking, *who has the final call?*

A 50/50 split with two equal voices and no tiebreaker is a **stalemate machine**. It works beautifully until the first real disagreement, and then it deadlocks the company on the exact decisions that matter most. The fix is not to avoid 50/50 equity (that can be fine). The fix is to agree, *in advance and in writing*, who breaks ties in which domain.

Map the decisions that actually come up in year one. A blank cell in this table is not "we trust each other" — it is a **future fight with no agreed resolution path.**

| Decision | Final call | Notes / tiebreak |
|---|---|---|
| Product direction / whether to pivot | ? | The decision most likely to deadlock |
| Hiring and firing | ? | Especially firing — the hardest |
| Pricing and packaging | ? | |
| Fundraising: whether and on what terms | ? | |
| Any spend above $X | ? | Set X explicitly |
| Brand / public messaging | ? | |
| Equity grants to new hires | ? | |

The pattern that works for most two-founder teams: **divide domains by competence and give each founder the final call inside their domain.** The CEO gets the final call on fundraising, hiring, and go-to-market; the CTO gets the final call on architecture, technical hiring, and the build roadmap. For the genuinely shared decisions (a pivot, taking the company in a new direction), agree a tiebreak — sometimes "the CEO breaks ties," sometimes "we don't do it unless both agree," which is a deliberate veto. Either can be right; *undecided* is always wrong.

> **The "would you say it to their face?" test.** Read your decision-rights table aloud to your co-founder. If any row makes you wince — "I gave myself the final call on firing and I don't want them to notice" — that wince is the conversation you must have now.

### The CEO question — someone has to be

The decision founders most love to dodge: who is the CEO? "We're co-CEOs" sounds equal and egalitarian, and it works in roughly zero high-pressure moments. The outside world — investors, big customers, candidates, the press — wants a single point of accountability, a single person who speaks for the company and owns the final call when the company itself must move. Co-CEO arrangements are the rare exception that works only between unusually aligned, unusually mature pairs, and even then most eventually pick one.

This does **not** mean the CEO outranks the CTO as a human or owns more equity. It means: in the residual category of "decisions that don't clearly sit in one person's domain and we genuinely can't agree on," someone breaks the tie, and that someone is usually the CEO. Picking a CEO on day one is not a power grab; it's removing a future source of paralysis. If deciding *who's CEO* is itself an unbearable conversation, that is loud, early evidence about whether this partnership can survive harder conversations later.

### A worked decision-rights walkthrough

Take a real situation: month 8, the company is nearly out of money, and a mediocre acquisition offer lands — enough to pay everyone back and a little more, but not the outcome you dreamed of. The two founders disagree: one (more wealth-leaning, tired) wants to take it; the other (more control-leaning, stubborn) wants to push on.

If you have **no tiebreak** for "whether to sell," you now have a deadlock at the single most consequential moment in the company's life, with the clock running and lawyers waiting. If you wrote "the CEO breaks ties on a sale, after a mandatory 72-hour cooling-off and a conversation," you have a *process* — slow enough to prevent a panic decision, decisive enough to avoid paralysis. Notice the table didn't tell you *what* to decide; it told you *how* you'd decide, which is the only thing you can fairly agree on in advance. That's the whole craft of decision rights: pre-committing to a process, not to an outcome.

## 3. Equity splits: a decision, not a reflex

Here is the conversation founders most want to avoid, and the one most worth having. The reflex is to split equally — 50/50, or evenly across three — because it *feels* fair and it dodges an awkward negotiation. Sometimes equal is exactly right. But equal should be a **decision you arrived at**, not an **avoidance you defaulted to.** A split nobody can explain is a resentment bomb with a multi-year fuse.

Weigh these five inputs honestly:

**1. Idea and prior work.** Who started this? Who took the early risk — quit the job, spent the savings, worked nights for six months before anyone else showed up? Prior work is real and it is the input founders most often *under*-credit when a skilled latecomer joins, and most often *over*-credit when they're protecting their own ego. The honest question: how much of the company's current value exists *because* of work already done?

**2. Time commitment — now and going forward.** Full-time beats part-time, and it beats it by a lot. Someone "advising on weekends while keeping their job" is not contributing what a full-time founder is, and the equity should say so. Be explicit about *future* commitment too: "I'll go full-time once we raise" is a different deal from "I'm full-time now."

**3. Capital.** Who put money in, and how much, and at what risk? Cash invested at the riskiest moment (before there's anything) is worth more per dollar than cash that shows up later. Decide whether early cash is *equity* or a *loan* — conflating the two is a classic mess.

**4. Skill and replaceability.** How hard is each person to replace? A founder with a rare, validated skill the company depends on (a specific deep-tech competence, a key relationship, a domain reputation) is worth more than a founder doing work you could hire for. This is uncomfortable to say out loud, which is exactly why you must.

**5. Opportunity cost.** What did each person give up to be here? The founder who walked away from a $400k senior role is taking a bigger personal bet than the founder between jobs. It's a real input — though weight it carefully; the company doesn't owe anyone for a salary they *chose* to leave.

**The number matters less than the reasoning.** A 55/45 split that both founders understand and accept survives the first hard stretch. A 50/50 split that one founder secretly resents — because they know they're carrying more — corrodes from day one. When you write your split, write the *why* next to the number.

### The five inputs, each with a mini-case

The inputs are easy to list and hard to weight. Here's each one with a short case to calibrate your judgment.

**Idea / prior work — calibration case.** Maria spent two years "thinking about" a fintech idea and has slide decks. Dev joined three months ago and has shipped a working beta that 30 people use weekly. Who did more *prior work*? Dev — by a lot. "Thinking about it" is not prior work; *de-risking value that still exists today* is. The reflex to over-credit the originator is one of the most common split distortions. Credit the idea, yes — but credit it as a *spark*, not as half the company. Ideas are cheap; execution that exists is expensive.

**Time — calibration case.** Two founders agree on 50/50. One is full-time; the other keeps a day job "until we raise," contributing 10 hours a week. Eighteen months later they're still not raised, and the part-timer has contributed maybe a fifth of the full-timer's hours — but owns half. This is the most predictable resentment in startups. The fix isn't to refuse part-time co-founders; it's to make the equity *reflect* the asymmetry, or to vest the part-timer's equity against a clear commitment milestone ("your full stake assumes you go full-time by month 6").

**Capital — calibration case.** Sam puts in $50k of the $60k the company needs to reach its first milestone, at the riskiest possible moment. Is that worth equity? Yes — but decide *whether it's equity or a loan*, and price it as the high-risk capital it is. A common clean approach: the cash is a convertible loan or priced as a separate, explicit slug of equity, *not* silently blended into "Sam feels like he should get more." Make money contributions visible and named.

**Skill / replaceability — calibration case.** Both founders are competent generalists; either could do most of the other's job. Now neither is irreplaceable, so this input is roughly neutral — which actually pushes *toward* a more equal split. Contrast: one founder holds a specific deep-learning competence the entire product depends on and the company can't hire for. That rarity is real and the split should reflect it, however awkward it is to say.

**Opportunity cost — calibration case.** One founder left a $350k staff-engineer role; the other was between jobs. The first is taking a materially bigger personal bet *right now*. It's a legitimate input — but weight it modestly: the company doesn't owe anyone for a salary they *chose* to walk away from, and over-weighting opportunity cost lets the highest-paid person argue for the most equity regardless of contribution. Use it as a tiebreaker, not a driver.

### Rough equity benchmarks (sanity checks, not rules)

Numbers move with stage and country, but for calibration in 2026:

- **Co-founders:** meaningful slices — rarely below ~10% for a true co-founder; most two-founder teams land somewhere between 50/50 and 65/35.
- **First few employees (post some validation):** typically **0.5–2%** each, as options, vested.
- **Advisors:** **0.1–1%**, vested over 1–2 years (the FAST agreement is the standard template).
- **The option pool** you reserve for future hires at a pre-seed: commonly **10–15%** of the company, set aside before or during your first round.

If your draft has a "co-founder" at 3% or an "advisor" at 8%, the benchmark is telling you the *category* is probably wrong — re-run the §1 filter.

### A rule of thumb worth knowing (and not over-trusting)

A widely-cited piece of founder advice: **be generous and split closer to equal than your ego wants.** The reasoning — most of the value is in front of you, not behind you, and a demotivated co-founder who feels cheated is far more expensive than a few extra points of equity. There is wisdom here. Many experienced founders argue that the difference between 50/50 and 60/40 is rounding error compared to the difference between a motivated and a resentful partner. Hold this in tension with the five inputs: the inputs tell you what's *fair*; the generosity heuristic reminds you that *motivation* is also a real, valuable thing you're buying.

## 4. The handshake-split trap

> "We'll figure out the equity later."

This is one of the most expensive sentences in startups, and it sounds responsible — "let's not get bogged down in legal stuff, let's just build." Here is why it's a trap.

**Today**, the company is worth nothing, nobody has put in more than anyone else yet, and the conversation is low-stakes and friendly. **Later**, the company has value, your two memories of "who did what" have quietly diverged in each person's favor (this is human and universal), and the conversation is now a negotiation over real money between two people who each privately believe they did more. The friendly conversation became an adversarial one. And if you wait until you're raising, you'll be doing it under deadline pressure with an investor watching — the worst possible time.

The discipline: **decide early, write it down, and revisit only by explicit mutual agreement.** "Later" is not a plan; it's a deferred fight.

### Dynamic splits — the counter-argument, and why most still go fixed

You'll encounter the **dynamic / "slicing pie"** philosophy: instead of fixing the split up front, you accrue equity continuously based on logged contributions (hours, cash, relationships), so the split self-corrects as people contribute. It's intellectually honest, and for very early, pre-funding, informal teams it can defuse exactly the divergent-memory problem above.

So why do most fundable, venture-track companies still use a **fixed split with vesting**? Because investors, employees, and the cap table want *certainty* about ownership, dynamic models are operationally heavy (someone has to log everything, forever, and trust the log), and a fixed-but-vested split achieves most of the same fairness protection (you don't *keep* equity you didn't earn — see Lecture 2) without the perpetual bookkeeping. Know the debate. For the agreement you draft this week, use a fixed split *with vesting* — that's the fundable default — unless you can make a strong, specific case otherwise.

## 4a. Choosing and testing a co-founder *before* you split anything

A surprising amount of co-founder grief comes from splitting equity with someone you've never actually *worked* with under stress — only talked to over coffee while you were both excited. The split conversation in this lecture assumes you've already decided this is the right person. Here's how serious founders de-risk *that* decision first.

**Work together before you commit.** The single best predictor of whether a co-founder relationship survives is whether you've shipped something hard together, on a deadline, when it wasn't fun. A weekend hackathon, a 30-day build sprint, a paid trial project — anything that puts you both under real pressure and lets you see: Do they do what they say? Do they communicate when things slip? How do they handle being wrong? How do *you* handle *them* being wrong? (This is exactly why C25 pairs naturally with the hackathon course — a hackathon is a cheap co-founder stress test.)

**Run the hard-conversation checklist on purpose.** Before you split equity, deliberately have the uncomfortable conversations, because *how* someone handles these previews how they'll handle them when there's money at stake:

- What do you each want from this — life-changing wealth, a sustainable business, a quick acqui-hire, something to prove?
- How long is each of you willing to go without a salary? What's your personal financial runway?
- What does "full-time" mean to each of you, concretely, in hours and in saying no to other things?
- What happens to the company if one of you wants out in a year? (You're answering this formally this week — but ask it as humans first.)
- Who's the CEO? (Someone has to be. Avoiding this is itself an answer, and a bad one.)

If a prospective co-founder bristles at *discussing* these, that's priceless information — it's a free preview of the equity conversation, and it's cheaper to learn it now.

**Reference-check your co-founder like a hire.** You'd check references on a key employee; you're about to give this person more equity and more power than any employee will ever have. Talk to people who've worked with them. The question that gets honest answers: "Would you work with them again, and what would you watch out for?"

## 5. Control vs. wealth, made concrete

Back in Week 1 you named a lean: do you want to be **rich** or do you want to be **in charge**? You usually can't maximize both. This week is where that abstract lean becomes a number on a cap table.

- A **control-leaning** founder keeps more equity and more decision rights, takes on co-founders sparingly and for narrow gaps, and accepts a smaller or slower outcome in exchange for staying at the wheel.
- A **wealth-leaning** founder gives a strong co-founder enough equity and enough real decision rights to maximize the company's odds of a big outcome, accepting less personal control in exchange for a bigger expected pie.

The trap is **inconsistency.** A founder who declared "control" in Week 1 and then, to avoid an awkward talk, gives away 55% and the final call on fundraising has quietly chosen "wealth" by accident — and will resent the consequences. When you write your split and your decision-rights table, check them against your Week 1 lean. If they disagree, *one of them is wrong* — either your lean has genuinely changed (fine, write down why) or your split is drifting away from what you actually want (fix it now).

## 6. The worked example: Ana and Ben

We'll carry one example through both lectures, the exercises, and the challenge.

**Ana** had the idea. She has been full-time for six months, has spent ~$5,000 of her own money on the first prototype and early customer outreach, and brings warm access to the exact customers the product serves. **Ben** joins now, full-time, bringing the engineering ability Ana lacks and cannot afford to hire — he is the reason the product can actually get built.

A reflexive **50/50** ignores Ana's six-month head start, her capital, and her customer access. But Ana over-correcting to **70/30** ignores that, going forward, Ben is full-time and indispensable, and a resentful Ben is the most expensive thing on this cap table.

Walk the five inputs:

| Input | Ana | Ben | Lean |
|---|---|---|---|
| Idea / prior work | 6 months full-time, the prototype | None yet | Ana |
| Time (going forward) | Full-time | Full-time | Even |
| Capital | $5,000 at the riskiest moment | None | Ana |
| Skill / replaceability | Customer access (rare, valuable) | The only one who can build it (indispensable) | Even-ish |
| Opportunity cost | Left her job 6 months ago | Leaving his job now | Even-ish |

After an honest conversation, they choose **55/45** — close enough to feel like partners, tilted to credit Ana's prior risk and capital, but not so far that Ben feels like a hired hand with a founder title. Crucially, they can both *explain* the number. That's the test it passes.

Then — and this is Lecture 2 — they put **all** founder equity on a four-year vest with a one-year cliff. Hold that thought; it's what makes the 55/45 safe to commit to even though they've worked together for exactly zero days.

## 6a. The seven most common, most expensive co-founder mistakes

Patterns repeat across thousands of dead startups. Here are the ones this week exists to prevent, each with the cheap fix.

1. **The handshake split.** "We'll figure equity out later." Fix: decide now, write it down (§4).
2. **No vesting "because we trust each other."** A co-founder leaves in month three with a permanent, dead-weight stake. Fix: everyone vests, with a cliff (Lecture 2). Investors will require it anyway.
3. **Reflexive 50/50 to dodge the conversation.** Fine if it's a decision; corrosive if it's an avoidance. Fix: run the five inputs and write the reasoning (§3).
4. **Two equal voices, no tiebreaker.** The company deadlocks on its most important decision. Fix: the decision-rights table with a tiebreak on every shared call (§2).
5. **Co-founder equity for an employee relationship.** Giving 25% to someone who's really a great early hire. Fix: the "50/50 with my own money" filter (§1); pay salary + small options instead.
6. **Splitting with someone you've never worked with under stress.** The coffee-chat co-founder. Fix: ship something hard together first (§4a).
7. **A split that contradicts your own stated lean.** Declaring "control," then giving away the company by accident. Fix: the consistency check against Week 1 (§5).

Notice that *all seven* are preventable this week, on paper, for free. That's the entire pitch of Week 4: every one of these killed a company that could have spent ten minutes avoiding it.

## 6b. Three founders (and why odd numbers help with ties)

Most of this lecture assumes two founders, but a lot of teams are three, and three changes the math.

- **Equity:** the five inputs still apply, per founder. Resist the urge to default to even thirds *for the same reason* you resist 50/50 — make it a decision. A 40/35/25 that everyone can explain beats a 33/33/33 someone quietly resents.
- **Decision rights:** three is actually *easier* for tiebreaks in one way — you can use a majority vote where two founders would deadlock. But it's harder in another: a 2-against-1 dynamic can leave one founder repeatedly outvoted and slowly alienated. Name domains clearly so each founder owns their lane and nobody's permanently in the minority.
- **The freeloader risk grows.** With three people it's easier for one to under-contribute while the other two carry — which is *exactly* what vesting plus clear decision rights is built to surface and correct.

Four or more co-founders is usually a yellow flag — equity gets thin, decision rights get muddy, and "everyone's a founder" often means "no one's accountable." If you're at four, ask hard whether two of them are really early employees.

## 6c. The reserved option pool — equity you give away before you have employees

One number belongs on your day-one cap table even though it has no name attached yet: the **option pool.** When you set up the company, you reserve a slice — commonly **10–15%** — for future employees, advisors, and early hires you haven't met. It sits on the cap table as "reserved, unissued," and you grant from it as you build the team.

Two things to understand about it now, because they bite founders later:

**The pool dilutes the founders, not the investors — and investors know it.** When you raise, investors often require the option pool be expanded *before* the round, inside the pre-money valuation. The practical effect (you'll model this in Week 8) is that founders absorb the dilution of the pool, not the new investor. It's called the "option pool shuffle," and being blindsided by it is a classic first-time-founder cost. For now, just reserve a sensible pool (say 10%) so your Week 4 cap-table preview is realistic.

**Granting from the pool is how you reward non-co-founders fairly.** That great early engineer who is *not* a co-founder (you ran the §1 filter) gets 0.5–2% from the pool, as options, on a 4-year/1-year-cliff vest — a real, motivating stake without giving away co-founder-sized equity. The advisor who opens doors gets 0.1–1%, often via the standard FAST agreement, vesting over a year or two. The pool is the instrument that lets you be generous to the right people in the right amounts.

The point for *this* lecture: when you draft your split, leave room for the pool. A founder split that sums to 100% with no option pool reserved means every future hire's equity will come out of *your* slice, by surprise, later. Reserve it now, on purpose.

## 7a. What getting it wrong actually costs

It's easy to treat this week as a paperwork formality. To inoculate against that, here's what the *failure modes* actually look like in practice — the bills that come due when the day-one conversation gets skipped.

**The frozen cap table.** A co-founder with un-vested, un-papered equity leaves on bad terms holding 40%. They won't sell, won't cooperate, and now sit as a hostile blocking stake on the cap table. Every investor who does diligence sees it and walks. The remaining founder can't raise, can't fully recruit (no equity to offer that isn't tangled), and watches the company they're still building slowly suffocate — not because the product failed, but because the *structure* did. This is the single most common preventable death, and the cliff alone would have prevented it.

**The deadlocked decision.** Two 50/50 founders with no tiebreaker hit a fundamental disagreement — sell vs. keep going, pivot vs. persevere — and simply cannot move. The company stalls in the exact moment it most needed to act decisively. Often it dies not from the disagreement itself but from the *months of paralysis* around it.

**The slow resentment.** No blow-up, no lawsuit — just a split nobody could quite justify, where one founder always quietly felt they were carrying more. It corrodes for two years until the under-credited founder disengages, stops giving the company their best, and eventually leaves with a stake that no longer matches anything. The product was fine; the partnership rotted from an unspoken unfairness set on day one.

**The litigation tax.** When it goes all the way to a dispute, founders spend money they don't have and time they can't spare fighting each other instead of competitors — and the legal bills for an equity or IP fight dwarf, by orders of magnitude, the cost of the lawyer who would have papered it cleanly at the start.

Every one of these traces back to a conversation that was avoided because it was uncomfortable, at a moment when having it would have been cheap and easy. That asymmetry — cheap now, ruinous later — is the entire argument for doing the work this week.

## 8. Recap

You should now be able to:

- Decide solo vs. co-founders as a deliberate trade, and tell a co-founder apart from an employee/advisor/contractor using the 50/50-with-my-own-money filter.
- Build a decision-rights table with no blank cells and a tiebreak for every shared call.
- Reason about an equity split from its five real inputs, and defend a number out loud.
- Recognize and refuse the handshake-split trap, and state why dynamic splits lose to fixed-and-vested for fundable companies.
- Check your split and decision rights against your Week 1 control-vs-wealth lean.

## Frequently asked (the questions that come up every cohort)

**"My co-founder and I are best friends. Isn't a formal split insulting?"**
The opposite. The formal split is *how you stay friends.* Friendship survives a hard disagreement when there's a clear, pre-agreed structure; it dies when "who owns what" becomes a fight between two friends who each remember the past differently. The agreement protects the friendship by taking the money question off the table before money exists.

**"What if we genuinely can't agree on the split?"**
First, that's valuable information — a split you can't agree on now is a fight you're deferring, not avoiding. Try this: each of you independently writes down the split you think is fair *and the reasoning*, then swap and read. Most of the gap is usually a factual disagreement about contribution, not a values clash, and seeing each other's reasoning closes most of it. If you're still far apart after an honest pass, that's a real signal about whether to co-found at all.

**"Should the idea person get a big premium?"**
Usually less than they think. Ideas are abundant; execution is scarce. A premium for *prior work that de-risked the company and still exists* (a working beta, real customers) is justified. A premium for "it was my idea" alone tends to be small — and over-weighting it is a classic way to demotivate the person who will do most of the building from here.

**"We're a couple / siblings / longtime friends co-founding. Anything different?"**
Same instruments, *more* discipline, not less — because the relationship raises the cost of a bad outcome and the temptation to skip the hard conversations ("we'd never sue each other"). Vesting and clear decision rights matter *more* when the personal stakes are higher. And consider, explicitly, what happens to the company if the personal relationship ends.

**"Can we change the split later?"**
Yes — by explicit mutual agreement, in writing, the same way you set it. What you must not do is leave it vague *hoping* to revisit it; "we'll adjust as we go" with no mechanism is just the handshake trap wearing a disguise.

**"We're not incorporated yet — is it even worth writing this down?"**
Yes, and arguably *especially* now. A founders' agreement (sometimes called a pre-incorporation or memorandum of understanding) captures the decisions while they're fresh and friendly; you then carry it into incorporation in Week 9, where a lawyer translates it into the binding documents (the stock purchase agreements, the bylaws, the CIIA). Writing it pre-incorporation is normal and smart — it means the legal step is *transcription*, not *negotiation*. The worst version is incorporating first, splitting "evenly" by reflex on the incorporation paperwork, and only discovering the real disagreements months later.

## One last frame before Lecture 2

Everything in this lecture — the split, the decision rights, the CEO question — is about *who owns and who decides*. None of it is safe to commit to on day one unless there's a mechanism that ties ownership to *staying and contributing*. A 55/45 split is terrifying to agree to with someone you've worked with for zero days... unless that 45% is *earned over time* and *returnable* if they walk. That mechanism — vesting, the cliff, and the departure terms — is Lecture 2, and it's what turns every decision you just made from a leap of faith into a reasonable, reversible commitment.
