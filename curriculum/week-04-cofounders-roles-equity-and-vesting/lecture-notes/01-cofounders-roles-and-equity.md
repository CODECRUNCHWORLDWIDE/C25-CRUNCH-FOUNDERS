# Lecture 1 — Co-founders, Roles, and Equity

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can decide solo vs. co-founders deliberately, assign decision rights, and reason about an equity split from its real inputs.

> **Co-founder conflict is one of the top killers of early startups — and almost all of it is preventable by deciding roles, decision rights, and equity in writing, early.**

## 1. Solo vs. co-founders

- **Solo:** simplest control and equity, but heavier load, no built-in accountability, and some investors hesitate.
- **Co-founders:** complementary skills, resilience, and someone to share the weight — at the price of the hardest decisions in the course. If you take co-founders, take them for *specific* gaps you cannot fill, not for company.

A useful filter: would you make this person a 50/50 partner in a small business with your own money on the line? If not, they may be an early employee, not a co-founder.

## 2. Roles and decision rights

Titles matter less than **decision rights**: who has the final call when you disagree. Map them explicitly for the decisions that actually come up:

| Decision | Final call |
|----------|-----------|
| Product direction / pivot | ? |
| Hiring and firing | ? |
| Pricing | ? |
| Fundraising terms | ? |
| Spending above $X | ? |

A blank in this table is a future fight. Two people with equal say and no tiebreaker is a stalemate machine; agree in advance who breaks ties in which domain.

## 3. Equity splits: a decision, not a reflex

Founders often default to an equal split to avoid an awkward conversation. Equal can be *right* — but it should be a decision, not an avoidance. The inputs to weigh:

- **Idea and prior work** — who started, who took early risk.
- **Time commitment** — full-time vs. part-time, now and going forward.
- **Capital** — who put money in.
- **Skill and replaceability** — how hard is each person to replace.
- **Opportunity cost** — what each gave up to be here.

The number matters less than the *reasoning*. A split both founders understand and accept survives stress; a split nobody can explain breeds resentment the first time things get hard.

## 4. The handshake-split trap

"We'll figure out equity later" is one of the most expensive sentences in startups. Later, the company has value, memories diverge, and the conversation becomes adversarial. Decide early, write it down, and revisit only by explicit agreement.

## 5. Control vs. wealth, again

Your Week 1 lean shows up here directly. A control-leaning founder keeps more equity and decision rights and accepts a smaller, slower outcome. A wealth-leaning founder gives a strong co-founder enough to maximize the odds of a big outcome, accepting less control. Make the split *consistent* with the lean you already chose.
