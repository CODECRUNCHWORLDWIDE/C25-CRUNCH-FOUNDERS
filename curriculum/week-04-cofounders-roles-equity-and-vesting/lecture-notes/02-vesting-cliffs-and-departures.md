# Lecture 2 — Vesting, Cliffs, and Departures

> **Duration:** ~45 min. **Outcome:** You can explain vesting and the cliff, why they protect everyone, and what should happen when a founder leaves.

## 1. What vesting is

**Vesting** means a founder earns their equity over time rather than owning it all on day one. A common shape:

- **Four-year vest** — equity is earned in monthly increments over four years.
- **One-year cliff** — nothing vests until you have been there a full year; at the one-year mark, the first 25% vests at once, then monthly thereafter.

So a founder who leaves at month eleven, under a one-year cliff, takes nothing. A founder who leaves at month thirteen keeps roughly 25%.

```
Founder equity earned over time (4-year vest, 1-year cliff)
100% │                                          ┌────
     │                                    ┌─────┘
 25% │                          ┌─────────┘
     │  (nothing vests)         │ cliff: 25% at once
   0%└──────────────────────────┴────────────────────
     0        12 months         24        36       48
```

## 2. Why vesting protects *everyone*

Vesting sounds like distrust, but it is the opposite — it is mutual protection:

- It protects the **company and remaining founders** from a co-founder who leaves early with a large, dead-weight stake.
- It protects the **founder who stays and carries the load** from watching an absent partner keep equal ownership.
- It makes the equity returnable to the pool, so you can recruit a replacement.

Founders who skip vesting to "show trust" usually regret it the first time someone leaves. Investors will typically require it anyway, so doing it yourself early is a sign of seriousness.

## 3. The departure clause

Decide in advance what happens when a co-founder leaves:

- **Unvested equity** returns to the company (that is the whole point of vesting).
- **Vested equity** — does the company have the right to buy it back? At what price? Voluntary vs. involuntary departure may differ.
- **IP and accounts** — anything they built belongs to the company (Week 9 covers IP assignment).

Write the unhappy path while everyone is happy. It is the only time you can do it fairly.

## 4. Acceleration (a brief note)

You may hear about "acceleration" — equity vesting faster if the company is acquired (single-trigger) or if the founder is also let go after an acquisition (double-trigger). You do not need to master it now; just know the terms exist and that they are negotiable. Flag them for a lawyer.

## 5. Tie it back to control vs. wealth

Vesting interacts with control: a founder who keeps a controlling stake but does not vest it risks a frozen cap table if they leave; a founder who vests cleanly keeps the company financeable and recruitable. Whichever lean you chose in Week 1, vesting with a cliff is the standard, founder-friendly default — for *all* the founders.

> **None of this is legal advice.** It is enough to brief a lawyer well and to avoid the obvious traps. When real money or real partners are involved, get a professional to paper it.
