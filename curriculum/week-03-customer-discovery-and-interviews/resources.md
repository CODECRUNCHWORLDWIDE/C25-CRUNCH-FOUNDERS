# Week 3 — Resources

Customer discovery is a thirty-year-old discipline with a small canon of genuinely good material and a large swamp of recycled blog posts. The list below is curated and current to 2026. Most of it is free; where the best source is a paid book, the author's free summary is linked instead, and the idea is reproduced well enough in the lectures that you can pass the week without buying anything.

> **How to use this page.** You do not need to read all of it. For the week's work, the two **required** items below are enough. The rest is there for the founder who wants to go deeper or who hits a specific wall (recruiting, note-taking, synthesis) and wants the definitive source on that one thing.

## Required (work it into your week)

- **The Mom Test — free summary and book site (Rob Fitzpatrick).** The single most useful book on talking to customers. The core rule: ask questions even your mother could not lie to you about — i.e., about their life and past, not your idea. Free summary, talks, and excerpts:
  <https://www.momtestbook.com/>
- **Steve Blank — "Customer Discovery" (the canon).** Blank invented the term and the Customer Development methodology. His blog has the original, free, and still-correct material; start with the customer-development tag:
  <https://steveblank.com/category/customer-development/>

## Foundational reading and viewing (free)

- **Y Combinator — "How to Talk to Users" (Eric Migicovsky).** A 25-minute Startup School talk that is the best free condensation of discovery interviewing technique. Watch it before your first interview:
  <https://www.ycombinator.com/library/6g-how-to-talk-to-users>
- **Y Combinator Startup School library.** A curated set of short, free essays and talks on finding and interviewing early users:
  <https://www.startupschool.org/library>
- **Paul Graham — "Do Things That Don't Scale."** Why founders must personally recruit and talk to early users rather than automating it away:
  <https://paulgraham.com/ds.html>
- **Steve Blank & Bob Dorf — *The Startup Owner's Manual*, customer-discovery chapters.** The operational manual behind the method; the discovery section is the part for this week. Library copies abound and many chapters preview free.
- **First Round Review — customer-research field guides.** Practitioner essays from operators, free and high-signal:
  <https://review.firstround.com/>

## Method references (free, skim by need)

- **Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) — the "switch" interview.** A complementary framework: interview customers about the moment they *switched* from one solution to another to surface the forces of progress. Primer:
  <https://jtbd.info/>
- **IDEO / design-thinking interview methods.** Empathy-interview structure and ethnographic technique:
  <https://www.designkit.org/methods.html>
- **NN/g (Nielsen Norman Group) — user interviews.** Rigorous, free articles on interview technique, leading-question avoidance, and sample size:
  <https://www.nngroup.com/articles/user-interviews/>
- **Wikipedia — Customer development.** The origin and shape of the method, for a neutral overview:
  <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Customer_development>

## On the traps (read at least one)

- **Rob Fitzpatrick — "The Mom Test" core rules.** The compliment trap, the trap of pitching, and the three rules, condensed. Linked from the book site above.
- **Lenny's Newsletter archives — "vanity metrics" and "false positives" essays.** Why "everyone I talked to loved it" is a warning sign, not a green light. Search the free archives:
  <https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/>

## Tools you'll actually use this week

| Tool | Use | Notes |
|------|-----|-------|
| A **notebook or plain text doc** | Field notes during the interview | Lowest friction, no screen between you and the person. Capture exact words. |
| **Calendly / Cal.com** | Let interviewees self-book | Removes the back-and-forth that kills recruiting momentum. Cal.com is open-source/free. |
| **Otter.ai / phone transcription / macOS dictation** | Transcribe recorded calls | Only record *with explicit permission*. A transcript is not synthesis; you still cluster by hand. |
| **A spreadsheet (Google Sheets / Excel)** | Interview tracker: who, segment, status, date, key finding | Your recruiting funnel lives here. One row per prospect. |
| **Sticky notes (physical or Miro/FigJam)** | Affinity-clustering in synthesis | One finding per note. Free tiers are plenty. |
| **Git + Markdown** | The dossier itself | Write-ups and synthesis live in your repo as `.md`. Evidence in version control beats memory. |

None of these need to be paid. The discipline matters far more than the tool. A founder with a notebook and the nerve to make the call out-discovers a founder with a $200/month research stack and no nerve.

## Sample size and rigor (for the skeptics)

- **"Why you only need to test with 5 users" (Jakob Nielsen, NN/g).** The classic argument that small qualitative samples surface most problems. Note the nuance: it's about *iterative* rounds, not one-and-done:
  <https://www.nngroup.com/articles/why-you-only-need-to-test-with-5-users/>
- **Saturation in qualitative research.** The point where new interviews stop producing new findings. For a focused B2B segment this is often 8–15 interviews; for a broad consumer problem it can be more. Your dossier targets ten as a floor, not a ceiling.

## Communities to recruit from (examples — find yours)

- **Reddit** — niche subreddits for almost every profession and hobby. Read the rules; most ban self-promotion but allow honest research asks via DM: <https://www.reddit.com/>
- **Slack / Discord communities** — most industries have one or several. Ask a moderator before posting.
- **Indie Hackers** — founders helping founders; good for B2B SaaS segments: <https://www.indiehackers.com/>
- **Meetup & local trade associations** — for non-tech segments (clinics, contractors, restaurants), in-person beats online: <https://www.meetup.com/>
- **LinkedIn** — for B2B roles, a respectful "I'm researching X, can I ask 15 minutes?" note has a high hit rate when it clearly isn't a sales pitch.

## A short glossary

Keep this open in a tab.

| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| **Customer discovery** | Talking to potential customers to learn whether a problem is real, before building a solution. |
| **Get out of the building** | Steve Blank's rule: the facts you need are outside your office, not in it. |
| **The Mom Test** | Rob Fitzpatrick's rule for asking questions even a biased person (your mom) couldn't lie to you about. |
| **Leading question** | A question that hints at the answer you want ("Don't you hate when…?"). Poisons the data. |
| **Hypothetical** | A future/imaginary question ("Would you use…?"). People are bad at predicting their own behavior. |
| **Compliment trap** | Mistaking politeness ("great idea!") for validation. |
| **Painkiller vs. vitamin** | A painkiller solves an acute, must-fix problem; a vitamin is nice-to-have. You want painkillers. |
| **Workaround** | What the customer does *today* to cope. A strong, paid workaround is the best signal a problem is real. |
| **Commitment / advancement** | A customer giving up something real — time, reputation, money, a referral. The only "opinion" that counts. |
| **Signal vs. noise** | Signal is a repeated, independent, specific finding; noise is one-off opinions and politeness. |
| **Affinity clustering** | Grouping individual findings into themes to see the pattern. |
| **Saturation** | The point where new interviews stop teaching you anything new. |
| **Kill criteria** | Pre-agreed conditions (set in Week 2) under which you drop or pivot the idea. |
| **Persevere / pivot / drop** | The three decisions after synthesis: keep going, re-aim, or abandon. |
| **The surprise** | The unprompted, repeated finding that wasn't your hypothesis — often where the real idea hides. |
