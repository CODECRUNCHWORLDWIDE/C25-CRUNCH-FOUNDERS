# Week 3 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 4. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

**Q1.** The primary purpose of a discovery interview is to:

- A) Sell the customer on your solution.
- B) Learn the truth about a problem without pitching.
- C) Collect email signups for a waitlist.
- D) Get a testimonial quote for your landing page.

---

**Q2.** Which question yields the most reliable data?

- A) "Would you use an app that does this?"
- B) "Don't you hate it when this happens?"
- C) "Tell me about the last time this happened — what did you do, and what did it cost you?"
- D) "Do you think this is a good idea?"

---

**Q3.** The **compliment trap** is:

- A) Paying interviewees too much, which biases them.
- B) Friendly people saying your idea is great, which *feels* like validation but is worthless data.
- C) Complimenting the interviewee to build rapport.
- D) Only interviewing people who are your friends.

---

**Q4.** During a discovery interview, the interviewer should aim to talk roughly:

- A) 80% of the time — you must convey the full vision.
- B) 50% — an even back-and-forth.
- C) 20% — listen far more than you speak; use silence.
- D) 0% — stay completely silent the whole time.

---

**Q5.** According to *The Mom Test*, a question passes the test when:

- A) Your mother would give you an encouraging answer.
- B) It's about the customer's life and past, so even a biased person couldn't lie to you with it.
- C) It clearly explains your idea so they can give informed feedback.
- D) It can be answered with a simple yes or no.

---

**Q6.** You're testing whether a problem is a painkiller or a vitamin. The **strongest** evidence that it's a painkiller is:

- A) The interviewee says "yeah, that's definitely a problem."
- B) The interviewee already built a janky spreadsheet and pays for a tool to cope with it.
- C) The interviewee says they'd "probably pay" for a solution.
- D) Many people on Reddit complain about it.

---

**Q7.** Which of the following counts as real **signal** (commitment/advancement), not noise?

- A) "That's a really cool idea."
- B) "I could see that being useful."
- C) "Let me introduce you to two colleagues who have this worse than I do."
- D) "Good luck with it!"

---

**Q8.** In synthesis, why does counting **independent, unprompted mentions** matter more than counting total positive reactions?

- A) It doesn't; total reactions are the better metric.
- B) Because a finding many strangers raise without prompting is a pattern, while reactions you fished for or that came from one shared source are weak.
- C) Because investors only care about raw counts.
- D) Because unprompted mentions are easier to put in a spreadsheet.

---

**Q9.** You ran five interviews. The pain is real but happens only once or twice a year for most of your segment, and your Week 2 kill criterion said "kill if it happens less than monthly." The disciplined response is:

- A) Soften the kill criterion to "a few times a year" so the idea survives.
- B) Treat the criterion as tripped; drop or pivot, and consider whether a *different* (rarer-but-severe) go-to-market fits.
- C) Ignore frequency — severity is all that matters.
- D) Run ten more interviews until enough people say "monthly."

---

**Q10.** Across your interviews you set out to test "no-show reminders," but four of five keep raising "filling the empty slot after a late cancellation," unprompted and with real heat. This is best described as:

- A) Noise to ignore — stick to your hypothesis.
- B) The **surprise** — an unprompted, repeated finding that often points to the real (pivot) idea.
- C) Proof your original idea is validated.
- D) A reason to immediately start building both ideas at once.

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — A discovery interview learns; it does not sell. The other options are downstream sales/marketing activities, not discovery. (L1 §1)
2. **C** — Past-behavior, specific, cost-anchored. A and D are hypotheticals/compliment-fishing; B is leading. (L1 §4, §7)
3. **B** — Politeness reads as validation and is worthless data. The cure is to steer back to their reality, not say thank you. (L1 §5)
4. **C** — ~20%. Silence draws out the honest second half of the story; talking too much means you're pitching or leading. (L1 §8)
5. **B** — The Mom Test's core: ask about their life/past so the answer can't be a flattering lie. (L1 §3)
6. **B** — A real, effortful, paid workaround is the trail of effort that proves acute pain. A and C are talk; D is desk signal, weaker than a behavioral one. (L1 §6)
7. **C** — A referral spends reputation — real commitment. A, B, and D are polite noise. (L1 §6)
8. **B** — Independent unprompted mentions are what make a pattern defensible rather than cherry-picked. (L2 §6)
9. **B** — Check the criterion as written; a trip is a cheap win. Don't move goalposts (A) or grind for the answer you want (D). Rare-but-severe *can* be a business, but with a different GTM. (L2 §7–8)
10. **B** — The surprise is the unprompted, repeated, off-hypothesis finding where good pivots are born; chase it, don't ignore it (A) or build everything (D). (L2 §6, §8)

</details>
