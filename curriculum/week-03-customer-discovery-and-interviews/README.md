# Week 3 — Customer Discovery & Interviews

Welcome to **C25 · Crunch Founders**. Week 3 is the week the course turns from *thinking* to *doing*. You stop reasoning about your idea at a desk and you go talk to the people who have the problem. In startup folklore this is called **getting out of the building**, and it is the single highest-leverage skill a first-time founder can develop. It is also the one most first-timers skip, because it is genuinely uncomfortable: you have to ask strangers about their problems without pitching your solution, you have to shut up and listen, and you have to be willing to walk away having learned that your idea is wrong.

Done badly, customer discovery is theater — a founder collecting compliments from friends and calling it validation. Done well, ten honest conversations save you six months of building the wrong thing. This week teaches you the difference, gives you a repeatable interview process, and has you run **five real interviews** and synthesize them into a defensible pattern. You will reach ten by the capstone in Week 10; the discovery dossier you start this week is worth 25% of the capstone rubric — the single largest line item.

We come into this week carrying the output of Weeks 1 and 2. From **Week 1** you have a founder brief: the problem space you have real standing in, your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean. From **Week 2** you have a triaged idea — one problem chosen from your idea-triage log — and, critically, a set of **kill criteria**: the conditions under which you have agreed in advance to abandon or pivot the idea. Discovery without kill criteria is confirmation bias with a clipboard. This week, your interviews go looking for the truth, and your kill criteria decide what the truth means.

> **The one sentence to carry all week.** *Discovery learns; it does not sell.* The moment you describe your solution, the person across the table starts being polite — and politeness destroys data. Keep your solution in your pocket.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** a discovery interview (learning about a problem) from a sales call, a demo, a survey, and a focus group — four things first-timers regularly conflate.
- **Run** a 15-to-20-minute discovery interview that surfaces real problems instead of confirming your bias, using a script as a safety net rather than a cage.
- **Ask about the past, not the future** — turn "would you use…?" into "tell me about the last time this happened," and understand *why* the second produces data and the first produces fiction.
- **Recognize and defuse the compliment trap**, applying the discipline from *The Mom Test*: ask questions even your mother could not lie to you about.
- **Recruit** ten-plus relevant interviewees cheaply and ethically from your warm network, communities, referral chains, and respectful cold outreach.
- **Take field notes and write up** an interview within an hour, so the evidence outlives your memory and survives scrutiny.
- **Synthesize** several conversations into a pattern — is the problem real, frequent, and expensive? — and resist cherry-picking the quotes you liked.
- **Check the pattern against your Week 2 kill criteria** and make an honest **persevere / pivot / drop** decision, treating a kill as a *successful* week, not a failure.
- **Spot the surprise** — the unprompted, repeated complaint that wasn't your hypothesis — because that is where most good pivots are hiding.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed **C25 Weeks 1 and 2**, or have equivalent founder groundwork. Specifically:

- **A founder brief (Week 1):** a problem space where you have genuine standing, written down. Discovery is far easier when you can credibly say "I've lived this."
- **A chosen idea framed as a problem (Week 2):** not "an app for X" but "people in segment Y struggle with Z." If your idea is still phrased as a solution, reframe it as a problem before Monday — you cannot interview about a solution.
- **Kill criteria (Week 2):** the 2–4 conditions under which you will drop or pivot. Bring them; you will use them in synthesis on Sunday.
- **A reachable network:** at least a rough list of people, communities, or channels where your target customer plausibly exists. If you have *zero* path to your segment, that is itself a finding worth surfacing Monday.

You do **not** need any sales background, and you do **not** need a finished product, a landing page, or even a name. This week is deliberately solution-free. The temptation to "just show them a mockup" is the exact instinct we are training out of you.

## Topics covered

- Why "get out of the building" beats any volume of desk research, internal debate, or survey data.
- The four conversations people confuse: discovery interview vs. sales call vs. survey vs. focus group — and when each is appropriate.
- *The Mom Test* in practice: talk about their life, not your idea; ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future; talk less, listen more.
- Good questions (last-time stories, workarounds, spend, consequences) vs. bad questions (hypotheticals, pitches, leading questions, compliment-fishing).
- The compliment trap, the cheerleader bias, and how "that's interesting" is a smell, not a signal.
- Signal vs. noise: commitment and advancement (time, reputation, money, introductions) as the only opinions that count.
- Recruiting interviewees ethically and cheaply: warm network, communities, referral chains, cold-but-warm outreach, and incentives done right.
- Field-note discipline: capturing exact quotes and emotion in the moment without breaking rapport.
- Same-day write-ups: a structured format so a stranger (or future-you) can audit the evidence.
- Synthesis: affinity-clustering findings, counting independent mentions, separating *frequency* from *severity*, and naming the dominant workaround.
- The "vitamin vs. painkiller" test, and how the strength of the existing workaround tells you how acute the pain really is.
- Checking findings against kill criteria; the persevere / pivot / drop decision; pivots that emerge from the surprise.

## In-class activities

- **Role-play, then critique.** Pairs run a five-minute interview while a third person tallies every leading question and every accidental pitch. Almost everyone pitches in the first thirty seconds. Watching yourself do it is the lesson.
- **Question surgery.** Take a batch of bad questions ("Would you pay for this?" "Don't you hate it when…?") and rewrite each one as a past-behavior question ("Last time this came up, what did you do — and what did it cost you?").
- **The compliment intercept.** Practice the reflex move: when someone says "that's a great idea," you do *not* say thank you. You say "what's the hardest part of dealing with this today?" and steer back to their reality.
- **Pattern wall.** After interviews, write each finding on a sticky note (one finding per note, in the customer's words) and cluster them on a wall. The clusters are your pattern; the biggest cluster you didn't predict is your surprise.

## Worked example

A founder is testing a clinic-no-show idea — software to reduce patients who book and don't show. Her instinct in interview one is to ask, "Would you use an app that reduces no-shows?" The office manager replies, "Sure, sounds useful." That is a polite nothing: zero data, but it *feels* like a yes, which is exactly how founders fool themselves.

She catches herself and switches to the past: "Tell me about last week. How many no-shows did you have, what happened, and what did you do about it?" Now the conversation produces evidence. She learns the front desk already pays for *two* reminder tools, that neither handles the **waitlist**, and that a single no-show on a specialist slot costs the clinic a specific, quotable dollar figure. She also hears, unprompted and with real heat, that the manager's actual nightmare is the *empty slot* — once someone cancels at 9 a.m. for a 2 p.m. appointment, filling that hole by hand is the painful, recurring, expensive job.

That last part is the **surprise**. The founder came in selling "reminders to reduce no-shows" and walked out with evidence pointing at "automatic waitlist fill for last-minute cancellations" — a sharper, more painful, less-served problem she never would have found by pitching. Five interviews later, three of five clinics describe the same empty-slot pain in the same words. That is a pattern, and it survives her kill criteria. She pivots the idea and keeps interviewing toward ten. The pitch she'll be judged on includes the cancellation log — real numbers from real clinics — which is exactly the evidence judges trust most.

## The "no pitch" promise

C25 uses one recurring marker for this week. After every interview, before you write it up, ask yourself:

> **Did I pitch? Did they commit anything real — time, a referral, money, a follow-up — or just say nice things?**

If the only thing you got was "sounds great," you ran a compliment-collection session, not a discovery interview. Redo it with a different person and a tighter script. The point of Week 3 is to make the *non*-pitch interview ordinary.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Read *The Mom Test* by Rob Fitzpatrick end to end (it's short, ~130 pages). The summary on the author's site is free: <https://www.momtestbook.com/>.
- Watch Steve Blank's free **"How to Build a Startup"** customer-discovery lessons on Udacity (course EP245), or read the *Four Steps to the Epiphany* discovery chapters.
- Run a **sixth** interview with someone who you suspect does *not* have the problem, specifically to find the boundary of your segment. Disconfirming interviews are gold.
- Re-interview your very first subject after your script tightened. Compare the data quality. The delta is the lesson.
- Write a one-page note to your future self: which of your Week 2 assumptions did the interviews *change*, and which did they confirm? Be specific about what surprised you.
