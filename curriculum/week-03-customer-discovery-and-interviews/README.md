# Week 3 — Customer Discovery & Interviews

This is the week the course turns from thinking to *doing*. You get out of the building. Customer discovery is the single highest-leverage founder skill, and it is the one most first-timers skip because it is uncomfortable: you have to ask people about their problems without pitching your solution, and you have to be willing to hear that your idea is wrong. Done well, ten honest conversations save you six months of building the wrong thing.

The deliverable is five completed discovery interviews (write-ups) plus a first pattern summary. You will reach ten by the capstone.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Run** a discovery interview that surfaces real problems instead of confirming your bias.
- **Ask about the past, not the future** — "tell me about the last time this happened" beats "would you use…".
- **Avoid the compliment trap** — friendly "yes, sounds great" is worthless data.
- **Recruit** interviewees from your reachable network and cold-but-warm channels.
- **Write up** an interview so the evidence outlives your memory.
- **Synthesize** several conversations into a pattern: is the problem real, frequent, and painful?

## Topics covered

- Why "get out of the building" beats any amount of desk work
- Good questions (past behavior, workarounds, cost) vs. bad questions (hypotheticals, pitches, leading)
- The compliment trap and how to defuse it ("what would have to be true for this to be a problem for you?")
- Recruiting interviewees ethically and cheaply
- Note-taking and write-up discipline
- Synthesis: spotting the pattern, and checking it against your Week 2 kill criteria

## In-class activities

- **Role-play, then critique.** Pairs run a five-minute interview while a third person tallies every leading question and pitch. Almost everyone pitches in the first thirty seconds.
- **Question surgery.** Take a batch of bad questions ("Would you pay for this?") and rewrite each as a past-behavior question ("When did you last pay to solve this, and what did you buy?").
- **Pattern wall.** After interviews, write each finding on a sticky note and cluster them. The clusters are your pattern.

## Worked example

A founder testing the clinic-no-show idea asks her first interviewee, "Would you use an app that reduces no-shows?" The answer is a polite "sure, sounds useful" — useless. She switches to the past: "Tell me about the last week you had no-shows. What happened, what did it cost, what did you do about it?" Now she learns the front desk already pays for two reminder tools, neither of which handles waitlists, and that a single no-show costs a specific dollar amount. That is real evidence — and it sharpens the idea toward "waitlist auto-fill," which she never would have found by pitching.
