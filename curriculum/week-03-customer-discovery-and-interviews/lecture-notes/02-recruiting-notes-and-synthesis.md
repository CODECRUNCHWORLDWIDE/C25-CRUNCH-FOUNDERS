# Lecture 2 — Recruiting, Notes, and Synthesis

> **Duration:** ~1.5 hours of reading + a synthesis walkthrough.
> **Outcome:** You can fill a recruiting funnel with ten-plus relevant people cheaply and ethically, run a tight interview arc, write up a conversation within the hour so the evidence survives, and turn five-then-ten write-ups into a defensible pattern with an honest persevere/pivot/drop decision.

Lecture 1 taught you how to *talk*. This lecture is the unglamorous machinery around the talking: getting people in the chair, capturing what they said, and turning a pile of conversations into a decision. Most founders are bad at this not because it's hard but because it's tedious, and tedium is where rigor either happens or doesn't.

---

## 1. Recruiting is a funnel, and it has lead time

The number one reason a founder ends Week 3 with two interviews instead of five is that they started recruiting on Wednesday. **Recruiting has lead time.** People are busy, calendars are full, and even warm contacts take a few days to schedule. Start Monday. Treat it like a sales funnel, because it is one:

```
Reach out to 20  →  ~10 reply  →  ~7 say yes  →  ~5 actually show up
```

Those ratios are realistic, not pessimistic. Plan for the no-shows. If you need five interviews, you need to *contact* roughly fifteen to twenty people. Build that list before you do anything else.

### Where to find people (cheapest first)

- **Your warm network.** The standing you claimed in your Week 1 founder brief should produce names. The person who has the problem, or who knows three people who do. Warmest, fastest, but watch the friend-bias from Lecture 1 — they'll be nice; you have to work harder to get past the politeness.
- **Referrals (the compounding source).** End *every* interview with "Who else has this problem that I should talk to?" One good interview becomes three. This is how you get from five to ten without burning out — and a referral comes pre-warmed by a trusted intermediary.
- **Communities.** Relevant subreddits, Slack/Discord groups, trade forums, LinkedIn groups, local meetups, trade associations. Ask honestly; do not spam. Message a moderator first.
- **Cold-but-relevant.** A short, respectful note to someone who clearly has the problem. Lower hit rate, but for B2B and niche segments it works better than founders expect — *if* it's obviously research, not a sale.

### The outreach message that works

Short, specific, no pitch, asks for a small ask:

> *"Hi [name] — I'm doing research on how [specific segment] handle [specific problem]. I'm not selling anything; I'm just trying to understand the problem. Could I ask you 15 minutes about your experience this week or next? Happy to work around your schedule."*

Why it works: it names a specific person/segment, it's explicitly not a sale (lowers their guard), it's a small time ask, and it offers to flex around them. Attach a Calendly/Cal.com link if you can — every back-and-forth email loses you prospects.

### Incentives — usually no, sometimes yes

For most B2B and professional segments, *don't* pay. People like talking about their problems to someone who listens, and money attracts professional survey-takers who'll tell you anything. For hard-to-reach or low-incentive consumer segments, a small gift card is acceptable — but know that paid subjects optimize for finishing, not honesty. When in doubt, lead with curiosity and respect, not cash.

## 2. Ethics — non-negotiable

You are asking people for their time and their candor. Earn it:

- **Be honest about who you are and why.** "I'm a founder/researcher studying this problem." Never pretend it's something it isn't.
- **It's research, not a sale.** Say so, and mean it. Don't bait-and-switch into a pitch.
- **Consent to record.** If you record, ask first, every time. If they say no, take notes.
- **Protect their privacy.** No private identifiers in your write-ups — use a role and a segment ("ER nurse, mid-size urban hospital"), not a name. Never publish anything identifiable without explicit permission.
- **Respect their time.** Ask for 15 minutes, and if you said 15, be willing to stop at 15. (If *they* keep going — great, that's a commitment signal.)
- **Don't waste it.** Show up prepared, with your script and a way to take notes.

Ethics isn't only the right thing; it's also good craft. People who trust you tell you the truth, and the truth is the entire product of this week.

---

## 3. The interview arc (15–20 minutes)

A loose structure that leaves room to follow curiosity:

1. **Frame (1 min).** "I'm researching how people handle [problem]. No pitch — I genuinely just want to learn from your experience. Mind if I ask about how this works for you? And is it okay if I take notes?" Set the no-pitch expectation explicitly; it relaxes them and disciplines you.
2. **Warm-up / context (2 min).** A soft opener about their role and day. "Tell me a bit about what you do." Gets them talking and gives you the context to interpret everything after.
3. **The last-time story (7–8 min).** The heart of it. "Walk me through the last time [problem] came up. What happened? What did you do? What did it cost you — time, money, stress?" Follow every interesting thread. Use silence. This is 80% of your value.
4. **Workarounds and spend (4 min).** "What do you use today to deal with this? How well does it work? What's missing? What do you pay for it?" Hunt for the workaround; if it exists, make them show you.
5. **The surprise prompt (1–2 min).** "What's the most frustrating part of all this that I haven't asked about?" / "What else should I have asked?" This question routinely surfaces the pivot.
6. **Referrals + close (1–2 min).** "Who else has this problem that I should talk to? Could you introduce me?" Then thank them, sincerely, and stop.

Through all of it, the Lecture 1 rules hold: talk ~20%, never pitch, anchor in the past, chase compliments back to reality.

## 4. Field notes without breaking rapport

You need quotes, but staring at a keyboard kills the conversation. Tactics:

- **Capture verbatim quotes, especially emotional ones.** "It's a nightmare every Monday" is gold; your paraphrase "the user experiences Monday friction" is dross. Write their exact words in quotation marks.
- **Use shorthand and fill in later.** Jot keywords and quotes during; expand to full sentences immediately after.
- **A notebook beats a laptop** for rapport — no screen-wall between you. A second person taking notes (so the interviewer can stay present) is even better when you can swing it.
- **Record only with consent**, and even then, take light notes — a transcript you never re-read is not data. The recording is a backup; your notes are the synthesis input.
- **Mark your own reactions.** When something surprises you or trips a kill criterion, flag it in the margin (a star, a "!"). Those margin marks become your synthesis later.

---

## 5. Write it up immediately — the same day, ideally the same hour

Memory decays fast and *flatters*. Within hours you'll remember the parts that confirmed your idea and forget the parts that threatened it. So write up each interview the same day, to a structured file: `discovery-dossier/interviews/interview-0N.md`. Use the template from [Exercise 2](../exercises/exercise-02-interview-writeup-template.md). It captures:

- **Segment / role** (no private identifiers) and date.
- **Context** — who they are, why they plausibly have the problem.
- **The problem in their words** — quotes, not your gloss.
- **The last-time story** — what happened, what it cost.
- **Current workaround** — what they use/pay today, and what's missing.
- **Signal** — any commitment (time, referral, money, willingness)?
- **Surprises** — anything you didn't expect, especially if repeated.
- **Support / threaten** — one line: does this support or threaten your idea, and does it trip a kill criterion?

The discipline that separates real founders from theater-founders: **write up the interviews that went badly with the same care as the ones that went well.** The disconfirming interview is the one your future self most needs and most wants to forget.

## 6. Synthesis: turn conversations into a pattern

After five (then ten) write-ups, you stop collecting and start *making sense*. This is where most founders either find the truth or rationalize past it. Use a method, not vibes. The full canvas is [Exercise 3](../exercises/exercise-03-synthesis-and-pattern-canvas.md); here's the thinking.

### Affinity-cluster the findings

Pull every distinct finding onto its own sticky note (physical, Miro, or FigJam) — one finding per note, in the customer's words. Then cluster notes that say the same thing. The clusters *are* your themes. The biggest cluster is your dominant pattern; a cluster you didn't predict is your surprise.

### Count independent mentions — this is the rigor

For each theme, count **how many distinct people raised it unprompted**. "4 of 5 brought up the empty-slot problem without me mentioning it" is a real finding. "One person said something I liked" is not. Independence matters: three referrals from the same source who all share a worldview is weaker than three strangers converging.

### Separate frequency from severity

Two different axes, and founders constantly conflate them:

- **Frequency:** how *often* does the problem occur? (Daily? Monthly? Once a year?)
- **Severity:** how *much* does it hurt when it does? (Mildly annoying? Costs $5k? Loses a customer?)

The best problems are **frequent AND severe** (the obvious target). Frequent-but-mild is a vitamin in disguise — annoying but never urgent enough to pay for. Rare-but-severe can be a real business (think disaster recovery) but needs a different go-to-market. Plot your themes on this 2×2; it clarifies which problem to chase.

### Name the dominant workaround

Across interviews, what do people *do today*? A shared workaround is a product-shaped hole in the market. If four of five keep a manual spreadsheet for the same thing, the spreadsheet is your competitor and your spec. Describe it precisely.

### Find the surprise

The unprompted finding that wasn't your hypothesis. Recall the clinic example: the founder came in selling "no-show reminders" and the data kept pointing at "empty-slot waitlist fill." Surprises are where most good pivots are born. Take them seriously *especially* when they're inconvenient.

---

## 7. Check the kill criteria — the moment of honesty

Now pull out the **kill criteria you wrote in Week 2**. For each one, ask the data: *did we trip it?*

Examples of kill criteria and what tripping them looks like:

- *"Kill if fewer than half describe this as a top-3 problem."* → You wrote up 5, only 1 ranked it top-3. **Tripped.**
- *"Kill if no one has spent money or real effort trying to solve it."* → Nobody had a workaround beyond shrugging. **Tripped.**
- *"Kill if the pain is rare (less than monthly) for the segment."* → Most said "couple times a year." **Tripped.**

If the evidence trips a kill criterion, **that is a successful week.** You spent ten hours to avoid wasting ten weeks. The founders who fail this course aren't the ones who killed an idea — they're the ones who couldn't, who moved the goalposts until any data "validated" the thing they'd already decided to build.

---

## 8. The decision: persevere, pivot, or drop

Synthesis ends in one of three calls. Make it explicitly and write down the reasoning.

- **Persevere.** The pattern confirms a real, frequent, painful problem in your segment, and no kill criterion tripped. Carry the idea forward into Week 4 (team) and Week 5 (MVP). Keep interviewing toward ten to deepen the evidence.
- **Pivot.** Your original hypothesis is weak, but the data revealed an *adjacent* problem that's clearly more painful — usually the surprise. Re-aim the idea at the real pain and keep interviewing the (possibly new) segment. The clinic founder pivoting from "reminders" to "waitlist fill" is a textbook pivot.
- **Drop.** The pain is mild, rare, or the segment is basically happy. A kill criterion tripped and the evidence doesn't point anywhere better. Painful — but a tenth the cost of discovering it after you build. Return to your Week 2 triage log, pick the next idea, and run discovery again. This is not failure; it's the method working.

> **The trap to name out loud:** "I'll just build it and see." That's not a fourth option; it's a refusal to decide, and it's the most expensive choice on the menu. Decide on the evidence now, while deciding is cheap.

A note on sample size: don't over-index on five. Five is enough to *detect* a strong pattern or a clear kill, but a muddy result at five usually means "keep interviewing toward ten," not "force a decision." Saturation — when new interviews stop teaching you anything — is the real signal you've talked to enough.

## 8b. A worked synthesis, start to finish

Abstract steps are easy to nod along to and hard to do. Here's a complete synthesis on the ShiftFill-style data so you can see the machinery turn. Suppose six interviews produced these raw findings (one per line, customer's words):

```
I-01: scheduling the weekly rota eats 3 hrs every Sunday
I-01: a single swap breaks the whole spreadsheet
I-02: covering a sick call is "annoying but manageable"
I-02: no-call-no-shows are "the nightmare," nothing fixes them
I-03: short-staffed every weekend; pays a temp agency $25/hr
I-03: tried three staffing apps, all sent unreliable people
I-04: fully staffed, no real pain
I-05: solo-ish operator, covers shifts himself, no "pool" exists
I-06: weekend coverage hard; keeps a personal "always-yes" text list
```

**Cluster into themes (Part B):**

- *Theme A — last-minute coverage is hard:* I-03, I-06, partially I-05 → 2–3 of 6.
- *Theme B — weekly scheduling/rota pain:* I-01 (strong), lurking behind swaps elsewhere → 1 strong of 6.
- *Theme C — no-call-no-shows:* I-02 (very strong) → 1 of 6.
- *Theme D — no pain at all:* I-04 → 1 of 6.

**Count the signal (Part C):** Theme A has the most *spread* (3 sources) and the only real **spend** (I-03's $25/hr agency) and a **churned competitor** (three apps tried) — strong behavioral signal. Themes B and C are each single sources but *intense* and unprompted. Theme D is a useful boundary marker (who does NOT have the problem).

**Frequency × severity (Part D):** Theme A is frequent + high-severity *for a specific sub-segment* (busy weekend-driven venues), but frequent + mild for others (I-02 absorbs it). That split is the whole story.

**Workaround (Part E):** temp agencies, personal text lists, "I cover it myself." A shared, costly, low-hit-rate manual process — product-shaped hole, but only in the high-volume venues.

**The surprise (Part F):** two *different* unprompted problems (scheduling, no-shows) each surfaced with more heat than the original hypothesis from at least one founder. Worth logging as separate ideas, not chasing yet.

**Kill criteria (Part G):** "kill if <half call coverage a top-3 headache" — across all six, it's *not* a majority; across the weekend-venue sub-segment, it clearly is. So the criterion is tripped *for the broad segment* and passed *for the narrow one*.

**Decision (Part H):** a **segment-narrowing pivot** — re-aim at busy weekend-driven mid-market venues (where A is frequent + severe + paid-workaround), run three more interviews in exactly that sub-segment to confirm, and log scheduling/no-shows as separate ideas. That decision *falls out of the evidence*; it isn't a vibe.

Notice what made this work: counting spread, separating frequency from severity, and refusing to average a split segment into a mush. That's the difference between synthesis and wishful thinking.

## 8c. Outreach templates you can steal

Recruiting stalls when founders agonize over the message. Don't. Use these and move on.

**Warm network:**
> "Hey [name] — quick one. I'm researching how [segment] deal with [problem] and I remember you've got experience there. Not selling anything, just learning. Could I borrow 15 min this week? Here's my calendar: [link]."

**Community (after clearing it with a mod):**
> "Hi all — I'm doing research (not promotion) on how people here handle [problem]. If you've dealt with it recently, I'd love 15 min to hear your story. Happy to share what I learn back with the group. DM me or grab a slot: [link]."

**Cold-but-relevant (B2B):**
> "Hi [name], I saw you [specific, real reason they fit — role/post/company]. I'm researching how [role] handle [problem] — purely learning, nothing to sell. Would you be open to 15 minutes? I'll work entirely around your schedule."

**Referral handoff (give your interviewee an easy forward):**
> "Thanks again! If it's easy, could you forward this to [colleague]? — 'A founder I spoke to is researching [problem]; not selling anything, just wants 15 min to learn. Worth a chat.'"

The common thread: specific, explicitly not-a-sale, small ask, you-flex-not-them. Personalize the bracketed parts; don't personalize the structure.

## 8d. When the data is messy (and it always is)

Real discovery rarely hands you a clean verdict. A few rules for the muddy middle:

- **A split segment is a finding, not a failure.** "Painful for busy venues, not for slow ones" tells you *who your customer is*. Narrow, don't average.
- **One intense data point is a lead, not a conclusion.** I-03's $25/hr agency spend is exciting — but it's *one* person. Weight it, then go find two more like her before you bet on it.
- **Don't let the loudest interview dominate.** The most articulate, enthusiastic person is not automatically the most representative. Count spread, not volume.
- **Inconvenient surprises deserve more scrutiny, not less.** The thread that threatens your plan is exactly the one your bias wants to dismiss. Chase it harder.
- **"Keep interviewing" is a legitimate decision at n=5.** If the picture is genuinely split, the honest move is often more data with a sharper screen — not a forced persevere/pivot/drop.

## 8e. Note-taking, in depth

Your synthesis is only as good as your raw notes, and good notes are a learnable craft. The failure mode is two extremes: typing a verbatim transcript (which kills rapport and produces an un-synthesizable wall of text) or jotting three vague words (which evaporate by Saturday). Aim for the middle.

A practical system:

- **One page per interview, three columns or zones:** (1) *quotes* — exact words, in quotation marks, especially emotional ones; (2) *facts* — numbers, frequencies, costs, tools named; (3) *my reactions* — a margin star when something surprises me or trips a kill criterion.
- **Capture the emotional words verbatim.** "It's a nightmare," "I dread Mondays," "I just eat the cost." These survive into your pitch; your paraphrases don't. When someone's voice changes — frustration, resignation, excitement — that's a flag to write down the *exact* phrase.
- **Numbers are gold; chase them gently.** "A few times a month" → "Was it more like two or ten last month?" One concrete number ("a no-show costs us about $180 in chair time") is worth a paragraph of adjectives, and it's the kind of thing investors trust.
- **Don't transcribe live.** If you're recording (with consent), take *light* notes and trust the recording for backup — but still write the synthesis-relevant bits, because you will never re-listen to a 20-minute recording in full, and a recording you don't process is not data.
- **Expand within the hour.** Right after, turn your shorthand into the Exercise 2 write-up while the conversation is vivid. Two interviews in a row with no write-up between them is a recipe for blurring them together.

> **A two-person trick.** If you can bring a co-founder or cohort-mate, split the roles: one person interviews and stays fully present (eye contact, follow-up, no laptop), the other takes detailed notes silently. The interview quality jumps when the interviewer isn't also stenographer. Swap roles between interviews so you both build both muscles.

## 8f. Documenting for the people who'll judge you

Everything you write up this week has a second audience beyond yourself: the investors, advisors, and demo-day judges who will eventually probe your evidence. Write the dossier as if one of them will read it cold, because at the capstone, effectively, they will.

What earns their trust:

- **Real quotes from real (anonymized) people.** "An office manager at a mid-size clinic told me: 'the empty chair is the part that actually costs us.'" That sentence does more than any market-size slide.
- **Counts, not adjectives.** "4 of 6 independently raised X" beats "lots of people care about X." Judges can smell a hand-wave.
- **Concrete numbers customers gave you.** What they pay, how often it happens, what it costs them. These become your traction and pricing assumptions later (Weeks 6–7).
- **The disconfirming evidence, acknowledged.** A founder who says "here's the one segment where this *didn't* land, and here's why I scoped around it" is far more credible than one whose data is suspiciously unanimous. Nothing tanks credibility like a deck where every single customer loved everything.

What destroys trust: cherry-picked praise, "everyone I talked to was excited," fabricated or suspiciously round numbers, and any whiff that you only counted the answers you liked. Build the honest version now; it's the version that survives the hard questions at demo day — the capstone's "survives the three obvious hard questions" line item is literally graded on this.

## 8g. Troubleshooting a stalled funnel

You will hit walls. Here's a field guide to the common ones and what to actually do.

**"Nobody is replying to my outreach."**
- Your message probably reads like a sale or is too long. Cut it to three sentences, lead with "I'm not selling anything," make the ask 15 minutes, and attach a booking link.
- You may be reaching the wrong channel. A cold LinkedIn note to a busy exec converts worse than a warm intro or a relevant community post. Re-route.
- Volume problem: if you contacted eight people and three ghosted, that's normal — contact fifteen more. The funnel only works at the top of the funnel.

**"I can't find anyone with this problem."**
- This is itself a *finding*. If your target segment is unreachable to you, that's a real strategic problem — note it honestly (your homework asks for exactly this). It may mean the segment is wrong, or that your Week 1 "standing" was thinner than you thought.
- Try the workaround channel: where do these people *complain* today? Subreddits, review sites, support forums, trade Slacks. Lurk, then ask.

**"People say yes but don't show up."**
- Build in a reminder and make it frictionless (self-booked slot + a day-before nudge). Over-recruit: book seven to net five.
- A no-show can be a mild signal the pain isn't acute (they didn't prioritize 15 minutes about it). Note it, don't agonize.

**"My interviews keep turning into me pitching."**
- Re-run your Exercise 1 bias audit; a pitch usually traces back to a leading question in the script. Tape the good-questions table next to your screen.
- Try the discipline of *not letting your idea come up at all*. If you make it to the end without the interviewee knowing what you're building, you did it right.

**"Everyone is just being nice."**
- You're collecting compliments. Push into the past and the cost: "When did this last actually happen? What did it cost you? Show me what you do today." Politeness can't survive specifics.
- Get to *strangers*. Friends are nice by default; strangers with the problem are your real signal.

> **The meta-lesson:** a stalled funnel is data too. *Why* you can't reach or move your segment often tells you something true about the business — sometimes the most important thing you'll learn all week.

## 8h. The persevere/pivot/drop decision, illustrated

To make the three outcomes concrete, here's what each looks like in practice with the same starting hypothesis ("indie dental clinics struggle with no-shows"):

- **Persevere.** 4 of 5 clinics describe no-shows as a frequent, costly, top-3 headache; they pay for tools that don't fully solve it; no kill criterion tripped. *Action:* carry the no-show idea into Week 4, keep interviewing toward ten to deepen the cost evidence, and start thinking about your riskiest assumption for the Week 5 MVP.

- **Pivot.** No-shows turn out to be mild and well-handled, but 4 of 5 keep raising the *empty-slot-after-late-cancellation* problem, unprompted, with real heat and a manual workaround. *Action:* re-aim at "waitlist auto-fill," rewrite the script for that workflow, and interview the same segment (plus front-desk staff) with the new lens. The original idea is dead; a sharper one took its place.

- **Drop.** No-shows are rare, cheap to absorb, and nobody has a workaround; no adjacent pain surfaced either. A kill criterion tripped and the data points nowhere better. *Action:* return to your Week 2 triage log, pick the next-best idea, and run discovery again. You spent a week and avoided a quarter — that's the method working, not failing.

Write your decision as a short paragraph that names the evidence, the kill-criteria result, and the concrete next step. "I'll just build it and see" is not on this list; it's the absence of a decision.

> **One more honesty check before you commit.** Ask a cohort-mate or advisor to read your synthesis *without* telling them your preferred answer, and ask them what decision the data supports. If their read differs from yours, your bias may be doing the deciding. The whole point of writing it down is to let someone — including future-you — audit the reasoning.

## 9. How this compounds through the course

This week is the foundation the rest of the course builds on:

- **Week 4 (co-founders & equity):** you decide team and ownership knowing what problem you're actually chasing.
- **Week 5 (lean MVP):** your riskiest assumption — the thing you'll build the smallest test of — comes straight out of this week's pattern and surprise.
- **Week 6 (unit economics):** the "what do you pay today" answers seed your pricing and CAC assumptions.
- **Week 7 (go-to-market):** the channels where you *found* your interviewees hint at where your customers actually congregate.
- **Week 10 (capstone):** ≥10 synthesized interviews are 25% of the rubric — the single biggest line item — and the cancellation-log-style real numbers are the evidence judges trust most.

Discovery doesn't stop at the end of this week. It's the founder habit you carry for the life of the company.

---

## Key takeaways

- **Recruiting is a funnel with lead time** — contact ~15–20 to land 5. Start Monday.
- **Referrals compound;** end every interview asking for the next one.
- **Ethics are craft:** honest framing, consent to record, no private identifiers, respect their time.
- **Run the arc:** frame → context → last-time story → workarounds/spend → surprise prompt → referral/close.
- **Capture verbatim quotes** and **write up the same day** — memory decays and flatters.
- **Synthesize with a method:** affinity-cluster, count independent mentions, separate frequency from severity, name the workaround, find the surprise.
- **Check kill criteria honestly;** a kill is a successful week.
- **Decide explicitly: persevere, pivot, or drop** — "I'll just build it and see" is not an option.
