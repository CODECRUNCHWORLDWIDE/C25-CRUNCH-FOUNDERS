# Lecture 1 — Getting Out of the Building

> **Duration:** ~2 hours of reading + role-play.
> **Outcome:** You can explain why discovery beats desk work, distinguish a discovery interview from the three things people confuse it with, apply The Mom Test to keep an interview honest, and tell a good question from a bad one on sight.

If you remember one sentence from this entire week, make it this one:

> **Discovery learns; it does not sell.** The instant you describe your solution, the person across from you starts being polite — and politeness is the enemy of data. Keep your solution in your pocket and keep your mouth mostly shut.

That sounds simple. It is not. In the in-class role-plays, somewhere between 80 and 90 percent of first-time founders pitch their idea inside the first thirty seconds, usually without noticing. The reflex to sell is wired deep, and this lecture is about rewiring it.

---

## 1. Why "get out of the building"

Steve Blank — serial founder, Stanford and Berkeley lecturer, and the person who turned "talk to customers" into an actual methodology called Customer Development — has a phrase that has outlived a hundred trends: **"There are no facts inside your building, so get the hell outside."**

Here is what he means. Sitting at your desk, you can generate an unlimited supply of *opinions* about your customer. You can argue with your co-founder for a week about whether dentists would pay for X. You can build a beautiful spreadsheet. You can read every competitor's website. None of it is a fact. Every one of those is a guess wearing a confident voice. The facts — what the customer actually does, what actually hurts, what they actually pay for — exist only in the customer's head and the customer's behavior, and the customer is *outside your building*.

First-time founders resist this for predictable reasons:

- **It's uncomfortable.** Cold-messaging a stranger to ask for fifteen minutes feels like begging. Building a product alone in a room feels productive and safe. The safe thing is the trap.
- **It feels slow.** "I could have built the MVP in the time it takes to schedule five interviews." Yes — and you'd have built the wrong MVP. Discovery is the cheapest experiment you will ever run. An interview costs an hour; a wrong build costs three months.
- **You're afraid of the answer.** Some part of you suspects the idea might not survive contact with reality. That fear is *exactly* why you must do it. An idea that can't survive five conversations was never going to survive a launch.

Desk research has a role — you did it in Week 2 (search demand, existing alternatives, vitamin-vs-painkiller). But desk research can only tell you what *already exists in public*. It cannot tell you why the office manager at a clinic quietly keeps a paper waitlist in a drawer because none of the three tools she pays for handle it. That fact is worth more than every competitor teardown you'll ever write, and the only way to get it is to ask her.

> **The asymmetry that makes this worth it.** A discovery interview can *only* cost you about an hour. But it can save you a quarter. There is almost no other activity in a startup with that payoff ratio. Treat the discomfort as the price of the best deal you'll get all year.

## 2. The four conversations people confuse

People lump four very different conversations under "talking to customers." They have different goals, and mixing them up is how founders fool themselves. Know which one you're in.

| Conversation | Goal | Who talks more | When you use it |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Discovery interview** | *Learn* whether a problem is real, frequent, and painful | The customer (≈80%) | Now — before you've built or committed to a solution |
| **Sales call** | *Persuade* someone to buy or commit | You (the seller) | Later — once you have something to sell (Week 7+) |
| **Survey** | *Quantify* something you already understand qualitatively | Neither (it's async) | After discovery, to size a known pattern across many people |
| **Focus group** | Gather *group* reactions to a stimulus | Everyone, over each other | Rarely for founders; groupthink corrupts the signal |

This week is **only** the first row. The others are not bad — they're just wrong *now*.

- A **survey** is seductive because it scales: blast 500 people, get a chart. But a survey can only ask questions you already know to ask, and it gives you no ability to follow a surprising thread. Surveys are for *confirming* a pattern you found in interviews, not for *finding* it. A survey that says "73% are interested" is a vanity chart; you don't know what "interested" cost them to click.
- A **focus group** is actively dangerous in discovery: one loud person anchors the room, everyone converges on a socially acceptable answer, and you mistake the resulting consensus for truth. The whole point of a one-on-one is that the person can't perform for a crowd.
- A **sales call** is the one you'll keep sliding into by accident. The tell: you start talking about your idea. The cure is the entire rest of this lecture.

## 3. The Mom Test

Rob Fitzpatrick wrote a short, brilliant book called *The Mom Test*. The premise: most advice about customer conversations is "don't ask your mom if your idea is good, because she'll lie to spare your feelings." Fitzpatrick says that's the wrong lesson. The real lesson is that **it's your fault, not hers** — you asked a bad question. A good question is one that *even your mom can't lie to you about*, because it's about her life and her past, not about your idea.

His three rules:

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Your idea should ideally never come up. The conversation is about *them* — their day, their last bad experience, what they do, what it costs.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics or opinions about the future.** "How do you usually handle X?" invites a tidy, flattering fiction. "Walk me through the last time X happened" forces an actual memory.
3. **Talk less, listen more.** If you're talking more than about a fifth of the time, you're either pitching or leading. Shut up. Silence makes people fill the gap with the real story.

Worked contrast — same founder, same goal, two questions:

> **Mom-fails-the-test (bad):** "I'm thinking of building an app that helps you eat healthier. Do you think you'd use something like that?"
> Mom: "Oh that sounds wonderful, dear, of course I would."
> **Data gathered: zero.** She loves you. She'd say yes to anything.

> **Passes the Mom Test (good):** "When was the last time you tried to change how you eat? What actually happened — walk me through that week."
> Mom: "Well, in January I bought a meal-kit subscription, used it twice, and cancelled because the boxes piled up and I felt guilty throwing food away. I went back to ordering the same three takeout meals."
> **Data gathered: a lot.** A real attempt, a real failure mode (guilt + waste), a real fallback behavior, and a hint at the actual job-to-be-done.

Notice the good version never mentions the founder's app. The founder learned that the *competition* isn't other apps — it's "ordering the same three takeout meals," and the *failure mode* of the obvious solution (meal kits) is guilt about waste. That's a foothold a pitch would never have surfaced.

## 4. Ask about the past, not the future

This deserves its own section because it is the highest-leverage habit in the whole discipline.

**Humans are terrible at predicting their own future behavior and pretty good at reporting their past.** Behavioral science has shown this for decades — the gap between stated intention and actual behavior is enormous (the "say-do gap"). Ask someone if they'd pay $10/month for a thing and they'll cheerfully say yes; charge them $10 and watch most of them vanish. So:

| Future / hypothetical (bad) | Past / behavioral (good) |
|---|---|
| "Would you use an app that…?" | "Tell me about the last time you dealt with this." |
| "Would you pay for this?" | "What do you currently pay to deal with this — tools, time, people?" |
| "How often do you think you'd…?" | "How many times did this happen last month?" |
| "Could you see yourself…?" | "What did you actually do the last time?" |
| "Do you think this is a problem?" | "When did this last bite you, specifically?" |

Anchor every question to **something that actually occurred**: the last time, what they did, what it cost, what they tried, what they're using now. A founder's interview transcript should read like a series of small documentaries about real events, not a survey of opinions about an imagined future.

And when they slip into hypotheticals (they will — "yeah I'd probably use something like that"), gently drag them back: *"When's the last time you were in that situation? What did you do then?"* The past is where the truth lives.

## 5. The compliment trap and the cheerleader bias

Here is the most expensive mistake in this lecture, because it *feels* like success.

You describe your idea. The person says, "Oh, that's a really cool idea, I'd totally use that." You walk away glowing. You've just collected a **compliment**, and a compliment is worth exactly nothing. Worse than nothing — it's a sugar high that makes you build the wrong thing with confidence.

Why compliments are worthless data:

- **Politeness is the default.** Most people will not look a hopeful founder in the eye and say "that's a bad idea." It's socially expensive. They'll say "interesting!" and move on.
- **Friends are the worst.** Your warm network *wants* you to succeed, which makes them the least reliable source of truth and the most reliable source of encouragement. Encouragement is not evidence.
- **Talk is free.** Saying "I'd use that" costs nothing. The only opinions that count are the ones that cost something.

How to defuse it. When you hear a compliment, do **not** say thank you and move on. Treat it as a flashing light that says "you've drifted toward pitching" and steer back to their reality:

> "That's kind, thank you — but I'm trying not to talk about my idea at all. Can I ask: what's the most annoying part of how you handle this *today*?"

Or zoom into the past:

> "Glad it resonates — when's the last time this actually came up for you, and what did you do?"

**The "that's interesting" smell.** Train your ear for soft positives — "interesting," "cool," "I could see that being useful," "you should totally build that." They feel like signal and are noise. Real signal sounds different (next section).

## 6. Signal vs. noise: commitment and advancement

If compliments are noise, what's signal? **Commitment and advancement** — the customer giving up something real.

Fitzpatrick's framing: the currencies of real commitment are **time, reputation, and money**. When a customer offers any of these unprompted, that's worth ten "sounds greats":

- **Time:** "Can we set up a follow-up call?" / "I'll walk you through our current process — got an hour next week?" / they spend 45 minutes when you asked for 15.
- **Reputation:** "You should talk to my colleague, I'll introduce you." / "Let me forward this to my boss." (They're spending their social capital — they wouldn't risk it on a bad bet.)
- **Money:** "When can I buy it?" / "We pay $400/month for a tool that doesn't even do this." / a pre-order, a deposit, a signed LOI (you're far from this in Week 3, but watch for the *willingness*).

And the strongest signal in discovery specifically is the **workaround**. If someone has built a janky personal spreadsheet, hired a part-time person, or pays for a tool they complain about — *to cope with this exact problem* — the problem is real and acute. People don't build workarounds for vitamins. They build them for painkillers. When you find a strong, effortful, paid workaround, you've found gold; dig into it relentlessly. "Show me the spreadsheet. How long did it take to build? How often do you update it? What breaks?"

> **The painkiller vs. vitamin test, operationalized.** Don't ask "is this a painkiller?" — that's an opinion question. *Infer* it from behavior: How recently did it bite them? How much does the workaround cost them in time/money? Have they searched for a solution? Spent money trying? Acute pain leaves a trail of effort. No trail = vitamin = walk away.

## 7. Good questions vs. bad questions (the full table)

Print this. Tape it next to your screen for the interviews.

| Bad (avoid) | Why it's bad | Good (use instead) |
|---|---|---|
| "Would you pay for X?" | Hypothetical; cheap to say yes | "What do you currently pay to deal with this?" |
| "Do you like this idea?" | Compliment-fishing; about your idea | "Walk me through the last time you faced this." |
| "Don't you hate when…?" | Leading; you supplied the answer | "How do you feel about how that works today?" |
| "Could you see yourself using…?" | Future + hypothetical | "What have you already tried?" |
| "How often do you usually do X?" | "Usually" invites a tidy average, not a real number | "How many times did this happen last month?" |
| "Would this feature be useful?" | Pitches a solution; invites politeness | "What's the hardest part of doing this now?" |
| "We're building Y — thoughts?" | Pure pitch; data-destroying | (Don't. Keep the solution in your pocket.) |
| "Is price a concern for you?" | Leading; primes a yes | "Walk me through how you decided to buy [current tool]." |

The pattern is consistent: **bad questions are about your idea, the future, or supply their own answer. Good questions are about the customer's past, specific, and open-ended.**

A couple of power-questions worth memorizing:

- **"Walk me through the last time…"** — the workhorse. Gets you a specific, recent, true story.
- **"What's the hardest part about…?"** — surfaces the acute pain inside a broad problem.
- **"What did you do about it?"** — reveals the workaround and how much they care.
- **"Why do you bother?" / "What would happen if you just didn't?"** — tests whether the problem is actually load-bearing or just annoying.
- **"Who else has this problem? Can you introduce me?"** — a referral is a commitment *and* it grows your funnel.
- **"What else have I not asked about that I should have?"** — the humility question; it routinely surfaces the surprise.

## 8. Talk less, listen more — the mechanics

The single most common technical failure in a real interview is the founder talking too much. Aim to speak roughly **20% of the time**. Concrete techniques:

- **Embrace silence.** After they finish a thought, count to three before you speak. They will almost always keep going, and the second half is usually the honest half. Beginners rush to fill silence and step on the best data.
- **Echo and wait.** Repeat their last few words as a question — "It piled up?" — and let them elaborate. It's almost free and incredibly effective.
- **Follow curiosity, not the script.** The script is a *safety net*, not a *cage*. When they say something surprising, abandon the script and chase it. The interesting stuff is never on your list of questions.
- **Don't correct, don't defend.** If they describe your space wrong, or dismiss a problem you think is huge, do not argue. You're there to learn their model of the world, not install yours.
- **Capture exact words.** Write down quotes verbatim, especially emotional ones. "It's a nightmare every Monday" is worth more in a pitch than your paraphrase. Their words, not your summary.

## 9. Quantity, honesty, and the falsifiability test

Two final principles that bridge into Lecture 2.

**One interview is an anecdote; ten are a pattern.** A single conversation can mislead you in either direction — the one person who loves it, the one curmudgeon who hates everything. You're looking for things that recur *independently* across many people who don't know each other. That's why the dossier targets ten and why this week's five is just the start.

**Discovery that cannot fail your idea is theater.** This is the honesty test, and it ties directly to your Week 2 kill criteria. If you've designed your interviews so that no possible answer would make you stop, you're not doing research — you're seeking applause. Before you start, write down: *what would I have to hear to kill or pivot this?* If you can't answer, you've already lost. A week that ends in an honest "drop" is a *successful* week — you spent ten hours to avoid wasting ten weeks. Hold that thought; Lecture 2 turns it into a synthesis process.

## 10. A full worked transcript (annotated)

Reading the rules is one thing; hearing them in motion is another. Below is a condensed, annotated transcript of a real-shaped discovery interview. The founder is testing **"freelance designers struggle to chase late invoices."** Watch how she stays on the past, never pitches, and chases the surprise.

> **Founder:** "Thanks for the 15 minutes. I'm just researching how freelance designers handle the money side of the business — I'm not selling anything. Mind if I take a few notes?"
> *(Frame: research, not sale; permission asked. Good.)*
>
> **Designer:** "Sure, go for it."
>
> **Founder:** "Tell me about the last time a client paid you late. What actually happened?"
> *(Past-behavior, specific event. Textbook opener.)*
>
> **Designer:** "Oh god. Last month. A 60-day-net client just… didn't pay. I didn't even notice for two weeks because I don't really track it — I just assume it'll come in. Then rent was due and I realized I was short."
> *(Gold already: the workaround is "don't track it / assume," and the trigger is a cash crunch. The founder did NOT mention invoicing software.)*
>
> **Founder:** "What did you do once you noticed?"
> *(What did you do about it — surfaces the workaround and effort.)*
>
> **Designer:** "Sent an awkward email. Felt gross about it. Waited another week, sent another. Eventually it came. But I hate that part so much I sometimes just… don't chase, and write it off in my head."
> *(Emotional, verbatim-worthy: "felt gross," "hate that part." And a huge finding: she sometimes doesn't chase AT ALL. That's revenue she's losing.)*
>
> **Founder:** "When you say you hate it — what's the worst part? The tracking, or the chasing?"
> *(Following curiosity, not the script. Splitting two problems she conflated.)*
>
> **Designer:** "Honestly? The chasing. Tracking I could figure out. It's that I don't want to seem desperate to a client I want more work from. So I sit on it."
> *(THE SURPRISE. The founder came in thinking the problem was tracking/visibility. The real pain is the relational discomfort of chasing a client you depend on. Completely different product.)*
>
> **Founder:** "That makes sense. What do you use today to keep track of what's owed — anything?"
> *(Workaround + spend.)*
>
> **Designer:** "A spreadsheet I update like twice a year. I tried an invoicing app, paid for it three months, never opened the reminders feature. Cancelled."
> *(Critical: she ALREADY churned out of the obvious solution. The "reminders feature" existed and she didn't use it — because the barrier isn't the reminder, it's hitting send on something that feels desperate.)*
>
> **Founder:** "Last thing — who else do you know who deals with this? I'd love to learn from a couple more people."
> *(Referral ask = commitment + funnel growth.)*
>
> **Designer:** "Tons of people. I'll drop you in our freelancer Slack, there's a whole thread about deadbeat clients."
> *(Reputation commitment + a recruiting goldmine.)*

What the founder learned that a pitch would have buried: the obvious solution (invoice reminders) *already exists and people churn out of it*, because the true blocker is **emotional, not informational** — the fear of looking desperate to a client you want to keep. That insight is worth more than fifty "would you use an invoicing app?" yeses. It points at a completely different product (maybe done-for-you, polite, third-party chasing) and it only surfaced because she asked about the past and chased the discomfort.

> **The lesson in one line:** the founder never once said what she was building. She let the customer narrate reality, and reality handed her a better idea than the one she walked in with.

## 11. Common failure modes (and the fix)

When the in-class role-plays go wrong, they go wrong in the same handful of ways. Name them now so you catch yourself in the moment.

| Failure mode | What it sounds like | The fix |
|---|---|---|
| **The accidental pitch** | "So I'm building a tool that…" | Bite your tongue. Your idea never has to come up. |
| **The hypothetical drift** | "Would you ever want something that…?" | "When's the last time this came up? What did you do?" |
| **The leading nudge** | "That must be really frustrating, right?" | Let *them* supply the emotion. Ask "how do you feel about that?" |
| **Compliment-fishing** | "Does this sound useful to you?" | Don't ask. Probe the problem, not your idea. |
| **The interrogation** | Rapid-fire questions, no silence | Slow down. Count to three after their answers. |
| **Talking too much** | You explaining your worldview | Aim for 20%. If you're teaching, you've lost. |
| **Confirmation listening** | Hearing only the supportive bits | Write down the threatening quotes *first*. |
| **The premature wrap** | Ending the moment they say "great idea" | A compliment is a cue to dig, not to close. |
| **The friends-only sample** | Five interviews, all buddies | Friends are bias machines. Get to strangers fast. |

If you record yourself (with consent) and listen back, you will hear at least three of these in your first interview. That's normal. The point of running five this week is that by interview five, most of them are gone.

## 12. How many, how long, and when to stop

Three practical calibration questions founders always ask.

**How many?** This week: five, as a floor. By the capstone: ten or more. The real answer is **saturation** — you stop when new interviews stop teaching you anything new. For a tight B2B segment, that's often 8–15; for a broad consumer problem, more. If your fifth interview still surprises you every time, you're nowhere near done. If interviews three through five all say the same thing, you're approaching saturation and can start trusting the pattern.

**How long?** 15–20 minutes is the sweet spot for discovery. Long enough for one good last-time story and the workaround; short enough that busy people say yes and stay present. If someone is generous and keeps going for 45 minutes, that's a *commitment signal* — let them. But never *ask* for more than 20 up front; the small ask is what gets the yes.

**One segment or many?** Start narrow — one clearly-defined segment ("independent dental clinics, 4–10 staff," not "healthcare"). A pattern across a narrow segment is interpretable; a smear across five segments is noise. You can widen later. And run at least one *disconfirming* interview with someone you suspect does NOT have the problem — finding the *edge* of your segment is as valuable as confirming its center.

> **A reassurance for the nervous.** Your first interview will be clumsy. You'll pitch, you'll lead, you'll talk too much. Everyone does. The skill is not innate; it's a muscle, and five reps this week build it. By the capstone you'll run these in your sleep — and you'll wonder how you ever thought building first was the safe move.

## 13. Why this is psychologically hard (and why naming it helps)

The mechanics of discovery are simple. The reason most founders still do it badly is psychological, and you'll resist less if you can name the forces working against you.

- **Confirmation bias.** You have already fallen a little in love with your idea, so your brain over-weights every supportive signal and quietly discards the threatening ones. The defense is procedural: write down the *threatening* quotes first, count *unprompted* mentions, and set kill criteria *before* you start — so your future, biased self can't move them.
- **Social discomfort.** Asking a stranger for time and candor triggers the same circuitry as asking for a favor. It feels like imposing. Reframe it: most people genuinely enjoy being asked thoughtful questions about their own experience by someone who actually listens. You're not taking; you're giving them a rare twenty minutes of being heard.
- **The sunk-cost reflex.** The more time you've spent imagining the product, the harder it is to hear it's wrong — so the temptation is to avoid the conversation that might say so. But the cost of being wrong only *grows* with time. The interview is at its cheapest right now, today, before you've built anything.
- **Fear of the "no."** Some founders unconsciously keep their interviews vague and flattering precisely so they never hear a clear no. A clear no is a *gift* — it's the cheapest possible exit from a bad idea. Go looking for it.

> **The founder's paradox.** The skills that make you a great *builder* — conviction, optimism, the ability to ignore doubters — are the exact skills that make you a *bad interviewer*. Discovery requires you to switch modes: from "I'm right and I'll prove it" to "I'm probably wrong and I want to find out how." Holding both at different times is the founder's real superpower.

## 14. A note on B2B vs. B2C discovery

The principles are identical; the texture differs. Knowing which world you're in saves you grief.

**B2B (you're selling to a business):**
- Your interviewees are *professionals* talking about *work*. They're often happy to discuss their problems — it's their job — and a respectful LinkedIn or cold note works better than founders expect.
- The "what do you pay today" question is concrete and answerable: there's usually a budget, a tool, a vendor, a line item. Spend signal is easy to read.
- Watch the **buyer vs. user** gap: the person who *feels* the pain (a nurse, a clerk) is often not the person who *signs the check* (an administrator). Interview both; their problems differ.
- A referral inside an organization ("talk to my ops lead") is a strong signal and a fast path to more interviews.

**B2C (you're selling to consumers):**
- Your interviewees are people talking about their *lives*. Emotion and embarrassment matter more; people under-report habits they're not proud of. Past-behavior questions matter even more here, because stated preferences and actual behavior diverge wildly.
- "Spend" is fuzzier — the competition is often "doing nothing," "a free habit," or "the cheapest convenient option." Map the real alternative, even when it's not a product.
- You typically need *more* interviews to find a pattern, because consumer segments are more varied than professional roles.

Either way: narrow your segment, anchor in the past, hunt the workaround. The world changes; the method doesn't.

## 15. "But can't AI just do this for me?" — 2026 edition

It's 2026, and you will be tempted to outsource discovery to a tool. Be precise about what is and isn't legitimate, because this is where a lot of founders fool themselves with a modern coat of paint.

**Legitimately useful:**
- **Desk-research synthesis.** Summarizing competitor reviews, public forum threads, and support tickets to *generate hypotheses and find where your customers congregate*. This is faster than it used to be — use it. But it's still desk research: it tells you what's already public, not what's in the office manager's drawer.
- **Logistics.** Drafting outreach, transcribing recorded calls (with consent), and helping you cluster your *own real notes* in synthesis. Fine — it's a faster sticky-note wall.

**Not a substitute for the conversation:**
- **"Synthetic users" / persona simulations.** A model role-playing your customer will cheerfully hallucinate a confident, plausible, *fictional* answer to "would you use this?" It has all the failure modes of a hypothetical question with none of the friction that makes a real human hesitate. It cannot tell you about the paper waitlist in the drawer because it doesn't know that exists. Treat simulated customers as a brainstorming toy, never as evidence.
- **Surveys at scale dressed up as discovery.** Blasting an AI-written survey to 500 people is still a survey — it can only confirm a known pattern, not surface an unknown one, and it gives you no thread to pull.

> **The 2026 rule of thumb.** Use modern tools to do the *boring* parts faster (drafting, transcribing, clustering, desk research) so you can spend your scarce nerve on the part no tool can do: sitting across from a real human and listening for the thing you didn't expect. The founders who "automate discovery" are automating the production of confident fiction. Don't be one of them.

Concretely, here's how Lecture 1 turns into action over the next seven days:

4. **Start recruiting today** — Lecture 2 covers the how, but the funnel has lead time, so don't wait for Lecture 2 to begin the list.

6. **Synthesize and decide** (Exercise 3) against your kill criteria.

You now know *how to talk*. Lecture 2 is the machinery around the talking — filling the funnel, capturing the words, and turning a pile of conversations into a decision.

## Key takeaways

- **Get out of the building.** Facts about customers live outside; opinions live inside. An interview costs an hour and can save a quarter.
- **Discovery learns; it does not sell.** Keep your solution in your pocket. The moment you pitch, the data turns to politeness.
- **Know which conversation you're in.** Discovery interview, not survey, not focus group, not sales call.
- **The Mom Test:** talk about their life not your idea; ask about specific past events not future hypotheticals; talk less, listen more.
- **Compliments are worthless; commitment is signal.** Time, reputation, money, and especially a real workaround are the currencies that count.
- **Painkiller, not vitamin** — inferred from the trail of effort the customer has already spent, not from asking.
- **Memorize the power questions**, especially "Walk me through the last time…" and "What did you do about it?"
- **Design interviews that can fail your idea**, against your Week 2 kill criteria — or you're doing theater.
