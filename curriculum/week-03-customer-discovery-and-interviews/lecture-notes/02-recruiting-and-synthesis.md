# Lecture 2 — Recruiting and Synthesis

> **Duration:** ~45 min. **Outcome:** You can find interviewees cheaply, write up a conversation so the evidence lasts, and turn several into a pattern.

## 1. Recruiting interviewees

You need people who plausibly *have* the problem. Cheap sources:

- **Your warm network** — the standing you claimed in Week 1 should produce names.
- **Communities** — relevant subreddits, Slack/Discord groups, forums, local meetups. Ask honestly; do not spam.
- **Referrals** — end every interview with "who else has this problem that I should talk to?"
- **Cold-but-relevant** — a short, respectful message that asks for 15 minutes to learn, not to sell.

Be ethical: say who you are, why you are asking, that it is research not a sale, and respect their time and privacy. Never publish private details without permission.

## 2. The 15-minute structure

1. **Frame (1 min):** "I'm researching how people handle [problem]. No pitch — I just want to learn. Okay if I ask about your experience?"
2. **Past behavior (8 min):** the last time it happened, what they did, what it cost, what they tried.
3. **Workarounds and spend (4 min):** what they use today, what they pay, what's missing.
4. **Referrals + close (2 min):** "Who else should I talk to?" Thank them.

## 3. Write it up immediately

Memory decays within hours. Right after each interview, write to `interviews/interview-NN.md`:

- Role / segment (no private identifiers).
- The problem in their words.
- Last-time story, cost, current workaround, what they pay.
- Surprises and direct quotes.
- A one-line "does this support or threaten my idea?"

## 4. Synthesis: find the pattern

After five (then ten) write-ups, cluster the findings:

- **Is the problem real?** How many independently described it unprompted?
- **Is it frequent and costly?** Look for recurring numbers and stories.
- **What is the current workaround?** A shared workaround is a product-shaped hole.
- **Did anything surprise you?** Surprises are where the real idea often hides (recall the waitlist pivot in the week's worked example).

Then check the pattern against your Week 2 **kill criteria**. If the evidence trips a kill criterion, that is a *successful* week — you avoided building the wrong thing. Pivot or drop honestly.

## 5. Persevere, pivot, or drop

- **Persevere:** the pattern confirms a real, frequent, painful problem with your segment. Carry it forward.
- **Pivot:** the real pain is adjacent to your hypothesis (the surprise). Re-aim and keep interviewing.
- **Drop:** the pain is mild or the segment is happy. Painful, but cheap compared to building.
