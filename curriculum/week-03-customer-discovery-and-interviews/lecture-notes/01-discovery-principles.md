# Lecture 1 — Discovery Principles

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can run an interview that surfaces real problems and avoid the traps that make founders hear only what they want.

> **Get out of the building.** No amount of desk work or internal debate substitutes for talking to the people who have the problem. Facts about customers live outside; opinions live inside.

## 1. Discovery learns; it does not sell

A discovery interview has one job: to learn the truth about a problem. It is not a sales call, not a demo, not a pitch. The moment you describe your solution, the interviewee starts being polite, and politeness destroys data. Keep your solution in your pocket.

## 2. Ask about the past, not the future

People are terrible at predicting their own future behavior and excellent at reporting their past. So:

- **Bad (hypothetical):** "Would you use an app that…?" → optimistic fiction.
- **Good (past behavior):** "Tell me about the last time this happened." → real data.

Anchor every question in something that actually occurred: the last time, what they did, what it cost, what they tried.

## 3. The compliment trap

Friends and polite strangers will tell you your idea is great. That feels like validation and is worth nothing. Defuse it by never asking for opinions on the idea and instead probing the problem:

- "What was the hardest part about that?"
- "What did you do to solve it? What did that cost you?"
- "When did this last bite you, specifically?"

If they have not tried to solve it and it has not recently bitten them, the pain is probably mild — a vitamin.

## 4. Good questions vs. bad questions

| Bad (avoid) | Good (use) |
|-------------|-----------|
| "Would you pay for X?" | "What do you currently pay to deal with this?" |
| "Do you like this idea?" | "Walk me through the last time you faced this." |
| "Don't you hate when…?" (leading) | "How do you feel about how that works today?" |
| "Could you see yourself using…?" | "What have you already tried?" |

## 5. Talk less, listen more

The interviewer should speak maybe 20% of the time. Silence is your friend — people fill it with the real story. Follow curiosity, not a rigid script; the script is a safety net, not a cage.

## 6. Quantity and honesty

One interview is an anecdote; ten are a pattern. And the pattern only means something if you let interviewees disagree with you. Hold your Week 2 kill criteria in view — discovery that cannot fail your idea is theater.
