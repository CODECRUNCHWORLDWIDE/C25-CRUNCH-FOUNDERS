# Week 3 — Challenges

The exercises drill the mechanics: build a script, write up an interview, synthesize a pattern. **Challenges stretch you into judgment** — the part of founding you can't drill, only practice. Each challenge is a realistic founder decision scenario: messy, incomplete data and a call you have to make and defend, the way you'll have to in front of investors and judges.

Challenges are **optional**. You can pass the week without them. But the judgment they build is exactly what the capstone rubric's "survives the three obvious hard questions" line item tests, and the founders who do them are measurably better at reading their own data honestly.

## How challenges are assessed

Unlike a coding challenge, there's no single right answer here — two thoughtful founders could read the same data and reasonably reach different decisions. So you're assessed on **reasoning, not the verdict**:

| Criterion | What "great" looks like |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Evidence handling** | You cite specific data points (counts, quotes, costs), not vibes. You distinguish signal from politeness. |
| **Honesty under bias** | You name the data that *threatens* your preferred answer, not just the data that supports it. |
| **Frequency vs. severity** | You correctly separate "how often" from "how much it hurts" and don't conflate them. |
| **Kill-criteria discipline** | You check the stated criteria as written and don't move the goalposts to save the idea. |
| **The decision** | The call is explicit (persevere/pivot/drop), specific, and falls out of the evidence you cited. |
| **What you'd do next** | You name the concrete next interviews or test that would *resolve* the ambiguity. |

A "drop" or a "pivot" earns full marks when the reasoning is sound. The only failing answer is one that rationalizes a predetermined conclusion or hand-waves past the inconvenient data.

## Submission

Write your answer as `challenges/challenge-01-answer.md` in your Week 3 repo and commit it. Where the challenge asks for a decision, state it in one bold line at the top, then defend it. Aim for a page — tight reasoning beats a wall of text.
