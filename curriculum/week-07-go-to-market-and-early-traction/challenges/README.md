# Week 7 — Challenges

The exercises drill the basics: build a funnel, pick a metric, plan a test. **Challenges stretch you** with a realistic, ambiguous founder decision — the kind with no clean answer, where the skill is reasoning under uncertainty and committing to a call you can defend.

Each challenge takes 60–120 minutes and produces a written decision memo you can commit to your portfolio. They're modeled on the real moment every founder hits: the data is mixed, the runway is finite, and you have to decide *this week.*

## Index

1. **[Challenge 1 — The channel pivot](challenge-01-channel-pivot.md)** — your first channel is sputtering and the runway clock is ticking. Persevere, fix the leak, or kill it and pivot? Write the decision memo. (~90 min)

## How challenges are assessed

Challenges are **optional** — you can pass the week without them. But they're where you build the muscle that demo day (Week 10) and investors actually test: can you make a hard call and defend it?

A strong challenge submission is graded on four things:

| Dimension | What "great" looks like |
|-----------|-------------------------|
| **Reasoning from evidence** | You read the funnel by *rate*, not raw counts; you separate signal from noise; you don't cherry-pick the flattering number. |
| **A clear decision** | You commit to one course of action — not "it depends" — and state it up front. |
| **Honest treatment of uncertainty** | You name what you *don't* know, what would change your mind, and the risk you're accepting. |
| **A concrete next step** | Your decision turns into a specific, time-boxed action with a new success bar — not a vague intention. |

The trap every challenge is designed to expose: the **"one more week"** reflex — extending a failing bet because quitting feels like losing — and its mirror image, **quitting too early** on a channel that just needed a fixable tweak. Good founders avoid both by reasoning from the funnel and a pre-set rule, not from how the week *felt*.

There is no single right answer. Two well-argued memos can reach opposite decisions and both score full marks. What fails is a memo that dodges the decision, ignores the numbers, or hides the risk.
