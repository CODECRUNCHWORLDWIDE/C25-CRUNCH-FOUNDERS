# Lecture 1 — Go-to-Market Motions

> **Duration:** ~2 hours of reading + working through your own model.
> **Outcome:** You can name the five-and-a-half go-to-market motions, explain how each one works and what it costs, match the right one to *your* model and price point, and explain why you start by doing things that don't scale. By the end you should be able to say, in one sentence, which motion you're betting on and why — and survive the room poking holes in it.

If you only remember one thing from this lecture, remember this:

> **"Build it and they will come" has never once been true.** Distribution is not a thing you bolt on after the product is done. It is a design decision you make *now*, and it is at least as hard as building the product. Most startups that die with a working product die of a distribution problem, not a product problem.

A founder who can't answer "how will the first hundred customers find you?" with something more specific than "marketing" or "we'll go viral" does not have a startup yet. They have a project. This week fixes that.

---

## 1. Motion vs. channel vs. campaign — the vocabulary that saves you

Founders throw these three words around interchangeably and it costs them clarity. Pin them down now.

| Term | Definition | Example (Maria's ClinicFlow) |
|------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Motion** | The repeatable *system* by which customers reliably come to you. The strategy layer. | Founder-led sales. |
| **Channel** | One specific path inside the motion. The tactic layer. | Cold email to clinic office managers. |
| **Campaign** | One time-boxed push inside a channel. The execution layer. | "Two weeks, 40 named clinics in Austin." |

Why the distinction matters: **you measure them differently, and you commit to them differently.** You pick *one* motion and stay with it for months. You test a *handful* of channels inside it. You run *many* campaigns, killing the ones that don't work. If a founder says "our go-to-market is a LinkedIn post," they've confused a campaign for a motion, and they will be lost the moment that one post flops.

A motion is a bet about *how your kind of customer prefers to buy your kind of thing.* A channel is a bet about *where they are.* A campaign is a bet about *what you say to them this time.* Keep the layers straight and your funnel data will actually mean something.

Why founders blur these layers: it feels productive to "try a bunch of stuff." But "we posted on LinkedIn, ran a Google ad, and emailed some people" isn't a strategy — it's three campaigns in three channels across two motions, none of them given a fair test. When none of it works (it won't, at that intensity), you've learned nothing, because you can't attribute the failure to anything. The layered vocabulary forces you to commit at the level that matters — the motion — and then test deliberately underneath it. Commit to the motion for months, test channels in weeks, run campaigns in days. Discipline at the top buys you freedom to experiment at the bottom.

## 2. The five-and-a-half motions

There are dozens of channels but only a handful of fundamental *motions*. Here are the ones a pre-traction founder should know, what each one is, when it fits, and how it fails.

### 2.1 Founder-led / direct sales

**What it is:** You, the founder, sell person-to-person. Email, calls, demos, in-person visits. You *are* the sales team. This is the default motion for early B2B and for anything with a meaningful price tag.

**When it fits:** Higher price (roughly $1,000+ annual contract value, ACV), a smaller and *nameable* set of customers, and a purchase that needs explaining or trust. If you can write down a list of every prospect you'd want, founder-led sales is almost always right at the start.

**How it works:** You build a target list, reach out one human at a time, take the call yourself, hear the objections firsthand, and close (or lose) deals you personally ran. The learning is the point — you are not just selling, you are doing the most concentrated customer discovery of your life.

**How it fails:** Founders avoid it because selling feels uncomfortable, or they try to automate it before it works (buying a list of 10,000 emails and blasting a template). It also fails if the price is too low to justify your time — you cannot afford to hand-sell a $9/month product.

> **Maria** runs founder-led sales: a named list of ~4,000 clinics, personal emails, a demo she gives herself. At $99/month ($1,188 ACV) it's right at the edge where hand-selling pays off — and crucially, she can *name every prospect.*

### 2.2 Community-led

**What it is:** You grow inside a community that already has the problem — a subreddit, a Discord, a Slack group, a professional association, a local meetup. Trust spreads by word of mouth from people the community already trusts.

**When it fits:** Your customers cluster in an identifiable community, the problem is something they talk about openly, and you can be a genuine, useful member rather than a lurking advertiser. Strong for developer tools, creator tools, and tight professional niches.

**How it works:** You show up, help people, answer questions, build a reputation, and let the product come up naturally. The first users become advocates who bring the next ones. This compounds — but slowly, and only if you're authentic.

**How it fails:** The moment you treat the community as a billboard, you get banned or ignored. It also fails when there *is* no real community for your customer (most SMB owners don't hang out in a "small clinic owners" Discord), or when the founder isn't willing to spend the months it takes to earn standing.

### 2.3 Content / inbound

**What it is:** You publish material — articles, videos, SEO pages, a newsletter, founder-led posts on LinkedIn/X — that pulls in people who are searching for or thinking about the problem. They find you instead of you finding them.

**When it fits:** A larger, harder-to-name audience; a problem people actively search for; and a founder (or team) who can produce genuinely useful content consistently. The economics are great *eventually* — content is an asset that keeps working — but it is **slow to start**, often 6–12 months to meaningful traffic.

**How it works:** You answer the questions your customer is already typing into a search bar or asking in their feed. Over time you accumulate an audience and rank for terms that signal buying intent.

**How it fails:** Founders expect it to work in week three. It doesn't. It also fails when the content is thin and undifferentiated — in 2026, the web is drowning in AI-generated filler, and only content with real expertise or a real point of view breaks through. "We'll do a blog" is not a strategy.

### 2.4 Paid acquisition

**What it is:** You buy attention. Search ads, social ads, sponsorships, influencer deals. You pay money to put your offer in front of strangers.

**When it fits:** **Only when you already know your unit economics hold** (from Week 6). Paid is the one motion that requires positive, *known* economics *before* you scale it — because if you spend $200 to acquire a customer worth $150, scaling paid scales your losses. It fits self-serve products with a fast, measurable conversion and a clear path from click to paid.

**How it works:** You run small tests, measure cost-per-acquisition against your target CAC, and only scale spend on the channels where the math works.

**How it fails:** Founders reach for paid because it feels like "real marketing" and because it's easy to start (just add a credit card). At the pre-traction stage, with no proven conversion funnel, **paid almost always loses money and teaches you little.** It also decays — Andrew Chen's "Law of Shitty Clickthroughs": every paid channel gets more expensive and less effective over time as it saturates. For most C25 founders, **paid is not your first motion.** It's something you graduate to once founder-led, community, or content has proven the funnel converts.

### 2.5 Partnerships / channel

**What it is:** Another company's reach distributes you. Integrations, reseller deals, referral arrangements, marketplace listings, "powered by" embeds.

**When it fits:** There's an established player who already has your customers and a non-competing reason to send them your way. Strong when your product complements theirs.

**How it works:** You find a partner whose customers are your customers, make it trivially easy and beneficial for them to refer or bundle you, and split the value.

**How it fails:** Partnerships are slow, political, and unreliable early on — big companies move at big-company speed, and a partnership you don't control can vanish overnight. "We'll partner with [enormous company]" is a fantasy 95% of the time at pre-traction. Real partnerships usually come *after* you have traction, not before.

### 2.6 The half: product-led growth (PLG)

**What it is:** The product sells itself through use. Free tier, self-serve signup, viral loops (people invite each other because the product is better with others), in-product upgrade prompts. Think Figma, Slack, Calendly.

**Why it's "the half":** PLG is real and powerful, but **most pre-traction startups are not ready for it.** It requires a product polished enough to deliver value with zero human help, an "aha moment" that happens fast, and ideally a built-in reason for users to invite others. You usually *earn your way into* PLG after a founder-led or community phase taught you what makes users activate. Aspire to it; don't start there.

## 3. Match the motion to the model — the price-point ladder

The single most useful heuristic: **the motion has to be affordable given what a customer is worth.** You cannot spend more winning a customer than they'll ever pay you. This produces a rough ladder by annual contract value (ACV):

| Customer worth (ACV) | Affordable motion | Why |
|----------------------|-------------------|-----|
| **< $100/yr** | Self-serve + content or community; PLG | No human touch is affordable. The product and content must do all the selling. |
| **~$100–$1,000/yr** | Low-touch: content/inbound, light community, maybe templated paid once proven | A sales call eats the whole margin. Automate acquisition; humans only for support. |
| **~$1,000–$10,000/yr** | **Founder-led sales**, then a small sales team | A demo and a few emails are affordable; trust needs a human. The sweet spot for early B2B. |
| **> $10,000/yr** | High-touch direct sales, partnerships | Long cycles, multiple stakeholders, real relationships. Worth significant per-deal effort. |

This is the heuristic the room will use to poke holes in your motion. Two classic red flags:

- **High-touch sales for a cheap product.** If you're planning to personally demo a $5/month app, the math never closes — you'd need thousands of demos a month and you'd starve. Either raise the price or pick a no-touch motion.
- **Paid ads for a tiny, nameable market.** If your entire market is 200 named accounts, don't spray ads at strangers — *name the 200 and email them.* Founder-led sales is cheaper, faster, and teaches you more.

> **Maria's check:** $99/month is $1,188 ACV — squarely in the founder-led band. Her market is ~4,000 named clinics — small enough to list, too valuable to win with $0.50 ad clicks that mostly hit the wrong person. Founder-led sales is the obvious fit. If she were selling a $4/month consumer habit-tracker to anyone with a phone, the answer would flip entirely to content/PLG.

There's a second fit beyond price, from Brian Balfour's "Four Fits": **channel/model fit.** A low-price, high-volume model *needs* a low-cost, high-volume channel (content, SEO, virality). A high-price, low-volume model *needs* a high-touch channel (sales). When the model and the channel don't match, nothing you do to the product fixes it. Hold your Week 6 model next to your motion and check they're the same shape.

## 4. Do things that don't scale — first, on purpose

Paul Graham's famous essay is required reading this week. The thesis: **the things that get a startup its first users are almost always unscalable** — and that's correct, not a failure.

Why founders resist it: "manually emailing 40 clinics doesn't scale." True. **It's not supposed to.** Its job is not to scale; its job is to *get you your first customers and teach you the channel.* You learn what makes someone reply, what objection kills the demo, what wording converts. That learning is the raw material for everything scalable that comes later. You cannot automate a process you haven't yet figured out by hand.

Concrete unscalable moves that work:

- **Recruit the first users one at a time.** Personally reach out to every single early customer. Stripe's founders installed the product *for* their first users ("Collison installation"). Airbnb's founders knocked on doors and photographed listings themselves.
- **Concierge onboarding.** Do manually, for each early customer, what the product will eventually do automatically. It's slow and it's the best customer research you'll ever get.
- **Be the product, temporarily.** Wizard-of-Oz it — humans behind the curtain doing what looks automated — until you've proven people want the output.
- **Go where they already are.** Show up at the conference, the clinic, the subreddit. Physical or digital, put yourself in the room.

The discipline: **automate only once a manual motion clearly works.** If you can't sell it by hand to ten people, a funnel of ads won't save you — it'll just lose money faster and quieter. Unscalable first, scalable second. Always that order.

> **Maria's unscalable move:** she offers to set up the no-show reminder flow *herself*, by hand, for the first five clinics — sitting with the office manager, importing their patient list, configuring the messages. It takes her an afternoon per clinic. It also teaches her exactly where the product confuses people, which becomes the thing she fixes first. That's concierge onboarding earning its keep.

### The "scale" test — when to stop doing things by hand

How do you know when an unscalable activity has done its job and it's time to automate? Three signals, in order:

1. **You can predict the outcome.** When you can reliably guess, before a call, whether a prospect will convert — and you're right most of the time — you understand the motion well enough to write it down.
2. **The bottleneck is your hours, not your knowledge.** Early on, the constraint is "I don't know what works." Once that flips to "I know what works but I physically can't do enough of it," you've earned the right to scale.
3. **You're repeating yourself.** When you've typed the same demo explanation for the tenth time, or set up the same onboarding by hand five times, the repetition itself is the signal to systematize *that specific thing* — not everything at once.

The failure mode on both ends: automating too early (before you know what works, so you scale noise) and automating too late (staying in artisanal hand-mode long after the playbook is obvious, capping your growth at your own stamina). Watch for the three signals and move when they fire.

## 5. Pick ONE motion to test now

You do **not** run five motions at once when you're pre-traction. You have no money and, more importantly, no focus to spare. Spreading yourself across channels with neither produces noise, not learning — five half-tests that each fail to reach a clear yes/no.

The Bullseye method (from *Traction* by Weinberg & Mares) formalizes this:

1. **Brainstorm** — list every channel that could *plausibly* work, even unlikely ones. Force yourself past the obvious two.
2. **Rank** into three rings: the inner ring (most promising, test now), the middle ring (possible, test later), the outer ring (long shots).
3. **Test** the inner ring with cheap, time-boxed experiments — the channel test you design in Lecture 2.
4. **Focus** all your effort on the *one* channel that works, until it stops working.

The mistake to avoid: testing one channel so half-heartedly you can't tell if it failed or you just didn't try. A real test has a real list, a real time box, and a real success bar set *in advance.* That's the whole point of this week's field work.

> **Maria's choice:** her inner ring is (a) cold email to named clinics, (b) a referral ask to the two clinics she already knows, and (c) a post in a dental-practice-management forum. She tests cold email first — biggest reachable volume, most learning per hour — with a two-week box and a pre-set bar. Community and referral wait for the middle ring.

### Concrete channels to brainstorm from

When you do the Bullseye brainstorm, don't stare at a blank page. Here's a starter list of channels, grouped by motion, to react against. Most won't fit you — that's fine; the point is to force breadth before you narrow.

- **Founder-led sales:** cold email to a named list, cold LinkedIn DMs, warm intros from your network, in-person at a conference/trade show, referral asks to existing users, "founder office hours" booked from your site.
- **Community-led:** answering questions in a relevant subreddit/Discord/Slack, becoming a regular at a professional association or meetup, hosting a small event or roundtable, building a free tool the community shares.
- **Content/inbound:** SEO articles targeting buying-intent searches, a founder newsletter, short-form video, a flagship "how we did X" post, comparison pages ("X vs. Y"), a free template or calculator.
- **Paid:** search ads on high-intent keywords, niche newsletter sponsorships, a podcast read, retargeting site visitors. (Remember: only after economics are known.)
- **Partnerships/channel:** a marketplace/app-store listing, an integration with a tool your customers use, a reseller or affiliate deal, a co-marketing webinar with a complementary company.
- **PLG:** a genuinely useful free tier, an invite/collaboration loop, public artifacts that show off the product (shareable links, embeds), in-product upgrade prompts.

Pick three for your inner ring, justify each in one line, and commit to testing the single most promising one first.

## 6. The 2026 reality of channels

The motions are timeless; the channels inside them shift constantly. A few things that are true *right now* and that should shape your bet:

- **Organic reach is mostly dead, and cold email got harder.** AI-assisted outbound flooded every inbox through 2024–2025; generic templated cold email reply rates fell sharply. The response is not "stop emailing" — it's *narrow and personalize.* A hand-written email to 40 named, well-researched prospects beats a templated blast to 4,000 strangers, and it's not close.
- **Founder-led content is a default B2B channel now.** A founder posting with a real point of view on LinkedIn or X often out-converts the polished company account, because people buy from people. If content is your motion, *you* are the content, at least at first.
- **"Community as a moat" cooled off.** The 2021–2022 hype that every company needed a Discord faded. What survived: communities that solve a *recurring* real need. Don't start a community as a marketing tactic; join one as a member.
- **Marketplaces and app stores are channels with gatekeepers.** Listing in the Shopify/Slack/Notion/GitHub marketplace can be a real partnership-style channel — but you're subject to their rules and their take rate. Weigh it like any partnership: real reach, limited control.

The lesson: pick the *motion* for durability, pick the *channel* for the moment, and re-check the channel often because it will decay.

One more 2026 nuance worth naming: **AI lowered the cost of producing outreach and content to nearly zero — which means volume is no longer a moat, it's a liability.** When anyone can generate a thousand "personalized" emails or fifty blog posts in an afternoon, the channels flood and the average quality craters. The counter-move is the opposite of volume: deeper research, a real point of view, genuine relationships. The founders winning in 2026 aren't the ones sending the most; they're the ones whose forty messages are so obviously hand-made and relevant that they cut through the AI sludge. Treat "I could automate this" as a reason to be *more* deliberate, not less.

## 6b. The motions at a glance

Before we go deeper, here's the whole menu on one card. Keep it next to your Week 6 model.

| Motion | Best price band | Speed to first customers | Compounds? | Main risk | Founder energy |
|--------|-----------------|--------------------------|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Founder-led sales** | $1k–$10k+ ACV | Fast (days–weeks) | No (linear in your time) | Doesn't scale past you without hiring | Very high, personal |
| **Community-led** | Any, esp. niche | Slow (months) | Yes (trust compounds) | No real community exists; inauthenticity | High, sustained |
| **Content / inbound** | Any, esp. mid | Very slow (6–12 mo) | Yes (assets keep working) | Thin content nobody finds | Medium, consistent |
| **Paid acquisition** | Self-serve, fast convert | Fast (if economics known) | No (decays, gets pricier) | Scaling a loss; channel saturation | Low effort, high cash risk |
| **Partnerships / channel** | Mid–high | Slow + unreliable | Sometimes | Out of your control; big-co speed | Medium, political |
| **Product-led (PLG)** | Low, self-serve | Medium | Yes (viral loops) | Product not ready; weak aha | Low once built, high to build |

The single most common mistake reading this table: picking the row that *feels* most like a "real startup" (paid, PLG) instead of the row that fits *your* price, market, and stage (usually founder-led or community early on). Fit beats flattery, every time.

## 7. How founder-led sales actually works (the motion most of you will pick)

Because founder-led sales is the right first motion for the majority of C25 founders, it's worth a concrete walkthrough. It is not mysterious; it is a craft with a shape.

**Step 1 — Build a named list.** Not "small businesses." A spreadsheet with rows: company, the specific human you'll contact, why they fit your ICP, and one detail you know about them. Sources: directories, professional associations, LinkedIn, your own network, the customers you interviewed in Weeks 2–3. Forty good rows beats four hundred guesses.

**Step 2 — Reach out, personally, one at a time.** A short message to a specific person about a specific problem you believe they have. Three sentences: a hook that proves you know who they are, the value in plain language, and one clear ask (a 15-minute call). No attachments, no nine-paragraph manifesto, no "hop on a quick call to pick your brain." Respect their time and you'll earn some of it.

**Step 3 — Take the call yourself.** This is customer discovery and selling at once. You listen more than you pitch. You ask what they do today, what it costs them, what they've tried. You show the product against *their* problem, not a generic feature tour. You hear the real objection — and the real objection is gold, because it's the thing every other prospect is silently thinking too.

**Step 4 — Make it absurdly easy to say yes.** Offer to set it up for them (concierge). Remove every step of friction — the contract, the payment, the onboarding. Early on, you are not optimizing for scalable process; you are optimizing for *one more believer.*

**Step 5 — Log everything and look for the pattern.** After ten conversations you'll start to hear the same objection, the same "aha," the same reason people stall. That pattern is the raw material for your messaging, your pricing, your product roadmap, and eventually your scalable, repeatable sales process. You cannot skip to the repeatable process; you earn it by doing the first ten by hand.

The emotional truth nobody tells first-time founders: **this feels uncomfortable, and the discomfort is not a signal that you're doing it wrong.** Selling your own thing to a stranger triggers fear of rejection. Every founder feels it. The ones who succeed do it anyway, because there is no other way to learn whether anyone wants what you're building. If founder-led sales is your motion, this week is partly about getting over that flinch.

## 8. Three real-world distribution stories

Frameworks stick better with examples. None of these companies "built it and waited."

- **Stripe (founder-led + unscalable).** The Collison brothers didn't send prospects a signup link. When someone showed interest, they'd say "give me your laptop" and install Stripe *for them* on the spot — "Collison installation." Wildly unscalable. It got them their first users and taught them exactly where integration hurt. Today Stripe is developer-led/PLG, but it *started* by hand.
- **Airbnb (community + unscalable + a channel hack).** The founders flew to New York, met hosts in person, and photographed listings themselves because amateur photos were killing conversion. Pure do-things-that-don't-scale. (They also famously bootstrapped via a Craigslist integration — a clever partnership/channel hack — but the foundational work was manual and personal.)
- **Notion (community-led + content).** After a near-death restart, Notion grew through a devoted community and template-sharing rather than a big paid push. Users made and shared templates; that user-generated content became the channel. It compounded slowly, then suddenly.

The common thread: the *first* customers came from concentrated, unscalable, founder-driven effort. The scalable motion came later, built on what that effort taught. Your job this week is the first part.

## 9. A quick word on B2B vs. B2C motions

The motion menu is the same, but the weighting differs:

- **B2B (selling to businesses):** founder-led sales dominates early, especially above ~$1k ACV. Customers are nameable, decisions are rational(ish), and a human relationship closes deals. Most C25 founders building B2B should default to founder-led sales first.
- **B2C (selling to consumers):** founder-led sales rarely works — you can't personally sell to a million individuals worth $5 each. Consumer plays lean on content, community, virality/PLG, and (once economics are proven) paid. The unscalable principle still holds, but it looks like "personally onboard your first 100 users in a Discord," not "demo to each one."

If you're unsure which you are: who signs the check? A business → B2B reflexes. An individual spending their own money → B2C reflexes. The price-point ladder still governs either way.

## 10. Common failure modes (so you can name yours)

- **"We'll do marketing later."** Distribution deferred is distribution never. Decide the motion now.
- **"Build it and they will come."** Already covered — it has never been true. The graveyard is full of great products nobody found.
- **Channel-hopping.** Trying a new channel every week because none "worked" after three days. Nothing works in three days. Time-box for real and respect the box.
- **Vanity motion.** Choosing the motion that *feels* like a startup (ads, a slick launch) over the one that fits (boring, manual founder-led sales). Fit beats flattery.
- **Premature scaling.** Automating, hiring a salesperson, or pouring money into ads before the manual version proved the funnel converts. Unscalable first.
- **Untracked effort.** Doing a bunch of outreach and not logging contact → reply → demo → paid, so you can't tell what worked. If you don't track it, you didn't really run the experiment.

## 10b. Motions are sequenced, not chosen once

A subtle point that trips up founders who treat this like a one-time decision: **your motion will evolve as the company grows, and that's correct.** What you pick this week is your *first* motion — the one that gets you from zero to your first handful of paying customers. It is almost never your *forever* motion.

A common, healthy sequence for B2B:

1. **Founder-led sales** (0 → first ~10 customers). The founder learns the channel, the objections, the message, the pricing. Unscalable on purpose.
2. **Repeatable sales** (10 → ~50). The founder turns the patterns into a playbook — a script, a deck, a standard demo — and maybe hires the first salesperson, who runs the playbook the founder proved.
3. **A scalable acquisition layer added on top** (50+). Now that the funnel converts, you layer in content (which has had time to compound), or paid (whose economics you can now predict), or partnerships (which you now have the credibility to land).

Notice that the *scalable* motions sit on top of the *learning* you only got from the unscalable one. You can't start at step 3. Founders who try — who launch with a content calendar and a paid budget before they've personally sold to ten people — burn money producing content and ads that don't convert, because they never learned what makes a customer say yes.

For B2C the sequence rhymes: manually onboard and delight the first ~100 users (unscalable), find the loop or the content that pulls in the next thousand (compounding), then pour fuel on whatever works (paid, once economics are proven).

**The practical takeaway for this week:** pick the motion that fits *zero-to-first-customers* right now. Don't agonize that it "won't scale" — it's not supposed to yet. Scaling is a Phase-4 and post-course problem. Your job this week is the first rung.

## 10c. A 90-second self-check before you commit

Run your chosen motion through these five questions. If you can't answer all five crisply, you haven't chosen yet — you've guessed.

1. **Can I name my prospects?** If yes → outbound/founder-led is on the table. If no → you need a pull motion (content, community, PLG) or you need to narrow your market until you *can* name them.
2. **What is a customer worth (ACV)?** This sets the price-point ladder and rules motions in or out. Below ~$100/yr, no human-touch motion survives.
3. **Where do my customers already gather?** A real community → community-led is viable. Nowhere obvious → it isn't.
4. **What's the one unscalable thing I'll do first?** If you can't name it, you haven't internalized "do things that don't scale."
5. **What's the single channel I'll test this week, and why that one first?** Most learning per hour, or most reachable volume. One answer, not three.

Maria answers all five in one breath: "I can name 4,000 clinics; ACV is $1,188; they don't gather anywhere online but they're directory-listed; I'll set up the first five by hand; I'll test cold email first because it's the most reachable volume." That's a founder who has *chosen*. Be that founder by Monday's end.

## 11. Recap

You should now be able to:

- Separate **motion** (the system), **channel** (the path), and **campaign** (the push), and explain why you measure and commit to each differently.
- Name the **five-and-a-half motions** — founder-led sales, community-led, content/inbound, paid, partnerships, and PLG — and say when each fits and how it fails.
- Use the **price-point ladder** and channel/model fit to match a motion to *your* Week 6 model, and spot the two classic mismatches.
- Explain why you **do things that don't scale first**, and name one unscalable move you'll make this week.
- Commit to **one motion and one channel** to test now, using the Bullseye discipline, instead of spreading thin.
- Account for the **2026 channel realities** — dead organic reach, harder cold email, founder-led content, cooled community hype, and the AI-volume trap.
- Walk through **how founder-led sales actually works**, the motion most of you will pick first.
- Understand that motions are **sequenced over the life of the company**, not chosen once — what you pick now is your *first* motion, not your forever one.
- Run the **90-second self-check** before committing, and pass all five questions crisply.

**Your homework before Lecture 2:** write your one-sentence motion statement ("We reach [customer] through [motion] because [reason tied to price + reach]"), name your inner-ring channel, and name the one unscalable thing you'll do first. Bring it to the motion-fit activity and let the room poke holes.
