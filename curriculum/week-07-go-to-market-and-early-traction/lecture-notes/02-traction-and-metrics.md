# Lecture 2 — Traction and Metrics

> **Duration:** ~45 min. **Outcome:** You can pick the one metric that matters, separate vanity from actionable metrics, and read a funnel.

> **Traction is evidence that people use and pay, not evidence that people noticed.**

## 1. The one metric that matters

At any early stage, one metric best captures whether you are making progress. Pick it and let it drive focus:

- Pre-revenue: activated users who do the core action repeatedly.
- Early revenue: paying customers, or recurring revenue.
- Marketplace: completed transactions.

Tracking forty metrics early is a way to avoid facing the one that matters.

## 2. Vanity vs. actionable metrics

- **Vanity:** big, flattering, and unactionable — total page views, total signups, social followers, "registered users" who never returned.
- **Actionable:** tied to real behavior and decisions — activation rate, retention, conversion to paid, revenue, repeat usage.

The test: *if this number went up, would I know what I did right and what to do next?* If not, it is vanity.

## 3. The funnel

Most acquisition can be read as a funnel:

```
Reach  →  Interest  →  Activation  →  Retention / Revenue
(saw it)  (clicked/   (did the core   (came back /
           replied)    action once)    paid)
```

Put real numbers on each step. The **leakiest step** — the biggest percentage drop — is where to focus, because fixing the worst leak moves the whole funnel most.

## 4. Cohorts and retention, briefly

The truest early signal is **retention**: do the people who try it come back? A leaky bucket (lots of signups, no one stays) means the product is not yet solving the problem, no matter how good acquisition looks. Retention is hard to fake and the metric investors trust most.

## 5. Design a cheap channel test

For your chosen motion: a specific action (e.g., contact 30 clinics), a time box (one week), and a pre-set success bar (e.g., 3 demos booked). Run it, record the funnel, find the leak. That is this week's experiment.
