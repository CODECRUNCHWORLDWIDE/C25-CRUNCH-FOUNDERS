# Week 7 — Go-to-Market & Early Traction

Welcome to **Phase 3, Week 2** of C25 · Crunch Founders. Last week you built a model that *could* make money on paper — a lean canvas and a unit-economics sheet where CAC, LTV, contribution margin, and payback all line up. This week we attack the question that kills more startups than any spreadsheet error: **even if the math works, how will the first hundred customers actually find you, and how will you know it's working before you've burned a year?**

A model that can make money is worthless if customers never arrive. This week is about the *motion* — the repeatable way the first customers reliably come — and about measuring early traction without lying to yourself. Most early founders fail one of two ways. Either they have no go-to-market plan at all ("we'll figure out marketing after we build the product"), or they chase **vanity metrics** that feel like progress but prove nothing: impressions, signups, followers, waitlist numbers. By Sunday you will have picked one motion, defined the single metric that matters at your stage, run a real time-boxed channel test, and built a funnel you can read honestly.

This is a business week. You will write **zero code**. You will write emails, count conversions, and make a defensible bet about where your first customers come from.

The deliverable is a **go-to-market one-pager**: the motion you're betting on, the first channel you'll test, and the single traction metric you'll hold yourself to — backed by a real funnel from a real test you ran this week.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** a go-to-market *motion* (the repeatable acquisition system) from a *channel* (one tactic inside it) and from a *campaign* (one time-boxed push) — three things founders constantly conflate.
- **Choose** a GTM motion that fits your model: founder-led sales, community-led, content/inbound, paid acquisition, partnerships/channel, or product-led growth — and defend why it matches your price point and customer.
- **Apply** the "do things that don't scale" principle correctly: when manual, unscalable acquisition is the *right* move and when it's an excuse to avoid distribution.
- **Pick** the One Metric That Matters (OMTM) for your stage and ignore the rest, without pretending the others don't exist.
- **Separate** vanity metrics from actionable metrics using three tests: does it change behavior, is it comparable over time, is it a ratio or a rate rather than a cumulative total.
- **Build** an AARRR / pirate-metrics funnel (Acquisition → Activation → Retention → Referral → Revenue) and diagnose the single leakiest step.
- **Design** a cheap, time-boxed channel experiment with a pre-registered success bar — so you decide *before* you see the data what counts as a win.
- **Compute** the early-stage numbers that matter: conversion rate per step, blended CAC from a real spend, and a back-of-envelope payback against last week's LTV.
- **Read** a funnel honestly, name what to fix first, and decide: double down, fix the leak, or kill the channel.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed **C25 Weeks 1–6**, or have equivalent founder progress. Specifically:

- A **chosen problem and customer segment** (Weeks 2–3) — you know who you're selling to and have talked to at least five of them.
- A **lean canvas and unit-economics sheet** (Week 6) — you have a price, a contribution margin, an estimated LTV, and a CAC you *need* to beat for the model to work. We will hold this week's channel test against that CAC number constantly.
- An **MVP or clickable prototype** (Week 5) — something a real prospect can be pointed at, even if it's a Figma flow or a concierge service behind a form. You cannot run an activation test with nothing to activate.
- Comfort sending **cold outreach** — a plain email or DM to someone you don't know. If that makes you flinch, this is the week you get over it.

You do **not** need a finished product, a budget for ads, or any marketing background. The whole point of early-stage GTM is that it's done by hand, by the founder, with almost no money.

## Topics covered

- The vocabulary: motion vs. channel vs. campaign vs. tactic, and why the distinction changes what you measure.
- The five-and-a-half GTM motions: founder-led sales, community-led, content/inbound, paid acquisition, partnerships/channel, and product-led growth (the half — most early companies aren't ready for it).
- Matching a motion to a model: the price-point heuristic (the "$X ACV → which motion" ladder) and why a $5/month product can't afford a salesperson.
- "Do things that don't scale" (Graham): concierge onboarding, manual recruiting of the first users, the founder as the first salesperson.
- The One Metric That Matters (Croll & Yoskovitz) and stage-appropriate focus.
- Vanity vs. actionable metrics; the three-question test; why cumulative totals lie.
- AARRR / pirate metrics (McClure) as a funnel skeleton; the difference between acquisition and activation.
- Leading vs. lagging indicators; why early founders should watch leading indicators.
- Funnel arithmetic: per-step conversion, blended vs. fully-loaded CAC, payback against LTV.
- Designing a channel experiment: the success bar, the sample size, the time box, the pre-registered decision rule.
- Reading the result: double down, fix the leak, or kill the channel — and the trap of "one more week."
- Modern 2026 realities: AI-assisted outbound and why reply rates fell, the death of organic reach, the rise of "founder-led content," LinkedIn/communities/Discord as B2B channels, and why "build it and they will come" never worked.

## In-class activities

- **Motion fit, defended.** Each founder states their chosen motion in one sentence — "We reach clinics through founder-led outbound because they're a small, named, high-ACV list" — and the room pokes holes. High-touch sales for a $5/month product is a red flag; running paid ads to a 200-account total market is another. You leave with your motion either confirmed or revised.
- **Vanity-metric hunt.** A list of 15 metrics on the board. The group classifies each as vanity or actionable *for an early startup*, and rewrites every vanity metric into an actionable one (e.g., "10,000 page views" → "5% of visitors start the trial").
- **Leakiest-step triage.** Given a funnel with real-looking numbers, the group finds the single biggest drop-off, distinguishes "small numbers everywhere" from "one catastrophic leak," and decides the *one* thing to fix before next week.
- **Pre-register the bet.** Before anyone runs anything, each founder writes their success bar on an index card and hands it in — so on Friday nobody can move the goalposts.

## Worked example (the thread we'll follow all week)

We carry one running example through every lecture, exercise, and the mini-project: **Maria, founder of "ClinicFlow,"** a $99/month no-show-reduction tool for small independent medical and dental clinics. (You met a version of her in Week 6's unit-economics sheet.)

Maria sells a $99/month tool to a **small, reachable, named** set of clinics. Broad paid ads — impersonal, sprayed at strangers — make no sense: her entire serviceable market is maybe 4,000 clinics in three states, and she can *name* them. The right motion is **founder-led sales**: she emails and visits clinics directly, doing things that don't scale. Her One Metric That Matters is not "website visits" (a vanity number that goes up no matter what) but **clinics that complete a paid onboarding** — money in the bank, product in use.

Her first real funnel, from a two-week test: **40 clinics contacted → 12 replied → 6 took a demo → 3 started a trial → 2 paid.** The leakiest step is demo → trial (6 down to 3 — half walk away after seeing it), so that's where she focuses next week. Two paying clinics is small. It is also *real* traction. A thousand newsletter signups would not be, because none of them proved willingness to pay. Against last week's LTV of roughly $1,400 per clinic and the ~$50 of her time-valued effort per contact, her early CAC math says this motion can work — if she fixes the demo.

When this week's instructions say "do this for your startup," Maria's version shows you the shape of a good answer.

## The "real traction" promise

C25 uses a recurring marker for the difference between motion and noise. Every claim you make this week about traction has to survive one question:

> **"Would a skeptical investor count this?"**

A thousand waitlist signups: no. Ten people who paid you money: yes. Five who renewed: absolutely. If your metric goes up when you do nothing, it's vanity. If it only moves when you do real work and reflects real customer behavior, it's traction. The point of this week is to make "two paying customers" feel like a bigger win than "ten thousand impressions," because to anyone who matters, it is.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Read Paul Graham's **"Do Things That Don't Scale"** in full and write a paragraph on which unscalable thing *you* are avoiding: <https://paulgraham.com/ds.html>.
- Map all five-and-a-half motions onto your idea and write the one-line reason each is or isn't viable — then commit to one with conviction.
- Run a *second*, deliberately different channel test (a contrasting motion) and compare the two funnels side by side. Most founders never test two; the comparison is where conviction comes from.
- Read Brian Balfour's **"Four Fits"** essay set on why a channel that works for one company fails for another: <https://brianbalfour.com/essays/key-lessons-for-100-million-growth>.
- Build a tiny tracking sheet that logs every contact, reply, demo, and conversion with a timestamp — so next week you have cohort data, not anecdotes.
