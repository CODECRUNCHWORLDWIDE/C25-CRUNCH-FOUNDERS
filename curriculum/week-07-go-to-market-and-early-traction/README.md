# Week 7 — Go-to-Market & Early Traction

A model that can make money is worthless if customers never find you. This week is about the *motion* — how the first customers will reliably come — and about measuring early traction honestly. Most early founders either have no go-to-market plan at all ("we'll do marketing later") or chase vanity metrics that feel like progress but prove nothing. You will pick one motion, define the single metric that matters at your stage, and build a small funnel you can read without lying to yourself.

The deliverable is a go-to-market one-pager naming the motion, the first channel to test, and the single traction metric you will hold yourself to.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Choose a go-to-market motion** that fits your model: founder-led sales, community, content, paid, or partnerships.
- **Pick the one metric that matters** for your stage, and ignore the rest.
- **Distinguish traction from vanity metrics**.
- **Sketch a funnel** (reach → interest → activation → retention/revenue) and identify the leakiest step.
- **Design a small, time-boxed channel test** with a pre-set success bar.

## Topics covered

- Go-to-market motions and how to match one to your model
- "Do things that don't scale" early: founder-led, manual acquisition
- The one-metric-that-matters idea for focus
- Vanity metrics vs. actionable metrics
- The funnel and the leakiest-step diagnosis
- Designing a cheap channel experiment

## In-class activities

- **Motion fit.** Each founder defends why their chosen motion matches their customer and price; the room pokes holes (high-touch sales for a $5/month product is a red flag, and so on).
- **Vanity-metric hunt.** A list of metrics; classify each as vanity or actionable for an early startup.
- **Leakiest step.** Given funnel numbers, the group finds where the biggest drop is and what to fix first.

## Worked example

The clinic founder sells a $99/month tool to a small, reachable set of clinics — so paid ads (broad, impersonal) make no sense. The right motion is **founder-led sales**: she emails and visits clinics directly, doing things that don't scale. Her one metric that matters is not "website visits" (vanity) but **clinics that complete a paid onboarding** (actionable). Her funnel: 40 clinics contacted → 12 demos → 5 trials → 2 paying. The leakiest step is demo → trial, so that is where she focuses next. Two paying clinics is small, but it is *real* traction; a thousand newsletter signups would not be.
