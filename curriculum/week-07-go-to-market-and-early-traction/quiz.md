# Week 7 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 8/10 before moving to Week 8. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

**Q1.** A go-to-market **motion** is best defined as:

- A) A single slide in your pitch deck.
- B) The repeatable *system* by which customers reliably come to you.
- C) One specific tactic, like a LinkedIn post.
- D) The product roadmap for the next quarter.

---

**Q2.** You're selling a $9/month consumer app to anyone with a smartphone. Which motion is the **worst** fit?

- A) Content / inbound.
- B) Product-led growth.
- C) Founder-led, high-touch sales (personal demos to each user).
- D) Community-led.

---

**Q3.** Founder-led sales is the strongest fit when:

- A) The product is cheap and the audience is enormous.
- B) The price is meaningful and the customers are few and *nameable*.
- C) You have a large paid-ads budget and want to scale fast.
- D) There is no product or prototype yet at all.

---

**Q4.** "Do things that don't scale" means, in practice:

- A) Avoid automation forever because scaling is bad.
- B) Manually recruit and onboard your first customers by hand, to learn the channel — then automate once it works.
- C) Only build features that can't scale.
- D) Hire a sales team before you have any customers.

---

**Q5.** Which of these is a **vanity** metric for an early startup?

- A) Conversion rate from trial to paid.
- B) Week-4 retention of a signup cohort.
- C) Total cumulative page views, with no usage behind them.
- D) Monthly recurring revenue.

---

**Q6.** The three-question test for whether a metric is actionable asks all of the following **except**:

- A) Does it change behavior — would you know what to do if it moved?
- B) Is it comparable over time / across cohorts?
- C) Is it a rate or ratio rather than a cumulative total?
- D) Is it large enough to impress an investor at a glance?

---

**Q7.** Your funnel reads: 40 contacted → 12 replied (30%) → 6 demoed (50%) → 3 trialed (50%) → 2 paid (67%). Which step should you focus on fixing first?

- A) Contacted → replied, because 30% is the lowest single rate at the top.
- B) Demo → trial, the biggest *controllable* drop where people see the product and walk away.
- C) Trial → paid, because it has the fewest people.
- D) None — 2 paid from 40 is fine, leave it alone.

---

**Q8.** In the AARRR funnel, the difference between **Acquisition** and **Activation** is:

- A) There is no difference; they're two words for "signup."
- B) Acquisition is "they showed up"; Activation is "they experienced the core value (the aha)."
- C) Acquisition is paid; Activation is organic.
- D) Acquisition is for B2B; Activation is for B2C.

---

**Q9.** Why do you **pre-register** a channel test's success bar before running it?

- A) Investors require it by law.
- B) So you can't retroactively invent a reason a weak result is "actually promising" — it keeps you honest and avoids the "one more week" trap.
- C) Because the bar can never be changed afterward under any circumstances.
- D) It makes the test run faster.

---

**Q10.** The truest early signal that your product delivers real value is:

- A) Social media followers.
- B) Total signups ever.
- C) Retention — whether the people who try it come back.
- D) Press coverage and impressions.

---

**Q11.** You ran a two-week test: ~$0 cash, 20 hours of your time valued at $50/hr, and won 2 paying customers. Your Week-6 LTV is $1,400. Which statement is most defensible?

- A) "My CAC is exactly $500 and my LTV:CAC is exactly 2.8:1 — proven."
- B) "Blended CAC is roughly $500; LTV:CAC ≈ 2.8:1 — directionally the motion looks affordable, but a sample of 2 is too small to call it precise."
- C) "CAC is $0 because I didn't spend cash."
- D) "LTV:CAC doesn't apply pre-traction, so the numbers are meaningless."

---

**Q12.** "Build it and they will come" is:

- A) A reliable strategy if the product is good enough.
- B) Sound advice for B2B but not B2C.
- C) Never true — distribution is a design decision you make now, as hard as building the product.
- D) Only false for paid channels.

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — a motion is the repeatable *system*, not a slide (A/D), and not a single tactic — that's a channel (C). (Lecture 1 §1)
2. **C** — at $9/month you cannot afford to personally demo to each user; the math never closes. Cheap + huge audience needs a no-touch motion. (Lecture 1 §3, price-point ladder)
3. **B** — meaningful price + few, nameable customers is the founder-led sweet spot. (Lecture 1 §2–3)
4. **B** — the point of unscalable work is to *learn the channel* and get the first customers, then automate once it works. (Lecture 1 §4)
5. **C** — a cumulative total with no behavior behind it only goes up and drives no decision; the others are rates/behaviors tied to money. (Lecture 2 §3)
6. **D** — "impressive to an investor" is not one of the three tests; that's the *vanity* instinct. The real tests are behavior, comparability, and rate-not-total. (Lecture 2 §3)
7. **B** — read by *rate*: demo → trial is the biggest controllable drop and a conviction problem worth fixing. Panicking over "2 of 40" (D) or chasing the top rate (A) misreads the funnel. (Lecture 2 §4)
8. **B** — Acquisition = showed up; Activation = got value. Conflating them hides the leaky-bucket problem. (Lecture 2 §4)
9. **B** — pre-registering stops goalpost-moving and the "one more week" trap. It's not a law (A) and a bar *can* inform a deliberate, honest re-test (C is too absolute). (Lecture 2 §8)
10. **C** — retention is hardest to fake and the signal investors trust most. (Lecture 2 §6)
11. **B** — the numbers are directional and useful, but a sample of 2 can't be treated as precise. Don't over-fit tiny samples; don't ignore your time as cost (C). (Lecture 2 §7)
12. **C** — it has never been true; distribution is a present-tense design decision. (Lecture 1, opening)

</details>
