# Week 5 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 6. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** An MVP is best understood as:

- A) Version one of the real product, just with fewer features.
- B) The smallest experiment that produces validated learning about your riskiest assumption.
- C) A fully featured private beta.
- D) The pitch deck you show investors.

---

**Q2.** Your **riskiest assumption** is the one that is:

- A) Easiest and cheapest to test.
- B) Most fun to build.
- C) Both most uncertain *and* most fatal if false.
- D) The one your competitors are working on.

---

**Q3.** For a typical (non-deep-tech) SMB software idea, which assumption is *usually* riskiest?

- A) "We can technically build it."
- B) "There's a clever algorithm at the core."
- C) "People will actually adopt it and change their behavior."
- D) "We can register the domain name."

---

**Q4.** A **Wizard of Oz** MVP differs from a **concierge** MVP because:

- A) The Wizard of Oz is fully automated; the concierge is manual.
- B) In a Wizard of Oz the customer thinks it's automated while humans do the work; in a concierge the customer knows you're doing it by hand.
- C) They are two names for the same thing.
- D) The concierge requires the most engineering.

---

**Q5.** You want to test "is there any demand at all?" before building anything. The cheapest fitting MVP type is:

- A) A single-feature build.
- B) A clickable prototype.
- C) A fake door / smoke test.
- D) A full private beta.

---

**Q6.** Why set the success threshold *before* launching?

- A) It's legally required.
- B) So you can't rationalize a weak result into a "win" after the fact.
- C) To make the MVP cheaper to build.
- D) Because investors mandate it.

---

**Q7.** Which of these is a **vanity metric**?

- A) The percentage of signups who completed the key action this week.
- B) Cumulative total signups since launch.
- C) Paid conversion rate.
- D) Day-3 retention of this week's cohort.

---

**Q8.** In the build → measure → learn loop, the thing you are trying to *minimize* is:

- A) The number of features in the MVP.
- B) The total time it takes to get all the way around the loop.
- C) The amount of money spent on design.
- D) The number of customers who see the MVP.

---

**Q9.** A founder set a threshold of 10% CTA conversion, got 2%, and now says "that's still early signal — we'll just improve the copy and keep going to a full build." This is most likely an example of:

- A) Healthy perseverance.
- B) A well-named pivot.
- C) Moving the goalposts / sunk-cost perseverance.
- D) Validated learning.

---

**Q10.** A **pivot** is:

- A) Quitting the idea entirely and starting from a blank page.
- B) Re-running the exact same experiment hoping for a kinder result.
- C) A structured change of *one* major variable, grounded in what you learned, keeping the validated parts.
- D) Raising money so you can keep going unchanged.

---

**Q11.** Maya's concierge test refilled 9 of 20 cancellations (45%) across 2 clinics against a pre-set 30% threshold. The *most defensible* next move is:

- A) Pivot the customer segment immediately.
- B) Persevere to the next-riskiest assumption (e.g., willingness to pay), noting confidence is moderate at n=2.
- C) Declare the company validated and start hiring engineers.
- D) Re-run the identical test five more times before doing anything.

---

**Q12.** A fake door's CTA advertises a product that doesn't exist yet. Which version is **ethically fine**?

- A) After the click, show "Order confirmed — shipping soon!" for an order that will never ship.
- B) After the click, charge the card and keep the money with no product or refund.
- C) After the click, show "We're not quite ready — join the waitlist and we'll email you."
- D) Collect emails, then sell them to a third party without telling anyone.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — an MVP is an experiment for validated learning, not a smaller product. (L1 §1)
2. **C** — riskiest = most uncertain *and* most fatal. (L1 §2)
3. **C** — for non-deep-tech ideas, adoption/behavior change usually outranks feasibility in risk. (L1 §2.3)
4. **B** — the key difference is whether the customer *knows* it's manual. (L1 §3.2–3.3)
5. **C** — a fake door / smoke test is the cheapest way to test raw demand. (L1 §3.1)
6. **B** — pre-committing the threshold stops post-hoc rationalization. (L2 §2.1)
7. **B** — a cumulative total only goes up and changes no decision; the others are actionable rates/cohorts. (L2 §3)
8. **B** — minimize total time through the loop; speed of *learning*, not of building. (L2 §1)
9. **C** — a clear miss reframed as "signal" with the goalpost effectively moved; the classic sunk-cost trap. (L2 §6, §8)
10. **C** — a learned change of one major variable, keeping a foot planted. (L2 §6.2)
11. **B** — a clean pass; persevere to the next risk, but hold confidence modest given n=2. (L2 §4.1, §6.1)
12. **C** — testing demand for a not-yet-built thing is fine; lying about the outcome, charging without honoring, or misusing data is not. (L1 §6)

</details>
