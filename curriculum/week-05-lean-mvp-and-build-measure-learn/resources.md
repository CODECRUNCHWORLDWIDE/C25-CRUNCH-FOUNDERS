## The pivot vocabulary (so you name the move, not just "change")

When your result misses, you don't just "pivot" — you pick *which* pivot. Skim the named types before Friday:

- Zoom-in / zoom-out (a feature becomes the whole product, or vice versa)
- Customer-segment (same problem, different buyer)
- Customer-need / problem (same buyer, different problem)
- Platform, channel, business-architecture, value-capture, engine-of-growth pivots
- Reference: <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lean_startup#Pivot>

## Free / low-cost tools to build a quick MVP

Pick the lightest tool that can test your assumption. You almost never need more than one of these.

- **Carrd** — single-page sites and fake doors; free tier covers a basic landing page: <https://carrd.co/>
- **Tally** — free, unlimited forms with logic; the modern Typeform alternative for concierge intake and waitlists: <https://tally.so/>
- **Google Forms** — zero-setup intake, surveys, signup capture: <https://docs.google.com/forms>
- **Figma** — clickable prototypes; free starter plan is plenty for a flow test: <https://www.figma.com/>
- **Framer** — design-to-live-site for a higher-fidelity fake door; free tier publishes a `.framer.website` URL: <https://www.framer.com/>
- **GitHub Pages** — host a static fake-door page for free on your own repo: <https://pages.github.com/>
- **Notion** — a "product" that's really a shared doc; great Wizard-of-Oz backend: <https://www.notion.so/>
- **Airtable** — the manual database behind a concierge/Wizard-of-Oz MVP: <https://airtable.com/>
- **Zapier / Make** — glue that fakes "automation" while a human does the real work: <https://zapier.com/> · <https://www.make.com/>

## Measuring intent and willingness to pay

- **Stripe Payment Links** — capture *real* willingness-to-pay with a no-code checkout; the strongest signal there is. (Only charge for something you can deliver; refund if you can't.) <https://stripe.com/payments/payment-links>
- **Buy-button / fake-door pattern** — measure click-through on a "Buy / Get started" button that leads to a "we're not quite ready — leave your email" capture. Honest version explained in the ethics section of Lecture 1.

## Instrumentation (so "measure" is real, not a guess)

- **Plausible Analytics** — privacy-friendly, simple page + event analytics; free trial, cheap after: <https://plausible.io/>
- **PostHog** — free generous tier for product analytics, funnels, and session capture if your MVP is interactive: <https://posthog.com/>
- **Google Analytics 4** — free; heavier, but everywhere: <https://analytics.google.com/>
- **A spreadsheet** — for a one-week concierge test, a Google Sheet with one row per attempt is better instrumentation than any analytics tool. Don't over-build measurement either.

## Ethics, honesty, and the law (read before you fake a door)

- **FTC — endorsements, testimonials, and "deceptive" marketing basics** (US): be careful what your fake door *promises*: <https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/advertising-marketing>
- **GDPR / privacy basics** — if you collect emails or any personal data, you owe people basic transparency and the ability to be forgotten: <https://gdpr.eu/what-is-gdpr/>
- Rule of thumb for fake doors: it's fine to test demand for something that doesn't exist yet; it's not fine to take money you can't honor or to imply a thing exists when it doesn't. "Coming soon — join the waitlist" after the click is honest. A charge with no product is not.

## Glossary cheat sheet

Keep this open in a tab.

| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| **MVP** | The smallest experiment that produces validated learning about your riskiest assumption. Not a tiny product. |
| **Riskiest assumption** | The belief that is both most uncertain and most fatal if false. Test it first. |
| **Fake door / smoke test** | A page advertising an offer with a buy/sign-up button, to measure intent before anything is built. |
| **Concierge MVP** | You deliver the service manually, by hand, to a few customers. Tests whether the solution helps. |
| **Wizard of Oz** | The customer sees an "automated" product; humans do the work behind the curtain. |
| **Clickable prototype** | A Figma/slide click-through that tests whether people understand and want the flow. |
| **Build-measure-learn** | The lean loop. The goal is to minimize *total time through the loop*, not time spent building. |
| **Validated learning** | A result that would actually change your mind. If a bad result wouldn't move you, it isn't validated learning. |
| **Vanity metric** | A number that goes up and feels good but doesn't change a decision (total signups, page views). |
| **Actionable metric** | A behavioral number tied to a decision (activation rate, refill rate, paid conversion). |
| **Threshold** | The success number you commit to *before* launch, so you can't move the goalposts. |
| **Pivot** | A learned change of direction — one major variable changed, then the loop re-runs. Not quitting; not repeating. |
| **Persevere** | The metric cleared the threshold; keep the hypothesis and build the next-cheapest layer. |
| **OMTM** | "One metric that matters" — the single number that defines success at your current stage. |
