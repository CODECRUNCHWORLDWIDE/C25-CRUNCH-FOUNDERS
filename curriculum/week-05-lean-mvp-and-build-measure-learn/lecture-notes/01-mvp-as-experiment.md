# Lecture 1 — MVP as Experiment

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can identify your riskiest assumption and choose the cheapest MVP type that tests it.

> **A minimum viable product is the smallest thing that produces validated learning about your riskiest assumption — not a tiny version of the final product.**

## 1. The reframe

Beginners build a "small version of the real app." That still takes weeks and tests nothing in particular. The lean reframe: an MVP is an *experiment* with a hypothesis, a method, and a measurable result. Ask "what is the cheapest thing that would teach me whether this works?" — often the answer involves little or no code.

## 2. Riskiest-assumption thinking

Every idea rests on a stack of assumptions. List them, then ask of each: *if this turns out false, does the whole thing collapse?* The one that is both most uncertain and most fatal is your **riskiest assumption**, and it is what the MVP must test first. Common riskiest assumptions:

- People will *adopt* a new tool for this (behavior change is hard).
- People will *pay* (or pay enough).
- You can *reach* customers affordably.
- The thing is *technically feasible* at all.

Test the scariest one first, not the easiest one.

## 3. MVP types (cheapest first)

- **Smoke test / fake door:** a landing page describing the offer with a "sign up / buy" button. Measures interest before anything exists.
- **Concierge:** you deliver the service manually, by hand, to a few customers. Tests whether the solution actually helps.
- **Wizard of Oz:** the customer sees an automated product; behind the curtain, humans do the work. Tests demand for the *experience* without building the engine.
- **Clickable prototype:** a Figma or slide click-through. Tests whether people understand and want the flow.
- **Single-feature build:** only when the riskiest assumption is genuinely technical and cannot be faked.

Match the type to the assumption. If the risk is "will they adopt," a fake door or concierge beats a build.

## 4. Keep it small on purpose

The discipline is to build *less* than feels comfortable. Anything that does not test the riskiest assumption is a distraction this week. You will build more later — once you know the core assumption holds.
