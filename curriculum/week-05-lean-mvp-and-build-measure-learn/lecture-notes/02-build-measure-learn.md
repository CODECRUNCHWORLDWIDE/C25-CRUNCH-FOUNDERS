# Lecture 2 — Build-Measure-Learn

> **Duration:** ~45 min. **Outcome:** You can run the lean loop, pick one honest metric, and decide persevere vs. pivot.

## 1. The loop

The lean methodology describes a cycle:

```
        ┌─────────┐
        │  IDEAS  │
        └────┬────┘
       learn │ build
        ┌────┴────┐
        │ MEASURE │ ← data ← PRODUCT(MVP)
        └─────────┘
```

You turn an idea into the smallest MVP (**build**), put it in front of real people and collect data (**measure**), and extract validated learning that updates the idea (**learn**). Then go again. The goal is to minimize the *total time through the loop* — speed of learning, not speed of building.

## 2. One metric, decided in advance

Pick the single metric that tells you whether the riskiest assumption held. Examples:

- Fake door: conversion rate on the sign-up/buy button.
- Concierge: how much of the job you completed and whether customers came back.
- Prototype: task completion / "would you use this weekly?" tied to observed behavior.

Then write the **threshold** before you launch. "Success = at least X." Pre-committing stops you from rationalizing a weak result into a "win."

## 3. Validated learning beats vanity

A metric is only useful if a bad result would change your mind. Beware vanity numbers (total page views, total signups with no usage) — Week 7 returns to this. The question is always: *did people do the thing that proves the assumption, or just say nice words?*

## 4. Persevere or pivot

- **Persevere:** the metric cleared the threshold. Keep the hypothesis; build the next-cheapest layer.
- **Pivot:** the metric missed. Change one major element of the hypothesis (segment, problem framing, solution mechanism, channel) and re-run the loop. A pivot is a *change of direction grounded in what you learned* — not giving up, and not stubbornly repeating.

Write the decision down with the number that drove it. That sentence is the most valuable artifact of the week.

## 5. Speed and cost

Each loop should be cheap and fast — days, not months. If your MVP took three weeks to build, it was probably a small product, not an experiment. Shrink it next time.
