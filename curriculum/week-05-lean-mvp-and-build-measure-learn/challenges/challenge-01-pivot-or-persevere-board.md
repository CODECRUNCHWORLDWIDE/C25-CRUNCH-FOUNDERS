# Challenge 1 — The Pivot-or-Persevere Board

**Time estimate:** ~90 minutes.

## Problem statement

You are sitting on the "board" for four startups — three fictional ones below, and your own (the MVP you're running this week). For each, you're handed an experiment result. Your job is to make a **named persevere-or-pivot call** and defend it with the number.

The catch: each fictional scenario contains a *trap* — a specific way founders misread evidence. Spot the trap, then decide. Real boardrooms are full of these traps wearing the costume of "good news," and the founders who survive are the ones who read the number, not the mood.

Produce a written decision memo: for each of the four cases, a short analysis and a one-sentence decision (metric → threshold → decision → named direction). Commit it to your Week 5 repo under `challenges/challenge-01/decision-memo.md`.

---

## Scenario A — "Tofu" (the meal-prep app)

**Riskiest assumption tested:** busy parents will pay for a weekly meal-prep planner.
**MVP:** a fake-door landing page with a "Start my plan — $12/mo" button leading to a "join the waitlist" capture.
**Pre-committed threshold (set before launch):** at least **8%** CTA click-through.
**Result:** The page got **240 visitors** and **31 clicked** the button (**12.9%**). The founder is thrilled and wants to start building the full app immediately. Buried in the notes: 180 of the 240 visitors came from the founder's personal Instagram and a parents' Facebook group she runs. Of the 31 clicks, 22 were from that warm audience.

> **Your call:** persevere, pivot, or gather more? Name the trap. Decide.

---

## Scenario B — "Relay" (the freelancer invoicing tool)

**Riskiest assumption tested:** freelancers will switch from spreadsheets to a dedicated invoicing tool.
**MVP:** a concierge — the founder manually created and chased invoices for 6 freelancers for two weeks.
**Pre-committed threshold:** at least **4 of 6** freelancers send a second batch of invoices through the founder (i.e., come back).
**Result:** **5 of 6** came back for a second batch; one even referred a friend. But the founder, reading industry blog posts about a crowded invoicing market, is spooked and wants to abandon invoicing entirely to chase a trendier "AI bookkeeping" idea a competitor just raised money for.

> **Your call:** persevere, pivot, or gather more? Name the trap. Decide.

---

## Scenario C — "Grove" (the community-garden marketplace)

**Riskiest assumption tested:** city dwellers will rent plots in neighbors' yards to grow food.
**MVP:** a clickable Figma prototype shown to 5 people in moderated sessions.
**Pre-committed threshold:** at least **4 of 5** complete the "find and book a plot" task *and* say they'd use it.
**Result:** **5 of 5** completed the task and all said "yeah, I'd totally use this." The founder counts it a clear pass and wants to persevere to a build. But no one was asked to do anything real — no deposit, no signup, no calendar hold. The founder also quietly notes the threshold was originally "4 of 5 *book a real plot*," which she softened to "complete the task and say they'd use it" *after* the sessions, because no real plots existed to book.

> **Your call:** persevere, pivot, or gather more? Name the trap (there are two). Decide.

---

## Scenario D — Your own MVP

Take the **real result** from your Week 5 mini-project (or, if it's not finished yet, your best honest projection of where it's heading). Apply the exact same rigor you applied to A–C:

- State your metric, threshold, and actual number.
- Identify any trap *you* might be falling into (it's hardest to see on your own idea).
- Make a named persevere-or-pivot call with the decision sentence.
- State your confidence given your sample size.

> This is the point of the whole challenge: the discipline you can apply coolly to someone else's data is the discipline you need on your own — when it's your savings and your ego on the line.

## What good looks like (peek only after you've decided)

<details>
<summary>Scenario A — the trap and a defensible call</summary>

**Trap:** *biased sample / signal vs. noise.* The headline 12.9% clears the 8% threshold, but the traffic was overwhelmingly the founder's warm network, who are not the cold market the business needs. Strip them out: ~60 cold visitors, ~9 cold clicks (~15%) — actually still promising, *but* the sample is now tiny and unrepresentative. **Defensible call:** *gather more* — re-run the fake door against cold, paid or unaffiliated traffic before persevering. Decision sentence: *"12.9% beat the 8% threshold but on a warm-biased sample, so we gather more with cold traffic before deciding."* (Persevering straight to a build is the sunk-cost-of-excitement trap.)

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario B — the trap and a defensible call</summary>

**Trap:** *panic-pivoting / shiny-object.* 5 of 6 came back against a threshold of 4 — a clean pass with deep behavioral signal (return usage + a referral). "Crowded market" blog posts and a competitor's raise are not evidence about *this* founder's validated traction. **Defensible call:** *persevere.* Decision sentence: *"5 of 6 returned against a 4-of-6 threshold, so we persevere to a willingness-to-pay test."* Abandoning a clear pass for a trendier idea is the classic panic pivot.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Scenario C — the two traps and a defensible call</summary>

**Traps:** (1) *said vs. did* — "I'd totally use this" with no real commitment (no deposit, no booking) is opinion, not behavior; and (2) *moved goalpost* — the threshold was softened from "book a real plot" to "say they'd use it" *after* the sessions. **Defensible call:** *gather more / treat as inconclusive* — the prototype validated comprehension, not demand. Re-run with a real commitment (a refundable deposit or a waitlist with a credit-card hold). Decision sentence: *"5/5 'said yes' but against a weakened, post-hoc threshold and with zero real commitment, so the demand question is still open — re-test with a deposit."*

</details>

## Why this matters

The persevere/pivot call is the highest-leverage decision a founder makes, and it's made *over and over*. Most startups don't die from a single bad bet; they die from a slow accumulation of misread experiments — persevering on vanity, or pivoting away from real signal. Build the muscle now, on other people's data, so that in Weeks 8–10 — when it's your cap table and your runway — you can read your own numbers with the same cold clarity you brought to Tofu, Relay, and Grove.
