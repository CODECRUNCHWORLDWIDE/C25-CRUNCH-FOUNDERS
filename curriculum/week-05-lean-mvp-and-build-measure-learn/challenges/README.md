# Week 5 — Challenges

The exercises and mini-project drill the loop on *your own* idea. **Challenges stretch you** by forcing the harder skill: making a clean persevere-or-pivot call on evidence that is messy, ambiguous, or emotionally loaded — the way it actually arrives in a real company. Each challenge takes 60–120 minutes and produces something you can commit to your portfolio and defend out loud.

## Index

1. **[Challenge 1 — The Pivot-or-Persevere Board](challenge-01-pivot-or-persevere-board.md)** — read three realistic founder experiment results (each a trap of a different kind), make and justify a named decision for each, then apply the same rigor to your *own* MVP result. (~90 min)

## How challenges are assessed

Challenges are graded on **decision quality**, not on whether you "got the right answer" — most real scenarios have no single right answer, only well-reasoned and poorly-reasoned calls. A strong submission:

- **Reads the evidence honestly** — names the sample size, separates signal from noise, and catches the vanity metric or the moved goalpost hiding in the scenario.
- **Makes a clear call** — persevere or pivot, no fence-sitting; and if pivot, *names which kind* (zoom-in, customer-segment, channel, value-capture, etc.).
- **Ties the call to a number** — every decision sentence references the metric, the threshold, and the gap between them.
- **Avoids the two failure modes** — sunk-cost perseverance (ignoring a clear miss because you're attached) and panic-pivoting (abandoning a clear pass on one bad data point).
- **States its confidence** — small samples mean tentative conclusions; the strongest submissions say "inconclusive, gather more" when that's the honest answer rather than forcing a verdict.

| Criterion | What "great" looks like |
|---|---|
| Evidence reading | Catches the trap in each scenario (vanity metric, tiny sample, moved threshold, said-vs-did) |
| Decision clarity | A named, unambiguous call per scenario |
| Number discipline | Every call cites metric vs. threshold |
| Avoiding the failure modes | No sunk-cost persevering, no panic pivoting |
| Application to own idea | The same rigor turned on the founder's own (harder, because it's personal) result |

Challenges are **optional** — you can pass the week without them. But the persevere/pivot muscle is the single most valuable thing this week teaches, and you only build it by deciding on cases that aren't yours and aren't tidy. Founders who do this challenge make calmer, faster calls when it's their own money on the line in Weeks 6–10.
