# Lecture 1 — When (Not) to Raise, and Funding Stages

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can decide whether raising is right for you now and name the early funding stages.

## 1. Raising is a tool, not a milestone

Outside money is expensive: you trade ownership and some control for cash and speed. It is the right tool when capital is the bottleneck *and* you have evidence the money will produce outsized growth. It is the wrong tool when you are raising to avoid finding customers, to feel validated, or because "that's what startups do."

> **The best time to raise is when you do not desperately need to** — when traction gives you leverage. Raising from weakness is expensive.

## 2. When not to raise

- You can reach profitability or sustainability without it (**bootstrapping**).
- You have not validated the problem or the model (you would be buying speed in the wrong direction).
- You value control highly (your Week 1 lean) and the round would cost you the company's direction.
- The unit economics are broken — raising just scales the loss.

## 3. The early funding stages (overview)

- **Bootstrapping** — fund it from savings or revenue. Maximum control, slower.
- **Friends & family** — small, early, relationship-laden money. Handle with care; paper it properly.
- **Angels** — individual investors writing smaller checks, often for the founder and the early bet.
- **Pre-seed** — the first institutional-ish money to get to a real product/early traction.
- **Seed and beyond** — larger rounds once there is traction. (Out of scope for a zero-to-validation course, but know the ladder exists.)

## 4. Cost of capital, in founder terms

Every round dilutes you and usually adds expectations: investors want growth and, eventually, a return (via acquisition or further rounds). That can be exactly what you want — or a treadmill you did not need. Decide deliberately, using the lean you already chose.

## 5. The honest question

For your venture, this week, answer plainly: *do I need to raise, and if so why, and what would I give up?* The answer might well be "not yet" — that is a perfectly good founder decision, not a failure.
