# Lecture 1 — When (Not) to Raise, and the Funding Ladder

> **Duration:** ~2 hours of reading + reflection.
> **Outcome:** You can decide whether raising is the right move for *your* venture right now, name the rungs of the funding ladder and the evidence each expects, explain what "venture-scale" means and whether you are it, and describe — without flinching — the return math that drives every investor across the table.

If you only remember one thing from this lecture, remember this:

> **Raising money is a tool, not a milestone.** The decision to raise is a decision to swap two things you have plenty of — *ownership* and *control* — for one thing you might be short of — *capital* — in service of growth you have evidence you can buy. If you do not have that evidence, the trade is bad at any valuation.

Most first-time founders get this exactly backwards. They treat "we raised" as the finish line, the proof, the thing that makes it real. It is none of those. It is the moment you acquired a boss, a clock, and an obligation to return roughly ten times the money or be considered a disappointment. That can be a fantastic trade. It can also be the worst decision you make. This lecture is about telling the two apart *before* you take the meeting.

---

## 1. The two questions

Every fundraising decision reduces to two questions. Answer them honestly and you have your answer.

1. **Is capital actually the bottleneck?** Not "would money be nice" — money is always nice. Is the *single thing* standing between you and meaningful growth a lack of cash? Or is it a lack of customers, a lack of product, a lack of clarity about who you serve? Money cannot buy those, and if you raise to avoid finding them, you will simply find them later with a smaller slice of your own company.

2. **Do you have evidence the money produces outsized growth?** A dollar of investor money is the most expensive dollar you will ever spend, because you are paying for it in equity that — if things go well — becomes worth far more than the dollar. You only want to spend it where it compounds: where $1 in reliably produces $3, $5, $10 out. That requires evidence. The Week 6 unit-economics sheet and the Week 7 traction metric are exactly that evidence.

If capital is the bottleneck *and* you have evidence it compounds, raising is probably right. If either is missing, the honest answer is usually "not yet."

> **The best time to raise is when you don't desperately need to.** Traction gives you leverage; leverage gives you good terms; good terms cost you less ownership. Raising from weakness — runway gone, no traction, panic in your voice — is the most expensive money in the world, because the investor knows it and prices accordingly. Raise from strength or wait until you have it.

## 2. When *not* to raise — the four conditions

There are four situations where raising is the wrong move. If your venture is in any of them, the discipline is to *not* raise, even if you could.

### 2.1 You can reach sustainability without it

Some of the best businesses in the world never raised a dollar of venture money. Mailchimp sold for $12 billion having taken no venture funding. 37signals (Basecamp), Atlassian for its first eight years, plenty of profitable SaaS and services businesses — all bootstrapped, all controlled by their founders, all free of the venture clock.

If your business can reach **default alive** — Paul Graham's term for "on its current trajectory, with no new money, you will become profitable before you run out of cash" — then raising is a *choice*, not a necessity. You might still choose to raise to grow faster. But you do it from a position of total strength, and you should be honest that you are trading control for speed, not survival.

The opposite, **default dead**, means on your current trajectory you run out of money before you reach profitability. A default-dead company is one that *must* either change trajectory or raise. Knowing which you are is the most clarifying number in your whole financial model.

### 2.2 You haven't validated the problem or the model

This is the most common and most expensive mistake. You raise money to "build the thing," before you have evidence anyone wants the thing. Now you have cash and a mandate to spend it, pointed in a direction you never validated. You will build faster — in the wrong direction. You'll burn the round confirming what three weeks of customer discovery would have told you for free.

Everything in Weeks 1–7 of this course exists to make sure you are not in this situation. Ten discovery interviews, an MVP that tested your riskiest assumption, unit economics that hold up, one traction metric you trust — *that* is what makes a raise a multiplier instead of an accelerant pointed at a wall.

### 2.3 You're a control-leaning founder and the round would cost you direction

Back in Week 1 you stated a control-vs-wealth lean. This is where it gets real. Every round you raise:

- dilutes your ownership (a wealth cost),
- adds investors who expect growth and, eventually, a return (a control cost),
- and at the priced-round stage, often adds a board seat — a literal vote on what the company does.

If you deeply want to *stay in charge* — to decide the direction, to refuse an acquisition you don't like, to run the company your way for a long time — then minimizing rounds is the rational move. The wealth-leaning founder accepts dilution to grow a bigger pie; the control-leaning founder keeps a bigger slice of a pie they steer. Neither is wrong. But raising hard while wanting control hard is a contradiction, and it ends in resentment at a board meeting three years from now.

### 2.4 The unit economics are broken

If you lose money on every customer, raising money lets you lose money on *more* customers, faster. That is not growth; it is subsidized decline with a growth chart attached. The 2021 era was full of companies doing exactly this — paying $200 to acquire customers worth $80, calling the customer count "traction," and raising on it. Most of them are gone.

Your Week 6 unit-economics sheet is the test. If contribution margin is positive, LTV:CAC is healthy (a rough rule of thumb people quote is 3:1 or better), and payback is reasonable for your stage, then capital scales a working machine. If those numbers are upside down, fix the machine *before* you pour fuel into it. Raising does not fix unit economics; it hides them until the next round, when they're someone else's problem and your problem all at once.

## 3. The cost of capital, in founder terms

Outside capital is not free, and it is not a loan you repay in cash (at the venture stage). You repay it in three currencies:

- **Ownership.** Every share issued to an investor is a share that is not yours. Across a typical path from pre-seed to a later stage, founders routinely end up owning a minority of their own company. That can still be a life-changing minority of something enormous — or a small slice of something modest.
- **Control.** Investors get information rights, sometimes board seats, sometimes protective provisions (a list of decisions they can veto). The more you raise, the more of these accumulate.
- **Expectations.** This is the subtle one. Venture money comes with an implicit contract: *we expect this to become very large.* A business that would be a wonderful $5M/year lifestyle company is, to a venture investor, a failure — because their math (Section 7) requires outliers. Once you take the money, "good" is redefined as "huge," and you've signed up for a different game than the one you might have wanted.

None of this is a warning *against* raising. It is a warning against raising *thoughtlessly*. Decide deliberately, using the lean you chose in Week 1 and the numbers you built in Week 6.

## 4. The funding ladder

When founders *do* raise, they generally climb a ladder. Each rung expects a different kind of evidence and writes a different size of check. You do not have to climb every rung, and plenty of great companies skip several. But you should know the shape.

### 4.1 Bootstrapping

**What it is:** funding the company from your savings, your day job, or — best of all — revenue from customers.

**What it expects:** nothing but your own resolve and, ideally, paying customers.

**What it costs:** time and personal risk. You grow as fast as the business can fund itself.

**What it buys you:** total control and 100% ownership. No investors, no clock, no board.

Bootstrapping is the *default*, not the fallback. For a large class of businesses — services, niche software, profitable-from-day-one products — it is the *correct* path, and venture money would only distort it. We spend real time on it because the venture narrative pretends it doesn't exist.

### 4.2 Friends & family

**What it is:** the earliest outside checks, from people who are investing in *you*, not your traction.

**What it expects:** that they trust you. There is rarely diligence; there is rarely a market-rate valuation.

**What it costs:** relationships, if you're careless. The cardinal rule: **paper it like a real investment** — a real SAFE or note, a real cap, clear terms — and only take money people can afford to lose. A Thanksgiving dinner ruined by a failed startup is a bad trade no matter the check size.

**Typical size:** a few thousand to a few tens of thousands of dollars.

### 4.3 Angels

**What it is:** individuals investing their *own* money — often successful operators or former founders — writing checks because they believe in the founder and the early bet.

**What they expect:** a credible founder, a real problem, and usually *some* early signal. Angels invest earlier and more on conviction than funds do. Many invest partly to stay close to the game and to help.

**What it costs:** dilution, but usually on founder-friendly terms (a SAFE with a cap). Good angels bring introductions, advice, and credibility for the next round.

**Typical check:** $5k–$100k individually; an "angel round" might aggregate several into $100k–$500k+ total. Angel *syndicates* and platforms (AngelList) can stack many small checks into a larger one.

### 4.4 Pre-seed

**What it is:** the first institutional-ish money — small funds and dedicated pre-seed firms — to get you from "promising" to "a real product with early traction."

**What it expects (2026):** more than it did in 2021. Typically a working product or a sharp prototype, evidence of a real problem (your discovery interviews are gold here), an early traction signal, and a founder who can articulate the unit economics. AI-adjacent companies are an outlier market where pre-seed valuations have run hot; ignore those headlines as a benchmark for a normal company.

**What it costs:** a meaningful slice — pre-seed founders commonly part with roughly 10–20% across the round, frequently via a stack of post-money SAFEs that convert later.

**Typical size:** roughly $250k–$1M+ in 2026, on a SAFE with a valuation cap.

### 4.5 Seed (and the gesture beyond)

**What it is:** a larger round once you have genuine traction; often the first *priced* round (actual equity at an agreed valuation, not a SAFE).

**What it expects:** traction that's beginning to look like a pattern — real users, real revenue or engagement, evidence the unit economics work at small scale and a credible story for scaling them.

This course is **zero-to-validation**, so seed and beyond are out of scope as something you'll *do*. But know the ladder continues: seed → Series A → B → C and onward, each larger, each more demanding, each diluting you further. The cap-table model you build this week is the same tool used at every rung; only the numbers grow.

### 4.6 The ladder at a glance

Pin this table. The 2026 ranges are rough and vary wildly by sector, geography, and (especially) AI-adjacency — treat them as orientation, not gospel.

| Rung | Typical check (2026) | What it expects | What it costs you | Instrument |
|------|---------------------|-----------------|-------------------|------------|
| Bootstrapping | $0 outside | Your resolve; ideally customers | Time, personal risk | None — you own 100% |
| Friends & family | $5k–$50k | They trust you | Relationships if careless; small dilution | SAFE / note (paper it!) |
| Angels | $5k–$100k each | Credible founder, real problem, some signal | Founder-friendly dilution | SAFE with a cap |
| Pre-seed | $250k–$1M+ | Working product/prototype, discovery evidence, early traction | ~10–20% across the round | Post-money SAFEs |
| Seed | $1M–$5M+ | Traction looking like a pattern; early economics | A meaningful equity slice + maybe a board seat | Often the first priced round |

Read the table top to bottom and notice the trade: as you climb, the checks grow, the evidence demanded grows, *and* what you give up grows. You don't have to climb every rung — many great companies stop at bootstrapping or one angel round — but each step up is a deliberate trade of ownership and control for capital and speed.

## 5. A worked decision: three founders, three right answers

Three founders finish Week 7. All three did the work. Watch how the *same* framework gives three different correct answers.

**Founder 1 — Maya, B2B scheduling tool for dental clinics.** Ten interviews, a working MVP, four clinics paying $200/month, CAC of about $400 recovered in two months, a control lean. Capital *is* a bottleneck: she's turning away clinics because she can't build fast enough alone. The economics work. **Decision: raise a small pre-seed** — enough to hire one engineer and convert demand she already has. The evidence makes it a multiplier, and she keeps the round small to protect the control she values.

**Founder 2 — Devon, consumer journaling app.** Ten interviews, a beautiful MVP, 2,000 free signups, *zero* paying users, no validated willingness to pay. Capital is *not* the bottleneck — willingness to pay is. **Decision: do not raise.** Raising now buys runway to keep building something no one has agreed to pay for. The right move is to test pricing first. Money would only postpone the lesson.

**Founder 3 — Priya, productized SEO service for law firms.** Ten interviews, a manual MVP, six firms paying $2,000/month, profitable from month one, strongly control-leaning. The business is *default alive* and throws off cash. **Decision: bootstrap.** She *could* raise — but venture money would push her toward a venture-scale story she doesn't want, when she'd rather own 100% of a profitable, growing services firm. Raising would be trading something she values for something she doesn't need.

Same framework. Three answers. None of them is "raise because that's what startups do."

## 6. What "venture-scale" actually means — and whether you are it

Venture investors are not in the business of funding good companies. They are in the business of funding companies that can become *enormous* — companies with a credible path to returning their *entire fund* on a single bet. That is a narrow, specific kind of company:

- A **large and growing market** (think billions, not millions, in addressable spend).
- A path to **outsized margins** — usually software-like economics, where one more customer costs almost nothing to serve.
- A **defensible moat** — network effects, hard technology, switching costs — so that winning means staying won.
- A plausible route to **10x–100x** the money invested.

Plenty of genuinely excellent businesses are *not* venture-scale: most services firms, most niche tools, most local businesses, most "great $10M/year company" outcomes. There is nothing wrong with them. The error is taking venture money for a non-venture business — because then you've promised an outlier outcome you have no path to, and you've handed control to people who will, correctly by their own math, push you toward swing-for-the-fences decisions that can break a perfectly good company.

Before you raise, answer honestly: *is this a venture-scale business, and do I want it to be?* "No" to either is a complete, respectable answer — it just routes you to bootstrapping or revenue-based financing instead.

## 7. The power law — why investors need outliers

To understand the person across the table, understand their math. Venture returns follow a **power law**, not a normal distribution. In a typical early-stage portfolio:

- Most investments return **zero or close to it.** Startups fail; this is expected.
- A handful return the money — fine, not exciting.
- **One or two** returns are enormous — 50x, 100x, occasionally more — and *those* carry the entire fund.

A fund that needs to roughly triple its money, knowing most bets go to zero, *cannot* be satisfied by a company that returns 3x. A 3x company is a rounding error against the one that has to return 100x. So the investor isn't being greedy when they push for the giant outcome; they are doing the only thing their structure allows. The single most important thing to understand about why investors behave the way they do is this:

> **A venture investor would rather you swing for a 100x outcome and miss than play it safe for a guaranteed 3x.** Their portfolio math means the guaranteed 3x is, to them, almost worthless. *Your* incentives — a life-changing 3x might be plenty for you — can diverge sharply from theirs. That divergence is the source of most founder-investor conflict, and it's why "is this person's math aligned with mine?" matters as much as "do I like them?"

This is not a reason to avoid investors. It is a reason to choose them deliberately, to know what you're signing up for, and — if your goals are a solid base hit rather than a moonshot — to consider whether venture is the right fuel at all.

## 8. The 2026 market, briefly

So your numbers are current, not nostalgic:

- The **2022–2023 correction** ended the era of giant rounds at any price. Investors do more diligence, value traction over story, and pay more disciplined prices than the 2021 peak. This is good for sober founders.
- **Post-money SAFEs** are the default pre-seed instrument (Lecture 2 covers them). The shift from pre-money to post-money SAFEs — and the stacking behavior it encourages — is the most important mechanical change of the last several years.
- **AI-native companies** are an outlier market with their own (often inflated) valuation norms. Do *not* benchmark a normal company against AI pre-seed headlines; you'll set expectations you can't meet.
- **"Default alive"** is back in fashion as advice. Investors increasingly want to see that you *could* survive without the next round — which, pleasingly, is also exactly the position that gets you the best terms.

The throughline: the market now rewards the same discipline this course teaches. Evidence over vibes. Traction over story. Knowing your numbers over knowing the lingo.

## 9. Alternatives to venture you should know exist

The startup-media monoculture pretends there are exactly two states: "raised" and "failed." There are many more ways to fund a company, and a sober founder knows the menu before defaulting to the loudest option.

- **Revenue (the best money there is).** A dollar from a customer costs you zero equity, sets no clock, and *proves demand* in the same motion. Every dollar of revenue you can manufacture before a raise is a dollar you don't have to buy with ownership — and it raises your valuation when you do raise. "Sell first" is not anti-venture; it's the strongest possible pre-raise position.
- **Grants and non-dilutive capital.** Government innovation grants, research grants, and some foundation programs hand you money for *nothing* in equity terms. They're slow and paperwork-heavy, but for the right company (deep tech, climate, biotech, civic) they can fund real progress with zero dilution. Worth a day of research before you assume venture is the only road.
- **Revenue-based financing (RBF).** A newer class of lenders advances cash against your recurring revenue and you repay a fixed percentage of revenue until a cap is hit. No equity, no board seat — but it's debt, and it only works once you *have* revenue to borrow against. Useful for a working business that wants growth capital without dilution.
- **Accelerators.** Programs like Y Combinator and Techstars give a small check plus a network and a deadline, in exchange for a small slice (YC's standard is a modest percentage on a SAFE). The check matters less than the demo-day audience and the alumni network. A genuine option — just know you're trading equity for access and structure, and the program is a full-time commitment.
- **Crowdfunding.** Reward crowdfunding (pre-orders) is *non-dilutive* and validates demand at once — arguably the purest pre-raise proof. Equity crowdfunding (selling small stakes to many small investors under modern regulations) is dilutive but widens who can back you. Different tools; know which is which.
- **Customer-funded development.** A flagship customer pays you to build the thing they need. You keep the IP, you keep the equity, and you've validated with the strongest signal there is — a check. Common and underrated in B2B.

The point isn't that any of these is universally better. It's that "raise venture" is *one option on a menu*, and a founder who never read the rest of the menu is making a default, not a decision.

## 10. The mechanics of actually running a raise

You won't run a real raise in this course — you'll *model* one. But knowing the shape keeps you from romanticizing it. A raise is a sales process, and like any sales process it has a funnel.

1. **Get ready before you start.** A raise has momentum or it has nothing. Investors talk to each other; a round that drags signals weakness. So you prepare *everything* first — the deck, the model, the data room, the references — and only then start taking meetings, so you can run the whole process in a tight window (a few weeks, not a few months).
2. **Build the list and the intros.** Cold outreach works occasionally; warm introductions work far better. You map which investors fit your stage and sector, then find the shortest warm path to each. Your discovery interviewees, advisors, and other founders are your intro network — this is partly *why* relationships built in Weeks 1–7 pay off here.
3. **Run meetings in parallel, not in series.** The single most common first-timer error is taking investors one at a time: pitch one, wait, get a no, pitch the next. That can burn your whole runway. You run them *in parallel*, so offers arrive close together and you have leverage and a real choice — competition is what gets you good terms.
4. **Get to a lead, then fill the round.** At pre-seed-on-SAFEs there may be no single "lead," but a credible first commitment de-risks you for everyone else. Social proof is real: "X already committed" turns maybes into yeses. Your job is to manufacture that first yes and let it cascade.
5. **Close fast and paper it properly.** Once you have commitments, you sign the documents — with a lawyer — and the money lands. SAFEs make this fast; priced rounds are slower. Then you stop raising and go back to building. A raise that never closes because you keep "taking one more meeting" is a raise that failed.

Two cultural notes worth internalizing now. First, **"no" is the default and it's not personal** — investors pass on the vast majority of what they see, including things that go on to be huge. Resilience through a string of nos is part of the job. Second, **fundraising is a distraction from building**, which is exactly why you compress it: every week you spend raising is a week you're not talking to customers or shipping. Get in, get the money, get out.

## 11. How much to raise — sizing the round to a milestone

"How much should I raise?" is the question every first-timer gets wrong in the same direction: they raise the *most* they can. That's backwards. You raise the *least* that gets you to the next milestone that meaningfully de-risks the company — plus a buffer. Here's the reasoning.

Every round is a promise to grow into a new, higher valuation by the next round. The more you raise, the higher the valuation you take (to limit dilution), and the higher the bar you've set for yourself next time. Raise too much at too high a price and you create a trap: you must grow into a number you may not be ready for, and if you can't, the next round is *flat or down* — which is harder to raise and damaging to morale and your existing cap table.

So size the round bottom-up:

1. **Name the next milestone.** What single achievement makes you obviously more valuable and fundable? "$30k MRR," "10 paying logos," "the integration that unlocks the enterprise segment." Not "build more"; a *specific, demonstrable* milestone.
2. **Cost the path to it.** How many months, at what burn, to hit that milestone? Be honest about hiring, tools, and the time things actually take. Multiply months by burn.
3. **Add a buffer.** Things slip. A common rule of thumb is to fund **18–24 months** of runway so you reach the milestone *and* have time to raise the next round from strength, not desperation. (Remember Lecture 1's whole thesis: you want to raise the *next* round before you need it.)
4. **That number — milestone cost plus buffer — is your raise.** Not the biggest check on offer.

A worked feel for it: a two-person team burning ~$25k/month that needs ~14 months to hit "$30k MRR and a repeatable sales motion" needs roughly `14 × $25k = $350k` to reach the milestone, plus buffer to ~18–20 months, landing around **$450k–$500k**. That's a clean pre-seed number tied to a concrete outcome — not "we're raising a million because that sounds like a real round."

> **The discipline:** a raise sized to a milestone is *defensible* — you can tell any investor exactly what the money buys and when you'll be back, bigger. A raise sized to "the most we could get" is *vanity*, and it quietly sets the trap you'll fall into at the next round.

## 12. What investors actually look for at pre-seed

If you do decide to raise, it helps to know what's running through the head of the person across the table. At pre-seed — the earliest institutional money — there's barely a company to analyze, so investors weight a specific, perhaps surprising, set of signals.

- **The team, first and most.** At pre-seed there's little traction to judge, so investors bet on *people*. Are you the right founders for this problem (your Week 1 founder-market fit)? Have you shown you can move fast, learn, and not quit? A strong team with a mediocre idea raises more easily than a weak team with a great one, because ideas change and teams mostly don't.
- **Evidence you talk to customers.** This is where your Weeks 3 discovery interviews become a fundraising asset. A founder who can say "I interviewed forty people in this segment and here's the exact pattern I found" stands out sharply from one pitching a hunch. Investors fund *learning machines*, and proof you get out of the building is proof you're one.
- **A real, painful problem.** Is this a painkiller or a vitamin (your Week 2 framing)? Investors want problems people will pay to make go away — not nice-to-haves. The sharper and more specific the pain, the better.
- **Some early signal.** Not necessarily revenue, but *something*: a waitlist that converts, an MVP with real usage, a pilot customer, a retention curve that doesn't immediately crater. The signal that makes them think "this could be the start of a curve."
- **A market that could be big.** Back to venture-scale (Section 6). The investor is underwriting an outlier; they need to believe the ceiling is high, even if today it's tiny. "Small now, obviously huge later" is the story; "small now, small later" doesn't get funded by venture (and shouldn't take venture money).
- **Founder clarity on the numbers.** A founder who knows their CAC, their payback, and their dilution (this week!) signals operational seriousness. You don't need perfect metrics at pre-seed; you need to *understand your own business* well enough that the investor trusts you to steward their money.

Notice how much of this you already built in Weeks 1–7. The fundraising-readiness checklist and the validation work of this course are *the same list*. That's not a coincidence — the discipline that makes a venture worth building is the discipline that makes it fundable. Which is exactly why "do the work first, raise second" is the throughline of both this lecture and the whole program.

## 13. Common first-timer mistakes

- **Raising to feel validated.** A check is not proof your idea is good; it's proof one investor's portfolio math liked your bet. Validation comes from customers.
- **Raising the maximum you can.** More money means more dilution and a higher bar for the *next* round (you have to grow into the valuation). Raise what you need to hit the next meaningful milestone, plus a buffer — not the largest number someone will give you.
- **Optimizing for valuation over terms.** A high cap with bad terms can be worse than a modest cap with clean ones. The headline number is the part founders brag about and the part that matters least.
- **Ignoring the option pool.** It comes out of *your* slice (Lecture 2). First-timers model the round and forget the pool, then are shocked at their post-round number.
- **Treating SAFEs as "free" because they're easy to sign.** They're easy *because* they defer the pain. Every SAFE you sign is dilution you've already promised; you just don't feel it until conversion.
- **Not knowing your own "no."** If you can't say what would make you *decline* a check, you don't have a strategy — you have hope, and investors can smell it.

## 14. Two contrasting cap-table journeys

To make the stakes concrete before Lecture 2 dives into the mechanics, here are two founders' ownership journeys — same starting point, opposite philosophies, both rational.

**The capital-efficient path (control lean).** A solo founder bootstraps to early revenue, raises a single small pre-seed of $400k on a SAFE to hire two people, reaches profitability, and *never raises again*. Across the whole journey she's diluted maybe 12–15%, retaining ~85% of a company she fully controls. If it sells for $20M, she takes home roughly $17M — and she ran it her way the entire time. The company is "small" by venture standards and enormous by hers.

**The venture path (wealth lean).** Two co-founders start 50/50, raise a $600k pre-seed (SAFEs), then a $3M seed, then a larger round. By the time the company is large, the founders together own perhaps 40% and each sits around 20%, with investors and the option pool holding the rest. They've given up most of the company and a board seat — but the company they're a minority of is worth, say, $300M. Each founder's ~20% is ~$60M, and they got there in a fraction of the time the bootstrapper took, by buying growth with equity.

Neither founder is smarter. They made *different bets aligned with different values*. The control-leaner optimized for autonomy and a clean cap table; the wealth-leaners optimized for the size of the eventual pie and accepted the dilution and the board to get there. The cap table is simply the ledger that records which bet you made — and Lecture 2 teaches you to compute, in advance, exactly what each bet costs.

The mistake is never "raised" or "didn't raise." The mistake is making the choice *by default* — drifting into venture because it's the loud option, or refusing it out of reflex — instead of reasoning from your lean and your numbers. This week's whole job is to make the choice deliberate.

## Before you move on — three questions for your journal

Spend ten minutes answering these honestly in your founder journal. They feed directly into the homework's raise-or-not memo.

1. **Is capital actually my bottleneck right now?** If money appeared tomorrow, would it solve my biggest problem — or would my biggest problem (customers, willingness to pay, a co-founder gap) still be exactly where it is?
2. **What would I have to give up, and am I genuinely okay with it?** Picture the dilution and, eventually, an investor with an opinion on direction. Does that fit the control-vs-wealth lean I wrote down in Week 1, or does it quietly contradict it?
3. **What is my "no"?** What terms, what valuation, what kind of investor would make me decline a check even when I need the money? If I can't answer this, I don't yet have a fundraising strategy — I have hope.

There are no right answers here, only honest ones. A founder who can answer these is ready to model a round in Lecture 2 and decide whether to actually run one.
