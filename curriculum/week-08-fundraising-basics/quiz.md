# Week 8 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 9. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek. **This is education, not investment or legal advice.**

---

**Q1.** The best time to raise is generally:

- A) As early as possible, before you have customers, to lock in capital.
- B) When you do not desperately need to — when traction gives you leverage.
- C) Only after you are fully profitable.
- D) Never; raising is always a mistake.

---

**Q2.** Which of the following is **NOT** one of the four conditions under which you generally should *not* raise?

- A) You can reach sustainability without it (default alive).
- B) You haven't validated the problem or the model.
- C) A respected investor offered you a check on standard terms.
- D) Your unit economics are broken.

---

**Q3.** You raise **$750,000 at a $4,250,000 pre-money** valuation. What is the investor's approximate ownership?

- A) ~17.6% (investment ÷ pre-money)
- B) ~15.0% (investment ÷ post-money)
- C) ~5.7% (pre-money ÷ post-money inverted)
- D) ~10.0%

---

**Q4.** "Dilution" most precisely refers to:

- A) Your share *count* decreasing.
- B) Your ownership *percentage* falling as new shares are issued, even though your share count is unchanged.
- C) The company losing market value.
- D) Selling some of your own shares to an investor.

---

**Q5.** A **post-money SAFE** differs from the older pre-money SAFE chiefly because:

- A) It pays interest and has a maturity date.
- B) The investor's ownership percentage is fixed and knowable the day it's signed; founders absorb the dilution of every *additional* SAFE.
- C) It can never have a valuation cap.
- D) It converts into debt instead of equity.

---

**Q6.** An investor requires a **10% option pool created pre-money** as part of a round. The effect is that:

- A) The investor absorbs the pool's dilution.
- B) Nobody dilutes; the pool is free.
- C) The founders dilute *more*, because the pool comes out of their slice before the investor's money lands.
- D) The post-money valuation falls by 10%.

---

**Q7.** A founder signs three post-money SAFEs: $300k at a $6M cap, $200k at a $6M cap, and $100k at a $5M cap. Approximately how much of the company have they promised to SAFE holders *before* the priced round?

- A) ~3.3%
- B) ~6.0%
- C) ~10.3%
- D) ~20.0%

---

**Q8.** Why does a venture investor push a founder toward a swing-for-the-fences outcome rather than a safe 3x?

- A) Because 3x is illegal for a fund to accept.
- B) Because venture returns follow a power law — most bets go to zero, and one or two outliers must carry the whole fund, so a 3x is nearly worthless to them.
- C) Because investors don't understand unit economics.
- D) Because a 3x return triggers higher taxes for the fund.

---

**Q9.** On a pre-seed term sheet, which cluster of terms actually decides a founder's outcome (vs. boilerplate)?

- A) Governing law, confidentiality, and reps & warranties.
- B) Valuation/cap, option pool size and timing, board composition, and liquidation preference.
- C) The no-shop clause and the signature block.
- D) The document's font and formatting.

---

**Q10.** A valuation **cap** on a SAFE does what for the early investor?

- A) Guarantees them a fixed dollar return regardless of outcome.
- B) Sets the *maximum* valuation at which their money converts, rewarding them with a better effective price if you later raise at a high valuation.
- C) Caps how much money the company can ever raise.
- D) Prevents the founders from ever diluting.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — Raise from leverage, not desperation. The best time is when you *don't* desperately need to. (L1 §1)
2. **C** — Being offered a check is not a reason *not* to raise; the four "don't raise" conditions are sustainability without it, an unvalidated model, a control lean, and broken unit economics. (L1 §2)
3. **B** — Post-money = $4,250,000 + $750,000 = $5,000,000; investor % = $750,000 ÷ $5,000,000 = 15.0%. Choice A wrongly divides by pre-money. (L2 §3)
4. **B** — Dilution is a falling *percentage* from a growing denominator, not a falling share count. (L2 §2)
5. **B** — The post-money SAFE fixes the investor's % at signing; the cost is that founders absorb each additional SAFE's dilution. SAFEs aren't loans (no interest/maturity — that's a convertible note), so A is wrong. (L2 §5.4)
6. **C** — A pre-money pool comes out of the existing holders' (founders') slice — the "pool shuffle." (L2 §4)
7. **C** — 300/6000 + 200/6000 + 100/5000 = 5.0% + 3.3% + 2.0% = ~10.3%, all before the priced round. (L2 §5–6, Exercise 2)
8. **B** — The power law: most bets go to zero, outliers carry the fund, so a guaranteed 3x is near-worthless to the fund — the root of most founder-investor incentive conflict. (L1 §7)
9. **B** — Valuation/cap, pool, board, and liquidation preference move your outcome; governing law and confidentiality are boilerplate. (L2 §7, Exercise 3)
10. **B** — The cap sets the maximum valuation at which the SAFE converts, giving the early investor a better effective price if you later raise high. (L2 §5.1)

</details>
