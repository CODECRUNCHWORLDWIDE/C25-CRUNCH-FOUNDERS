# Exercise 1 — Problem Inventory

**Goal:** Generate a problem-first inventory of 15+ problems drawn from the four wells, first-cut each as painkiller or vitamin, and narrow to a shortlist of 5–10 painkillers where you have standing. This shortlist is the raw material for Exercise 2 (triage scoring) and the [mini-project](../mini-project/README.md).

**Estimated time:** 60 minutes.

**You will produce:** `exercises/exercise-01-problem-inventory.md` in your Week 2 repo.

---

## Why this exercise

Volume breaks attachment. If you walk into triage with one idea, you'll defend it. If you walk in with fifteen problems, you can hold them at arm's length and compare. The first three you write down are the obvious ones everyone in your situation would name; problems 10–15 are where the interesting, less-contested ones live. So the only goal of this exercise is **quantity, generated honestly, stated as problems.**

---

## Step 1 — Warm up with a friction sweep (10 min)

Before the structured pass, do a fast, messy brain-dump. Set a 5-minute timer and list every problem you have personally hit in the last seven days — phone, work, errands, money, health, relationships, tools that annoyed you. No solutions. No judging. Just pain.

Then take a second 5 minutes and add problems you *watched someone else* struggle with this week.

You should have 8–12 raw lines. They're rough; that's fine. This is the warm-up.

---

## Step 2 — Work the four wells (25 min)

Now go deliberately through each of the four wells from Lecture 1, §3. Spend ~6 minutes on each and aim for at least 3–4 problems per well. Don't worry about overlap.

- **Well 1 — Your own friction.** Problems you hit repeatedly. Where do you personally hack a workaround?
- **Well 2 — Your industry's workarounds.** Walk through a normal day at your current or last job. Flag every step that was manual, clumsy, or duct-taped. What did everyone accept as "just how it works"?
- **Well 3 — Expert annoyances.** Pick a domain you (or people you know) are genuinely good at. What infuriates the experts that novices don't even notice?
- **Well 4 — Expensive workarounds.** Where have you seen people pay — in money or hours — to patch a gap? A tool subscription, a VA, a manual reconciliation, a recurring chore.

Write each as a one-sentence **problem**, no solution. If you catch yourself writing "an app that…", stop, and write the pain instead.

By the end you should have **15+ problems** total (warm-up + wells, de-duplicated).

---

## Step 3 — First cut: painkiller or vitamin (10 min)

Go down your list. For each problem, label it **P** (painkiller) or **V** (vitamin) with a one-word reason. Use the test from Lecture 1, §4: a painkiller is urgent, recurring, and costly; a vitamin is a nice-to-have.

Be honest. **Most of your 15 will be vitamins.** That is the expected and correct outcome — generating quantity means most candidates are weak, and seeing that is the lesson.

---

## Step 4 — Shortlist (15 min)

Pull the problems that are **both** painkillers **and** where you have standing (you've lived it, or you can reach the people who have it). Aim for **5–10**. If you have more than 10 strong ones, you're either inflating the painkiller labels or you're unusually well-positioned — re-check the labels first.

This shortlist is what you carry into Exercise 2.

## Hints

<details>
<summary>I keep writing solutions instead of problems</summary>

Add "the problem is that…" in front of each line and rewrite. "An app that reminds you of meds" becomes "the problem is that people forget to take their meds." Then delete the "the problem is that" — you're left with a clean problem statement.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Almost everything I wrote is a vitamin</summary>

Good — that's the point of generating quantity. Don't inflate vitamins into painkillers to feel better about the list. If your shortlist is only 2–3 painkillers, that's a useful finding: either generate more (return to the wells, especially "industry" and "expensive workarounds"), or accept that your strongest 2–3 are your real candidates and go deep on them in Exercise 2.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I don't have "an industry"</summary>

Use any place you've spent serious time: a job, a hobby you're good at, a community you're embedded in, even managing a household or a side hustle. Standing comes from lived experience, not a corporate title.

</details>
