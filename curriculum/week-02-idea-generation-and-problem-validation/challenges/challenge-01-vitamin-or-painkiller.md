# Challenge 1 — Vitamin or Painkiller? The Three-Idea Triage Call

**Time estimate:** ~90 minutes.

## The setup

You are the founder. You've done the Week 2 work on your *own* ideas — now you'll do it on a harder case: three ideas handed to you with messy, partial, real-feeling evidence, the way they actually arrive. Some of the evidence is a vanity signal dressed up to look strong. Some of the strongest signals are buried in an offhand sentence. Your job is to triage all three, **kill two**, defend the survivor, and write its kill criteria — under the constraint that the evidence is incomplete and you can't run interviews yet.

This is the call founders make constantly and usually make badly: which of several plausible ideas deserves the next month of your life?

---

## The three ideas

You may treat the founder in each scenario as *you* — assume their standing is your standing — or evaluate purely on the evidence given. State which lens you're using.

### Idea 1 — "ShiftSwap" — a marketplace for nurses to trade shifts

> A nurse you know is constantly trying to swap shifts and does it over a chaotic group chat. The hospital's official system is clunky and managers have to approve every swap by email. There are 4 million registered nurses in the country. A VC blog post you found calls healthcare staffing "a $20B market." A Reddit thread in r/nursing about shift-swapping has 600 upvotes and dozens of comments saying "ugh, yes, the group chat is a nightmare." No nurse you've talked to currently pays for anything to solve this; the group chat is free. Hospital IT departments are notoriously slow to adopt new tools and procurement takes 9–18 months.

### Idea 2 — "InvoiceSweep" — auto-reconcile freelancer invoices against bank deposits

> Three freelancers you know each spend ~3 hours a month matching client payments to invoices by hand, chasing late ones, and updating a spreadsheet. Two of them already pay $15–25/month for accounting tools (a popular one and a cheaper competitor) but say "it still doesn't connect the dots between the deposit and which invoice it was." App-store reviews of the popular tool are full of 2-star complaints: "reconciliation is a nightmare," "I still do it manually." You freelanced for two years and felt this exact pain. There are an estimated 70M freelancers in the country. Google Trends for "freelance invoice reconciliation" is low-volume but flat-to-rising over three years. You can name 15 freelancers you could message this week.

### Idea 3 — "MoodBoardAI" — an AI that generates aesthetic mood boards for interior designers

> An interior-designer friend says she'd "totally use" an AI that spins up mood boards. It sounds fun and the demos you imagine look beautiful. There are several well-funded AI design startups already, two of which raised large rounds this year. Your friend currently makes mood boards in Canva and Pinterest, which are free and which she says she "actually kind of enjoys." You have no background in design. On a quick poll in a designer Facebook group, 40 people clicked "I'd try that" on your post. Nobody in the thread mentioned paying for anything.

---

## Your task

Produce a written decision (commit as `challenges/challenge-01/decision.md`) with these sections:

### 1. Score all three on the five-axis filter

Use the Exercise 2 rubric (painkiller, frequency, standing, reachability, pay signal; 1–5 each; total /25). Put all three in one table. **Justify every score in one phrase** tied to the evidence — especially any score of 5 or 1.

### 2. Separate the real signals from the vanity signals

For each idea, explicitly label the strongest *real* signal and the most misleading *vanity* signal in its evidence. (Hint: market-size numbers, upvotes, and "I'd try that" clicks are doing a lot of suspicious work in these scenarios.)

### 3. Kill two, with reasons

Name the two you're killing and the specific axis (or combination) that kills each. "Park" vs "hard kill" is allowed — say which and why.

### 4. Defend the survivor

In one paragraph: why this one, referencing its scores and the real (not vanity) signals. If your top two were close, state your tie-break (reachability → standing → pay signal).

### 5. Write the survivor's kill criteria

Three to five **specific, falsifiable, numeric** kill criteria — the interview evidence from Week 3 that would make you drop even this one. (Lecture 2, §4.)

### 6. Survive the hard question

Answer this devil's-advocate prompt in a short paragraph:

> "Your survivor's whole market already pays for accounting/scheduling/design tools and *still hasn't* switched to a better reconciliation/swap/board feature. If the gap were really that painful, wouldn't an incumbent have shipped it already? Why is this a real opportunity and not just a feature the big players will bolt on the moment it matters?"

---

## What "great" looks like (and a note on the intended answer)

There is a defensible "intended" call here — **InvoiceSweep (Idea 2)** scores highest on the honest reading: a clear painkiller (real cost in hours), monthly frequency, genuine standing (you lived it), strong reachability (15 nameable contacts), and the strongest pay signal in the set (people *already pay* for adjacent tools and complain in reviews about the exact gap). ShiftSwap has a real pain but is gutted by reachability and pay signal (hospital procurement is brutal; nobody pays today; the $20B number and 600 upvotes are classic vanity signals). MoodBoardAI is a vitamin in a crowded, well-funded space where you have no standing, the free alternatives are *enjoyed*, and "I'd try that" clicks cost nothing.

**But the grade is on the reasoning, not the verdict.** A founder who picks ShiftSwap and honestly argues that their personal hospital-insider standing flips reachability, while naming that as the bet's biggest risk and writing a kill criterion around procurement timelines, can score full marks. What fails: treating the $20B market or the 600 upvotes or the 40 clicks as validation; hedging instead of killing two; and kill criteria like "if it doesn't work out."

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] `challenges/challenge-01/decision.md` committed to your Week 2 repo.
- [ ] All three ideas scored on all five axes, totals /25, **every notable score justified**.
- [ ] Real vs vanity signal called out for each idea.
- [ ] Two ideas killed (or parked) with the specific axis that kills each.
- [ ] One survivor defended with reference to scores and real signals.
- [ ] 3–5 falsifiable, numeric kill criteria for the survivor.
- [ ] A paragraph answering the incumbent / "why hasn't it been built" hard question.

## Stretch

- Re-run the call assuming you are a **hospital scheduling insider** with 30 warm nurse-manager contacts. Does ShiftSwap now beat InvoiceSweep? Show the changed scores. (This rehearses how *standing* re-weights everything — the Week 1 lesson, applied.)
- For your real surviving idea, write the one-paragraph "why now" — what changed recently (a regulation, a tech shift, a behavior change) that makes this the right moment. You'll need this exact paragraph for your Week 10 pitch.

## Why this matters

Demo day (Week 10) is, structurally, this challenge with your own idea and a skeptical investor asking the §6 hard question to your face. Rehearsing the "kill two, defend one, survive the obvious objection" motion now — on someone else's ideas, where it's easier to be honest — builds the reflex you'll need when it's your own.
