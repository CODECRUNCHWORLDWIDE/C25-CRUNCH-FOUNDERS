# Week 2 — Quiz

Twelve questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 10/12 before moving to Week 3. Answer key with explanations is at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** "Problem-first" idea generation means:

- A) Picking the most technically impressive solution and finding a use for it.
- B) Starting from a felt, recurring problem and deciding the solution last.
- C) Copying whatever competitor is growing fastest.
- D) Protecting your idea as a secret until launch.

---

**Q2.** A founder says: *"I want to build an AI tool that auto-generates social posts."* Applying the one-sentence problem test, which is the correct next move?

- A) Start building; the solution is clear.
- B) Restate it as a problem without naming the solution, then check if a real painkiller survives.
- C) Patent the idea before someone steals it.
- D) Survey 100 people on whether they'd use it.

---

**Q3.** Which is the *strongest* willingness-to-pay signal you can find during desk validation?

- A) The problem sounds interesting when you describe it at a party.
- B) People already pay for a clumsy existing tool and complain about it in reviews.
- C) You personally think the product should exist.
- D) A subreddit thread about the problem has 600 upvotes.

---

**Q4.** Why write **kill criteria** *before* talking to any customers?

- A) To impress investors with rigor.
- B) To pre-commit to falsifiable evidence that would make you drop the idea, before confirmation bias sets in.
- C) Because every idea must eventually be killed.
- D) To make the idea harder for competitors to copy.

---

**Q5.** A candidate scores high on painkiller-ness and pay signal, but you have no way to reach the customers. The honest read is:

- A) Build immediately — the pain is real, so users will find you.
- B) Reachability is a real constraint; without it you cannot run discovery, so the idea is weaker *for you* than the other scores suggest.
- C) Ignore reachability; it sorts itself out after launch.
- D) Raise money first so you can buy access.

---

**Q6.** During desk validation you find **no competitors at all** in the space. The correct default interpretation is:

- A) Goldmine — an untapped market with no competition.
- B) A red flag — more often "no money grows here" than "untapped"; you must actively disprove "there's no market" by finding the expensive manual workaround.
- C) Proof the idea is original and therefore valuable.
- D) Irrelevant; competitor presence doesn't matter at this stage.

---

**Q7.** Which of these is a **vanity signal** that should be *discounted*, not treated as validation?

- A) A clinic pays $300/month for an SMS reminder tool and complains it doesn't fill empty slots.
- B) A freelancer spends three hours a month reconciling invoices by hand.
- C) Forty people clicked "I'd try that" on a free poll in a Facebook group.
- D) A 2-star review reads "I pay for this and it still doesn't connect deposits to invoices."

---

**Q8.** You leave Week 2 carrying how many ideas into Week 3 discovery?

- A) As many as scored above 15/25, to keep options open.
- B) Three, to hedge against any one failing.
- C) One — the highest-scoring, reachable, kill-criteria-armed idea.
- D) Zero; discovery is where the idea gets generated.

---

**Q9.** Your top two ideas score within two points of each other. According to the lecture, the *first* tie-break to apply is:

- A) Which has the bigger total addressable market.
- B) Which you can interview *this week* (reachability).
- C) Which sounds more impressive to investors.
- D) Which you personally find more exciting.

---

**Q10.** Which of the following is a **well-formed, falsifiable** kill criterion?

- A) "If the idea stops feeling exciting, I'll drop it."
- B) "If competitors are too strong, I'll drop it."
- C) "If fewer than 6 of 10 interviewees describe this as a recurring, costly pain they acted on in the last month, I'll drop it."
- D) "If the market turns out to be too small, I'll drop it."

---

**Q11.** A founder finds that Google Trends for their problem's keyword is flat and low-volume. The most defensible response is:

- A) Kill the idea immediately — flat Trends means no demand.
- B) Treat Trends as directional only; cross-check forum/Reddit chatter and whether anyone pays for a workaround, since niche or hard-to-name pains often show flat Trends despite real demand.
- C) Buy ads to manufacture demand and re-check Trends.
- D) Ignore demand evidence entirely; it's all noise.

---

**Q12.** In 2026, a founder pitches "we'll add an AI agent to automate support tickets." Per the lecture, the honest assessment is:

- A) Strong idea — AI is the moat.
- B) The problem is real but the space is saturated; the AI is a commodity, so the idea only works if there's a specific under-served wedge (a vertical, language, or compliance regime) the incumbents are weak in.
- C) Avoid anything involving AI; it's overdone.
- D) The McKinsey "$4T of AI value" figure is the addressable market.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers and explanations</summary>

1. **B** — Problem first, solution last. Solution-first is the default failure mode; you reverse-engineer a problem to justify a build you already love. (L1 §1)
2. **B** — The one-sentence problem test: restate without naming the solution. "Creators struggle to post consistently and hate writing daily" — then ask if that's a painkiller or a vitamin. Surveys (D) about future behavior are weak; secrecy (C) buys nothing this early. (L1 §2)
3. **B** — An existing *paid* tool with angry reviews proves both demand (they pay) and dissatisfaction (your wedge). Upvotes (D) and party interest (A) are vanity signals; your own opinion (C) is worth nothing. (L2 §2–3)
4. **B** — Kill criteria are the antidote to confirmation bias. Once you're attached and have sunk interviews, you'll hear "yes" in every "maybe." Pre-committing while neutral is the only reliable defense. (L2 §4)
5. **B** — Reachability is the silent killer. A 5-painkiller you can't reach scores like a dud *for you*, because you can't even run discovery. (L2 §1)
6. **B** — "No competitors" is a question, not an answer. Markets are efficient enough that valuable, reachable problems usually have someone swinging at them. Default hypothesis: no market — disprove it by finding the expensive manual workaround. (L2 §3)
7. **C** — Free clicks cost the respondent nothing and predict behavior badly. A, B, and D all cost someone money or hours — those are the costly signals you weight heavily. Rule of thumb: if it cost them nothing to produce, it's worth nothing. (L2 §3)
8. **C** — One idea, done justice. Carrying three means three under-powered, inconclusive read-outs instead of one decisive one. The hedge feels safe but guarantees you learn nothing clearly. (L2 §5)
9. **B** — Reachability breaks the first tie: pick the one you can interview *this week*. Access is the binding constraint and momentum compounds. Standing breaks the next tie, pay signal the last. (L2 §5)
10. **C** — It's specific (a number), falsifiable (an outcome proves it true or false), and decided in advance. A, B, and D are all vague — you couldn't test any of them in a Week 3 interview. (L2 §4)
11. **B** — Flat Trends isn't a death sentence: niche terms have low volume, and people can't search for solutions they don't know could exist. Cross-check forums and pay signals before letting a flat line kill an idea. The 2026 AI-answer-box also flattens informational terms. (L2 §3)
12. **B** — The pain (overwhelmed support teams) is real, but the solution is a commodity and the space is saturated. The moat is the *wedge* — a specific under-served slice where incumbents are weak — not "we use AI." The macro AI-value figure (D) is never your market. (L1 §7)

</details>
