# Week 2 — Resources

Everything on this page is **free** and publicly accessible. No paywalled books, no course logins, no "request a demo" gates. Where a famous resource is paywalled, we link the free essay or talk that covers the same ground. If a link 404s, open an issue so we can replace it.

A note on 2026: the desk-validation landscape shifted. Search engines now answer many informational queries with an AI summary box, which deflects clicks away from articles and changes how Google Trends reads. Some keyword tools have tightened free tiers. The tools below are the ones still genuinely free and useful as of mid-2026, with the caveats noted inline.

## Required reading / watching (work it into your week)

- **Y Combinator — "How to Get Startup Ideas" (Paul Graham essay)** — the canonical problem-first sourcing piece; "live in the future and build what's missing," and "well-suited founders" as the real filter:
  <https://www.paulgraham.com/startupideas.html>
- **Y Combinator Startup Library — Idea & validation tracks** — free, current, founder-tested talks and essays; filter by "Idea" and "Customers":
  <https://www.ycombinator.com/library>
- **Steve Blank — "Customer Development Manifesto"** — the origin of "get out of the building" and problem-before-solution. Read at least the first three principles:
  <https://steveblank.com/2009/10/29/customer-development-manifesto-reasons-for-the-revolution-part-1/>
- **"The Mom Test" — author's free summary/talk by Rob Fitzpatrick** — the book is cheap and worth buying, but his free talks and the chapter summaries online teach the core: validate problems, not pitches. Search "Rob Fitzpatrick Mom Test talk" on YouTube for the current upload.
- **Lenny Rachitsky — "How to validate your startup idea" (free posts)** — modern, 2020s-current validation playbooks from operators:
  <https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/>

## Free desk-validation tools

- **Google Trends** — relative search demand over time and by region. Free, no account. Compare two terms; watch the slope, not the absolute number:
  <https://trends.google.com/trends/>
- **Google Search autocomplete & "People also ask"** — free, instant demand signal: type your problem and read what real people append. The questions are the pain in their words.
- **Reddit search** — find people describing your problem unprompted, in their own words. Use `site:reddit.com <your problem>` in Google, or Reddit's own search:
  <https://www.reddit.com/>
- **Answer the Public (free tier)** — visualizes the questions people search around a keyword:
  <https://answerthepublic.com/>
- **Exploding Topics (free trends feed)** — surfacing rising search terms before they peak:
  <https://explodingtopics.com/>
- **App-store reviews (Apple App Store / Google Play)** — the 1- and 2-star reviews of existing tools are a free, brutally honest list of unmet needs and willingness-to-pay-for-better signals.
- **G2 / Capterra / Trustpilot** — read the critical reviews of incumbent B2B/SaaS tools; the complaints are your wedge:
  <https://www.g2.com/> · <https://www.capterra.com/> · <https://www.trustpilot.com/>
- **Crunchbase (free tier)** — who's funded in the space, when, and how much. "Recently shut down" and "raised then went quiet" are both signals worth reading:
  <https://www.crunchbase.com/>

## Tools you'll use this week

- **A browser with the tools above bookmarked** — desk validation is a browser sport.

## What we deliberately do NOT use this week

- **Paid keyword tools (Ahrefs, SEMrush full tiers).** Useful later; overkill and paywalled now. Free Trends + autocomplete + Reddit gets you 80% of the signal at this stage.
- **Surveys.** Survey data this early is mostly noise — people predict their future behavior badly. Week 3 interviews about the *past* beat any survey about the *future*.
- **Building anything.** No landing page, no Figma, no code. Desk validation is research, not a product. The build is Week 5.

## Glossary cheat sheet

Keep this open in a tab.

| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| **Problem-first** | Start from a real, felt problem; decide the solution last. |
| **Solution-first** | Start from a cool idea and hunt for a problem to justify it. Usually a trap. |
| **Painkiller** | A problem that is urgent, recurring, and costly. People hack workarounds for it today. |
| **Vitamin** | A "nice to have." Pleasant, not urgent. Easy to ignore. |
| **Standing** | Your earned right to a problem space — you lived it, or you can reach the people who have it. |
| **Reachability** | Whether you can actually find and talk to the people with the problem, cheaply, this week. |
| **Triage** | Scoring many candidate ideas against one filter to keep exactly one. |
| **Desk validation** | Cheap, no-conversation research (search, competitors, reviews) before you interview. |
| **Willingness-to-pay signal** | Evidence people already spend money or hours to fix the problem. The strongest desk signal. |
| **Existing alternative** | What people use today — a competitor, a spreadsheet, an intern, duct tape, or nothing. |
| **Kill criteria** | Pre-committed, falsifiable, numeric evidence that would make you drop the idea. |
| **Confirmation bias** | The instinct to hear "yes" in every "maybe." Kill criteria are the antidote. |
| **TAM / SAM / SOM** | Total / serviceable / obtainable market. You'll size these properly in Week 6. |
