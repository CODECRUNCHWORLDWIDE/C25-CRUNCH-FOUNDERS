# Lecture 1 — Problem-First Idea Generation

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can generate startup ideas starting from real problems rather than exciting solutions, and you know where good problems reliably come from.

> **The best startup ideas are problems someone actually has, not solutions someone wishes existed.**

## 1. Solution-first is the default, and it's a trap

The natural instinct is solution-first: "wouldn't it be cool if there were an app that…". The trouble is the cool solution arrives without a problem attached, so you spend months building before discovering nobody hurts. Problem-first inverts this: start from a felt, recurring pain and let the solution be the last thing you decide.

## 2. Where good problems come from

- **Your own friction.** Problems you personally hit, repeatedly. You have built-in standing.
- **Your industry.** Problems you saw insiders work around clumsily. Outsiders never see these.
- **Expert annoyances.** Things that frustrate people who are very good at something — they notice friction novices accept.
- **Expensive workarounds.** Anywhere people pay, in money or hours, to patch over a gap. Payment is proof of pain.

Notice all four are about *observation*, not invention. Idea generation is mostly noticing.

## 3. Problem worth solving vs. solution you like

A quick test for any candidate:

- Can you state the **problem** in one sentence without naming your solution? If not, you have a solution looking for a problem.
- Does the problem pass the **painkiller test** (urgent, recurring, costly) from Week 1?
- Do you have **standing** — can you reach people who have it?

If a candidate fails the first test, you are in love with a solution. Set it down.

## 4. Quantity first, then ruthless cuts

Generate many candidates before judging any. Ten weak ideas surfaced honestly beat one idea you defend because it was first. Judgment comes in Lecture 2, with the triage filter. For now: notice problems, write them down, name the solution last.
