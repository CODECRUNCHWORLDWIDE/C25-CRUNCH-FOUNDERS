# Week 2 Homework

Five problems that revisit and deepen the week's topics. The full set should take about **5 hours**. Work in your Week 2 Git repository so each problem produces a committed artifact you can point to later — several of these feed directly into the [mini-project](./mini-project/README.md) and into Week 3.

Each problem includes a **problem statement**, **acceptance criteria** so you know when you're done, a **hint**, and an **estimated time**. A grading rubric for the whole set is at the bottom.

This is a business course: you will write zero code. You will write plain, falsifiable English, which is harder, because nothing compiles it but your own honesty.

---

## Problem 1 — Reverse three solutions to their problems

**Problem statement.** Take three "cool product ideas" — yours, a friend's, or ones you've heard pitched. At `notes/reverse-solutions.md`, run each through the reversal procedure from Lecture 1, §5: write the idea as stated (solution-shaped), force a one-sentence problem-only restatement, run the three-question gut filter, and decide whether a real painkiller survives. At least one of your three should reveal "no actual problem underneath" — if all three survive, you picked easy ones; swap one for a genuinely solution-first idea.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/reverse-solutions.md` exists.
- Three ideas, each with: the solution-shaped statement, the problem-only restatement, the three-question filter applied, and a verdict (painkiller / vitamin / no problem).
- At least one verdict is "no problem survives."
- Committed.

**Hint.** Add "the problem is that…" in front of the idea and rewrite, then delete the scaffold. "An app that gamifies grocery shopping" → "the problem is that people forget items and overspend" → "people forget items and overspend" → mild, non-recurring cost → **vitamin**.

**Estimated time.** 40 minutes.

## Problem 2 — Score five problems on the five-axis filter

**Problem statement.** At `notes/triage-practice.md`, take **five** problems (your shortlist from Exercise 1, or fresh ones) and score each on the full five-axis filter (painkiller, frequency, standing, reachability, pay signal; 1–5; total /25), using the Exercise 2 rubric. Then write two short paragraphs: (1) name your top idea's **soft spot** — its weakest axis — and tell the truth about it; (2) confirm that no idea has a straight column of 5s, or defend each 5 if one genuinely does.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File exists with a five-row scored table, totals /25, sorted descending.
- Every axis scored against the rubric (not vibes).
- The soft-spot paragraph names a specific weak axis and is honest about it.
- No un-defended all-5s column.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Score each axis *blind to the total*, then sum. If you peek at the total while scoring, you'll nudge axes to hit a number you like — that's the exact dishonesty the filter is meant to catch. A real painkiller has a soft spot somewhere.

**Estimated time.** 1 hour.

## Problem 3 — Desk validation on your chosen idea

**Problem statement.** For your top candidate, run the four desk-validation passes at `notes/desk-validation.md`: (1) search demand — what Google Trends, autocomplete, and "people also ask" showed; (2) existing alternatives — **named, with links**, and typed (competitor / spreadsheet / intern-VA / duct-tape / nothing); (3) willingness-to-pay signal — the strongest *costly* signal you found, with a real price or hours figure; (4) incumbent reviews — at least three critical reviews of the current alternative and the wedge they reveal. End with a one-line verdict (stronger or weaker than you thought) and the one question only Week 3 interviews can answer.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File exists with all four passes completed.
- Alternatives are **named with links** and typed.
- At least one **costly** signal cited (a paid tool + price, or hours spent), not a vanity signal.
- A one-line verdict and a named "only-interviews-can-answer" question.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Free tools only — Google Trends, search autocomplete, `site:reddit.com <your problem>`, competitor pricing pages, and App Store / G2 / Capterra reviews sorted lowest-first. Twenty critical reviews of an incumbent teach you more in 20 minutes than a week of brainstorming.

**Estimated time.** 1 hour 15 minutes.

## Problem 4 — Write your kill criteria

**Problem statement.** At `notes/kill-criteria.md`, write **three to five** specific, falsifiable, numeric kill criteria for your chosen idea — the evidence from Week 3 interviews that would make you drop it. Cover at least: problem reality (a count threshold), workaround existence, reachability, and dissatisfaction with the current alternative. Then, for each criterion, write one sentence on *how you'll measure it* in a discovery interview.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File exists with 3–5 kill criteria.
- Each is specific (a number or clear condition), falsifiable, and decided in advance.
- Each has a one-sentence "how I'll measure it in an interview" note.
- Committed.

**Hint.** "If fewer than 5 of 10 interviewees call this a recurring pain they acted on this month, I drop it" is good — it has a number and you can test it. "If it seems bad" is not. The measurement note forces you to confirm the criterion is actually answerable by a conversation.

**Estimated time.** 35 minutes.

## Problem 5 — Mini reflection: why this idea, and what could kill it

**Problem statement.** Write a 300–400 word reflection at `notes/week-02-reflection.md` answering, each in its own paragraph:

1. Which well (your friction / your industry / expert annoyances / expensive workarounds) produced your strongest candidate, and why?
2. Did any "cool idea" you arrived with this week die when you restated it as a problem? What did that teach you about your own instincts?
3. What is your committed idea's **biggest risk** — the single thing most likely to kill it in Week 3 — and how will you know if it's fatal?
4. What is your idea's **"why now"** — what changed recently that makes this the right moment?

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File exists, 300–400 words.
- Each of the four questions is addressed in its own paragraph.
- The "biggest risk" paragraph names a *specific* risk tied to a kill criterion, not a generic worry.
- Committed.

**Hint.** This is for *you* and for future-you in Week 10, not for a grade you should game. The most useful thing you can write is the honest sentence about the risk you'd rather not name. Question 4's answer is the seed of your demo-day "why now" slide — save it.

**Estimated time.** 30 minutes.

## Grading rubric

The homework is graded out of 100, weighted as follows:

| Criterion | Weight | What "great" looks like |
|-----------|------:|--------------------------|
| Problems stated without solutions | 20% | Every reversal and every triage row passes the one-sentence test; no "an app that…" |
| Honest scoring and self-assessment | 20% | No inflated columns; the soft spot is named and genuinely a weakness |
| Desk validation cites real, costly signals | 25% | Alternatives named and linked; a real price or hours figure; a wedge from actual review language |
| Kill criteria are falsifiable and measurable | 25% | Every criterion has a number/threshold and a stated way to test it in an interview |
| Reflection is honest and specific | 10% | Names a real risk tied to a kill criterion; a concrete "why now," not a platitude |

**Automatic deductions.** A straight column of 5s with no defense (−10). Vanity signals (upvotes, "I'd try that" clicks, market-size headlines) treated as validation (−10). Kill criteria you couldn't test in a Week 3 interview (−10). Any committed file containing private contact information (−10 and fix it before resubmitting).
