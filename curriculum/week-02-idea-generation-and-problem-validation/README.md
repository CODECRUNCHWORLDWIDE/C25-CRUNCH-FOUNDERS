# Week 2 — Idea Generation & Problem Validation

This week you stop being precious about ideas. Most first-time founders treat their idea like a secret worth protecting and a solution worth defending. Both instincts are wrong. Ideas are cheap and abundant; what is scarce is a *problem worth solving* and the discipline to triage many candidates without falling in love with any one of them. Week 2 reframes idea generation as a **problem-first** activity and gives you a lightweight way to validate from your desk before you ever build or even interview.

The deliverable is an idea-triage log: 5–10 ideas scored against a problem-first filter, with one chosen to carry into the discovery interviews of Week 3.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Generate** ideas problem-first (start from a felt problem, not a cool solution).
- **Apply** a triage filter — painkiller-ness, frequency, your standing, market reachability, and willingness-to-pay signal.
- **Run** lightweight desk validation: search demand, existing alternatives, and what people already pay for.
- **Distinguish** a problem worth solving from a solution you happen to like.
- **Choose** one idea to carry into discovery, and write down what would *kill* it.

## Topics covered

- Problem-first vs. solution-first idea generation
- Where good problems come from (your own friction, your industry, things that annoy experts)
- The triage filter and how to score honestly
- Desk validation: search trends, competitors, "what is the current workaround"
- The kill criteria: deciding in advance what evidence would make you drop the idea

## In-class activities

- **Problem dump.** Five minutes, silent, everyone lists every problem they have felt in the last week. No solutions allowed.
- **Solution-to-problem reversal.** Take a "cool idea" someone brought and trace it back to the actual problem — half the time there isn't one.
- **Kill-criteria pact.** Each founder reads their chosen idea's kill criteria aloud. The group's job is to make them sharper, not nicer.

## Worked example

A founder lists "an app that gamifies grocery shopping." Reversing it to a problem yields: *"people forget items and overspend."* Desk validation finds dozens of free list apps and store loyalty apps already solving it, and no evidence anyone hacks a workaround because the pain is mild. Verdict: **vitamin**, crowded, weak standing — parked. The same founder's second idea, "scheduling no-shows kill small clinics' revenue," survives the filter: painkiller, recurring, the founder worked in a clinic (standing), and clinics already pay for clumsy reminder tools (willingness-to-pay signal). That one moves to Week 3.
