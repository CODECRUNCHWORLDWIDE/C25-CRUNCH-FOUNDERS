# Week 2 — Idea Generation & Problem Validation

Welcome to **C25 · Crunch Founders**. Week 1 made you do the uncomfortable mirror work: founder-market fit, your control-vs-wealth lean, a problem space you have real standing in. Week 2 is where you stop being precious about *ideas* and start being ruthless about *problems*.

Here is the reframe the whole week turns on. Most first-time founders treat an idea like a secret worth protecting and a solution worth defending. Both instincts are wrong. Ideas are cheap, abundant, and mostly worthless on their own — what is scarce is **a problem worth solving** and the **discipline to triage many candidates without falling in love with any one of them.** By Friday you will have generated 15+ problems problem-first, scored 5–10 of them against an honest filter, run free desk validation on your top two, and committed to exactly one idea — with written kill criteria — to carry into the Week 3 discovery interviews.

This is a business course. You will write zero code. You will, however, write a great deal of plain, falsifiable English — which is harder, because there is no compiler to tell you that you are lying to yourself. The triage log is the compiler. Honest scores are the build. A vague "this feels big" is a warning, and this week we treat warnings as bugs.

We assume you finished Week 1 and have a one-page founder brief: your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, and a problem space you can defend. If you don't, go back — this week generates ideas *inside* the space you already have standing in, and standing is the thing that makes your discovery interviews even possible.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** problem-first from solution-first idea generation — and catch yourself, on sight, when you have fallen back into solution-first.
- **Source** problems from the four reliable wells: your own friction, your industry's workarounds, expert annoyances, and expensive workarounds where someone already pays in money or hours.
- **Reverse** a "cool idea" back to the problem it claims to solve, and notice when no real problem survives the reversal.
- **Score** candidate ideas on a five-axis triage filter — painkiller-ness, frequency, your standing, reachability, and willingness-to-pay signal — without inflating the numbers.
- **Run** lightweight desk validation in under an hour per idea using only free tools: search demand, existing alternatives, and what people already pay for.
- **Read** a search-demand or competitor signal honestly, including the trap where "no competitors" means "no market" far more often than "untapped goldmine."
- **Write** falsifiable kill criteria — pre-committed, numeric evidence that would make you drop the idea — *before* you talk to a single customer.
- **Commit** to a single idea, stated as a problem, with five reachable interview targets named by role.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed **C25 Week 1 — Founder mindset & founder-market fit**, or have equivalent grounding. Specifically:

- You have a **founder brief**: your unfair advantages, a stated control-vs-wealth lean, and at least one problem space you have standing in.
- You understand the Week 1 **painkiller vs. vitamin** distinction (a painkiller is urgent, recurring, and costly; a vitamin is a "nice to have").
- You can spend ~3 hours this week on field-adjacent work — desk research, not interviews yet, but real searching and reading.
- You have a place to write: a Git repo, a Notion, a doc. This week produces five or six markdown artifacts; keep them together.

You do **not** need a co-founder, a product, a logo, a domain name, or a single line of code. Anyone who shows up to Week 2 with a built MVP has already lost the plot — they solved before they triaged. We will gently un-build that instinct.

## Topics covered

- Problem-first vs. solution-first generation, and why solution-first is the default failure mode.
- The four wells good problems come from, and why idea generation is mostly *noticing*, not *inventing*.
- The one-sentence problem test ("state it without naming your solution").
- Vitamin vs. painkiller as a gut check; frequency and cost as the sharpening.
- The five-axis **triage filter**: painkiller-ness, frequency, standing, reachability, pay signal — scored 1–5, summed out of 25.
- Honest scoring: why inflating only fools you, and how a column of all-5s is itself a red flag.
- **Desk validation** with free tools: Google Trends, search autocomplete, Reddit/forum threads, competitor pricing pages, app-store reviews.
- Reading demand signals: the "no competitors" trap, the "everyone uses a spreadsheet" tell, and what 2026 changed (LLM-answer-box deflection of informational search).
- Existing alternatives as the real benchmark — including "a spreadsheet," "an intern," "duct tape," and "nothing."
- Willingness-to-pay signals, ranked from weakest (someone said it sounds cool) to strongest (people pay today for a clumsy tool).
- **Kill criteria**: pre-committed, falsifiable, numeric. The antidote to confirmation bias.
- Choosing one. Why carrying three ideas into discovery means doing none of them justice.

## In-class / cohort activities

If you are running this with a cohort or a peer, these three live activities do most of the work that solo reading can't:

- **Problem dump (5 min, silent).** Everyone lists every problem they personally hit in the last seven days. No solutions allowed — if you catch yourself writing "an app that…", cross it out and write the pain instead.
- **Solution-to-problem reversal.** Someone names a "cool idea." The group traces it back to the actual problem out loud. Roughly half the time there isn't one, and watching that happen to *someone else's* idea inoculates you against doing it to your own.
- **Kill-criteria pact.** Each founder reads their chosen idea's kill criteria aloud. The group's job is to make them **sharper, not nicer** — to replace "if it seems bad" with "if fewer than 5 of 10 call it a recurring pain."

## Worked example (carries through the week)

A founder, Maya, lists "an app that gamifies grocery shopping." Reversing it to a problem yields *"people forget items and overspend."* Desk validation finds dozens of free list apps and every grocery chain's loyalty app already solving it, Google Trends flat, and no evidence anyone hacks a workaround because the pain is mild. Verdict: **vitamin, crowded, weak standing — parked.**

Maya's second idea isn't an idea at first, it's a memory: at the dental clinic where she worked front desk for three years, scheduling no-shows quietly bled revenue, and the office manager tracked them in a color-coded spreadsheet she'd rebuilt by hand. Stated as a problem: *"small clinics lose 10–15% of bookable revenue to no-shows and last-minute cancellations they can't fill in time."* It survives the filter — painkiller (revenue, urgent), recurring (daily), standing (she lived it), reachable (she still knows twenty office managers), and a pay signal (clinics already pay for clumsy SMS-reminder tools). That one moves to Week 3, and it is the example we return to in every lecture and exercise below.

## The "stated as a problem" promise

C25 uses one recurring discipline marker all course long, and it starts this week:

> **Every idea must be stateable as a problem in one sentence, without naming your solution.**

If you cannot do that, you do not have a validated problem — you have a solution you like. The whole point of Week 2 is to make "I have a problem worth solving, and here's the evidence" the ordinary thing you say, and "I have an idea for an app" the thing you catch yourself saying and rewrite.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Run the full triage filter on **ten** ideas instead of five. The discipline of scoring losers honestly is the actual skill.
- Pick one idea you are *sure* is a painkiller and try to kill it on purpose with desk validation alone — find the three strongest pieces of disconfirming evidence you can.
- Read Y Combinator's current "How to Get Startup Ideas" essay and write one paragraph on where your sourcing differs from theirs: <https://www.ycombinator.com/library>.
- For your chosen idea, sketch the *current alternative* in detail — the spreadsheet, the intern, the manual process. The clearer you can describe what people do today, the sharper your Week 3 interviews will be.
