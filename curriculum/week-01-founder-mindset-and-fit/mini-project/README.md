# Mini-Project — Your Founder Operating Document

> Build a single, durable document that captures who you are as a founder: your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, your standing in a problem space, your co-founder needs, your decision principles, and the kill criteria that will keep you honest. You will update it after every major decision in this course and carry it into whatever you build next.

This is the Week 1 deliverable that is meant to outlive the week — and it is **the first compounding artifact of your capstone.** C25 is a sequential course: each week's mini-deliverable becomes an input to the next, and by Week 10 they assemble into a validated startup concept and a three-minute pitch. The Founder Operating Document (FOD) is the root of that tree. Your founder brief (Exercise 1), your tested control-vs-wealth lean (Exercise 2), your founder-market-fit canvas (Exercise 3), and your kill criteria (Challenge 1, if you did it) all collapse into this one file. One source of truth, version-controlled, updated for ten weeks.

**Estimated time:** ~8 hours of real work this week is generous; the writing is ~2 hours, but doing it *well* means it has absorbed the thinking from all three exercises and the challenge. Split across Friday and the weekend in the suggested schedule.

## How this compounds toward the capstone

| Week | What you add to (or pull from) the FOD |
|------|----------------------------------------|
| **1 (now)** | The whole founding self: advantages, lean, standing, co-founder posture, principles, kill criteria |
| 2 | The chosen idea, triaged problem-first against your Section 3 standing |
| 3 | Discovery interview synthesis tested against your Section 7 kill criteria |
| 4 | Your Section 4 co-founder posture becomes a real agreement; your lean sets the equity split |
| 5 | Persevere/pivot decision logged against the kill criteria you wrote this week |
| 6–7 | Business model and GTM decisions, each logged with the lean that drove them |
| 8 | Raise-or-don't, decided by your Section 2 lean and your cap-table model |
| 9–10 | Incorporation choices and the pitch — which *is* the FOD, told as a story with evidence |

The capstone rubric rewards *internal consistency* — a pitch where the equity split, the fundraising choice, and the founder story all hang together. That consistency is impossible to fake at the end; it's only achievable if you've been logging decisions against a stable spine since Week 1. This document is that spine.

---

## What you will produce

A single Markdown file, `mini-project/FOUNDER-OPERATING-DOC.md`, committed to your Week 1 repo *and* copied into your personal portfolio repo (you'll keep updating it from there). Roughly 700–1,000 words. Not an essay — a document of *decisions*, in the C25 voice: problem-first, evidence over adjectives, no "visionary / disruptor / 10x."

---

## Required sections

Each section below must appear, in this order, with the heading text shown.

### 1. Who I am as a founder
Two or three short paragraphs: your unfair advantages (mark each with an evidence letter), what you're missing, and your obsession test. Pull from Exercise 1, but tighten it — this is the polished version.

### 2. My control-vs-wealth lean
One paragraph stating your default and *why*, plus one line naming the scenario from Exercise 2 that most tested it and whether your lean bent. State your real lean, not your aspirational one.

### 3. My problem space and standing
The space you're entering this course with (the winner from your Exercise 3 canvas), your "why me, why now," and the result of the "name three reachable people" test. Note the weakest of your five advantages here and your plan to cover it.

### 4. My co-founder posture
Solo or co-founders, and if co-founders, the *specific* gap(s) you need filled (not "someone technical"). You'll revisit this hard in Week 4, so be precise now.

### 5. My decision principles
Three to five short rules you commit to. Make them yours, but they should be concrete enough that a stranger could hold you to them. Examples:
- "I will not build before I've talked to ten people who have the problem."
- "I will decide equity and vesting in writing, with a cliff, before any money moves."
- "I will not chase signups when I should be measuring retention."
- "I will state the evidence behind every claim I make in a pitch."

### 6. My anti-patterns to watch
The founder self-deceptions from Lecture 1 §5 that *you specifically* are most at risk of (name at least two), and the concrete tripwire you'll use to catch yourself in each.

### 7. My kill criteria
At least two pre-committed kill/pivot criteria — measurable, time-boxed, with the response written down. (If you did Challenge 1, pull your best two here; if not, write them now — Lecture 1 §6 and the challenge brief show the format.) At least one must be testable in Week 3.

### 8. Version log

| Date | What changed | Trigger |
|------|--------------|---------|
| (today) | First draft | C25 Week 1 |
| ... | ... | ... |

Every major decision in this course should produce a line here. This log is what makes your Week 10 pitch internally consistent — it's the audit trail of how your thinking evolved.

## Rules

- **You may** reuse text from Exercises 1–3 and Challenge 1 — they were always meant to feed in here.
- **You must** anchor the strongest claim in Sections 1 and 3 to the evidence ladder.
- **You will not** make this a pitch. No "visionary," no "disruptor," no "10x," no "rockstar." Plain language only — you're writing for future-you, who needs the truth, not a sales document.
- **You may** keep the canonical copy in your portfolio repo, but commit a copy under Week 1 for grading.

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] File at `mini-project/FOUNDER-OPERATING-DOC.md` in your Week 1 repo.
- [ ] All eight sections present, in order, with the exact headings.
- [ ] Section 1's strongest advantage and Section 3's standing claim are marked with an evidence letter.
- [ ] Section 2 states a single lean (not "both") and names the scenario that tested it.
- [ ] Section 3's chosen space is the winner of your Exercise 3 canvas (or you explain the change).
- [ ] Section 4's co-founder gap is *specific*, not "someone technical."
- [ ] Section 5 has at least three concrete, holdable decision principles.
- [ ] Section 6 names at least two personal anti-patterns, each with a tripwire.
- [ ] Section 7 has at least two measurable, time-boxed, pre-committed kill criteria; at least one testable in Week 3.

- [ ] 700–1,000 words; no banned words.
- [ ] A copy also committed in your portfolio repo (if you have one). Committed.

## Rubric

| Criterion | Weight | What "great" looks like |
|-----------|-------:|-------------------------|
| Specificity | 20% | Every section names real choices and concrete evidence, not platitudes |
| Honesty | 20% | The "what I'm missing," anti-patterns, and tested lean feel true, not flattering |
| Lean is decided | 15% | Section 2 commits to control or wealth with a reason and a tested scenario |
| Principles are actionable | 15% | A stranger could hold you to Section 5 |
| Kill criteria are real | 15% | Section 7 criteria are measurable, time-boxed, and pre-committed — not "we'll see" |
| Consistency + version log | 15% | The sections cohere; Section 8 exists; the chosen space is consistent across the doc |

## What this prepares you for

- **Week 2** — you'll triage ideas against your Section 3 standing and your Section 7 kill criteria.
- **Week 3** — your kill criteria become your interview objectives; you'll know what evidence you're hunting.
- **Week 4** — your Section 4 co-founder posture and Section 2 lean drive a real equity split with vesting.
- **Week 8** — your Section 2 lean decides whether you raise; your kill criteria decide whether the signal justifies it.
- **Week 10** — the FOD, told as a story with evidence, *is* your pitch's founder-and-problem narrative.
- **Every week** — Section 8 logs the decisions that define your company, and that log is what makes the capstone hang together.

## Submission

1. Push your Week 1 repo public.
2. Copy the FOD to your portfolio repo (or create one).
3. End the document with a confidence statement on its single strongest claim.
4. In the cohort channel, post the URL and read one other person's FOD, leaving one *specific* comment — ideally naming a kill criterion they should add or an advantage they're overstating.

You are now ready for **Week 2 — Idea Generation & Problem Validation**, where the standing you claimed here gets turned into a triaged, problem-first idea you carry the rest of the cycle.
