# Exercise 3 — Founder-Market-Fit Canvas

**Goal:** Score yourself, honestly and side by side, on the five unfair advantages across **three** candidate problem spaces — then pick the one with the best founder-market fit to carry into Week 2.

**Estimated time:** 45 minutes.

**Type:** Scoring canvas / template. Fill it in and commit it.

---

## Why this exercise exists

Founder-market fit is the single best predictor of whether *you* (not someone else) can win in a given space, and it is fully checkable before you build anything. But it's almost impossible to judge in the abstract — "do I have good founder-market fit?" is unanswerable. It only becomes answerable *comparatively*: across three concrete spaces, which one lights up the most advantages with the strongest evidence?

Most founders never do this comparison. They commit to the first idea that excites them and never ask whether a *different* space would have given them a structural edge. This canvas forces the comparison while it's cheap — before you've sunk a quarter into the wrong one. The space that wins here is the space you should carry into Week 2's idea triage.

---

## The scoring rule

For each of the three spaces, score each of the five unfair advantages **0–3**:

- **0** — none. You have nothing here.
- **1** — weak. Interest, or a single thin thread.
- **2** — real. A genuine, specific advantage you can point to.
- **3** — strong. A hard-to-copy edge backed by lived experience or direct access.

Then, next to each score, write the **evidence letter** from the ladder: (a) lived it, (b) talked to people, (c) read it, (d) assumed it. A score of 3 backed by "(d) assumed it" is a contradiction — fix one or the other. The honest combination is the whole point.

Sum the five scores for each space (max 15). But do not pick purely on the total — read the *shape*. A space scoring 11 with a 3 in distribution and obsession may beat a space scoring 12 that's all skill and no access. Distribution and obsession are the advantages that compound; skill is the one most easily hired or commoditized by AI.

## Worked example (for shape, not content)

```markdown
## Scoring

| Unfair advantage | A: Logistics dispatch | B: AI coding tools | C: Clinic scheduling |
|------------------|:---:|:---:|:---:|
| Lived experience | 3 [a] | 1 [a] | 0 [d] |
| Domain access    | 3 [a] | 1 [c] | 0 [d] |
| Distribution     | 3 [a] | 0 [d] | 0 [d] |
| Skill            | 2 [a] | 3 [a] | 3 [a] |
| Obsession        | 2 [a] | 1 [b] | 0 [d] |
| **Total (/15)**  | **13** | **6** | **3** |

## My choice and why
Space A (logistics dispatch). It wins on both total (13) and shape — three 3s in the advantages that
compound (lived experience, access, distribution). Space B is seductive because I can *build* it, but
skill alone in a crowded space isn't an unfair advantage; everyone there has skill. Space C is a skill
looking for a problem. The deciding advantage is distribution: I have a 4,000-person newsletter in
logistics and zero audience anywhere else.

## The weakest advantage of my chosen space
Skill (2): I can build the backend but I'm weak on the field-ops UX dispatchers need. Plan: recruit a
co-founder or design partner who has run a dispatch desk, or co-design with three users in Week 3.
```

## Expected outcome

You'll leave with one chosen problem space, defensible on founder-market-fit grounds rather than on excitement, plus a clear-eyed view of its weakest advantage and how you'll cover it. This chosen space is what you carry into Week 2's idea triage — and because you picked it on *fit*, not novelty, you're far less likely to stall when the discovery interviews start in Week 3.

---

## Hints

- **Pick genuinely different spaces.** If all three are variations of the same idea, you're not testing fit, you're rationalizing a decision you already made.
- **Let the canvas overrule your gut.** The whole value is that it sometimes tells you the boring space beats the exciting one. If it does, believe it — or at least notice how hard you're resisting, and ask why.
- **Distribution is the 2026 tiebreaker.** In a year when AI made building trivial, the founder who can *reach customers* wins. Weight it accordingly.
- If your top space scores below ~8, you may not have strong founder-market fit anywhere yet — that's a real finding, and Week 2 (idea generation from your strengths) is built for exactly that situation.
