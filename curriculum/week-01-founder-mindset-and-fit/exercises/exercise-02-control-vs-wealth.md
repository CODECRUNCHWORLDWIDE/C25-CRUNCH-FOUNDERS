# Exercise 2 — Control vs. Wealth, Made Concrete

**Goal:** Move your control-vs-wealth lean from a vague feeling to a tested position by running it against four concrete decisions you will actually face as a founder.

**Estimated time:** 30 minutes.

**Type:** Worksheet / template. Fill in the template below and commit it.

---

## Why this exercise exists

It is easy to say "I want both." The trade only becomes real when a specific decision forces a choice — and those decisions arrive fast once you start a company. This exercise puts you in front of four such decisions *now*, in writing, so your answer is not improvised later under pressure with a term sheet on the table and a co-founder waiting on the phone.

The most valuable output is not your stated lean — it's the *gap* between your stated lean and your actual choices. If you wrote "CONTROL" in your founder brief but then give away control in every scenario below, your real lean is wealth, and knowing that changes how you'll approach Week 4 (equity) and Week 8 (fundraising). The exercise is a lie detector for the answer you gave in Exercise 1.

---

## How to do it

For each scenario: state your **choice** and — this is the part most people skip — the **cost you accept** by making it. Every choice in the control-vs-wealth trade has a cost; naming it is how you prove to yourself the choice is real and not wishful. There are no graded "right" answers. A consistent control lean and a consistent wealth lean are both fine. An *inconsistent* one is the useful finding.

---

---

## Worked example (for shape, not as the "right" answer)

```markdown
My default lean: CONTROL, because I left a job specifically to stop having a boss.

## Scenario 1 — The strong co-founder
- My choice: I take the 50/50 split. The gap they fill (enterprise sales) is one I cannot fill myself,
  and 50% of a company that works beats 90% of one that doesn't.
- The cost I accept: I now share every major decision and can be outvoted on a 2-person team. That's a
  real surrender of control, and I'm making it knowingly.

## Scenario 2 — The funding round
- My choice: I pass, or raise a much smaller amount on a SAFE with no board seat.
- The cost I accept: We grow slower and may lose to a faster-moving, better-funded competitor.
```

Notice the contradiction this surfaces: this founder leans control, yet took the 50/50 co-founder. That's not wrong — but it means their *real* lean is more nuanced than the one-word answer, and that nuance is the finding.

---

## Hints

- If you found every scenario easy, you probably didn't take the *cost* seriously. Go back and make the cost sting — that's where the honesty lives.
- "It depends on the numbers" is a legitimate answer to Scenario 4 — but then *state the number* that would flip you. A founder who knows their walk-away number is a founder who has thought about it.
- There is no prize for being maximally control-leaning or maximally wealth-leaning. The prize is consistency between what you say and what you'd actually do.
