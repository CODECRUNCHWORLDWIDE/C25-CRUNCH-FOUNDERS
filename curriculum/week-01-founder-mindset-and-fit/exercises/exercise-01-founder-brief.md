# Exercise 1 — Founder Brief

**Goal:** Write a one-page brief of who *you* are as a founder — your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, a problem space you have standing in, and what (if anything) you need from a co-founder. This brief travels with you through the whole course and is the spine of the Week 1 mini-project.

**Estimated time:** 45 minutes.

**Type:** Guided worksheet. You'll work through five steps, then assemble the brief.

---

## Why this exercise exists

Most first-time founders pick a problem because it is interesting, then discover halfway in that they have no access to customers and no real stamina for it. A pre-written brief forces the honesty up front: it makes you state your advantages, your trade-off lean, and your standing *before* you fall in love with an idea in Week 2. The founders who skip this step are the ones who, six weeks later, can't get a single discovery interview and quietly give up — never realizing the problem was their standing, not their idea.

The brief is also a forcing function for the course's core discipline: **evidence over enthusiasm.** Every line you write, you'll mark with where it sits on the evidence ladder. By the end you'll see, at a glance, which of your beliefs about yourself are lived truth and which are flattering assumption.

---

## Step 1 — Inventory your unfair advantages (10 min)

Before writing anything formal, brainstorm freely. For each of the five unfair advantages from Lecture 2, write down anything you have — even if it feels small. Be concrete; "I understand healthcare" is worthless, "I spent three years in dental-office operations" is gold.

| Advantage | Do you have it here? Be specific. | Evidence: (a) lived / (b) talked / (c) read / (d) assumed |
|-----------|-----------------------------------|----------|
| **Lived experience** | | |
| **Domain access** (who can you reach?) | | |
| **Distribution** (audience / network / channel) | | |
| **Skill** (build or sell) | | |
| **Obsession** (still care in 3 years?) | | |

If most of your rows are blank or marked "(d) assumed," that is a finding — you may be looking at the wrong space. Don't panic; Exercise 3 lets you compare three spaces and pick the strongest.

## Step 2 — Name what you're missing (5 min)

This is the section that separates serious founders from optimists. Write down, plainly, what you do **not** have. Examples: "I cannot ship production code." "I have no network in this industry." "I've never sold anything." "I don't know how cap tables work." A brief with no honest gaps is a brief written by someone who isn't being honest.

## Step 3 — Pick your control-vs-wealth lean (10 min)

Re-read Lecture 2 §1 if you need to. Then pick a single default lean — *more toward CONTROL* or *more toward WEALTH* — and write two sentences on why. You are not allowed to write "both." If you genuinely feel both, write down which one you'd protect when forced to choose only one; that's your real lean. (Exercise 2 will test it against three concrete scenarios, so a rough answer now is fine — you'll refine it.)

## Step 4 — Claim a problem space and your standing (10 min)

Pick one problem space you might work on. Then write the one-sentence **"why me, why now."** Apply the test from Lecture 2 §3: *can you name three specific people (by role) you could interview about this in the next two weeks?* If you can, your standing is real. If you stall, write that down honestly — it changes which space you should pick.

## Step 5 — Co-founder posture (5 min)

Solo or co-founders? If co-founders, name the **one specific gap** you need filled. "Someone technical" is too vague. "Someone who has sold enterprise software into hospitals and has the relationships" is specific and useful. The specificity of your answer is a readout of how well you understand your own gaps.

---

## What to produce

Create `exercises/exercise-01-founder-brief.md` in your Week 1 repo with the headings below. One to three honest sentences per section. No essays. One printed page (~400 words). Mark each advantage and standing claim with its evidence-ladder letter.

```markdown
# Founder brief — <your name>

## My unfair advantages
(One to three. Be specific. Mark each with (a)/(b)/(c)/(d). "I worked three years in
dental-office operations [a]" beats "I understand healthcare [d].")

## What I am missing
(Honesty. "I cannot build software." "I have no network in this industry." "I've never sold anything.")

## My control-vs-wealth lean
(Pick a default: more toward CONTROL or more toward WEALTH. Two sentences on why. Not "both.")

## A problem space I have standing in
(One space. Then the one-sentence "why me, why now." Note whether you can name three reachable interviewees.)

## Solo or co-founders?
(Your current lean, and IF co-founders: the one specific thing you need that you do not have.)

## My obsession test
(Will I still care about this problem in three years when it is boring? One honest sentence.)

## What I want to get out of this course
(One sentence. Not "get rich." A concrete learning or decision goal.)

## Confidence statement
Claim (the brief's strongest line): ____________________
Evidence behind it: (a) lived it / (b) talked to N / (c) read it / (d) assumed it
Confidence: low / medium / high — and why
```

## Worked example (use for shape, not content)

```markdown
# Founder brief — Maya

## My unfair advantages
- Four years as an office manager at a busy dental practice; I lived the scheduling chaos daily. [a]
- ~15 warm contacts still working in dental offices I can interview this week. [a]
- I can build a clickable prototype in Figma, though not production software. [a]

## What I am missing
- I cannot ship production code. I'll need a technical co-founder or a no-code path for the MVP.
- I have never raised money and do not know how cap tables work yet.

## My control-vs-wealth lean
More toward CONTROL. I'd rather own the decisions and grow slower than raise a large round and
answer to a board early. I'll pressure-test this in Exercise 2 and revisit it in Week 8.

## A problem space I have standing in
Scheduling and patient no-shows for small dental practices. Why me, why now: I lived the pain, I can
reach the customers cheaply, and post-pandemic staffing shortages made it worse. I can name three
reachable interviewees (a practice owner, an office manager, a hygienist). [a]

## Solo or co-founders?
Leaning toward one technical co-founder. The specific thing I need: someone who can build and run a
real web app, not just prototype it.

## My obsession test
Yes — I was still ranting about no-shows two years after I left the job. That's a good sign.

## What I want to get out of this course
A defensible go/no-go decision on this idea backed by real interviews, not my own certainty.

## Confidence statement
Claim: "Small dental practices lose real money to no-shows and have no good tool for it."
Evidence: (a) lived it + (b) talked to ~5 informally.
Confidence: medium — strong on the pain, unproven on willingness to pay. That's Week 3's job.
```

Notice every section names *specifics*, and the weakest part of the claim (willingness to pay) is stated plainly rather than hidden. There is no "passionate," no "visionary." Those words mean nothing on a founder brief.

---

## Expected outcome

When you're done you will have a one-page document that:

- Tells a stranger, in under two minutes, why you specifically might win in this space.
- Surfaces your real gaps so Week 4 (co-founders) and Week 8 (fundraising) aren't surprises.
- Contains at least one honestly-labeled assumption that becomes a Week 3 interview question.
- Reads like *you*, not like a pitch deck.

The deliverable feeds directly into the mini-project (the Founder Operating Document), so a strong brief here saves you time on Friday.

## Hints

- **Do not inflate your advantages.** "I find this interesting" is not an advantage; lived experience, access, and distribution are. If you catch yourself writing "I'm passionate about," delete it and write the concrete thing the passion is made of.
- **Pick a control-vs-wealth lean even if it feels premature.** You can change it; you cannot skip it. A drifting founder is worse off than one who picks wrong.
- **The "name three people" test is the real one.** If you can't, your standing is weaker than it feels — and it's far better to learn that now than after a month of failed outreach.
- **Share it with one trusted person.** Ask them two questions: "Does this sound like me?" and "Is the standing claim real, or am I flattering myself?"
