# Week 1 — Founder Mindset & Founder-Market Fit

Welcome to **C25 · Crunch Founders — Zero to Launch**. This is week one of a ten-week course that takes you from "I have an idea" to "I have *evidence*." Week 1 is the week we point the lens at you before we ever point it at a market. By Sunday you will have written a one-page founder brief that states, in plain language, your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, and a problem space you have genuine standing in — and you will have pressure-tested that brief against the kinds of decisions that break founders in year one.

This course is **business, not engineering**. You will write zero code this week and very little across the cycle; the building lives in [C8](../../../C8-CRUNCH-LABS-WEB-DEV/) and [C16](../../../C16-CRUNCH-PRO-WEB-BACKEND/). What you will do here is harder for most technical people than code: you will make irreversible people-and-money decisions under uncertainty, and you will learn to prefer evidence over enthusiasm. We move at the pace of a real pre-seed founder — fast, opinionated, allergic to vanity.

The first thing to internalize is that **the idea is the cheap part**. The cultural myth says a founder is a lone genius with a flash of inspiration who builds something in a garage and gets rich. Almost every clause of that sentence is wrong, and this week dismantles it. The founder's actual job is to decide *what is worth building, for whom, and on what terms with which people* — and to survive the consequences of those decisions. In 2026, with SAFEs standardized, AI collapsing build costs, and distribution harder than ever, the founders who win are the ones who validate before they build. That discipline starts here.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Separate** the founder *myth* from the founder *job* — and name the daily decisions that actually fill a founder's first year.
- **Apply** the painkiller-vs-vitamin test to distinguish a problem worth solving from a solution you happen to like.
- **Name** the recurring failure modes of first-time founders (falling in love with the solution, building in secret, deferring the equity conversation, chasing vanity metrics, raising to avoid customers) and catch yourself in them.
- **Articulate** the control-vs-wealth tension and commit to a *default lean* in writing, knowing what each lean costs you in equity, fundraising, and hiring decisions later.
- **Assess** your own founder-market fit honestly across the five unfair advantages: lived experience, domain access, distribution, skill, and obsession.
- **Distinguish** a problem that is merely *interesting to you* from one you have *standing* in — answer "why you, why now?" without flinching.
- **Form** a defensible opinion on solo vs. co-founders, and state specifically what you would need a co-founder *for*.
- **Produce** a one-page founder brief that the next nine weeks will build on directly.

## Prerequisites

This course assumes you are a working adult or near-graduate who is seriously considering starting a company — not someone who needs convincing that startups exist. Specifically:

- You have at least a rough idea, an industry you know, or a problem you keep noticing. (If you have nothing yet, Week 2's idea-generation discipline will help — but bring *something* to react to.)
- You can write clearly in short prose. Every deliverable in this course is a written argument, not a slide of bullet points.
- You are willing to talk to strangers. The capstone requires ten real discovery interviews; this week you will line up the access for them.
- You are comfortable with Markdown and Git — every deliverable is committed to your own repo.
- You can be honest with yourself on paper. The exercises are worthless if you write what sounds good instead of what is true.

You do **not** need any prior startup experience, any technical background, or any money. You do not need a co-founder yet — deciding whether you want one is part of the work. You do not need an idea yet either; Week 2 is where idea generation happens. Week 1 is about *you*.

## Topics covered

- The founder myth vs. the founder job: what founders actually decide, week by week, across a company's first year.
- Why inspiration is overrated and disciplined discovery is underrated — the reliable problem-first order of operations.
- The painkiller / vitamin test, and why most first ideas are vitamins dressed as painkillers.
- The five classic first-time-founder failure modes and how each one shows up in this course.
- The control-vs-wealth tension (the "Rich vs. King" trade): what each lean means concretely for equity, fundraising, board seats, and hiring.
- Founder-market fit and the five unfair advantages: lived experience, domain access, distribution, skill, obsession.
- Standing: "interesting to me" vs. "mine to solve," and the "why you, why now?" test.
- Solo founder vs. co-founders — the first irreversible fork, and how to reason about it before Week 4's equity mechanics.
- How the founder brief seeds the whole capstone, and how this week's decisions reappear in Weeks 4 (equity), 8 (fundraising), and 10 (pitch).

## In-class activities (or solo equivalents)

If you are running this in a cohort, do these out loud. If you are solo, do them on paper — the point is to force a position, not to perform.

- **The control-vs-wealth poll.** Everyone places themselves on a line from "I want to be in charge" to "I want to be rich" and defends the position for 60 seconds. Most people lie to themselves on the first pass and correct on the second. (Solo: record yourself answering, then watch it back.)
- **Standing speed-round.** Each person names a problem space; the group asks exactly one question — "Why you?" Spaces with no good answer get parked, not killed.
- **Myth-busting.** List every founder cliché you have absorbed from movies and headlines, then strike the ones that don't survive scrutiny.

## The "evidence over enthusiasm" promise

C25 has one recurring marker, and it is the opposite of a build-status line. Every deliverable in this course ends with a confidence statement:

```
Claim: ____________________________________
Evidence behind it: (a) lived it, (b) talked to N people, (c) read it, (d) assumed it
Confidence: low / medium / high — and why
```

If your confidence is "high" and your evidence is "(d) assumed it," you are not done — you are exactly the founder this course exists to save. The point of Week 1 is to make that confidence statement a reflex, starting with your own brief: the strongest line in a founder brief is one you can back with "(a) lived it," and the weakest is one you can only back with "(d) assumed it."

## Worked example

Consider two would-be founders looking at the same space — scheduling for small dental practices.

- **Founder A** thinks the idea is "cool" and likes building calendars. No dental access, no distribution, no lived pain. Standing: weak. Founder-market fit: low. The honest read is "interesting to me," not "mine to solve." When the discovery interviews of Week 3 get hard to schedule — because Founder A knows no dentists — the project stalls and gets rationalized away.
- **Founder B** spent four years as an office manager at a dental practice, still has fifteen warm contacts in the industry, and personally lost hours every week to the scheduling mess. Standing: strong. She also admits, when pushed, that she would rather *own the decisions* than maximize a future exit — a control lean. That lean tells her something concrete about how she will later split equity (Week 4) and whether she wants investors (Week 8).

Same idea, completely different odds — because founder-market fit and standing are about the *person*, not the idea. Week 1 is about being Founder B on purpose.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Read Paul Graham's **"How to Get Startup Ideas"** and **"Do Things That Don't Scale"** back to back: <https://paulgraham.com/articles.html>. Note where they contradict your instincts.
- Read the introduction to Noam Wasserman's **"The Founder's Dilemmas"** — the source of the rich-vs-king research the control-vs-wealth lecture is built on.
- Listen to one episode of **Y Combinator's Startup School** or a **Lenny's Podcast** founder-fit episode and write a one-paragraph reaction.
- Draft your "why you, why now?" sentence five different ways and pick the one that is *most* specific, not most impressive.
- Find three companies that solved a problem you have standing in. For each, write one sentence on why you would have been a better or worse founder for it than the people who actually did it.
