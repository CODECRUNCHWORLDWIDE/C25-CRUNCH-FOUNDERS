# Week 1 — Founder Mindset & Founder-Market Fit

Welcome to **C25 · Crunch Founders**, a free, open-source ten-week founder track. Week 1 is the cheap week — no interviews to schedule yet, no MVP to build, no equity to split. It is the week you change how you see the job itself.

If you have never started a company, you probably picture the founder's job like this: "a visionary has a brilliant idea, raises money, builds the product, and gets rich." That is wrong on almost every clause. The founder's job is mostly *deciding what is worth building and for whom*, and *surviving the people decisions* around it — co-founders, early hires, investors. The idea is the cheap part. By the end of the week you will hold a more useful model, and you will have confronted the first uncomfortable question the course will ask repeatedly: do you want to be **rich** or do you want to be **in charge**? You usually cannot maximize both, and pretending otherwise is how early founders sleepwalk into avoidable pain.

The deliverable for the week is a single one-page founder brief — your unfair advantages, your control-vs-wealth lean, and a problem space you have genuine standing in. You will reference it every week after this one.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Describe** the founder's actual job and contrast it with the "visionary genius" myth.
- **Explain** the **control-vs-wealth tension** and state your own honest lean, with reasons.
- **Assess founder-market fit** for yourself against a problem space: do you have unfair advantages — domain access, distribution, lived experience, or skill?
- **Distinguish** a problem you have *standing* in from one you merely find interesting.
- **Name** the most common early-founder self-deceptions (falling in love with the solution, building before talking, hiding from the hard conversations) and where you are most at risk.
- **Decide** whether you are looking for co-founders, and what you would need from them — a question Week 4 will make concrete.

## Prerequisites

- **No business background required.**
- **Willingness to talk to strangers** — the course gets interview-heavy by Week 3.
- **Comfortable with Markdown and Git** — you will commit a brief this week.

You do not need an idea yet. Week 2 is where idea generation happens. Week 1 is about *you*.

## In-class activities (or solo equivalents)

- **The control-vs-wealth poll.** Everyone places themselves on a line from "I want to be in charge" to "I want to be rich" and defends the position out loud for 60 seconds. Most people lie to themselves first and correct on the second pass.
- **Standing speed-round.** Each person names a problem space and the group asks one question: "Why you?" Spaces with no good answer get parked.
- **Myth-busting.** As a group, list every founder cliché you have absorbed from movies and headlines, then strike the ones that don't survive scrutiny.

## Worked example

Consider two would-be founders looking at the same space — scheduling for small dental practices.

- **Founder A** thinks the idea is "cool" and likes building calendars. No dental access, no distribution, no lived pain. Standing: weak. Founder-market fit: low. The honest read is "interesting to me," not "mine to solve."
- **Founder B** spent four years as an office manager at a dental practice, still has fifteen warm contacts in the industry, and personally lost hours every week to the scheduling mess. Standing: strong. She also admits, when pushed, that she would rather *own the decisions* than maximize a future exit — a control lean. That lean tells her something about how she will later split equity and whether she wants investors.

Same idea, completely different odds — because founder-market fit and standing are about the *person*, not the idea. Week 1 is about being Founder B on purpose.
