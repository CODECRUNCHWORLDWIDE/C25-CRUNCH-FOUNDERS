# Week 1 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before moving to Week 2. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** According to Lecture 1, what is the founder's *real* job?

- A) Having a brilliant, unique idea no one has thought of.
- B) Deciding what is worth building and for whom, and surviving the people decisions around it.
- C) Raising as much money as possible, as early as possible.
- D) Building the product alone in secret until it is perfect.

---

**Q2.** The **control-vs-wealth** ("Rich vs. King") tension says that:

- A) Founders should always choose wealth, because money buys control later.
- B) You can fully maximize both control and wealth at once if you work hard enough.
- C) Many founder decisions trade control against wealth, and the mistake is refusing to decide your default lean.
- D) Control only matters for founders who refuse outside investment.

---

**Q3.** Which of the following best describes a **painkiller** problem (vs. a vitamin)?

- A) A mild, occasional annoyance that is easy to ignore.
- B) A problem the founder finds intellectually interesting.
- C) An urgent, recurring problem that already costs people time, money, or pain — they're already hacking workarounds.
- D) Any problem that involves new technology.

---

**Q4.** When testing whether a problem is a painkiller, which signal is the **strongest**?

- A) The founder is excited about it.
- B) The market is large according to an industry report.
- C) People already build their own workaround (a spreadsheet, a manual process, a hated competitor product).
- D) The technology to solve it has just become available.

---

**Q5.** A founder claims strong **founder-market fit**. Which is the *weakest* supporting evidence?

- A) They personally had the problem for years.
- B) They can reach a hundred potential customers this week through existing relationships.
- C) They read an article about the space and found it fascinating.
- D) They have an audience or distribution channel in that market.

---

**Q6.** What does it mean to have **standing** in a problem space?

- A) You were the first person ever to notice the problem.
- B) You have a credible answer to "why you, and why now" — usually lived experience, access, or distribution.
- C) You have already raised money to work on it.
- D) You find the problem more interesting than other problems.

---

**Q7.** Of the five unfair advantages, which one does Lecture 2 single out as the **rarest and most valuable in 2026**, specifically because AI made building cheap?

- A) Skill (the ability to build the product).
- B) Lived experience.
- C) Distribution (an existing audience, network, or channel).
- D) Obsession.

---

**Q8.** A founder writes "I want both control and wealth" on their brief. According to Lecture 2, the most useful next step is to:

- A) Accept that they can have both and move on.
- B) Identify which one they'd protect when *forced* to choose only one — that's their real lean.
- C) Choose wealth, since it's the more common founder goal.
- D) Skip the question; it only matters at fundraising time.

---

**Q9.** Which of these is a **kill criterion** as defined in the pre-mortem challenge, rather than a vague worry?

- A) "I'm worried we won't get enough traction."
- B) "If the market turns, we might struggle."
- C) "If fewer than 3 of 10 discovery interviewees describe a current paid workaround by end of Week 3, I will pivot the problem."
- D) "Competitors could copy us eventually."

---

**Q10.** You're deciding whether to bring on a 50/50 co-founder who fills a real gap. Lecture 2 frames this primarily as:

- A) A purely financial decision about dividing future profit.
- B) One of the biggest **control** decisions you'll make — trading half the decisions to raise the odds of success.
- C) A formality that can always be renegotiated later.
- D) Irrelevant until you've raised a round.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — The idea is the cheap part; the job is deciding what to build, for whom, and surviving the people decisions. (Lecture 1, §1–2)
2. **C** — The two often pull apart; the error is drifting without deciding a default lean. Wasserman's data even shows giving up control often produced *more* wealth. (Lecture 2, §1)
3. **C** — Painkillers are urgent, recurring, and already costly; people pay to make pain stop. (Lecture 1, §4)
4. **C** — The existing-workaround signal is the most reliable: it proves the pain is real enough that people spend time or money on it *without your product existing*. Excitement (A) is not evidence; a big market on paper (B) doesn't prove urgency. (Lecture 1, §4)
5. **C** — Interest is not an advantage; thousands find any space interesting. Lived experience, access, and distribution are the real advantages. (Lecture 2, §2)
6. **B** — Standing is the honest answer to "why you, why now," grounded in experience, access, or distribution — not mere interest, and not being first. (Lecture 2, §3)
7. **C** — In 2026, with building commoditized by AI, distribution (the ability to *reach* customers) is the rarest edge. Skill (A) is increasingly table stakes. (Lecture 2, §2)
8. **B** — "Both" isn't a lean. The real lean is whichever you'd protect under a forced choice; naming it makes every later decision more honest. (Lecture 2, §1)
9. **C** — A kill criterion is measurable, time-boxed, and pre-committed with a response. A, B, and D are vague worries with no threshold and no decision attached. (Challenge 1 / Lecture 1 §6)
10. **B** — A 50/50 co-founder is one of the largest control surrenders a founder makes; it's the control-vs-wealth trade in concrete form, which is exactly why Exercise 2's Scenario 1 uses it. (Lecture 2, §5)

</details>
