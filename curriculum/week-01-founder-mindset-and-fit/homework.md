# Week 1 Homework

Six practice problems that revisit the week's topics and feed your mini-project. The full set takes about **6 hours**. Work in your Week 1 repository so each problem produces at least one commit you can point to later.

Each problem includes a **problem statement**, **acceptance criteria**, a **hint**, and an **estimated time**. A consolidated **grading rubric** is at the bottom — read it before you start so you know what "great" looks like.

---

## Problem 1 — Founder teardown

**Problem statement.** Pick one founder you can read freely about online (a free public startup talk, a freely available founder essay, a podcast interview, or a Wikipedia biography). Write a teardown at `notes/founder-teardown.md`:

- Who they are and what they built (two sentences).
- Their apparent **founder-market fit** — which of the five unfair advantages did they have, with the evidence you inferred each from?
- Their apparent **control-vs-wealth lean**, and the specific decision(s) you inferred it from (did they raise heavily? stay CEO? sell early?).
- One decision they made that you would make differently, and why.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/founder-teardown.md`, all four bullets present.
- Founder-market fit is mapped to the *five named advantages*, not described vaguely.
- The control-vs-wealth read cites at least one concrete decision as evidence.
- The source is free and the link works. Committed.

**Hint.** Founder-market fit is easiest to read in founders who built for an industry they came from. Their control-vs-wealth lean is easiest to infer from how much they raised and whether they're still CEO.

**Estimated time.** 50 minutes.

## Problem 2 — Painkiller vs. vitamin sort

**Problem statement.** List eight problems you've personally felt in the last month — anything, work or life. At `notes/painkiller-sort.md`, sort each into **painkiller** or **vitamin** and justify in one sentence using the three tests from Lecture 1 §4: frequency, intensity, and *existing workaround*.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/painkiller-sort.md`, eight problems, each labeled and justified.
- Each justification references at least one of the three tests (frequency / intensity / workaround).
- At least two are honestly labeled vitamins (most things are).
- For the strongest painkiller, name the workaround people currently use. Committed.

**Hint.** Be ruthless. "It would be nice if…" is a vitamin. "I lose two hours every week to… and I've built a spreadsheet to cope" is a painkiller with a workaround signal.

**Estimated time.** 40 minutes.

## Problem 3 — The standing test

**Problem statement.** Take the problem space from your founder-market-fit canvas (Exercise 3). At `notes/standing-test.md`, answer "why me, why now" in detail, then list **three specific people** you could interview about it in the next two weeks (first names or roles only — no private info), and for each, note your *relationship* to them (warm intro, former colleague, cold).

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/standing-test.md`.
- A detailed "why me, why now" (a paragraph, not one line) that addresses both halves.
- Three reachable interview targets named by role, each with your relationship noted.
- An honest note if you *cannot* name three — that's a finding about your standing, not a failure of the homework. Committed.

**Hint.** If you can't name three reachable people, your standing in this space is weaker than it felt — and catching that now is exactly what Week 1 is for. Consider re-running Exercise 3 with a space where you *can*.

**Estimated time.** 40 minutes.

## Problem 4 — The "why now?" investigation

**Problem statement.** For your chosen space, investigate the **"why now?"** half of standing — the part founders most often skip. At `notes/why-now.md`, identify and briefly support (with a link or a fact each) **three** things that have changed recently enough to make *this* the moment: a technology shift, a cost that dropped, a regulation, a behavior change, a new platform, etc. Then state whether "why now?" points to *this is the moment*, *too early*, or *too late*.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/why-now.md`, three concrete changes, each with a one-line support (link or fact).
- A clear verdict: this-is-the-moment / too-early / too-late, with one sentence of reasoning.
- At least one change is recent (within ~2 years). Committed.

**Hint.** "AI got cheaper" is too generic — get specific about *what* it now makes possible *for your customer* that wasn't feasible 18 months ago. "Too early" and "too late" are legitimate, valuable verdicts; don't force "this is the moment."

**Estimated time.** 50 minutes.

## Problem 5 — Two kill criteria, pre-committed

**Problem statement.** Write **two** kill/pivot criteria for your idea at `notes/kill-criteria.md`, using the format from Challenge 1 (risk / I'll know by / threshold / response). At least one must be testable in Week 3's discovery interviews. These will fold into Section 7 of your Founder Operating Document.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/kill-criteria.md`, two criteria, each with all four parts.
- Each threshold is measurable (a number or clear binary), not a feeling.
- Each has a date/milestone and a pre-written response (kill / pivot / persevere-but-change).
- At least one is testable by end of Week 3. Committed.

**Hint.** A measurable threshold names a count or a fraction: "fewer than 4 of 10 interviewees…", "zero of my three warm contacts will introduce me to a buyer…". If you can't put a number on it, it's a worry, not a criterion.

**Estimated time.** 40 minutes.

## Problem 6 — Week 1 reflection

**Problem statement.** Write a 300–400 word reflection at `notes/week-01-reflection.md`:

1. Which idea from Lecture 1 or 2 most changed how you see the founder's job, and why?
2. Did your control-vs-wealth lean survive Exercise 2's four scenarios, or did it bend? Where?
3. Did your founder-market-fit canvas (Exercise 3) confirm the space you started with, or did the scoring push you somewhere else?
4. One belief about startups you held on Monday that you now doubt.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- File `notes/week-01-reflection.md`, 300–400 words, each question in its own paragraph.
- Question 2 and 3 reference your actual exercise results, not generic answers. Committed.

**Hint.** This one is for *you*, not a grader. Be honest — future-you will reread it after Week 10 and want the truth, not the version that sounded good.

**Estimated time.** 30 minutes.

## Grading rubric

Each problem is worth the weight below. Across all six, graders look for the same three things the whole course rewards: **specificity, honesty, and evidence**.

| Problem | Weight | "Meets" | "Exceeds" |
|---------|-------:|---------|-----------|
| 1 — Founder teardown | 15% | All four bullets, FMF mapped to the five advantages | Control-vs-wealth read is sharp and cites multiple concrete decisions |
| 2 — Painkiller sort | 15% | Eight problems sorted with the three tests; ≥2 honest vitamins | Workaround named for the top painkiller; ruthless, non-flattering sorting |
| 3 — Standing test | 20% | "Why me, why now" paragraph + three reachable people, or an honest "I can't" | Relationships noted; the gap (if any) is turned into a concrete next action |
| 4 — Why now | 15% | Three changes with support; a verdict | At least one genuinely recent, customer-specific shift; verdict well-argued |
| 5 — Kill criteria | 20% | Two criteria, measurable, time-boxed, pre-committed | Thresholds are sharp and one is wired directly into Week 3's interview plan |
| 6 — Reflection | 15% | 300–400 words, four questions, references real exercise results | Honest, specific, names a belief that actually changed |

**Automatic deductions** (any problem): use of banned words (visionary / disruptor / rockstar / 10x / passionate); claims marked "high confidence" with only "(d) assumed it" evidence; a missing commit.

When you've finished all six, push your repo and finalize the [mini-project](./mini-project/README.md) — your Founder Operating Document.
