# Lecture 2 — Control vs. Wealth and Founder-Market Fit

> **Duration:** ~45 minutes of reading.
> **Outcome:** You can explain the control-vs-wealth tension, assess your own founder-market fit honestly, and tell a problem you have standing in from one you merely like.

---

## 1. The trade you cannot escape: control vs. wealth

A widely observed pattern in early-stage companies is that founders implicitly chase one of two things, and the two often pull in opposite directions:

- **Wealth** — maximizing the financial outcome. Usually this means raising money, bringing in experienced executives, giving up board seats, and accepting that you may not run the company by the end.
- **Control** — staying the decision-maker. Usually this means raising less (or nothing), owning more, hiring slower, and accepting a likely smaller financial outcome.

You can sometimes get a bit of both, but most decisions a founder makes — whether to raise, how much equity to give a co-founder, whether to take a board seat in exchange for capital — trade one against the other. The mistake is not choosing; it is *pretending you don't have to choose* and then being surprised when a funding round dilutes your control or a bootstrapped pace caps your upside.

> **Decide your default lean now, in writing.** It will not be permanent, but it makes every later decision (equity, fundraising, board structure) faster and more honest.

```
        CONTROL  ←─────────────────────────────────────→  WEALTH
        (be in charge)                                    (be rich)
        raise less / nothing                              raise more
        own more, slower growth                           dilute, faster growth
        you stay CEO                                      best operator runs it
```

Neither end is "right." The error is drifting without deciding.

## 2. Founder-market fit

Founder-market fit asks: *are you, specifically, well-suited to this problem and market?* It is the personal analogue of product-market fit, and it is checkable before you build anything. Strong founder-market fit usually comes from one or more **unfair advantages**:

- **Lived experience** — you have personally had the problem, repeatedly.
- **Domain access** — you can reach the customers cheaply (you worked in the industry, you have the relationships).
- **Distribution** — you already have an audience, a network, or a channel.
- **Skill** — you can build or sell the solution unusually well.
- **Obsession** — you will still care about this problem in three years, when it is no longer novel.

A founder with two or three of these in a space has real fit. A founder with none has a hobby.

## 3. Standing: "interesting to me" vs. "mine to solve"

There is a difference between a problem that is *interesting to you* and a problem you have *standing* in. Standing is the honest answer to one question:

> **"Why you, and why now?"**

If the answer is "I read an article and it seemed cool," you have interest, not standing. If the answer is "I have lived this problem for years and can reach a hundred people who share it by the end of the week," you have standing. Standing predicts whether your discovery interviews (Week 3) will even be possible, and whether you will outlast the boredom.

## 4. Solo founder vs. co-founders — the first fork

You do not have to decide finally this week, but you should form an opinion:

- **Solo** keeps control simple and equity uncomplicated, but the workload and the loneliness are real, and many investors prefer teams.
- **Co-founders** add skills, accountability, and resilience — but introduce the hardest decisions in the course: roles, decision rights, and the equity split. Week 4 is dedicated to getting that right *before* it becomes irreversible.

The relevant question for the founder brief is: *if I want co-founders, what specifically do I need from them that I do not have myself?* Vague answers ("someone technical") are a warning sign; specific answers ("someone who has sold into hospitals") are a head start.

## 5. Putting it together

Your founder brief (Exercise 1) forces three honest answers:

1. **Unfair advantages** — what do you actually bring?
2. **Control-vs-wealth lean** — which do you default to, and why?
3. **A problem space you have standing in** — and the one-sentence "why you, why now."

Get these right and the next nine weeks have a spine. Get them wrong — pick a space you have no standing in, or refuse to decide your control lean — and you will feel the drift by Week 4.
