# Week 1 — Challenges

The exercises drill the basics — who you are, what you lean toward, where you have fit. **Challenges stretch you.** Each one takes 60–120 minutes and produces something you can defend in front of a skeptic and keep in your founder portfolio.

This week's challenge is a **realistic founder decision scenario**, which is the format every challenge in C25 uses. You won't write code; you'll make a judgment call under uncertainty and *defend the reasoning*, because that is the actual job. There is rarely a single correct answer — the assessment is on the *quality of your thinking*, not on matching a key.

## Index

1. **[Challenge 1 — The pre-mortem and your kill criteria](challenge-01-pre-mortem-and-kill-criteria.md)** — imagine your chosen idea has already failed, work backward to the causes, then commit *in advance* to the specific evidence that would make you kill or pivot it. (~90 min)

## How challenges are assessed

Challenges are optional. If you skip them, you can still pass the week. If you do them, you'll be measurably ahead of someone who didn't — and this particular challenge's output (pre-defined kill criteria) directly protects you in Week 3 (when the interviews come back ambiguous), Week 5 (persevere-or-pivot), and Week 8 (whether to raise into a weak signal).

Each challenge is graded on four things, weighted equally:

- **Honesty** — does the work confront the uncomfortable truth, or dodge it? A pre-mortem that concludes "we'll probably be fine" is a failed pre-mortem.
- **Specificity** — are the failure modes and kill criteria concrete and measurable, or vague? "Not enough traction" fails; "fewer than 3 of 10 interviewees describe an existing paid workaround" passes.
- **Decision quality** — given the scenario's constraints, is the reasoning sound and the trade-off named?
- **Defensibility** — could you defend this in front of a sharp investor or advisor without flinching?

A great challenge submission is one a stranger could read and immediately understand both *what you decided* and *why you'd be willing to kill your own idea on that evidence*. That willingness — to pre-commit to disconfirmation — is the rarest and most valuable founder trait there is.
