# Challenge 1 — The Pre-Mortem and Your Kill Criteria

**Time estimate:** ~90 minutes.

**Type:** Founder decision scenario. You'll run a pre-mortem on your own chosen idea, then pre-commit to the evidence that would make you kill or pivot it.

---

## The scenario

It is eighteen months from today. Your startup — built on the problem space you chose in Exercise 3 — has **failed**. It's dead. You shut it down last week. This is not a hypothetical risk; in this exercise it is a settled fact. You're now sitting with a mentor doing the post-mortem.

Your job in this challenge is to write that post-mortem *now*, before you've started — a **pre-mortem** — and then do the thing almost no founder does: commit, in writing, in advance, to the specific evidence that would tell you to stop *before* you've burned eighteen months finding out the hard way.

Why this matters: founders are systematically overconfident (they have to be a little, to start at all), and that overconfidence makes them ignore disconfirming evidence right up until the money runs out. The single most effective antidote is to decide your **kill criteria** while you're still clear-headed — before you're emotionally and financially committed, before sunk costs and a co-founder's hopes are pulling you to keep going. A founder with pre-defined kill criteria fails *fast and cheap*; a founder without them fails *slow and expensive*.

This is also a direct rehearsal of decisions you'll make for real later: Week 5's persevere-or-pivot call, and Week 8's "should I raise into this signal or not."

---

## Part 1 — The pre-mortem (~40 min)

Assume the failure. Now work backward and answer: **why did it die?** Generate at least **six** distinct, *specific* failure modes. Spread them across these categories (at least one from each of the first four):

1. **Problem failure** — the problem wasn't real, frequent, or painful enough. (Vitamin, not painkiller.)
2. **Customer failure** — you couldn't reach, convince, or retain the people who had it.
3. **Founder/standing failure** — *you specifically* were the wrong person; you lacked an advantage you assumed you had, or your obsession ran out.
4. **Model/distribution failure** — even with a happy user, the economics or the channel never worked.
5. **Team/control failure** *(if applicable)* — a co-founder split, an equity dispute, a control decision that backfired.
6. **External failure** — a "why now?" that turned out to be "why not yet" or "why too late."

For each failure mode, write **one or two sentences** that are concrete enough to be falsifiable. Compare:

- ❌ "We didn't get enough traction." (vague — useless)
- ✅ "Office managers liked the demo but every one of them said the dentist controls software purchases, and we never got in front of a single dentist — so the buyer and the user were different people and we only ever talked to the user." (specific — actionable)

Then **rank** your six failure modes by likelihood. The top two or three are your *real* risks — the ones your Week 3 discovery and Week 5 MVP must be designed to test first.

## Part 2 — Your kill criteria (~30 min)

Now the hard part. For your **top three** failure modes, define a **kill criterion**: a specific, measurable threshold of evidence that, if you hit it, means you stop or pivot. A real kill criterion has three properties:

- **Measurable** — a number or a clear binary, not a feeling.
- **Time-boxed** — by when you'll have the evidence.
- **Pre-committed** — you write down the *response* (kill / pivot / persevere) now, so future-you can't rationalize it away.

Template per criterion:

```
Risk: <the failure mode>
I will know by: <date / milestone, e.g. "after 10 discovery interviews in Week 3">
Kill/pivot threshold: <specific, measurable condition>
If I hit it, I will: <kill / pivot to ___ / keep going but change ___>
```

Worked example:

```
Risk: The problem is a vitamin, not a painkiller.
I will know by: end of Week 3, after 10 discovery interviews.
Kill/pivot threshold: fewer than 3 of 10 interviewees describe a current, costly workaround they
   already spend time or money on. (No workaround = no real pain.)
If I hit it: pivot the problem within the same space (re-interview around the pain that DID show up),
   not the solution. If two pivots both miss, kill it.
```

Write at least **three** kill criteria. At least one must be testable in Week 3 (discovery), so this challenge literally sets up your interview goals.

## Part 3 — The defense (~20 min)

Write a short closing section (150–250 words) defending your kill criteria against the objection every founder faces: *"Isn't this just planning to give up? Successful founders push through."*

Address it honestly. The strong answer distinguishes between **persevering through hardship** (good — execution is hard) and **persevering against evidence** (fatal — the market has spoken). Name where your specific kill criteria fall on that line, and why a founder who pre-commits to disconfirmation is *more* likely to succeed, not less, because they redeploy their life toward something that can work instead of grinding a dead idea.

## Stretch

- **Swap pre-mortems with a peer.** Read each other's and try to find a failure mode the author *missed* — especially in the founder/standing category, which people are blindest to about themselves.
- **Write the inverse: a "pre-parade."** Imagine it succeeded wildly. What had to go *right*? Compare the list to your pre-mortem — the items that appear, mirrored, on both lists are your true make-or-break variables, and those are exactly what to test first.
- **Pre-commit publicly.** Post your top kill criterion in the cohort channel. Public pre-commitment is dramatically harder to rationalize away later — that's the point.

---

## Why this matters

Pre-mortems and kill criteria are not pessimism — they are the discipline that lets founders take *bigger* swings safely, because they've capped the downside in time and money. Three places this exact output pays off:

- **Week 3 (discovery):** your kill criteria become your interview objectives — you'll know exactly what evidence you're hunting for instead of fishing for compliments.
- **Week 5 (build-measure-learn):** the persevere-or-pivot decision is brutal without pre-set thresholds; with them, it's almost mechanical.
- **Week 8 (fundraising):** investors respect a founder who can articulate what would make them *stop*. It signals intellectual honesty, which is the trait that predicts good decisions under pressure.

The founders who flame out spectacularly are usually the ones who never wrote down what failure would look like, so they couldn't recognize it while it was happening. Don't be that founder. Decide now, while it's cheap.
