# Week 10 — Challenges

One challenge this week, and it is the whole point of the course: deliver the pitch live at demo day and survive the Q&A. No solution is provided — only acceptance criteria, because in a real room nobody hands you the answer key. The challenge subsumes the mini-project's assembly work and adds the *live delivery* bar: you present your three minutes to a room (or a recorded camera standing in for one), and then you defend it against the questions a seed investor actually asks.

## How it's assessed

This challenge maps to two lines of the capstone rubric: **the deck + 3-minute recording land the structure (20%)** and **the pitch survives the three most obvious hard questions (10%)**. The single hardest gate is the live Q&A — a polished pitch followed by a fumbled "how do you know this is real?" loses the room you just won. You are graded on:

- **Delivery under the clock.** You land at or under 3:00, open with the pain (not the product or "hi, I'm excited to be here"), and end on a single specific ask. Over-time pitches are cut off mid-sentence, exactly as at a real demo day — the clock is the lesson.
- **Evidence-first structure.** The six beats in order, evidence before solution, every claim traceable to an artifact in your repo. A claim a number can't back is a deduction.
- **Surviving the hard questions.** You field "is the problem real," "why you / why now," "can this make money / how big," and ideally name your own riskiest assumption before being asked. Answers are scored on evidence and posture: acknowledge, answer with a number, own the weakness, connect to the next experiment. Defensiveness, bluffing, and arguing with the question all lose points.
- **Turning pushback into the repo.** The sharpest questions the room asks become the "open questions / next experiments" section of your capstone README — a repo that names what's still unproven is *more* credible, not less.

## What "open-ended" means here

There is no single right pitch. Within the structure, you make and defend choices: the fundraising shape vs. the evidence shape (Lecture 1, §1.9d), which traction number to lead with, which risk to name first, how you size the market. The challenge is not to match a reference pitch; it is to present *your* evidence so clearly that the right person in the room wants the next conversation — and to defend the choices you made when the room pushes. That defense, and the self-named risk, is where you demonstrate you understand your own concept, which is the entire point of the course.

This is the headline portfolio moment. Treat it like a launch, not an assignment.
