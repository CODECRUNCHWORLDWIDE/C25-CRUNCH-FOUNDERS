# Challenge 1 — Deliver the Pitch Live at Demo Day, and Survive the Q&A

> **Estimated time:** the demo-day slot itself (3 minutes of pitch + ~5 minutes of questions), on top of the rehearsal that earns it. **This is the assessed capstone delivery.** No solution is provided — only acceptance criteria, because in a real room nobody hands you the answer key.

You will deliver your **3-minute pitch** — the problem → evidence → solution → model → traction → ask argument you have been compounding since Week 01 — **live, in front of a room**, and then defend it against the questions a seed investor actually asks. "Live" is the operative word. The room does not read your deck and award points. You present, the clock runs, you get cut off if you run over, and then the room throws the hard questions at you and watches how you answer. If the pitch is polished but the Q&A collapses, the delivery does not pass — because the Q&A is where the room decides whether your three minutes were evidence or performance.

This challenge is harder than the mini-project assembly in two specific ways: (1) it must survive a **live, unscripted Q&A** — the three obvious hard questions, asked in whatever order and wording the room chooses — and (2) it must land **under the clock**, opening with the pain and ending on a specific ask, with no over-run.

## The pitch

The full six-beat structure from the lectures, delivered live (Lecture 1, §1.2):

- **Problem (~20s):** who hurts, how badly, one real person made vivid.
- **Evidence (~40s):** the count of interviews, the pattern, what people pay today. Your credibility beat.
- **Solution (~30s):** what you built, the mechanism, optionally a 10-second prototype clip (with a static-screenshot fallback staged in case it won't play).
- **Model (~30s):** price, one unit-economics headline ratio, payback.
- **Traction (~30s):** the strongest *real* number and the honest leak.
- **Ask (~20s):** the single specific request, matched to this room, the last thing on screen.

You deliver the pitch shape that matches your actual situation — the **fundraising pitch** (money ask, leans on the model) or the **evidence pitch** (next-experiment ask, leans on what you've validated). Most of you, this cycle, give the evidence pitch, and saying "I'm not raising yet — here's what I've proven and the next thing I'm testing" reads as *more* disciplined, not less.

## Acceptance criteria (the room checks every one of these)

### Live delivery

- [ ] **The pitch lands at or under 3:00.** You rehearsed to 2:50; you do not get cut off before the ask. Running long reads as "couldn't prioritize," not "thorough."
- [ ] **You open with the pain, not the product.** The first 20 seconds make one person's problem vivid — not "hi, I'm excited," not a logo animation, not "we built an app that…".
- [ ] **Evidence comes before solution.** The room feels the problem and sees the proof before you reveal what you built.
- [ ] **You end on a single specific ask**, matched to this room, held on screen.

### Evidence integrity

- [ ] **Every claim traces to an artifact in your repo.** "Customers love it" has a discovery dossier behind it; "the economics work" has a unit-economics sheet. A claim a number can't back is a deduction.
- [ ] **No vanity traction.** You lead with the strongest *real* number (paying customers > trials > signups > followers), not the biggest-sounding one.
- [ ] **The pivot, if you had one, is told honestly** — "the interviews changed my mind, here's how" — not hidden for a cleaner story.

### Surviving the hard questions

- [ ] **You field the three obvious hard questions** — "how do you know this is real," "why you / why now," "can this make money / how big" — each answered with evidence (a count, a ratio, a bottom-up market size), not adjectives.
- [ ] **You name your own riskiest assumption** calmly, ideally before being asked, with the test you're running. (Insisting there's no big risk is a fail.)
- [ ] **You answer without defensiveness:** acknowledge, answer with evidence, own the weakness, say "I don't know yet, here's how I'd find out" when that's the truth. You never argue with the question and never bluff a number.

### Delivery artifacts (in the repo, linked from the top README)

- [ ] **The 8–10 slide deck** (PDF), one idea per slide, every adjective a number can't back cut.
- [ ] **The 3-minute recorded pitch** (link), edited to time, audio clean, ending on the ask.
- [ ] **The hard-questions canvas** (Exercise 3), the evidence-based answers you rehearsed.
- [ ] **The "open questions / next experiments" section** of the README, populated from the room's sharpest questions after you present.

## How you're graded

This challenge maps to the **deck + 3-minute recording (20%)** and **survives the three hard questions (10%)** lines of the capstone rubric. The single hardest gate is the live Q&A: a concept you can only defend when you're in the room narrating it is not a validated concept; it's a story you're attached to. Build the evidence trail so the repo defends itself, and rehearse the four hard-question answers (Exercise 3) until they're automatic — because under the lights you fall to the level of your preparation.

The deductions mirror the production-shop non-negotiables: a slide whose claim your repo can't back, a vanity metric led with instead of real traction, a bluffed number that collapses on the follow-up, or an over-run that gets you cut off before the ask. None of these is fatal alone, but each is the kind of thing a real investor remembers, so the challenge treats them as real.

## What "open-ended" means here

There is no single right pitch. Within the spec, you make and defend choices: the fundraising vs. evidence shape, which traction number leads, which risk you name first, how you size the market, whether you show a prototype clip. The challenge is not to match a reference; it is to make defensible choices, present your evidence so clearly the right person leans in, and explain the tradeoffs when the room pushes. The self-named risk and the honest pivot are where you demonstrate you understand your own concept — which is, in the end, the entire point of C25.

## After you present

Turn the room's pushback into your repo. The sharpest questions become the "open questions / next experiments" section of your capstone README — in priority order, each with how you'd test it. Reviewers read that section first, because it's where they learn whether you can see your own company clearly. A repo that says "here are the three things I still need to prove" is dramatically more credible than one that pretends the concept is finished. That honesty — not this one idea — is what you leave the course with.
