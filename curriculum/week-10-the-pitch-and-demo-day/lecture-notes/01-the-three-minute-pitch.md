# Lecture 1 — The Three-Minute Pitch

> **Reading time:** ~75 minutes. **Hands-on time:** ~45 minutes (you draft your six-beat outline and storyboard the deck).

> **An early-stage pitch is evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo.**

This is the lecture that turns "I have an idea I believe in" into "I can present evidence a stranger believes." You have spent nine weeks accumulating proof — ten discovery interviews, an MVP result, a canvas, unit economics, a traction metric. This week you assemble it and stand in front of people who will decide, in three minutes, whether your concept is real or whether you are a founder in love with a story. That moment has a name — the **pitch** — and at a real demo day it is the gate between your evidence and the next conversation (an intro, a pilot, a check). If you have never given one, you imagine it as a performance: charisma, a big vision, a confident voice. It is not. A good early-stage pitch is a *structured argument from evidence*, with a known shape, a known clock, and a known set of objections. By the end of this lecture you will know the six-beat structure, the timing budget for each beat, the 8–10 slide deck that carries it, and how the room actually works — so the three minutes you get are spent making your strongest case instead of warming up.

## 1.1 — What a pitch is actually for

A pitch exists to do one thing: **convert the evidence you already have into the next conversation.** That is the whole job. Everything in the format serves that goal.

It is **not** a sales close — nobody writes a check at the end of a three-minute pitch. It is **not** a product demo — the audience does not care about your feature list. It is **not** a vision monologue — "we're going to change how the world does X" earns nothing without proof. A pitch is the *trailer*, not the movie. Its job is to make the right person in the room lean over afterward and say "tell me more about the part where two clinics are already paying you." The deliverable of a pitch is not applause; it is a follow-up. A pitch that ends with claps and no conversations failed, no matter how smooth it was.

There are three audiences you will pitch to, and the demo-day pitch is built to serve all three at once:

1. **The investor** (an angel, a pre-seed fund, an accelerator). They are pattern-matching for *risk that's been removed*. Every interview you ran, every paying customer, every honest pivot is a risk you took off their table. They are not buying the idea; they are buying the evidence that you can find truth faster than your competitors.
2. **The customer / partner.** Sometimes the most valuable person in the room is a future pilot customer or a channel partner. They are listening for "is this for someone like me, and is it real?"
3. **The future teammate or co-founder.** Strong people join evidence and momentum, not slideware. A pitch that shows real traction recruits.

Your demo-day pitch is an investor-style pitch with the customer and recruit reading over the investor's shoulder. That is deliberate, and it is exactly the shape a hiring panel, an accelerator interview, or a first angel meeting uses when they put you on the spot and say "so, what are you building and why should I care?"

## 1.2 — The structure: six beats, three minutes

A pitch that wanders is a pitch that runs out of clock before it makes its case. Here is the reliable shape and the timing budget — internalize it until you can run it in your sleep:

```
Problem    (~20s)  Who hurts, and how badly. One sentence, one person.
Evidence   (~40s)  Proof you talked to them: how many, what you learned, what they pay today.
Solution   (~30s)  What you built; a short demo/prototype clip if you have one.
Model      (~30s)  How it makes money; the unit-economics headline (one ratio, one number).
Traction   (~30s)  What is real so far. Paying customers > trials > signups > followers.
Ask        (~20s)  The single specific thing you want from this room.
```

That sums to ~170 seconds, which leaves a ~10-second buffer under the 3:00 cap — and you will need it, because live pitches always run slightly long. Three minutes is short *on purpose*. It is not a cruelty; it is a forcing function. You cannot hide a weak problem behind a long product tour when you have 20 seconds for the problem. The clock makes you lead with what matters and cut everything else, which is exactly the discipline a founder needs when a partner gives them five minutes at the end of a long day.

Notice the order. **Problem and evidence come first, before the solution.** This is the single most common thing beginners get backwards. They open with "we built an app that…" — and the room has no reason to care yet, because they don't know whose pain it solves. Open with the pain and the proof, and by the time you reveal the solution the audience is already nodding.

## 1.3 — Beat by beat, with the clinic example

Let's walk each beat using the running example — a founder building waitlist auto-fill for small clinics — so the abstract structure gets concrete.

### Problem (~20s)

State who hurts and how badly, in one sentence, ideally about one real person. **Weak:** "Healthcare scheduling is broken." (Too big, too vague, no one to picture.) **Strong:** "A two-doctor clinic loses 8–12 appointment slots a week to no-shows, and every empty slot is revenue that never comes back." Now the room can picture the clinic and feel the bleed. The problem beat is not where you describe a market; it is where you make one person's pain vivid.

### Evidence (~40s)

This is the beat that separates a validated concept from a hopeful one, and it gets the most seconds for a reason. Lead with the count and the pattern: "I interviewed 12 clinic managers. Every one of them already cobbles together two tools — a reminder service and a spreadsheet — to fight no-shows, and they still lose slots. They told me the painful part isn't the missed appointment; it's the *empty slot they can't fill in time*." The evidence beat earns more credibility in 40 seconds than any amount of "huge market" rhetoric, because it proves you did the work this course is about: you got out of the building and found truth.

### Solution (~30s)

*Now* you reveal what you built — and only now, because the audience is primed. "So instead of yet another reminder tool, I built waitlist auto-fill: when a patient cancels, the system instantly offers the slot to the next person on a waitlist and books whoever says yes first." If you have a clickable prototype or a 10-second screen clip, this is where it goes. Keep it to the *mechanism*, not a feature tour — one sentence of how it works, one visual.

### Model (~30s)

How it makes money, as one headline, not a spreadsheet. "It's \$99 a month per clinic. My early unit economics are roughly 6-to-1 lifetime-value to acquisition cost, with about a three-month payback." One price, one ratio, one payback number. The room does not want your full P&L; they want to know you understand whether scaling this *helps or hurts* — which is the entire point of the unit-economics work from Week 06.

### Traction (~30s)

What is *real* so far, and the honest version of it. "Two clinics are paying today, both closed through founder-led sales. The honest leak I found: of the clinics that took a demo, only about a third started a trial, and I think the onboarding is the problem — that's the thing I'm fixing next." Naming a real number *and* a real leak is far more credible than "we're seeing incredible demand." Paying customers beat trials beat signups beat followers — cite the strongest real evidence you have, not the biggest-sounding number.

### Ask (~20s)

The single most-wasted sentence in early pitches, and the easiest to fix. End with one specific thing matched to *this* audience: "What I need right now is three warm introductions to multi-location clinic groups — that's the channel I want to test next." Not "we're looking for support" (means nothing), not a grab-bag of five asks (the room remembers none). One ask, specific, the last thing they hear.

## 1.4 — Lead with the problem and the evidence

It is worth dwelling on *why* evidence-first wins, because it is the whole posture of this course condensed into a pitch.

A seed investor's job is to find founders who remove risk faster than the market. Every claim you make is a risk from their perspective: *is the problem real? do customers want this? can it make money? can this person execute?* You de-risk each one with evidence, not adjectives. "We interviewed 12 clinics; they already pay for two clumsy tools; here is the cost per no-show" removes the "is the problem real" risk in one sentence — it is *proof*, and proof is the only thing that moves a skeptical room. "Healthcare is a massive market and we're revolutionizing scheduling" removes nothing; it is the founder asserting their own conclusion.

The practical rule: **every claim on a slide must trace to an artifact in your repo.** If you say "customers are desperate for this," there must be a discovery dossier behind it. If you say "the economics work," there must be a unit-economics sheet. The pitch is not where you generate belief; it is where you *present the belief you already earned through evidence*. A founder who has done the work does not need adjectives — the numbers do the persuading.

## 1.5 — The lean deck: one idea per slide

The deck is the visual spine of the pitch. Eight to ten slides, each carrying exactly one idea. The canonical order, mapped to your beats and your prior weeks:

1. **Title** — company name and a one-line "what it is" (the sentence a stranger could repeat). *Backed by: your founder brief's framing.*
2. **Problem** — who hurts, how badly, made concrete. *Backed by: discovery dossier.*
3. **Evidence / discovery** — number of interviews, the pattern, what people pay today. *Backed by: ≥10 synthesized interviews.* **This is your credibility slide.**
4. **Solution** — what you built, the mechanism, a visual or prototype clip. *Backed by: MVP + result.*
5. **How it works / product** — one diagram or screenshot; the mechanism, not a feature list. *Backed by: MVP.*
6. **Business model** — price, who pays, the revenue motion. *Backed by: lean canvas.*
7. **Unit-economics headline** — the LTV:CAC ratio, contribution margin, payback. *Backed by: unit-economics sheet.*
8. **Traction** — the real number and the honest leak. *Backed by: GTM one-pager + traction metric.*
9. **Team** — why *you* are the right person for *this* problem (founder-market fit). *Backed by: founder brief + co-founder agreement.*
10. **The ask** — the single specific request. *Backed by: cap-table model if raising; ops checklist if not.*

Two disciplines make a deck lean. First: **one idea per slide.** If a slide has two ideas, split it or cut one. A slide with a headline, three bullets, two charts, and a logo cloud is six ideas, which means the audience reads none of them and listens to you not at all. Second: **delete every adjective a number cannot back.** "Revolutionary," "massive," "game-changing," "best-in-class" — each one is a claim with no evidence, and a skeptical room reads each as a small lie. Run the red-pen pass: if "huge market" has a number behind it, replace the word with the number ("a \$2,400/year problem for each of ~90,000 small clinics"); if it does not, cut the word. The deck that survives the red-pen pass is the deck that survives the room.

The reason one-idea-per-slide matters so much in a *three-minute* pitch: you have roughly 18 seconds per slide if you use all ten. There is no time for the audience to parse a busy slide. The slide should be readable in two seconds so they can spend the other sixteen *listening to you*. The slide supports your voice; it does not compete with it.

## 1.6 — Tell the discovery story honestly (including the pivot)

If your nine weeks of discovery led you to pivot — and they should have, for most of you — *say so in the pitch.* This feels counterintuitive: surely a clean, never-wavered story is more impressive? It is not, and understanding why is a mark of a real founder.

"We started building a no-show *reminder* tool. Twelve interviews in, clinics told us reminders weren't the pain — they already had those — the pain was the *empty slot they couldn't fill in time*. So we re-aimed at waitlist auto-fill." That sentence does something a clean story cannot: it *proves your evidence changed your mind.* It demonstrates exactly the disciplined, problem-first, evidence-led founder this course trained you to be. An investor hears it and thinks "this person updates on data" — which is the single most valuable founder trait, because the plan will be wrong many more times and they want someone who will notice.

The clean too-perfect story has the opposite effect. "We knew from day one customers wanted exactly this" reads as either *you didn't really talk to anyone* or *you weren't listening when you did.* The pivot is not a confession of failure; it is your evidence working. Lead into it with pride: "the interviews changed our mind, and here's how."

## 1.7 — The ask: the most important sentence

The ask is the last thing the room hears and the most commonly fumbled beat in the entire pitch. Three rules:

1. **Be specific.** Not "we're looking for support" or "we'd love to chat." Name the thing: "three intros to multi-location clinic groups," "a pilot customer in the dental vertical," "feedback on our onboarding flow from anyone who's sold to clinics," or — if you are raising — "we're raising a \$400K pre-seed on a SAFE to get to \$15K MRR." A vague ask wastes your closing sentence; the room cannot help you with "support" but they can absolutely make an intro.
2. **Match it to *this* audience.** Asking a room of student founders for a \$2M check is a mismatch; asking them to test your prototype or share it with one clinic owner they know is not. The right ask is the thing *these specific people* can actually give you.
3. **Make it the last thing on screen.** End on the ask slide and leave it up during Q&A, so the request is the lasting image. If you raise a deck and the last slide is a "thank you," you wasted your final frame on a courtesy.

For the founders not raising money this cycle (most of you), the ask is not "give me capital." It is the *next piece of evidence you need*: a pilot, an intro to a channel, a specific kind of customer, a co-founder with a particular skill. The ask names the next risk you want to remove, which ties it straight back to §1.4 — the whole pitch is about removing risk, and the ask says which risk is next.

## 1.8 — How a demo-day room actually works

You will pitch into a real room (or a recorded camera standing in for one), and knowing the room's mechanics keeps you from being surprised by them.

- **The clock is hard.** At a real demo day, you are cut off at time — mid-sentence, mid-slide, no mercy. This is why the timing budget in §1.2 exists and why you rehearse to land at 2:50, not 3:05. A pitch that runs long doesn't read as thorough; it reads as someone who couldn't prioritize.
- **The first 20 seconds set the frame.** The room decides early whether to lean in or drift. This is why you open with vivid pain, not a logo animation or "hi, I'm so excited to be here." Earn the lean-in immediately.
- **Q&A is where the real evaluation happens.** The pitch is the trailer; the questions are the interview. A polished pitch followed by a fumbled "how do you know this is real?" loses the room you just won. Lecture 2 is entirely about this.
- **The room talks afterward.** The most valuable outcomes — the intro, the pilot, the "let's grab coffee" — happen in the hallway after, triggered by a specific thing you said. This is why the ask is specific and why traction is real: you are giving the room something concrete to follow up on.

The founder who understands the room *runs* their three minutes — paces to the clock, frames the first 20 seconds, and ends on a specific ask — instead of being run by nerves. That composure reads as exactly the founder who can run a customer call, a board meeting, or a hiring loop later.

## 1.9 — A worked transcript: the pitch out loud

Reading the structure is one thing; hearing how a tight pitch *sounds* is another. Here is the clinic founder's full three-minute pitch, lightly annotated with the beat and the running clock. Read it out loud — it should take about 2:50.

> **[Problem · 0:00]** "A two-doctor clinic loses eight to twelve appointment slots a week to no-shows. Every empty slot is revenue that's gone for good — and right now, nobody fills it.
>
> **[Evidence · 0:18]** I interviewed twelve clinic managers. Every single one already runs two tools to fight this — a reminder service and a spreadsheet waitlist — and they *still* lose slots. The thing they all said, almost word for word: the painful part isn't the missed appointment, it's the empty slot they can't fill fast enough. They're already paying for tools that don't solve the real problem.
>
> **[Solution · 0:55]** So I built waitlist auto-fill. The moment a patient cancels, the system instantly offers that exact slot to the next person on the waitlist and books whoever says yes first — no front-desk scramble. *[10-second prototype clip plays.]*
>
> **[Model · 1:28]** It's ninety-nine dollars a month per clinic. My early unit economics are about six-to-one lifetime-value to acquisition cost, with a roughly three-month payback — so each clinic pays back its acquisition cost fast and is profitable well inside the first year.
>
> **[Traction · 1:58]** Two clinics are paying today, both closed by me directly. And here's the honest part: of the clinics that took a demo, only about a third started a trial. I think onboarding is the leak, and that's exactly what I'm fixing next.
>
> **[Ask · 2:32]** What I need right now is three warm introductions to multi-location clinic groups — that's the channel I most want to test next. If you know one, find me after. Thank you.
>
> **[End · 2:52]**"

Notice what makes it strong. It leads with vivid pain and immediately backs it with evidence. The solution arrives only after the room already feels the problem. Every number is real and modest — no "billion-dollar market," no "incredible demand." The traction beat *names its own leak*, which builds more trust than hiding it. And the ask is one specific, actionable thing the room can actually deliver. That is the shape of every fundable early-stage pitch: concrete, evidence-led, honest about the weakness, and ending on a single clear request.

## 1.9a — Designing the slide itself: what goes on it, what stays off

Knowing the slide order (§1.5) is not the same as knowing what belongs *on* each slide. The single rule that governs every slide is: **the slide is read in two seconds so you can be listened to for the other sixteen.** That means the slide is a billboard, not a document. Three concrete disciplines follow from it.

**One headline, stated as a claim.** The top of every slide is a single sentence that *is the point of the slide*, not a topic label. "Problem" is a label; "Small clinics lose 8–12 paid slots a week to no-shows" is a claim. The headline-as-claim does the work even if the audience reads nothing else, which is what you must assume in a three-minute pitch. If your headlines, read in sequence with nothing else, tell the whole story — problem, evidence, solution, model, traction, ask — your deck is structurally sound. That is a test you can run in thirty seconds: read only the headlines aloud and check that they form a coherent argument.

**One piece of supporting evidence, visualized.** Below the headline goes *one* thing that proves it: a single chart, a single screenshot, a single number set in large type, or one customer quote. Not three. A slide with one striking chart and a headline beats a slide with four small charts and six bullets, because the room can absorb one thing in two seconds and absorbs nothing from six. The evidence slide (slide 3) is the clearest case: a single line — "12 of 12 clinics already pay for two tools and still lose slots" — in big type, with maybe one verbatim customer quote underneath, beats a wall of interview excerpts.

**No logo clouds, no buzzword bingo, no nine-point footnotes.** The things that make a slide look "professional" to a beginner — a grid of partner logos, a "powered by AI/blockchain/cloud" banner, a dense methodology footnote — are the things that make it look unserious to an investor. They are noise that competes with your voice. Cut them. The clean slide with one claim and one proof reads as *confidence*; the busy slide reads as someone hiding a thin argument behind decoration.

A useful production habit: design every slide so it would still make sense as the *only* slide a distracted investor glances at. If they look up for exactly one slide during your pitch, any slide, it should land a complete idea. That standard forces the one-idea discipline harder than any rule about bullet counts.

## 1.9b — Sizing the market without lying: TAM/SAM/SOM done honestly

Somewhere around the model slide, a sharp room wants to know "how big can this get?" — and this is where early founders reach for the single most discrediting move in the deck: the top-down billion-dollar number. "Healthcare is a \$4 trillion market and we just need 1% of it." Every experienced investor has heard that sentence a thousand times, and every one of them discounts the founder who says it, because it proves the opposite of what it intends: it proves you have *not* thought about who your actual first customers are. "1% of a huge market" is not a plan; it is a wish with a multiplication sign.

The honest way to size a market is **bottom-up**, and it uses three numbers you can actually defend:

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market)** — everyone who could conceivably buy this, ever. For the clinic founder: every small clinic in the country that loses revenue to no-shows.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market)** — the slice you can actually reach with your model and motion. For her: small US clinics on cloud scheduling systems she can integrate with, in the verticals she can sell to.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market)** — what you can realistically win in the next few years given your channel. For her: the clinics she can reach through founder-led sales and word of mouth in her first two or three target verticals.

The discipline is to build it from the unit up: "There are about 90,000 small clinics in the US. Say a third are on a scheduling system I can integrate with — that's 30,000 reachable clinics. At \$99 a month, that's roughly \$36 million a year of serviceable market. I don't need all of it; a few thousand clinics is a real business." That is a number a room *trusts*, because every step is visible and every step is defensible. It is a smaller number than "\$4 trillion," and that is exactly why it is more credible — it shows you know your actual customer, your actual price, and your actual reach. The bottom-up number says "I've done the arithmetic of my own business." The top-down number says "I found a big figure on a slide." Use bottom-up, and put the assumption (the one-third integration rate) on the slide so the room knows which number to push on.

## 1.9c — Founder-market fit: the team slide that isn't a résumé

The team slide (slide 9) is where early founders waste their credibility on the wrong thing. They list degrees, former employers, and titles — a résumé. But at the seed stage, the room is not asking "is this person impressive?"; they are asking "is this person *uniquely suited to this specific problem*?" That is **founder-market fit**, and it is the entire point of the team slide and of Week 01's founder brief.

Founder-market fit is the answer to "why you, and not the dozen other smart people who could build this?" The strongest answers are some combination of:

- **Earned insight.** You lived the problem or worked next to it. "I ran the front desk at a clinic for two years; I watched us lose slots every single week and there was nothing to do about it." That sentence beats any degree, because it means you understand the pain in your bones and you'll notice things outsiders miss.
- **Unfair distribution.** You can reach the customers cheaply because of who you already know. "I know forty clinic managers from my years in the industry, and they talk to each other." Distribution is the most underrated founder advantage and the most defensible.
- **Durable motivation.** You will still be working on this problem in three years when it's hard and boring, because you care about it for reasons deeper than the upside. The room is betting on a person for the long haul; show them you won't quit at the first wall.

The clinic founder's team slide is one sentence: "I spent two years running a clinic front desk, I know forty clinic managers by name, and the empty-slot problem is the thing I personally couldn't fix from the inside — that's why I'm building the fix from the outside." No résumé, no logos. Just the three things that make her the right person for *this* problem. That is founder-market fit, and it is far more persuasive than a list of credentials, because credentials prove you're capable in general while founder-market fit proves you're the right bet for *this*.

## 1.9d — The two pitch shapes: with a check vs. with evidence

Not everyone in this course is raising money this cycle, and the pitch flexes to fit. There are two shapes, and the difference is entirely in the model, traction, and ask beats — the problem, evidence, and solution beats are identical.

**The fundraising pitch** ends with a money ask and leans harder on the model. The traction beat sets up *why now is the moment to invest*, and the ask is specific about the raise: "I'm raising a \$400K pre-seed on a post-money SAFE at a \$4M cap. That money is eighteen months of runway to get from two paying clinics to \$15K in monthly recurring revenue, which is the milestone that makes a seed round makeable." Notice it ties straight to the Week 08 cap-table model (the SAFE, the cap, the dilution) and names *what the money buys* — a milestone, not just "growth." A room funds a milestone; it does not fund "we'll figure it out."

**The evidence pitch** (most of you, this cycle) ends with a *next-experiment* ask and leans on what you've validated. The traction beat shows the evidence you've gathered, and the ask names the next risk you want to remove: "I'm not raising yet — I'm proving the onboarding works before I scale. What I need is three intros to multi-location clinic groups so I can test whether founder-led sales survives outside my own network." This is the more honest pitch for a pre-traction concept, and it reads as *more* disciplined, not less, because it shows you know you haven't earned the right to raise yet. Raising before you have evidence is a classic early mistake; an evidence pitch that says "here's what I've proven and here's the next thing I'm testing" is exactly the founder a future investor wants to back *when* the time comes.

Pick the shape that matches your actual situation. Do not give a fundraising pitch if you have no business raising — a room can smell "I'm asking for money because that's what founders do" from across the room, and it undercuts every honest thing you said before it.

## 1.9e — The demo or prototype clip: show, don't tell

The solution beat (slide 4–5) is dramatically stronger with ten seconds of the thing *actually working* than with a paragraph describing it. A short clip of your clickable prototype, your MVP, or even a Wizard-of-Oz mock doing the one core action is worth more than any feature list, because it converts "they say it does X" into "I watched it do X." This is the payoff of the Week 05 MVP build: you have an artifact, so use it on camera.

But the clip has rules, and breaking them costs you the room:

- **Ten seconds, one action.** Show the single core thing — the slot opens, the waitlist fires, the patient books — not a tour of every screen. A two-minute walkthrough eats your whole clock and bores the room. Show the magic moment, then cut back to your face.
- **It must be the *core* action, not a settings page.** Founders love to show the part they spent the most time building (the dashboard, the admin panel). Show the part the customer *cares* about — the moment the pain gets solved.
- **Have a static screenshot fallback.** Clips fail to play in live rooms constantly — codec issues, projector trouble, a tab that won't share. A founder who freezes when the video won't load loses the room; a founder who says "here's a screenshot, the live version does X in real time, I'll show anyone after" loses nothing. Stage the fallback before you ever need it.
- **No narration over a silent clip you're also explaining.** Either let the clip's own captions carry it for ten seconds while you pause, or narrate a screenshot — don't talk over a moving video, because the room can't read motion and listen to you at once.

A prototype clip is not required to pass — a clear verbal solution beat works — but if you have a working artifact, ten honest seconds of it is the single highest-leverage visual in the whole deck. It is the difference between claiming you built something and proving it.

## 1.9f — Delivery and nerves: composure is a skill, not a trait

Everything above is about *content*. A word on *delivery*, because a perfectly structured pitch delivered by someone visibly terrified loses some of its credibility — the room reads the nerves as "is this person sure about their own numbers?" The good news: composure under a clock is a learnable skill, not a personality you're born with, and three practices build it.

- **Over-rehearse the first 20 seconds specifically.** Most pitch nerves peak in the opening and settle once you're rolling. If your first two sentences are so rehearsed they're automatic, you get past the worst of the adrenaline before it can derail you, and the rest flows. Memorize the problem beat cold.
- **Slow down on purpose.** Nerves speed everyone up; the antidote is a deliberate, slightly-slower-than-feels-natural pace. A pitch delivered slightly slow reads as *confident and in command of the clock*; a pitch delivered fast reads as nervous and is also harder to follow. You rehearsed to 2:50 precisely so you can afford to slow down.
- **Anchor to the evidence, not the performance.** When nerves spike, return to a number you know cold — "twelve interviews, two paying clinics." Facts are a handrail; you can't fumble a number you actually have. This is another reason evidence-first pitching is calmer: you're reporting what's true, not selling a feeling, and reporting is far less stressful than performing.

The founders who read as most fundable are rarely the most charismatic; they're the ones who are *calm and specific*, because calm-and-specific is what "I have my numbers and I'm not bluffing" looks like from the outside. You build that by rehearsing until the content is automatic and then trusting the evidence to do the persuading.

## 1.10 — Common ways founders lose the pitch

To round out the playbook, the recurring own-goals. Each is avoidable with preparation:

- **Opening with the solution.** "We built an app that…" before the room knows whose pain it solves. Open with the pain and the evidence; reveal the solution third.
- **Adjectives instead of numbers.** "Massive market, revolutionary product, incredible traction." A skeptical room hears three claims with zero evidence. Replace each adjective with the number behind it, or cut it.
- **Vanity traction.** Citing 10,000 newsletter signups instead of 2 paying customers because the bigger number *sounds* better. Investors discount signups to near zero; two paying customers is real revenue and real proof. Lead with the strongest *real* evidence, not the biggest-sounding number.
- **A too-clean story.** Pretending the original idea was right all along. It reads as "didn't really talk to customers." Own the pivot — it's your evidence working.
- **Running over time.** Cramming five minutes of content into three and getting cut off at the model slide, before the traction and the ask. Budget the beats (§1.2), rehearse to 2:50, cut ruthlessly.
- **The vague ask.** Ending on "we're looking for support" or a list of five things. One specific ask, matched to the audience, the last thing on screen.
- **Reading the slides.** Turning your back to the room to read bullet points aloud. The slide is readable in two seconds *so that* you can spend the rest looking at the room and talking. If you're reading the deck, the deck has too many words.

Prepare against all seven and the pitch becomes what it is supposed to be: three minutes that present your evidence so clearly that the right person in the room wants the next conversation.

## 1.11 — Building the deck: the storyboard before the slides

Do not open a slide tool and start designing. Storyboard first, in text — it is faster, it forces the structure, and it keeps you from polishing a slide you will later cut. (This is exactly what Exercise 2 walks you through.) For each of the 8–10 slides, write three things and nothing else:

- **The one idea** the slide carries (a single sentence).
- **The headline** the audience reads in two seconds.
- **The evidence** behind it (which deliverable in your repo backs this slide).

If you cannot name the deliverable behind a slide, you have found a problem *before* you built the slide: either go get the evidence, or cut the claim. The storyboard is where the deck gets honest. Only once every slide has an idea, a headline, and an evidence source do you open the slide tool — and then it is a half-hour of formatting, not an evening of agonizing over what to say. The agonizing already happened in the storyboard, where it's cheap to fix.

A test you can apply before you build a single slide: hand your storyboard to someone who has never heard your pitch and ask them to repeat your concept back. If they can — "small clinics lose slots to no-shows, you auto-fill them from a waitlist, two clinics pay you" — the structure is good. If they stall — "wait, what does this actually do?" — you have a buried idea or a missing evidence source, and you fix it in the cheap text storyboard, not in the expensive slide.

## 1.10b — The one-liner: the sentence a stranger can repeat

Before any of the six beats, there is a sentence that has to do more work than any other in your entire pitch: the **one-liner** — the single line on your title slide that says what this is. It is the thing a person who saw your pitch repeats to a colleague the next day, and if they can't repeat it, the most valuable post-pitch event (someone telling someone else about you) never happens. Crafting it well is worth a disproportionate amount of your time.

The test of a good one-liner is brutal and simple: **a stranger hears it once and can repeat it accurately.** That rules out almost everything founders instinctively reach for:

- **Not a category claim.** "We're the future of healthcare scheduling" tells a stranger nothing they can repeat — it's a vibe, not a description.
- **Not a feature list.** "We do waitlist management, automated reminders, patient messaging, and analytics" is four things, so they remember none.
- **Not a metaphor.** "We're the Uber of clinics" makes the listener do work to decode it, and they usually decode it wrong.

What passes the test is a plain sentence with a *who*, a *what*, and ideally the *mechanism*: **"When a patient cancels, we instantly fill the slot from the clinic's waitlist."** A stranger can repeat that verbatim because it's concrete and it has only one idea. The formula that works most often is "[for whom], we [do the specific thing] so that [the outcome]" — and then you cut it down until only the load-bearing words remain. "Waitlist auto-fill for small clinics" is even shorter and still repeatable.

Notice the one-liner is *not* your mission, your vision, or your tagline. Those have their place, but the one-liner's only job is comprehension: the listener must walk away able to say, accurately, what you do. Test it on someone outside your space — a friend who's never heard the pitch. Say it once, wait an hour, and ask them to repeat it. If it comes back garbled, the line is too clever or too vague, and you rewrite it until it survives the relay. The whole pitch leads with this sentence, so it had better be the clearest one you've got.

## 1.11b — The same evidence, three audiences: adapting the emphasis

You built one body of evidence over nine weeks, but the *emphasis* shifts with who's in the room (Lecture 1, §1.1 named the three audiences). The beats stay the same; what changes is which one you lean on and which ask you end with. Knowing how to flex this is the difference between a pitch that lands and one that's aimed at no one.

- **Pitching an investor.** Lean on evidence and the model. They're pattern-matching for *risk removed*, so your discovery count, your paying customers, and your unit-economics ratio do the heavy lifting. The ask is for capital or a specific intro that advances the raise. The riskiest-assumption move (Lecture 2) matters most here, because de-risking is the entire frame.
- **Pitching a potential customer or pilot partner.** Lean on the problem and the solution. They don't care about your LTV:CAC; they care "is this for someone like me, and is it real?" Lead even harder on the specific pain and show the prototype doing the one thing that solves it. The ask is "will you pilot it?" — and the strongest proof is that other customers like them already pay.
- **Pitching a future co-founder or teammate.** Lean on traction and the mission. Strong people join *momentum and evidence*, not slideware. Show that the thing is real and moving, and that the problem is worth years of their life. The ask is "join me, and here's the specific gap you'd own."

The demo-day pitch is built for the investor with the other two reading over their shoulder — but if you know your room skews toward customers or recruits, shift the weight accordingly. The mistake is delivering an identical investor-shaped pitch to a room of potential pilot customers, who tune out the cap-table talk and never hear that the product would solve their Tuesday. Same evidence, re-aimed.

## 1.11c — Why the pitch is the forcing function for the whole course

Step back from the mechanics. The reason demo day is the capstone — and not, say, a written business plan — is that the three-minute pitch is the most *unforgiving compression* of everything the course taught. You cannot fake it. A business plan can hide a thin idea behind forty pages; three minutes cannot. Every weakness in your nine weeks of work surfaces under the clock:

- If your discovery is thin, the evidence beat has nothing to say, and the room feels the hollowness in the most important forty seconds.
- If your MVP never tested a real assumption, the solution beat is a description of a wish, and the "how do you know customers want this" question has no answer.
- If your unit economics don't hold, the model beat collapses to "we'll charge money somehow."
- If you never found a traction signal, you're left citing vanity metrics, and a sharp room discounts them to zero.

This is why the pitch is the right final exam: it doesn't test whether you can *talk* about a startup; it tests whether you *did the work*. The founders who sail through demo day are not the most charismatic — they're the ones whose evidence is real, so the pitch is just honest reporting. The founders who struggle are usually the ones who skipped the unglamorous middle weeks and are now trying to perform their way past the gap. The clock is merciless precisely so that it rewards the work, not the performance. That's the whole design of the course condensed into three minutes: find truth, then present it plainly.

And it's why "validated concept," not "funded company," is the bar. You are not graded on whether the idea is a billion-dollar business — nobody can know that yet. You are graded on whether you talked to enough of the right people, built the smallest thing that tests the idea, and can defend the result. The pitch is where you prove you can do all three out loud, in three minutes, to a stranger. That proof — the *evidence and the honesty*, not this one idea — is the thing you carry into every company you ever start.

## 1.12 — Rehearsing the three minutes

The pitch is the highest-stakes three minutes of the week, and it involves a clock that does not forgive. Rehearse it at least three times before demo day:

1. **Read it aloud, timed.** Not in your head — out loud, with a timer. People read silently faster than they speak, so a pitch that "feels" like three minutes in your head is often four out loud. Cut until you land at 2:50 spoken.
2. **Record yourself and watch it back.** Cringe is normal and temporary. Watch for the two worst moments — the rambling beat, the place you lost the thread — and fix exactly those. Two or three deliberate takes beat ten anxious ones.
3. **Rehearse the prototype clip and the handoff.** If your solution beat includes a clip, practice the transition into and out of it so it doesn't eat your clock or break your rhythm. Have a static screenshot ready as a fallback in case the clip won't play — saying "here's a screenshot, the live version does X" is completely fine and far better than freezing while a video buffers.

The recorded 3-minute pitch (a capstone deliverable) is essentially this rehearsal, captured. If you rehearse the live pitch well, the recording is a 20-minute session, not a 30-take ordeal. Lecture 2 covers the recording mechanics and the part that actually decides the room: the hard questions.

## 1.12b — What the room is *actually* deciding while you pitch

It sharpens every choice above to understand what the listener is really doing during your three minutes. They are not grading your presentation skills. They are running a fast, mostly subconscious risk assessment, asking four questions in sequence — and your six beats exist precisely to answer them in order:

1. **"Is this a real problem?"** — answered by your problem and evidence beats. If they don't believe the pain is real, nothing else matters; they've already left.
2. **"Is this a real solution to it?"** — answered by your solution beat (and the prototype clip). They're checking whether your fix actually addresses the pain you just established, or whether it's a solution in search of a problem.
3. **"Can it become a real business?"** — answered by your model and traction beats. Now they care about money and momentum: does scaling help, and is anything actually happening?
4. **"Is this the right person to bet on?"** — answered by your team beat and, more than any slide, by *how you handle the whole thing* — especially the Q&A.

Notice that this is exactly the order of your pitch, which is not a coincidence: the structure is reverse-engineered from how a listener evaluates. It's also why leading with the solution fails — you're answering question 2 before they've accepted question 1, so the answer lands on deaf ears. And it's why the riskiest-assumption move (Lecture 2) is so powerful: it speaks directly to question 4, demonstrating self-awareness, which is the single trait that most predicts whether a founder will survive contact with reality.

The practical upshot: when you cut a pitch for time, cut the *least* risk-removing material first. The team slide can compress to one sentence; the evidence beat cannot, because it's load-bearing for question 1. A founder who understands they're being *risk-assessed*, not *entertained*, makes the right cuts under pressure and aims every second at removing a doubt rather than adding a flourish.

## 1.13 — The math of the clock: why 2:50, not 3:00

A closing word on the clock, because the timing discipline is more arithmetic than nerve. People speak at roughly 130–160 words per minute when they're calm and slightly slower than feels natural — call it 150. Three minutes is therefore about **450 words of spoken pitch**, and that is genuinely all you get. Write your six beats out as a script, count the words, and if you're over 450 you *will* run over live, because nerves speed you up but the clock doesn't move.

You aim for **2:50, not 3:00**, for three concrete reasons:

- **Live pitches drift long.** A pause to find a word, a beat that runs an extra few seconds, a clip that takes a moment to load — these always add up *toward* the cap, never away from it. Building in a ten-second buffer means the drift lands you at 3:00 instead of past it.
- **You want to slow down, not speed up.** A pitch delivered slightly slow reads as confident and in command; a pitch crammed to the edge forces you to rush, which reads as nervous and is harder to follow. The 2:50 target is what *buys* you the option to slow down.
- **Getting cut off costs you the ask.** At a real demo day the clock is hard — you're cut off mid-sentence. If you've budgeted to 3:00 exactly and drift even slightly, the thing that gets cut is your *last* beat: the ask. Losing the ask is losing the entire point of the pitch.

So the in-class **pitch gauntlet** is not cruelty — it's calibration. Everyone delivers timed, over-time pitches get cut off mid-sentence exactly as at a real demo day, and the lesson lands in the body, not the notes: you feel the cost of an un-budgeted solution beat when the timer kills you before the ask. Rehearse with a visible timer until landing at 2:50 is automatic, and the clock stops being a threat and becomes a tool you control.

## Summary

A pitch is a structured argument from evidence whose job is to earn the *next conversation*, not applause. It runs on six beats — problem, evidence, solution, model, traction, ask — in three minutes, with a timing budget that lands at 2:50. You lead with the problem and the evidence, *before* the solution, because evidence removes the risk the room cares about and adjectives remove nothing. The deck is 8–10 slides, one idea each, with every adjective a number cannot back deleted by the red-pen pass. You tell the discovery pivot honestly, because it proves your evidence changed your mind — the most valuable founder trait. You end on a single specific ask, matched to the audience, the last thing on screen. And every claim traces to an artifact in your repo, because a pitch is evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo. Storyboard before you build, rehearse to 2:50, and the three minutes you get will present your strongest case.

One last reframe before you build: the pitch is not where you *make* your case strong — it is where you *present* a case you already made, over nine weeks, by doing the work. If the evidence is real, the pitch is honest reporting and the three minutes are easy. If the evidence is thin, no amount of polish saves the pitch, and that is the system working as designed. So the highest-leverage thing you can do this week is not rehearse harder; it is make sure every beat traces to an artifact you can show. Build the evidence trail first, then the pitch writes itself.
