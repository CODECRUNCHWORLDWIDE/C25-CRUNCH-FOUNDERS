# Lecture 1 — The Three-Minute Pitch

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can structure a 3-minute pitch and build a lean deck where evidence carries every claim.

> **An early-stage pitch is evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo.**

## 1. The structure

A reliable 3-minute shape, with rough timing:

```
Problem    (~20s)  Who hurts, and how badly.
Evidence   (~40s)  Proof you talked to them: interviews, what you learned.
Solution   (~30s)  What you built; a short demo/prototype clip.
Model      (~30s)  How it makes money; the unit economics headline.
Traction   (~30s)  What is real so far (paying customers > signups).
Ask        (~20s)  What you want from this audience, specifically.
```

Three minutes is short on purpose — it forces you to lead with what matters and cut the rest.

## 2. Lead with the problem and the evidence

Beginners open with their solution or their vision. Open instead with the *problem* and immediately the *evidence* that it is real. "We interviewed 12 clinics; they already pay for two clumsy tools; here is the cost per no-show" earns more credibility in 40 seconds than any amount of "huge market" rhetoric.

## 3. The lean deck

About 8–10 slides, one idea each:

1. Title (company, one-line what-it-is).
2. Problem.
3. Evidence / discovery.
4. Solution (with a visual or demo).
5. How it works / product.
6. Business model.
7. Unit economics headline.
8. Traction.
9. Team (why you — founder-market fit).
10. The ask.

No slide should make a claim your capstone repo cannot back. Delete adjectives ("revolutionary," "massive") unless a number stands behind them.

## 4. Tell the discovery story honestly

If your discovery led to a pivot, *say so* — it is a strength, not a weakness. "We started with X, interviews showed the real pain was Y, so we re-aimed" signals exactly the disciplined founder this course trained you to be. Honesty about what you learned is more persuasive than a too-clean story.

## 5. The ask

End with a specific ask matched to the audience: an introduction, a pilot customer, feedback on a particular risk, or (if raising) a round and its purpose. A vague "we're looking for support" wastes the most important sentence.
