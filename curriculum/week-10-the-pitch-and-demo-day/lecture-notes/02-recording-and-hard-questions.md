# Lecture 2 — Recording and Hard Questions

> **Duration:** ~45 min. **Outcome:** You can record a clean 3-minute pitch and field the obvious hard questions without flinching.

## 1. Recording cleanly

You do not need fancy gear. A clear, well-lit, audible recording beats a slick one with bad sound.

- **Tool:** OBS Studio (free, screen + camera) or Loom (free tier). Record the deck with your voice; splice in the short product/prototype clip.
- **Audio first:** bad audio loses an audience faster than bad video. Use a quiet room; get close to the mic.
- **Edit to time:** three minutes means three minutes. Cut filler. Re-record sections rather than rambling.
- **One clear call:** end on the ask, on screen, so it is the last thing the viewer sees.

## 2. Rehearse out loud, then watch yourself

Read it aloud until the timing is natural; then record, watch it back (cringe is normal), and fix the two worst moments. Two or three takes usually beats ten. The goal is *clear and credible*, not theatrical.

## 3. The three hard questions

Almost every early pitch faces some version of these. Prepare evidence-based answers:

- **"How do you know this is a real problem?"** → your discovery dossier: number of interviews, the pattern, what people pay today.
- **"Why will customers choose you / why now?"** → founder-market fit, the gap in current workarounds, your MVP result.
- **"Can this make money / how big can it get?"** → your unit economics headline and the honest path, including the fragile assumption.

A fourth common one: **"What's the riskiest thing?"** Naming your own riskiest assumption calmly is a strength.

## 4. Answering without defensiveness

- **Acknowledge, then answer with evidence.** "Good question — here's what our interviews showed."
- **It is fine to say "we don't know yet, and here's how we'd find out."** Honesty about uncertainty, paired with a test plan, beats bluffing.
- **Do not argue with the question.** Defensiveness reads as fragility. Treat hard questions as free advice.

## 5. Demo day

Present live (or play your recording), take questions, and then *assemble the full capstone repo* so a stranger can follow your evidence trail from problem to ask. The repo is the lasting artifact; the pitch is its trailer.
