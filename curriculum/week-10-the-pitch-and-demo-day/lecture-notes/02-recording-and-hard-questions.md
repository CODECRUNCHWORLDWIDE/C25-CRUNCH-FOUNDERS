# Lecture 2 — Recording the Pitch and Surviving the Hard Questions

> **Reading time:** ~70 minutes. **Hands-on time:** ~50 minutes (you record a first take and draft your hard-question canvas).

C25 has said it since Week 01: *a validated concept is one you can defend with evidence, not one you can describe with enthusiasm.* This is the lecture that cashes that promise. The recording captures your pitch so a stranger — an accelerator interviewer, a future angel, a co-founder you haven't met — can watch it without you in the room. And the hard questions are where the *actual* evaluation happens: the polished three minutes is the trailer, but the Q&A is the interview, and a founder who nails the pitch and then fumbles "how do you know this is real?" loses the room they just won. By the end of this lecture you can record a clean three-minute pitch with free tools, and you can field the four questions every early pitch faces without flinching, defensiveness, or a bluff.

## 2.1 — Why record it at all

People hear "record the pitch" and assume it's a formality — a checkbox for the capstone. It is not. You record for three reasons, none of which is the checkbox:

1. **It scales your one shot.** You can give a live pitch to a few rooms. A recording can be watched by an accelerator reviewer at midnight, forwarded by a friendly investor to a colleague, or embedded in your portfolio for a recruiter. The live pitch reaches the room; the recording reaches everyone the room knows.
2. **It forces you to make the pitch *tight*.** Live, you can wave your hands and recover from a rambling beat. On camera, the ramble is permanent, so recording forces you to cut until every sentence earns its place. The discipline of "this will be watched, unedited, forever" makes the pitch better.
3. **It is the comprehension test you administer to yourself.** Watching yourself pitch is excruciating and indispensable. Every place you lose the thread on camera is a place your *argument* is weak, not just your delivery. The recording shows you exactly which beats you don't actually believe yet — and those are the beats where your evidence is thin.

The honesty rule is absolute here too: a recording that hides the leak in your traction, or claims a number you can't back, is a credibility bomb with a long fuse. The moment a viewer asks for the evidence behind a claim you smoothed over, every other number is suspect. Record the *honest* pitch — the one where you name the leak and the riskiest assumption — because that's the one that survives scrutiny.

## 2.2 — Recording cleanly with free tools

You do not need fancy gear. A clear, well-lit, audible recording beats a slick one with bad sound every time. The setup:

- **Tool.** **OBS Studio** (free, open-source) records your screen and your camera together — show the deck with your face in the corner, splice in the prototype clip. **Loom** (free tier) is faster if you don't need heavy editing and just want a shareable link. Either is fine.
- **Audio first.** Bad audio loses an audience faster than bad video. Record in a quiet room — no fan, no street noise — and get close to the mic (even cheap earbuds with a mic beat your laptop's built-in mic across the room). If you have **Audacity**, run a quick noise-reduction and leveling pass on the audio track. People forgive a plain webcam; they click away from muddy, echoey sound in seconds.
- **Light your face.** Sit facing a window or a lamp, not with the light behind you. A silhouette reads as low-effort; a lit face reads as a person you'd take a meeting with.
- **Edit to time.** Three minutes means three minutes. **Shotcut** (free) cuts filler and trims dead air. Re-record a weak section rather than rambling through it — a clean splice beats a long apology.
- **End on the ask, on screen.** The last frame the viewer sees should be your single specific ask, held for a beat. Don't end on a "thank you" slide; end on the thing you want.

The whole recording, done well, is a 20–30 minute session, because you already rehearsed the pitch live (Lecture 1, §1.12). If you're on take fifteen, the problem isn't the camera — it's that the pitch isn't rehearsed yet. Go back and rehearse it out loud, then record.

## 2.2b — The recording workflow, step by step

Here is the concrete sequence that gets you a clean three-minute recording in one session, so you're not improvising the production on top of the pitch:

1. **Stage the deck and the clip.** Have your slides open and your 10-second prototype clip ready to play (or a static screenshot as the fallback). Practice the handoff into and out of the clip once before you hit record, so it doesn't break your rhythm live.
2. **Frame and light.** Webcam at eye level (stack it on a few books if it's a laptop), face toward a window or lamp, plain background. Close the door, kill the fan, silence your phone.
3. **Do a 10-second sound test.** Record ten seconds, play it back, listen on headphones. If it's muddy or echoey, fix it *now* — move closer to the mic, add a soft surface to the room — because no editing rescues bad source audio.
4. **Record one full take, don't stop for small flubs.** Get a complete run end to end. Stopping at every stumble guarantees you never get a whole take and your energy dies by take eight.
5. **Watch it once, find the two worst moments** (§2.3), and re-record just those or do one more full take with those fixes in mind.
6. **Edit to time in Shotcut:** trim dead air at the start and end, cut the one rambling beat, drop in the prototype clip if you recorded it separately, and confirm the runtime is at or under 3:00.
7. **Run an audio pass in Audacity** if needed: noise reduction, then normalize/level so your voice is consistently audible. This single step does more for perceived quality than any video tweak.
8. **Export, watch the final once on a phone speaker** (where most people will actually watch it), and confirm the ask is the last thing on screen.

The whole loop is 20–40 minutes when the pitch is rehearsed. If it's taking two hours, stop editing and go rehearse the *pitch* — you're trying to fix a content problem with a production tool, and that never works.

## 2.3 — Rehearse out loud, then watch yourself

The single most effective recording technique is also the most uncomfortable: record a take, then *watch it back* before you re-record. Most founders record blind, hate the result vaguely, and re-record the whole thing ten times hoping it gets better by luck. Instead:

1. **Record one full take.** Don't stop for small mistakes; get a complete run.
2. **Watch it once, all the way through, taking notes.** Find the *two worst moments* — the beat where you rambled, the place your energy dropped, the claim you mumbled because you don't quite believe it.
3. **Fix exactly those two.** Re-record just those sections (OBS and Shotcut both let you splice) or do one more full take with those two fixes in mind.

Two or three deliberate, diagnosed takes beat ten anxious ones. The goal is *clear and credible*, not theatrical — you are not auditioning, you are presenting evidence. A calm, well-lit founder saying true things in order will outperform a slick, over-rehearsed performance every time, because the audience is pattern-matching for "can I trust this person's numbers," not "is this person charismatic."

## 2.3b — A self-critique rubric for the playback

When you watch your take back (§2.3), don't just feel vaguely good or bad about it — score it against a rubric, so the re-record is targeted. Run this checklist on the playback:

| Dimension | What you're checking | Fail signal |
|---|---|---|
| **Opening** | Did the first 20 seconds make the pain vivid? | You opened with "hi, I'm excited to…" or the product, not the problem |
| **Evidence** | Is the discovery proof concrete and early? | You said "customers love it" with no count or pattern |
| **Adjectives** | Did any claim ride on a word instead of a number? | "Massive," "revolutionary," "incredible" with nothing behind it |
| **Traction honesty** | Did you cite the strongest *real* number, and name the leak? | You led with a vanity metric or hid the weak spot |
| **Ask** | Is it one specific, audience-matched request? | "We're looking for support" or a list of five things |
| **Time** | Did you land at or under 3:00? | You ran long or got cut off before the ask |
| **Audio** | Is your voice consistently clear on a phone speaker? | Echo, muddiness, or volume that dips below audible |
| **Energy** | Calm and specific, or rushed and nervous? | You sped up, especially after the opening |

Anything that throws a fail signal is a targeted re-record, not a reason to redo the whole thing. Most founders fix two or three of these between take one and the final, and the difference between the muddy first take and the clean third is almost always exactly these items — not talent, not charisma, just hitting the checklist.

## 2.3c — The recording as a durable portfolio asset

It's worth pausing on what the recording *becomes* after this week, because that reframes how much care it deserves. The live pitch is gone the moment you finish it — a few people in a room, a moment in time. The recording is permanent and *portable*: it sits in your capstone repo, embeds in your portfolio, and travels to anyone the room knows. Months from now, an accelerator reviewer screening hundreds of applications, a recruiter deciding whether to take a call, or an angel a friend forwarded it to will watch *this file* — without you there to narrate, recover, or explain.

That permanence cuts both ways. A clean, honest three minutes is the single best artifact you can hand a stranger to make the case for you; it does the persuading while you sleep. A muddy, over-long, or evasive recording does the opposite, and you can't un-send it. This is why the production care (§2.2) and the honesty rule (§2.1) matter more for the recording than for the live pitch: the live pitch is forgiven its stumbles in the moment, but the recording is judged cold, on its own, by people with no reason to extend you grace.

So treat the recording as the durable version of you that goes out into the world. Three implications:

- **It must stand alone.** No "as I mentioned earlier," no inside references to the cohort, no reliance on a room that the eventual viewer wasn't in. The recording assumes zero context — exactly like the repo it lives in.
- **The link belongs everywhere your work is seen.** The repo README, your portfolio, your LinkedIn if you're job-hunting, the application form for the next accelerator. The recording is reusable; reuse it.
- **It ages, so date it and be ready to re-cut.** "Two clinics paying" is true today; in three months it might be five, or the concept might have evolved. A recording with a visible date and a repo that's current beats a stale recording presented as if it were live. Re-record when the evidence has moved enough that the old number undersells you.

The founders who get the most mileage out of this week are the ones who stop thinking of the recording as a homework upload and start thinking of it as a *salesperson for the concept that works 24/7*. That's what it is.

## 2.3d — When the live demo breaks: recover without losing the room

Live demos fail. The clip won't play, the projector mangles your colors, the tab won't share, the wifi drops mid-prototype. This happens to everyone, and the *only* thing that separates a founder who recovers from one who unravels is whether they prepared for it. The room does not hold a technical glitch against you — it holds your *reaction* to it against you, because that reaction is a live preview of how you'll handle the chaos of actually running a company.

The discipline is to stage the fallback before you ever need it:

- **Always have a static screenshot of the magic moment.** If the 10-second clip won't play, you say, calmly, "Here's a screenshot — the live version does this in real time, and I'll show anyone who wants after." Then you keep moving. That sentence costs you four seconds and zero credibility. Freezing, apologizing, and fiddling with the laptop for thirty seconds costs you the room.
- **Know your pitch cold *without* the slides.** The deck supports your voice; it is not the pitch. If the whole deck fails to load, a founder who knows their six beats can deliver the entire pitch from memory looking straight at the room — and honestly, that often lands *better*, because nothing competes with you. Rehearse at least once with the slides off.
- **Never debug live.** The instant something doesn't work, do not try to fix it in front of the room. Switch to the fallback and move on. Debugging live is the single most common way a glitch turns into a lost pitch: you've now spent your clock troubleshooting instead of pitching, and the room watched you panic.
- **Name it, normalize it, move on.** "Tech's not cooperating — happens. Here's the screenshot." Said lightly, this actually *builds* rapport, because everyone in the room has been there and they watch you stay composed. The glitch becomes a small demonstration of poise instead of a catastrophe.

The meta-lesson connects straight back to the recording (§2.2b, step 1): you stage the fallback *before* you hit record or take the stage, never during. A founder who has the screenshot ready, knows the pitch without the deck, and refuses to debug live is a founder who can lose the demo and keep the room. That composure under a small failure is exactly the signal the room is reading for — because the road ahead is one small failure after another, and they're betting on someone who doesn't come apart at the first one.

## 2.4 — The hard questions: where the room is actually won

The pitch is the trailer; the Q&A is the interview. This is where a sharp investor, a skeptical customer, or an accelerator panel decides whether your three polished minutes were *evidence* or *performance*. Almost every early-stage pitch faces some version of four questions. Prepare an evidence-based answer to each *before* you pitch, so the ones they pick are easy.

### "How do you know this is a real problem?"

This tests whether your problem slide was evidence or assertion. The answer is your **discovery dossier**: "I ran twelve interviews with clinic managers. Eleven of twelve described the same pain in nearly the same words — the empty slot they can't fill — and all of them already pay for tools that don't solve it. That's not me guessing; that's the pattern across twelve conversations." Name the count, the pattern, and what people pay today. A founder who answers this with "well, we just feel like it's a big problem" has revealed they didn't do the work — and the rest of the pitch is now suspect.

### "Why will customers choose *you* — and why now?"

This is two questions. *Why you* is **founder-market fit**: the standing you have in this problem (Week 01's brief), plus the gap in current workarounds your interviews surfaced. *Why now* is the change that makes this the moment — a new behavior, a new cost, a new technology, a regulation. "Clinics already cobble together two tools, so the demand is proven; what's new is that small clinics now run cloud scheduling systems I can integrate with, which wasn't true three years ago." If you can't answer "why now," the honest version is "the timing isn't the wedge; the wedge is that the existing tools are bad and I can reach these customers cheaply" — which is a fine answer if it's true.

### "Can this make money — and how big can it get?"

This tests your **unit economics** and your honesty about scale. Lead with the headline ratio: "Each clinic is \$99/month, my early LTV:CAC is about 6:1, payback about three months." Then the honest path *and the fragile assumption*: "The number I'm least sure of is retention — I'm projecting clinics stay 18 months and I only have a few months of data, so that's the assumption I'm watching." Naming the fragile assumption *yourself* is a strength: it shows you know which number is load-bearing. The "how big" answer should be grounded too — "~90,000 small clinics in the US, so even a modest share is a real business" beats "the healthcare market is \$4 trillion," which tells the room nothing about *your* slice.

### "What's the riskiest thing — the assumption that, if wrong, sinks this?"

This is the question that most separates founders, and it is often *not* asked directly — but you should answer it anyway, ideally before they ask. Naming your own riskiest assumption calmly is the single most senior move in a Q&A. "The riskiest assumption is that clinics will trust an automated system to message their patients without a front-desk human in the loop. If that's wrong, the whole model breaks. I'm testing it right now by [the test], and here's the early signal." A founder who names their own biggest risk reads as someone who sees the company clearly; a founder who insists there's no big risk reads as someone who hasn't looked.

## 2.5 — Answering without defensiveness

*How* you answer matters as much as *what* you answer. The hard questions feel like attacks; they are not. They are free diligence — the room telling you exactly which risks worry them, which is information you want. The posture:

- **Acknowledge, then answer with evidence.** "Good question — here's what the interviews showed." The brief acknowledgment buys you a half-second to compose and signals you're not rattled.
- **It is fine to say "I don't know yet, and here's how I'd find out."** Honesty about uncertainty, paired with a concrete test plan, beats a confident bluff every time. "I don't have churn data yet because we're three months in — but here's the cohort I'm watching and the number that would worry me." That answer reads as a founder who thinks in experiments, which is the founder this course built.
- **Never argue with the question.** Defensiveness reads as fragility. If a reviewer challenges your market size, the move is not "no, you're wrong, it's huge" — it's "you might be right that the *whole* market is smaller than it looks; the slice I'm going after first is [specific], and here's why that's enough to start." Treat the hard question as a gift, not a fight.
- **Don't bluff a number.** If you don't know your exact CAC, "I don't have a precise CAC yet — my first two customers were essentially free because I closed them myself, so the real number comes once I start paid channels" is a strong answer. "Around fifty bucks" when you're guessing is a weak one, and a sharp investor can always tell, because the follow-up "how'd you get to fifty?" exposes the guess.

The throughline: the founder who treats hard questions as free advice, answers with evidence, and is honest about the edges of what they know reads as exactly the person you'd trust with your money or your career. The founder who gets defensive, argues, or bluffs reads as fragile — and fragility is the one thing early-stage investors won't fund, because the road ahead is *all* hard questions.

## 2.6 — A worked Q&A transcript

Reading the question bank is one thing; hearing a good answer *sound* is another. Here is a reconstructed exchange from a demo-day-style Q&A, lightly edited. The clinic founder is presenting; "Q" is an investor in the room.

> **Q:** You said two clinics are paying. Two is a small number — how do you know this isn't just two clinics being nice to a founder they like?
>
> **Founder:** Fair challenge, and it's the right one to push on. Two things separate "paying" from "being nice." First, they're past the free trial and on the \$99 plan — they had to actively decide to keep paying, and they did. Second, and more telling: one of them asked me last week whether they could add their second location. People don't try to *expand* a tool they're tolerating out of kindness. It's a small number, I won't pretend otherwise, but the *behavior* behind it is real demand, not politeness.
>
> **Q:** Okay. What stops a competitor — or the scheduling system they already use — from just building waitlist auto-fill themselves?
>
> **Founder:** Honestly, nothing stops them from building the feature; it's not deep technology. What's hard to copy is the distribution and the trust. I'm reaching these clinics through founder-led sales and word of mouth between clinic managers who know each other — that network is the moat I'm building, not the feature. And the incumbents have bigger fish than a no-show feature for two-doctor practices; that neglect is exactly my opening. If a big player does decide this segment matters, that's a signal I picked a real market — and by then I want to be the trusted default in it.
>
> **Q:** What's the assumption that worries *you* most?
>
> **Founder:** That clinics will let an automated system message their patients without a human approving each one. My current customers are comfortable with it, but they're early adopters — I don't know yet if mainstream clinics will be. That's the riskiest thing in the whole model, and it's the next thing I'm testing: I'm rolling out an "approve-before-send" mode to see whether the more cautious clinics need a human in the loop. If they all turn it on, my onboarding and my pitch change.

Notice what made those answers strong. The founder *acknowledged* each challenge instead of getting defensive, answered with *specific evidence* (the expansion request, the network, the behavior), was *honest about the weakness* (two is small; competitors can build the feature), and *named her own biggest risk* before being pushed — with the test already running. That is the shape of every good Q&A answer: acknowledge, answer with evidence, own the weakness, connect it to the next experiment.

## 2.6b — The wider objection bank: what else the room throws

The four core questions (§2.4) are the ones you *will* face. Below them sits a wider bank of objections a sharp investor reaches for, clustered by what they're probing. You won't get all of these — a good investor asks the three your deck makes them nervous about — but having an answer ready for each means the three they pick are easy.

### Competition and defensibility

- **"What stops a bigger player from building this?"** Never answer "nothing can replicate us" — that reads as naïve. The honest answer separates the *feature* (often copyable) from the *moat* (distribution, trust, data, switching cost). "The feature is copyable; my distribution into clinic-manager word-of-mouth is the moat, and the incumbents don't care about two-doctor practices — that neglect is my opening."
- **"Who else is doing this, and why are you better?"** Knowing your competitors *cold* is table stakes; not knowing them is disqualifying. The strong answer names the real alternatives (including "a spreadsheet and the front desk," which is usually the true competitor) and the specific gap you fill.

### Go-to-market and scale

- **"This works because you're closing customers yourself. Does it work when you're not in the room?"** This probes whether your traction is *founder-dependent*. The honest answer: "Right now it's founder-led sales, which is normal at this stage. The question I'm testing next is whether word-of-mouth between clinic managers carries it past my own network — that's exactly the experiment in my ask." Naming founder-led sales as a *stage*, not a permanent strategy, reads as someone who's read the GTM playbook.
- **"What's your CAC going to be on a paid channel?"** If you've only done founder-led sales, you don't know yet — say so. "My first customers were essentially free because I closed them; the real CAC comes when I turn on a paid channel, and that's a number I don't want to claim before I've measured it." Honesty here beats a fabricated number every time.

### Money and the raise

- **"How much are you raising and what does it buy?"** (Only if you're doing the fundraising pitch.) The answer ties to your Week 08 cap-table model and names a *milestone*: "\$400K on a post-money SAFE at a \$4M cap; that's eighteen months to get to \$15K MRR, which is the bar that makes a seed round makeable." Never answer "as much as we can get."
- **"Why raise now instead of bootstrapping?"** A real answer names the specific thing the money unlocks that time alone can't. If you don't have one, the honest answer is "I'm not sure I should raise yet" — which is a *strong* answer for a pre-traction concept.

### The team and the founder

- **"Why is this a company and not a feature?"** The trap is defensiveness. The answer: "It might start as a feature, but the wedge gets me the customer relationship, and from there [the expansion path]." Naming the expansion honestly beats insisting it's obviously a billion-dollar company.
- **"What happens if your co-founder leaves?"** This is where your Week 04 vesting-with-a-cliff answer pays off: "We have a four-year vest with a one-year cliff, exactly so that an early departure doesn't leave dead equity on the cap table. We thought about this before it could become a problem." A founder who can answer this calmly has done the unglamorous work.

### The "what's your traction *really*" probe

- **"Walk me through your last five customer conversations."** This is the deepest probe in the bank, because it can't be faked. A founder who's done the work answers it instantly and specifically — "Tuesday I talked to a two-location practice that's deciding between me and their spreadsheet; the blocker is they want approve-before-send" — and a founder who's been hiding behind a deck stalls. The fix isn't a clever answer; it's having actually had the conversations. If you have, this question is a gift, because it's where your nine weeks of discovery show their depth. Lead the room *into* it if you can.
- **"What's the metric you check every morning?"** Probes whether you have a *single* metric that matters (Week 07) or a vanity dashboard. The strong answer names one number and why it's the one: "Trial-to-paid conversion, because at my stage the question isn't awareness, it's whether the product survives onboarding." A founder who lists six metrics has none.

The meta-skill across all of these: the room is not trying to *defeat* you, it's trying to find the risk before it costs them. Every objection is a free piece of diligence telling you which risk worries them most. Write it down; it's your next experiment. And the deepest objections — the customer-conversation probe, the riskiest-assumption question — can't be answered with cleverness, only with work you've actually done. That's by design: they're the questions that separate the founders who did the discovery from the ones who decorated a thin idea, and they're the reason the whole course front-loads the unglamorous middle weeks. The Q&A is where that investment pays off or its absence gets exposed.

## 2.6c — The one answer that fails every time: the bluff

Across the entire objection bank there is exactly one answer that fails universally, regardless of how confidently it's delivered: **the bluffed number.** A founder who, asked for their CAC, says "around fifty bucks" when they're guessing has not bought themselves safety — they've planted a landmine. The follow-up is always "how'd you get to fifty?", and now the guess is exposed *and* every other number in the pitch is retroactively suspect. One bluff caught poisons the well.

The reason bluffing is so tempting and so fatal is the same: a number *sounds* like evidence. But a real investor has heard ten thousand pitches and has finely-tuned instruments for "this person is making this up" — a slight hesitation, a number too round, an answer that doesn't connect to the others. They probe exactly where they smell it, and the bluff collapses. Whereas "I don't have a real CAC yet — my first two customers were essentially free because I closed them myself, so the honest number comes when I turn on a paid channel" is *unfalsifiable and credible*, because it's true and it shows you know which numbers you've earned the right to claim.

The rule, then, is absolute and worth memorizing: **never state a number you can't back, even when not stating it feels like weakness.** "I don't know yet, here's how I'd find out" is not the weak answer it feels like in the moment — it's the strong one, because it reads as a founder who distinguishes measured fact from hopeful guess, which is the single most important discipline in the whole course. The room is not grading you on whether you have every number; it's grading you on whether you *know which numbers you have.* A founder who's honest about the edge of their data is one a room can trust with the data they don't yet have.

## 2.7 — Preparing the canvas: rehearse the answers, don't wing them

The hard questions feel improvisational, but the strong answers are *rehearsed*. Before you pitch, build a hard-question canvas (Exercise 3 walks you through it): for each of the four questions, write the one-line evidence-based answer and name the deliverable it draws on. Then rehearse them out loud the same way you rehearse the pitch — because under the lights, you fall to the level of your preparation, not the height of your hopes.

The discipline the canvas forces: if you cannot write an evidence-based answer to "how do you know this is real," you have found a hole in your evidence *before* the room finds it. That's a gift. Either go get the evidence (one more interview, one more data point) or be ready to answer honestly: "I have eight interviews, not the ten I want; here's the pattern so far and the two I'm still scheduling." Honesty about a known gap beats a bluff that collapses on the first follow-up.

## 2.8 — Demo day: deliver, defend, and assemble

Demo day is the convergence of everything. The shape of your Friday:

1. **Deliver the three minutes** — live, or play your recording. Land at 2:50. Open with the pain, end on the ask.
2. **Take the questions** — calm, evidence-based, honest. This is where the room is actually won or lost (§2.4–2.5).
3. **Turn the pushback into your repo** — the sharpest questions the room asked become the "Open questions / next experiments" section of your capstone README. A repo that says "here are the three things I still need to prove, in priority order, and how I'd test each" is *more* credible than one that pretends the concept is finished, not less. Reviewers read the open-questions section first, because it's where they learn whether you can see your own company clearly.
4. **Post your repo URL** — so a stranger can follow the evidence trail from problem to ask without you in the room. The repo is the lasting artifact; the pitch is its trailer.

The capstone repo is the thing that outlives demo day. The pitch is three minutes; the repo is what an investor reads next week, what a recruiter reads next month, what a co-founder reads when deciding whether to join. Build it so it defends itself — every claim traced to an artifact, the pivot documented, the open questions named. That is what "validated concept" means: not that you have all the answers, but that you have the *evidence* and you know exactly which questions are still open.

## 2.7b — The fundraising Q&A: terms questions you must not fumble

If you give the fundraising pitch (Lecture 1, §1.9d), the Q&A adds a cluster of questions about *terms* — and fumbling these is uniquely damaging, because terms are the one area where vagueness reads as "this person doesn't understand how their own raise works." You do not need to be a lawyer; you need to speak the Week 08 vocabulary cleanly. The questions and the shape of a clean answer:

- **"How much are you raising, and on what instrument?"** Name the number and the instrument in one breath: "I'm raising a $400K pre-seed on a post-money SAFE." A SAFE — Simple Agreement for Future Equity — is the standard early-stage instrument in 2026: it's not debt, it has no maturity date or interest, and it converts to equity at your next priced round. Knowing it's *post-money* (the YC standard since 2018) matters because post-money SAFEs make your dilution math exact and stackable — you can sum the ownership sold across multiple SAFEs directly, which is why the cap-table model from Week 08 works the way it does.

- **"What's the cap, and why that number?"** The valuation cap is the ceiling valuation at which the SAFE converts — a lower cap means the investor gets more ownership for the same check. Don't defend the cap on ego ("we're worth $8M"); defend it on the round math: "A $4M post-money cap on $400K means I'm selling about 10% to this round, which leaves me enough ownership to raise a seed later without being underwater. The cap is set by the dilution I can afford, not by a valuation I'm claiming." That answer shows you understand the cap is a *dilution decision*, not a trophy.

- **"What does the money buy — what milestone?"** Never answer "growth" or "as much runway as possible." Name the specific milestone that makes the *next* round raisable: "Eighteen months to get from two paying clinics to $15K MRR, which is the bar a seed investor wants to see." A room funds a milestone, not a vibe.

- **"Why raise now instead of bootstrapping?"** Name the specific thing the money unlocks that time alone can't. And if you don't have a sharp answer, the honest one — "I'm not sure I should raise yet; I might be better off proving onboarding first" — is *stronger* than a manufactured reason. Raising before you have evidence is the classic early mistake, and a room respects a founder who knows they haven't earned the round yet.

The meta-point: terms questions are not a trap, they're a literacy check. A founder who answers them cleanly has done the Week 08 work and reads as someone who'll be a competent steward of the money. A founder who waffles on "post-money vs. pre-money" or can't connect the cap to the dilution reads as someone who'll be a problem on the cap table later — and that's a reason to pass.

## 2.8b — The failure modes of the *room*, and how to steer them

You are being evaluated, but you are also *running* the eight minutes you get (three to pitch, five for questions). Knowing the room's failure modes lets you steer around them, because a derailed Q&A wastes the one window you have. Each of these is a real archetype you will meet at demo days, office hours, and first investor meetings:

- **The feature-not-a-company skeptic.** They've decided in the first thirty seconds that your concept is "a feature, not a company," and every question is a variation on it. Don't get defensive and don't insist it's obviously huge. Acknowledge the wedge honestly and name the expansion path: "You're right that auto-fill alone is a feature. The wedge gets me the clinic relationship and the scheduling data; from there the company is the operating layer for small-clinic revenue, and here's the first expansion I'd test." You won't always convert them, but a calm expansion story beats a wounded "no, it's definitely a company."

- **The "I would have built it differently" reviewer.** They want to redesign your concept as theirs — a different market, a different model, a different feature. Acknowledge and redirect to *your* evidence: "That's a reasonable bet too; here's the specific reason the interviews pointed me at *this* segment first. Is there a risk in the choice I made that I'm not seeing?" You move them from redesigning to stress-testing, which is the productive version of the same energy.

- **The rabbit-holer.** They fixate on one narrow thing — your exact churn definition, the pricing of a competitor — and burn three of your five minutes there. Time-box it gently: "Happy to go deeper on that after; while I have the room, I want to make sure I answer the bigger question about whether this works outside founder-led sales." You are allowed to manage the clock even though you're the one being questioned.

- **The silent panel.** A row of faces that asks nothing and gives no signal. Silence reads as bad but often isn't — they're taking notes. Draw one thread out yourself: "If it's useful, the question I'd most want pushed on is whether mainstream clinics will trust the automation — that's my riskiest assumption." Volunteering your own hardest question fills the silence productively and reads as confidence.

The throughline: the candidate who *manages* the room — keeps it on the high-value questions, surfaces their own known risks before the room finds them, and writes down what's asked — reads as the founder who can later run a board meeting or a customer call. That composure is the same skill the architecture-review candidate uses in C18: you are the one being reviewed, and you are also the one running the meeting.

## 2.8c — After the room: the follow-up that converts

The most valuable outcomes of a pitch happen *after* the room — the intro, the pilot, the "let's grab coffee" — and they are triggered by a specific thing you said and then *followed up on*. Most founders deliver the pitch, feel the relief, and let the warm moment die in the hallway. The disciplined ones convert it. The mechanics are unglamorous and they matter:

- **Make the follow-up trivially easy in the ask itself.** "If you know a multi-location clinic group, find me after or email me — one line, I'll do the rest." You're removing the friction between their goodwill and an actual action. A vague ask gets vague goodwill; a specific, low-effort ask gets an intro.
- **Send the follow-up within 24 hours, while the pitch is fresh.** A short note: the one-line reminder of what you do, the specific thing you asked for, and the repo link. Not a pitch deck attachment — a link to the repo that defends itself. The person who heard your pitch is your warmest channel to the person who didn't; make it effortless for them to forward.
- **Log every promise the room makes, and the questions it asked.** "Talk to my friend at X," "send me the retention data" — these decay fast. Write them down during or immediately after, and close each loop. A founder who follows through on a hallway promise within two days reads as someone who *executes*, which is exactly the signal that turns a casual intro into a real one.

This is the same "do things that don't scale" discipline that closed your first customers (Week 07): the early pitch isn't a broadcast, it's a set of individual relationships you start in the room and continue by hand. The repo is what they read; the follow-up is what makes them read it. Treat the hour after the pitch as part of the pitch.

## 2.8c-2 — The elevator cut: the 60-second version, and why you build it

The stretch goal asks you to record a 60-second "elevator" cut of the pitch, and it is worth understanding *why* — because the discipline of compressing three minutes to one teaches you which beats actually carry your concept. When you have sixty seconds, you cannot keep all six beats at full length. You are forced to choose, and the choices are revealing:

- **What survives:** the problem (one vivid sentence), the evidence headline (the single most credible number), the solution (one clause), and the ask. Roughly: "Small clinics lose a dozen paid slots a week to no-shows; I interviewed twelve and all of them already pay for tools that don't fix it; I auto-fill the slot from their waitlist; two clinics pay me today; I need intros to multi-location groups." That's about 55 seconds and it's a complete argument.
- **What gets cut:** the model detail, the how-it-works mechanism, the team slide, the market sizing. Not because they don't matter, but because in sixty seconds they're the parts a listener can ask about if they're interested. The elevator cut is *bait for the three-minute version*, the same way the three-minute version is bait for the meeting.

The beats that survive the compression are, by definition, the load-bearing ones — and if you find that your evidence beat doesn't survive (because you led with the product), the compression just told you your three-minute pitch is mis-ordered. The elevator cut is a diagnostic disguised as a deliverable: build it, notice what you instinctively keep, and make sure your full pitch leads with those same things. A founder who can give a clean sixty-second version can give a clean three-minute version; the reverse is not always true, because three minutes lets you hide a buried lead that sixty seconds exposes.

There's also a practical reason: you will give the sixty-second version *far* more often than the three-minute one. The hallway, the coffee line, the "so what do you do?" at a meetup — these are sixty-second moments, and they're where most real intros actually start. The polished demo-day three minutes is the rehearsal; the elevator cut is the version you live in. Build it, rehearse it until it's automatic, and you'll never again waste the most common pitch opportunity there is.

## 2.8d — A note on demo-day nerves for the introvert founder

A word for the founders who read all of this and think "the structure I can do, but the *room* terrifies me." Two things are worth saying plainly. First, the room is not pattern-matching for charisma — it is pattern-matching for "can I trust this person's numbers." A calm, specific, slightly-introverted founder who knows their evidence cold outperforms a slick performer who waves at the gaps, every single time, because the room has seen a thousand slick performers and learned to discount them. Your steadiness *is* the signal.

Second, the antidote to nerves is the same as the antidote to a weak answer: anchor to the evidence. When the adrenaline spikes — and it will, mostly in the opening — return to a number you know cold. "Twelve interviews, two paying clinics." You cannot fumble a fact you actually have, and reporting what's true is far less stressful than performing a feeling you're trying to project. This is the deepest reason the whole course pushes evidence-first: it is not only more *persuasive*, it is more *calming*, because you spend the three minutes reporting reality instead of selling a story you're worried someone will see through. The founders who internalize that walk into the room lighter, because they have nothing to hide and a stack of true things to say.

## 2.8e — The eight minutes, on a clock: a demo-day timeline

It helps to see the whole demo-day slot as a single timed unit, because the parts connect. A typical slot is **three minutes of pitch and about five minutes of questions** — eight minutes total — and here is how a strong one actually runs, beat by beat, so nothing surprises you:

```
0:00–0:20  Problem.    Open with the vivid pain. No "hi, I'm excited." Earn the lean-in.
0:20–1:00  Evidence.   The count, the pattern, what they pay today. Your credibility beat.
1:00–1:30  Solution.   The mechanism + a 10s clip (with screenshot fallback staged).
1:30–2:00  Model.      One price, one ratio, one payback. Not a P&L.
2:00–2:30  Traction.   The strongest REAL number + the honest leak.
2:30–2:50  Ask.        One specific request, matched to the room, left on screen.
2:50       Stop.       You land with a buffer. The ask slide stays up for Q&A.
---
2:50–7:50  Q&A.        ~5 min. Acknowledge → answer with evidence → own the weakness →
                       connect to the next experiment. Name your riskiest assumption
                       before they ask. Write down every question.
7:50–8:00  Close.      "If you know a clinic group, find me after." Make follow-up trivial.
```

Three things this timeline makes visible. First, the **ask slide stays up during Q&A** — it's the lasting image and the thing the room acts on, so you never replace it with a "thank you" slide. Second, the Q&A is *longer than the pitch* — five minutes versus three — which is the quantitative reason Lecture 2 insists the questions are where the room is won. You spend more clock there than on the polished pitch, so prepare accordingly. Third, the **close is part of the slot**: the last ten seconds, where you make the follow-up trivially easy, are not an afterthought — they're where the intro actually gets triggered.

Rehearse against this clock, not just against the three-minute pitch. Have a peer play the room: fire questions for five minutes after your three, and practice the hand-off from pitch to Q&A and from Q&A to close. The founders who freeze are almost always the ones who rehearsed only the pitch and treated the Q&A as something that "just happens." It doesn't just happen — it's five of your eight minutes, and it's the half the room remembers.

## 2.8f — The room's questions are your experiment backlog

Here is the reframe that turns a stressful Q&A into the most useful fifteen minutes of your week: **every question the room asks is a vote on which risk worries an outside observer most.** You are too close to your concept to see its weakest points clearly; the room is not. So the questions are free, expert prioritization of your own risk list — and the disciplined founder captures them as an *experiment backlog*, not a list of wounds.

The mechanic is simple and it's part of the homework retro (Problem 6): during and right after the Q&A, write down every question, especially the ones you couldn't fully answer. Then sort them. The questions that came up most, or that you fumbled, are your next experiments, in priority order:

- "How do you know mainstream clinics will trust the automation?" → the next test is the approve-before-send rollout; the signal is adoption rate.
- "Does it work when you're not the one selling?" → the next test is whether one customer refers another without your involvement.
- "What's your CAC on a paid channel?" → the next test is a small paid pilot once founder-led sales is saturated.

These go straight into the "open questions / next experiments" section of your capstone README (§2.8). That section is not an admission that the concept is unfinished — it's evidence that you can see your own company clearly, which is exactly what a reviewer reads it to find out. A founder who walks out of a Q&A with a prioritized experiment backlog has converted an evaluation into a roadmap. That conversion — turning the hardest questions into the next things you'll go prove — is the same discipline as the whole course: you don't defend a story, you find the next piece of truth and go get it.

The contrast is stark. The founder who treats the Q&A as an attack leaves defensive and learns nothing. The founder who treats it as free diligence leaves with a better-prioritized risk list than they could have built alone, *and* with the relationships started by every person who asked a sharp question and now feels invested in the answer. Same fifteen minutes, opposite outcomes, decided entirely by whether you wrote the questions down as wounds or as experiments.

## 2.9 — What "validated" actually means (and what it doesn't)

A closing point on honesty, because it's the whole posture of this course. "Validated concept" does not mean "proven business." It does not mean you've removed all risk, found product-market fit, or guaranteed success. It means something more modest and more honest: **you talked to enough of the right people, built the smallest thing that tests the idea, and you can defend the result.** You have *evidence*, you know what it does and doesn't prove, and you can name what's still open.

This is why the pitch leads with evidence and the Q&A rewards naming your own risk. The course is not training you to *sell* a dream — it's training you to *present what's true* so clearly that the right people want to help you find out the rest. The founders who internalize this have a durable advantage: while others burn months selling a story they haven't tested, you've built the habit of finding truth fast and saying it plainly. That habit — not this specific idea — is the thing you leave C25 with. The idea may change; the discipline is yours for every company you ever start.

## 2.9b — A note on the camera as a coach

One small practice worth adopting beyond this week: the camera is the most honest coach you will ever have. A mentor is kind; a peer is forgiving; the camera shows you exactly where you lost the thread, sped up, mumbled the claim you don't quite believe, or ran long. Every founder who watches themselves back the first time cringes — and every one of them gets materially better between take one and take three, not because their talent changed in twenty minutes but because the camera *diagnosed* the two weak spots and they fixed exactly those.

Keep using it after the course. Before any high-stakes pitch — an accelerator interview, a first investor call, an important customer demo — record yourself once and watch it back against the self-critique rubric (§2.3b). It feels like overkill and it is the cheapest performance upgrade available. The founders who plateau are usually the ones who stopped watching themselves; the ones who keep improving treat the camera as a permanent, free, brutally honest coach. The discomfort is the whole value: it shows you the gap between how you think you come across and how you actually do, which is the only gap you can't close any other way.

## 2.10 — The course, in one habit

If you take one thing from C25 — not from this lecture, from the whole ten weeks — let it be this: **the founder's edge is finding truth fast and saying it plainly.** Every piece of the course was a rep of that one habit. Discovery interviews: find the truth about the problem. The MVP: find the truth about whether anyone wants the solution. Unit economics: find the truth about whether scaling helps. The pitch and the Q&A: say all of it plainly to a stranger and let them stress-test it.

The reason this matters more than any specific framework is that *the idea will be wrong many times.* The clinic-scheduling concept might pivot three more times before it becomes a business, or it might never become one. What survives every pivot is the founder who notices the idea is wrong quickly — because they're looking for truth, not defending a story — and who can say so plainly enough to bring customers, teammates, and investors along. That founder compounds. The founder who falls in love with a story and sells it past the evidence burns months on each wrong turn, because they can't see the wall until they hit it.

This is why the pitch leads with evidence, why the Q&A rewards naming your own risk, and why "validated concept" — not "funded company" — is the bar. The course is not trying to make you a great *presenter*. It's trying to install the habit of finding truth fast and reporting it without flinching, because that habit is the one durable advantage in a game where almost everything else is uncertain. You leave with a validated concept, yes — but more importantly, you leave with the reflex that produced it. Use it on this idea, and on every idea after it.

And notice that this same reflex is what every artifact this week is built to demonstrate. The recording is honest reporting, edited. The deck is evidence with the adjectives cut. The hard-question canvas is you naming your own risks before the room does. The capstone repo is the whole evidence trail, laid out so a stranger can audit it. None of these is a presentation trick — each is the truth-finding habit made visible. So when you build them this week, you're not assembling a portfolio of pitch theater; you're producing the proof that you can find what's true and say it plainly. That proof is what gets the next conversation, and the habit behind it is what gets the one after that.

## Summary

You record the pitch not as a checkbox but to scale your one shot, to force the pitch tight, and as a comprehension test — every place you lose the thread on camera is a place your evidence is thin. Record clean and audio-first with free tools (OBS or Loom, Audacity for sound, Shotcut to cut to time), watch yourself back, fix the two worst moments, and end on the ask. The Q&A is where the room is actually won: prepare evidence-based answers to the four questions — is the problem real, why you/why now, can it make money/how big, and what's the riskiest assumption — and rehearse them on a canvas so you don't wing them. Answer without defensiveness: acknowledge, answer with evidence, own the weakness, say "I don't know yet, here's how I'd find out" when that's the truth, and never bluff a number. At demo day you deliver, defend, turn the pushback into your repo's open-questions section, and post a repo that defends itself. "Validated" means you have evidence and know what's still open — not that you've proven a business. That honest discipline, not this one idea, is what you take with you.
