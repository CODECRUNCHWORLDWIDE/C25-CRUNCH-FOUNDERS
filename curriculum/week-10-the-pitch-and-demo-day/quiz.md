# Week 10 — Quiz

Twelve questions. This is the last quiz of the course; it mixes pitch-week material (the structure, the deck, recording, the hard questions) with the synthesis questions a demo-day room or an accelerator interviewer actually asks. Take it with your notes closed. Aim for 10/12. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** A strong early-stage pitch is best described as:

- A) A vision statement full of ambitious adjectives.
- B) Evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo.
- C) A live product demo with every feature shown.
- D) A detailed five-year financial forecast.

---

**Q2.** The reliable 3-minute structure opens with:

- A) The ask, to grab attention.
- B) The team's résumés and former employers.
- C) The problem and the evidence it is real.
- D) The valuation and the round size.

---

**Q3.** In the six-beat timing budget, which beat gets the *most* seconds, and why?

- A) Solution, because the product is what matters.
- B) Evidence, because the discovery proof is the credibility that removes the risk the room cares about.
- C) Ask, because it's the close.
- D) Model, because investors only care about money.

---

**Q4.** If your discovery work led you to pivot, in the pitch you should:

- A) Hide it for a cleaner, never-wavered story.
- B) Say so — "we started with X, the interviews showed Y, so we re-aimed" — because it proves your evidence changed your mind.
- C) Pretend the original idea was right all along.
- D) Drop the evidence slide so no one notices.

---

**Q5.** On a lean deck, the discipline that most separates a strong slide from a weak one is:

- A) Adding more bullet points so nothing is missed.
- B) One idea per slide, with a headline written as a *claim* (not a label) and every adjective a number can't back deleted.
- C) A grid of partner logos to look established.
- D) A "powered by AI" banner on every slide.

---

**Q6.** The single best way to size your market in the pitch is:

- A) Top-down: "Healthcare is a $4 trillion market and we just need 1%."
- B) Bottom-up: reachable customers × price = serviceable market, with the key assumption stated.
- C) Quote the largest industry figure you can find.
- D) Skip market size entirely; it never matters.

**Q7.** Which counts as the *strongest* traction to lead with on a pitch?

- A) 10,000 newsletter signups.
- B) 50,000 social followers.
- C) Two paying customers past the free trial.
- D) 100,000 website visits.

**Q8.** Why do you record the pitch (beyond it being a capstone checkbox)?

- A) Only because the grader requires a file.
- B) It scales your one shot to people not in the room, forces the pitch tight, and is a comprehension test — every place you lose the thread on camera is a place your evidence is thin.
- C) To prove you own a good camera.
- D) Because live pitches are never allowed.

**Q9.** When recording, which matters *most* to perceived quality?

- A) A 4K camera and a cinematic background.
- B) Clean, audible audio (a quiet room, close to the mic) — bad audio loses an audience faster than a plain webcam.
- C) Animated slide transitions.
- D) A professional studio.

**Q10.** A reviewer asks a hard question you can't fully answer. The strongest response is:

- A) Argue that the question is wrong.
- B) Bluff a confident number.
- C) "I don't know yet, and here's exactly how I'd find out" — honesty plus a concrete test plan.
- D) Change the subject quickly.

**Q11.** Naming your own *riskiest assumption* — calmly, before being asked — is:

- A) A confession of failure that sinks the pitch.
- B) The most senior move in a Q&A: it shows you see the company clearly, whereas insisting there's no big risk shows you haven't looked.
- C) Irrelevant; never volunteer a weakness.
- D) Only for fundraising pitches, never for evidence pitches.

---

**Q12.** The "it survives a stranger" capstone gate means:

- A) The deck has the most slides in the cohort.
- B) A grader opens your repo cold, follows the README from problem to ask, and finds every pitch claim traced to an artifact — a claim the repo can't back fails it.
- C) Your recording has the highest production value.
- D) You delivered the pitch fastest.

## Answer key

**Q1 — B.** A pitch is a structured argument from evidence whose job is to earn the next conversation, not applause. (Lecture 1, §1.1.)

**Q2 — C.** Problem and evidence first, before the solution — evidence removes the risk the room cares about; opening with the ask or the team earns nothing yet. (Lecture 1, §1.2, §1.4.)

**Q3 — B.** The evidence beat gets ~40s — the most — because it's your credibility slide; proof you did the discovery is what de-risks the concept for the room. (Lecture 1, §1.2, §1.3.)

**Q4 — B.** Owning the pivot proves your evidence changed your mind — the single most valuable founder trait. A too-clean story reads as "didn't really talk to customers." (Lecture 1, §1.6.)

**Q5 — B.** One idea per slide, headline-as-claim, and the red-pen pass on adjectives. Logo clouds and buzzword banners read as decoration hiding a thin argument. (Lecture 1, §1.5, §1.9a.)

**Q6 — B.** Bottom-up sizing is defensible and credible; "1% of a huge market" proves you haven't thought about your actual first customers. State the key assumption on the slide. (Lecture 1, §1.9b.)

**Q7 — C.** Paying customers > trials > signups > followers. Investors discount vanity metrics to near zero; two paying customers is real revenue and real proof. (Lecture 1, §1.10; Week 07.)

**Q8 — B.** You record to scale the one shot, to force the pitch tight, and as a self-administered comprehension test — not as a checkbox. (Lecture 2, §2.1.)

**Q9 — B.** Audio first. People forgive a plain webcam; they click away from muddy, echoey sound in seconds. (Lecture 2, §2.2.)

**Q10 — C.** Honesty about uncertainty paired with a test plan beats a bluff every time, and reads as a founder who thinks in experiments. Arguing or changing the subject reads as fragile. (Lecture 2, §2.5.)

**Q11 — B.** Naming your own biggest risk calmly is the most senior Q&A move; insisting there's no risk reads as someone who hasn't looked. It applies to both pitch shapes. (Lecture 2, §2.4.)

**Q12 — B.** "Validated" means the repo defends itself: a stranger follows the evidence trail from problem to ask, and every claim traces to an artifact. (README, "it survives a stranger"; Lecture 2, §2.8–2.9.)
