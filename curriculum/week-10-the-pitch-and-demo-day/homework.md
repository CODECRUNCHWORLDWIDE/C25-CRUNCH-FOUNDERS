# Week 10 Homework

This is the last homework of C25, and it is not "more problems" — it is the set of capstone deliverables that are not already covered by the mini-project assembly itself. Each one is a concrete artifact a real angel, an accelerator interviewer, or a future co-founder will read or watch. The full set should take about **5.5 hours** spread across the week. Work in your capstone repo so each deliverable is a commit you can point to.

Each problem includes a **statement**, **deliverable**, **acceptance criteria**, a **hint**, and an **estimated time**. The rubric at the bottom is how the week is graded.

---

## Problem 1 — Build the deck

**Statement.** Build the 8–10 slide pitch deck from your Exercise 2 storyboard, following the Lecture 1 §1.5 order. One idea per slide; each headline a *claim*, not a label; one visual per slide. Export to PDF and note which deliverable backs each slide.

**Deliverable.** `pitch/deck.pdf` (8–10 slides) plus `notes/deck-notes.md` mapping each slide to the artifact behind it.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] 8–10 slides in the canonical order (title, problem, evidence, solution, how-it-works, model, unit economics, traction, team, ask).
- [ ] One idea per slide; every headline reads as a claim a stranger could repeat.
- [ ] Every adjective a number can't back is cut (the red-pen pass is visible — no "revolutionary," no logo clouds).
- [ ] `deck-notes.md` names the repo artifact behind each slide.
- [ ] Exported to PDF and committed.

**Hint.** Build *from the storyboard*, not from scratch — the deciding already happened in Exercise 2, so this is half an hour of formatting. Run the headline-sequence test on the finished deck: read only the headlines aloud and confirm they tell the whole story.

**Estimated time.** 90 minutes.

## Problem 2 — Record the 3-minute pitch

**Statement.** Record your 3-minute pitch (OBS or Loom), audio-first, edited to time, ending on the ask. Watch it back, fix the two worst moments, and link it.

**Deliverable.** `pitch/RECORDING.md` with the link and a one-line note on what you fixed between the first take and the final.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] Runs **≤3:00** and ends on the single specific ask, held on screen.
- [ ] Audio is consistently clear on a phone speaker (no echo, no muddiness, no dipping below audible).
- [ ] Opens with the pain, not the product or "hi, I'm excited."
- [ ] Leads with the strongest *real* traction and names the honest leak.
- [ ] Linked and committed.

**Hint.** Audio quality matters more than video. Record one full take without stopping, watch it once against the self-critique rubric (Lecture 2, §2.3b), fix the two worst moments, and re-record just those. Two or three diagnosed takes beat ten anxious ones.

**Estimated time.** 90 minutes.

---

## Problem 3 — The hard-questions canvas, completed and rehearsed

**Statement.** From your Exercise 3 canvas, finalize evidence-based answers to the four core questions and your riskiest-assumption line, then rehearse them out loud with a peer firing the questions at you in random order.

**Deliverable.** `notes/hard-questions.md` (the completed canvas) plus a one-line note that you rehearsed it live and which answer needed the most work.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] An evidence-based answer to each of the four core questions, each citing a deliverable or a concrete test plan.
- [ ] Market sizing is bottom-up; no answer relies on a bluffed number.
- [ ] The riskiest assumption is one calm sentence with the live test and the early signal.
- [ ] You rehearsed the four answers out loud (noted who fired the questions).
- [ ] Committed.

**Hint.** Treat the questions as free diligence, not attacks. If you can't write an evidence-based answer to "how do you know this is real," that's a hole in your evidence — go get the interview, or be ready with the honest "I have 8, not 10; here's the pattern so far."

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

## Problem 4 — The one-page evidence appendix

**Statement.** Build a single appendix page an investor can request *after* the pitch: the artifact behind your strongest claim, made glanceable. Usually the discovery pattern (counts + the verbatim quote), or the unit-economics sensitivity, or the cohort/retention view you have data for.

**Deliverable.** `pitch/appendix.md` (or one extra slide), one page, with the source artifact linked.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] One page, one claim, the evidence made glanceable (a count, a small table, or one quote — not a wall of text).
- [ ] The source artifact in the repo is linked.
- [ ] It backs a claim that actually appears in the pitch (no orphan appendix).
- [ ] Committed.

**Hint.** Investors who lean in after a pitch ask for *one* thing — the data behind the number that surprised them. Stage the appendix for that moment so you can put it up instead of saying "I'll send it later."

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

---

## Problem 5 — Assemble the capstone repo README

**Statement.** Write the top `README.md` of your capstone repo so a stranger can follow the evidence trail from problem to ask with no other context. Cross-link every artifact in order, and add an honest "open questions / next experiments" section.

**Deliverable.** A top `README.md` linking all capstone artifacts, with an open-questions section populated from your own analysis (expanded after demo day with the room's sharpest questions).

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] Tells the story in order (problem → evidence → solution → model → traction → ask) and links each artifact.
- [ ] Every pitch claim is traceable to a linked artifact (the "survives a stranger" gate).
- [ ] An "open questions / next experiments" section names what's still unproven, in priority order, each with how you'd test it.
- [ ] No private identifiers; no copyrighted text.
- [ ] Public and committed.

**Hint.** Reviewers read the open-questions section *first* — it's where they learn whether you see your own company clearly. A repo that says "here are the three things I still need to prove" is more credible than one that pretends the concept is finished.

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

---

## Problem 6 — The demo-day retrospective

**Statement.** After you deliver the pitch and take questions, write a half-page retrospective: the two questions you answered well, the one you fumbled, what you'd say differently, and the single sharpest question the room asked that you hadn't prepared.

**Deliverable.** `notes/demo-day-retro.md`.

**Acceptance criteria.**

- [ ] Names two strengths and one fumble, with the better answer for the fumble.
- [ ] Records the sharpest unprepared question and where it now lives (the open-questions section).
- [ ] Honest — a retro that says "I nailed everything" fails this problem.
- [ ] Committed.

**Hint.** The fumble is the valuable part. A founder who can name their own weak answer and articulate the better one has the metacognition that makes the *next* pitch go better. The sharpest unprepared question is your next experiment.

**Estimated time.** 35 minutes.

## Rubric

Graded out of 100. This homework is the deliverable scaffolding for the capstone, so the weights mirror the capstone's emphasis on the deck, the recording, and surviving the questions.

| Problem | Artifact | Points | What earns full marks |
|---|---|---:|---|
| 1 | `pitch/deck.pdf` + `deck-notes.md` | 20 | 8–10 slides, one idea each, claim-headlines, red-pen done, every slide mapped to an artifact. |
| 2 | `pitch/RECORDING.md` | 20 | ≤3:00, clean audio, opens with pain, real traction + honest leak, ends on the ask. |
| 3 | `notes/hard-questions.md` | 20 | Four evidence-based answers + a calm riskiest-assumption line; bottom-up sizing; rehearsed live. |
| 4 | `pitch/appendix.md` | 10 | One page, one claim made glanceable, source linked, backs a real pitch claim. |
| 5 | top `README.md` | 20 | Story in order, every claim traceable, honest open-questions section, public. |
| 6 | `notes/demo-day-retro.md` | 10 | Two strengths, one honest fumble + the better answer, the sharpest unprepared question captured. |

**Passing the week** requires ≥70 on this rubric *and* a capstone that survives a stranger (the mini-project / Challenge 1 gate). The two are scored together; a perfect homework with a repo where a pitch claim has no artifact behind it does not pass.

**Deductions.** Any pitch claim with no artifact behind it in the repo: −15 and a required fix. Leading with a vanity metric instead of real traction: −10. A bluffed number in the hard-questions canvas that collapses on the obvious follow-up: −10. An over-run that gets you cut off before the ask in the live delivery: −10. A "validated concept" repo that hides its open questions: −10. These are the production-shop non-negotiables — the deductions are deliberately harsh because in a real raise they are the difference between an intro and a pass.
