# Week 10 — The Pitch + Demo Day (Capstone)

Welcome to the last week of **C25 · Crunch Founders**. You do not learn a new framework this week. You assemble, you record, and you defend.

Everything you have built since Week 01 — the founder brief, the triaged idea, the ten discovery interviews, the co-founder agreement, the lean MVP and its result, the business model canvas, the unit-economics sheet, the go-to-market test, the cap-table model, and the incorporation-and-ops checklist — gets assembled into one coherent story: a **validated startup concept**, told in a deck and a three-minute pitch, in front of a room. Then you defend it.

"Defend it" is not a metaphor. Demo day is a pre-investment review with a Q&A bolted on. You stand in front of peers (or a cohort lead, or a recorded camera that a real angel or accelerator interviewer will eventually watch), you walk one customer's pain through your evidence, and you answer the questions a seed investor asks when they are deciding whether your traction is real or whether you are selling a story. You produce an 8–10 slide deck, a three-minute recorded pitch edited to time, a one-page evidence appendix, and a public repo that lets a stranger follow your evidence trail from problem to ask without you in the room. And you survive the **three obvious hard questions** live — that is the gate, not a participation slot.

The pitch is not where you start believing your idea. It is where you *present the evidence* you spent nine weeks gathering. A good early-stage pitch is **evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo.** This week the rhythm is: assemble the artifacts early, build the deck and cut it to one-idea-per-slide mid-week, record and rehearse the hard-question drill, then deliver and defend at demo day. If your Week 03 dossier is still at five interviews, fix that first — the capstone gate is ten *synthesized* interviews, and a grader who finds five cherry-picked write-ups behind a slide that claims "customers are desperate for this" stops trusting every other number in the deck.

This is the week the course has been building toward. Treat it like a launch.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Assemble** every prior-week artifact into one public capstone repo that walks a stranger from problem to ask, cross-linked from a top `README.md`.
- **Structure** a 3-minute pitch on the reliable shape — problem → evidence → solution → model → traction → ask — with a per-beat timing budget that lands under 3:00.
- **Build** a lean deck of about 8–10 slides where each slide carries exactly one idea and no claim outruns the evidence behind it.
- **Lead with evidence**, not vision: your interviews, your MVP result, and your traction metric are the credibility, and every adjective on a slide either earns its place with a number or gets cut.
- **Tell the discovery story honestly**, including the pivot your interviews forced — and understand why owning the pivot reads as *more* fundable, not less.
- **Record and edit** a clean three-minute pitch with free tools, where audio is prioritized over polish and the ask is the last thing on screen.
- **Anticipate and answer** the three obvious hard questions — "is the problem real," "why you / why now," "can this make money / how big" — plus "what's your riskiest assumption," each answered with evidence and without defensiveness.
- **Make a specific ask** matched to the audience, instead of wasting the most important sentence of the pitch on "we're looking for support."
- **Defend the concept live** at demo day, take questions, and turn the room's pushback into the "open questions" section of your repo rather than a list of wounds.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed Weeks 01–09 of C25 and that each produced a real, committed artifact. Specifically, you need:

- A **founder brief** with your unfair advantages and your control-vs-wealth lean. (Week 01.)
- A **chosen idea** carried out of the idea-triage log against a problem-first filter. (Week 02.)
- A **discovery dossier** of interview write-ups and a first pattern summary — you need to reach **≥10 synthesized interviews** this week if you have not already. (Week 03, grown.)
- A **co-founder agreement draft** — roles, decision rights, an equity split with stated reasoning, and a vesting schedule with a cliff. (Week 04. If you are solo, the single-founder version: who joins next and on what terms.)
- A **lean MVP** wired to one metric, plus its result and your persevere/pivot decision. (Week 05.)
- A **lean canvas** plus a **unit-economics sheet** — CAC, LTV, contribution margin, payback — with assumptions stated. (Week 06.)
- A **go-to-market one-pager** naming the motion, the first channel tested, and the single traction metric you hold yourself to, with its result. (Week 07.)
- A **cap-table model** showing founder ownership before and after a hypothetical pre-seed round, with dilution computed. (Week 08.)
- An **incorporation & ops checklist** tailored to your concept, with open questions flagged for a real advisor. (Week 09.)

If any of those is missing, thin, or stale, this week will expose it. That is the point. The pitch is only as strong as the weakest artifact behind it.

## Topics covered

- How a demo-day pitch actually works: the room, the clock, the Q&A, and the failure modes of the *founder* as well as the pitch.
- The 3-minute pitch structure and timing budget: the six beats, the seconds each gets, and why three minutes is a feature, not a constraint.
- The lean deck: one idea per slide, the canonical 8–10 slide order, and the discipline of cutting every adjective a number cannot back.
- Evidence-first storytelling: leading with the problem and the proof it is real, and why the discovery dossier is the credibility, not the vision slide.
- Telling the pivot honestly: why "we started with X, the interviews showed Y, so we re-aimed" reads as the disciplined founder this course trained, not as a flaw.
- The ask: making it specific, matched to the audience, and the last thing on screen.
- Recording cleanly with free tools (OBS, Loom): audio-first, editing to time, ending on the ask.
- The hard-question bank: the investor question set, evidence-based answers, naming your own riskiest assumption, and answering without defensiveness.
- Demo-day delivery and the capstone repo: assembling the evidence trail so a stranger can follow it from problem to ask without you in the room.

## In-class activities

- **Pitch gauntlet.** Everyone delivers their 3-minute pitch to the room and gets timed. Over-time pitches are cut off mid-sentence, exactly as at a real demo day — the clock is the lesson.
- **Hard-question fire drill.** The room throws the three obvious tough questions at each founder in rapid succession; calm, evidence-based answers score, defensiveness and bluffing do not.
- **Deck red-pen.** Pairs swap decks and delete every adjective that a number does not back. Whoever cuts more words usually has the stronger deck afterward.

## Worked example (the through-line you'll see all week)

A clinic-scheduling founder runs the week. Her 3-minute pitch: **Problem** — no-shows quietly bleed revenue from small clinics (~20s). **Evidence** — 12 interviews; clinics already pay for two clumsy tools to fight this; cited cost per no-show (~40s). **Solution** — waitlist auto-fill, the pivot her discovery surfaced when interviews showed the real pain was the empty slot, not the missed appointment (~30s, with a 10s clickable-prototype clip). **Model** — \$99/month per clinic, ~6:1 LTV:CAC, ~3-month payback (~30s). **Traction** — 2 paying clinics from founder-led sales, and the honest leak she found at the demo→trial step (~30s). **Ask** — three warm intros to multi-location clinic groups (~20s). Every claim traces to a deliverable in her repo. No "revolutionary," no "huge market" hand-waving — just evidence, clearly told. You will see her appear in the lectures, the exercises, and the challenge as the running example.

## The "it survives a stranger" promise

C25 has one recurring marker, and Week 10 is where it cashes out:

```
A stranger reads your repo cold and can answer: who hurts, how you know, what you built, and what you want.
```

The grader will open your capstone repo with no other context, follow the top `README.md` from problem to ask, and check that each claim in the pitch traces to an artifact in the repo. If a slide makes a claim your repo cannot back — "customers love it" with no interview behind it, "huge market" with no number — the capstone does not pass, regardless of how slick the deck looks. A concept you can only defend when you are in the room narrating it is not a validated concept; it is a story you are attached to. Build the evidence trail so the repo defends itself.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Record a **second, 60-second** version of the pitch (the "elevator" cut) and note which beats survived the compression. The beats you keep are the ones that actually carry the concept.
- Get your pitch in front of **one real outsider** — a founder you don't know, a local meetup, an accelerator office-hours slot — and write a one-paragraph note on the single hardest question they asked that the cohort didn't.
- Build the **one-slide appendix** investors actually request after a pitch: the cohort/retention curve, or the unit-economics sensitivity table, and be ready to put it up if asked.
- Write the **raise memo** version of the ask: if you *were* raising a pre-seed on a SAFE, the amount, the cap, the discount, and exactly what 12 months of that money buys (tie it to the cap-table model from Week 08).
- Read one **published demo-day deck or seed memo** (the YC and a16z resources in `resources.md`) and write a half-page note on one structural move they made that yours doesn't.
