# Exercise 3 — The Hard-Questions Canvas

> **Estimated time:** ~50 minutes (30 min drafting answers, 10 min the riskiest-assumption line, 10 min rehearsing out loud). **Output:** a committed `exercise-03-hard-questions-canvas.md` you rehearse from before demo day.

This is a **rehearsal canvas**, not an essay. The pitch is the trailer; the Q&A is the interview — it's where a sharp investor, a skeptical customer, or an accelerator panel decides whether your three polished minutes were *evidence* or *performance*. A founder who nails the pitch and then fumbles "how do you know this is real?" loses the room they just won. The hard questions *feel* improvisational, but the strong answers are rehearsed. This canvas is where you write the evidence-based answer to each question you *will* face, name the deliverable it draws on, and — the most senior move in any Q&A — state your own riskiest assumption before anyone asks.

## Goal

Build a one-page canvas with: an evidence-based answer to each of the four core hard questions (each citing a deliverable or a concrete test plan), short answers to the wider objection bank, and your single riskiest assumption stated calmly in one sentence with the test you're running. Then rehearse the four core answers out loud until they're automatic.

## Why a canvas, not winging it

Under the lights you fall to the level of your preparation, not the height of your hopes. Three reasons this canvas earns its 50 minutes:

1. **The gaps surface here, cheaply.** If you cannot write an evidence-based answer to "how do you know this is real," you've found a hole in your evidence *before* the room finds it. Go get the evidence, or be ready to answer the honest version: "I have 8 interviews, not the 10 I want; here's the pattern so far."
2. **Rehearsed answers sound calm; winged answers sound defensive.** The difference between "acknowledge, answer with evidence, own the weakness" and a rattled bluff is almost entirely preparation.
3. **The hard questions are free diligence.** Each one tells you exactly which risk worries the room. Writing them down turns the Q&A from an ambush into your next experiment list.

## Step 1 — Copy the template into your repo

Create `exercises/exercise-03-hard-questions-canvas.md` and paste this.

```markdown
# Hard-questions canvas — <company / concept>

## The four core questions (you WILL face these)

### 1. "How do you know this is a real problem?"
- Answer (evidence-based): <____>
- Backed by: <discovery dossier — count + pattern + what they pay today>

### 2. "Why will customers choose you — and why now?"
- Why YOU (founder-market fit): <____>  (backed by: founder brief)
- Why NOW (the change that makes this the moment): <____>
- If there's no "why now": <the honest wedge — existing tools are bad + I reach these customers cheaply>

### 3. "Can this make money — and how big can it get?"
- The headline ratio: <LTV:CAC, payback>  (backed by: unit economics)
- The fragile assumption I'm watching: <e.g. retention — I project X months, have Y data>
- How big (bottom-up, NOT top-down): <reachable customers × price = serviceable market>

### 4. "What's the riskiest assumption — the thing that, if wrong, sinks this?"
- The riskiest assumption: <____>
- How I'm testing it right now: <____>
- The early signal: <____>

## The wider objection bank (one line each, ready but not memorized)
- Competition: "What stops a bigger player building this?" → <feature is copyable; my MOAT is ____>
- Competition: "Who else does this, why are you better?" → <name real alternatives incl. "a spreadsheet + the front desk">
- GTM: "Does it work when you're not in the room?" → <founder-led sales is a STAGE; next test is ____>
- GTM: "What's your CAC on a paid channel?" → <don't know yet; first customers were ~free; won't claim a number I haven't measured>
- Money: "How much are you raising / what does it buy?" → <only if raising: $X SAFE @ $Y cap = Z months to MILESTONE>
- Money: "Why raise now vs. bootstrap?" → <the specific thing the money unlocks; or honestly "not sure I should raise yet">
- Team: "Why is this a company and not a feature?" → <the wedge gets the relationship; expansion path is ____>
- Team: "What if your co-founder leaves?" → <4-year vest, 1-year cliff — we handled this before it could be a problem>

## My posture reminders (re-read before Q&A)
- Acknowledge, THEN answer with evidence.
- "I don't know yet, and here's how I'd find out" beats a bluff every time.
- Never argue with the question. Never bluff a number.
- Every objection is free diligence — write it down, it's my next experiment.
```

## Step 2 — Draft the four core answers

These are the four you *will* face (Lecture 2, §2.4). The shape of a strong answer is always the same: **acknowledge, answer with specific evidence, own the weakness, connect to the next experiment.**

- **"How do you know this is real?"** Name the count, the pattern, and what people pay today. "11 of 12 described the same pain in nearly the same words, and all 12 already pay for tools that don't solve it." Not "we feel like it's a big problem" — that reveals you didn't do the work.
- **"Why you / why now?"** *Why you* is founder-market fit (earned insight, unfair distribution, durable motivation). *Why now* is the change that makes this the moment. If there's no clean "why now," the honest wedge — "the existing tools are bad and I can reach these customers cheaply" — is a fine answer if true.
- **"Can it make money / how big?"** Lead with the headline ratio, then name the *fragile* assumption yourself (retention, CAC on paid channels). Size the market **bottom-up** ("~30,000 reachable clinics × $99/mo ≈ $36M serviceable"), never top-down ("1% of a $4 trillion market" — the single most discrediting move in the deck).
- **"What's your riskiest assumption?"** Often not asked directly — answer it anyway. Naming your own biggest risk calmly is the most senior move in a Q&A. See Step 3.

## Step 3 — Write your riskiest-assumption line

This is the line that most separates founders. In one calm sentence: name the assumption that, if wrong, sinks the company; name the test you're running right now; name the early signal. The clinic founder's:

> "The riskiest assumption is that clinics will trust an automated system to message their patients without a human in the loop. I'm testing it now with an 'approve-before-send' mode — early adopters are comfortable, but mainstream clinics are the unknown, and if they all turn it on, my onboarding and my pitch change."

A founder who names their own biggest risk reads as someone who sees the company clearly. A founder who insists there's no big risk reads as someone who hasn't looked. Write yours; rehearse it until it's calm, not nervous.

## Step 4 — Stock the wider objection bank

Fill the one-line answers for the wider bank. You won't get all of these — a good investor asks the three your deck makes them nervous about — but having an answer ready means the three they pick are easy. The meta-skill: the room is not trying to *defeat* you, it's trying to find the risk before it costs them. Every objection is a free piece of diligence.

## Step 5 — Rehearse the four core answers out loud

Out loud, the same way you rehearse the pitch. Have someone fire the four questions at you in random order and answer without notes. You're not memorizing a script — you're getting the *evidence* and the *posture* automatic, so under pressure you acknowledge, cite the number, own the weakness, and connect to the next experiment, every time. If an answer comes out defensive or bluffed in rehearsal, that's the one to drill.

## Expected output

A completed canvas you can rehearse from, where every core answer cites a real deliverable and your riskiest assumption is one honest sentence with a live test. A strong drafted answer, for reference (the "two paying customers" challenge):

```markdown
### Objection: "Two clinics is a small number — aren't they just being nice?"
Answer: "Fair challenge. Two things separate paying from politeness. One, they're past
the free trial on the $99 plan — they actively chose to keep paying. Two, one asked last
week to add their second location. People don't try to EXPAND a tool they're tolerating.
It's a small number, but the behavior behind it is real demand."
Backed by: gtm/ (the expansion request logged), billing records (post-trial).
```

## Common mistakes (and the fix)

- **A top-down market answer.** "1% of a huge market." Fix: bottom-up — reachable customers × price, with the assumption on the slide.
- **Insisting there's no big risk.** Reads as someone who hasn't looked. Fix: name your riskiest assumption proudly, with the test.
- **Bluffing CAC or churn.** A guessed number collapses on the follow-up. Fix: "I don't have that yet; here's why and how I'll get it."
- **Memorizing scripts instead of evidence.** You'll sound robotic and freeze when the wording shifts. Fix: rehearse the *evidence and posture*, not the exact words.
