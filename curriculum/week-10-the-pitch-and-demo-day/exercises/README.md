# Week 10 — Exercises

Three exercises, in the order you do them. They are the proof-of-readiness steps that gate the capstone delivery: you cannot deliver a pitch you have not outlined and timed, you cannot stand behind a deck you have not storyboarded down to one idea per slide, and you cannot survive the Q&A for questions you have not rehearsed an evidence-based answer to. Do them against your *actual* capstone — your real interviews, your real MVP result, your real traction number — not against a hypothetical. The clinic-scheduling founder from the lectures is the worked example throughout; your job is to fill the same templates with your own evidence.

| 1 | [exercise-01-pitch-outline.md](./exercise-01-pitch-outline.md) | Draft your 3-minute pitch beat by beat with a timing budget, and tie every claim to a deliverable in your repo. | ~50 min |

| 3 | [exercise-03-hard-questions-canvas.md](./exercise-03-hard-questions-canvas.md) | Build the hard-question canvas: an evidence-based answer to each of the four investor questions, plus your riskiest assumption stated calmly. | ~50 min |

## What these are (and aren't)

- **All three are Markdown worksheets / canvases / templates.** This is a founder course, not a programming course — you write no code. Exercise 1 is a pitch outline, Exercise 2 is a deck storyboard, Exercise 3 is a Q&A rehearsal canvas. Each ships a fill-in template you copy into your capstone repo and complete with your own evidence.
- They are **sequential and compounding.** The pitch outline (Ex 1) becomes the spine of the deck storyboard (Ex 2); the riskiest-assumption line in Ex 1 expands into the full hard-question canvas (Ex 3). Do them in order.
- They feed the homework and the mini-project directly: Ex 2 is the blueprint for Homework Problem 1 (build the deck), and Ex 3 is the rehearsal for Homework Problem 3 (hard-question prep) and the Friday demo-day Q&A.

## The non-negotiable rule

**Every claim traces to an artifact in your repo.** If a beat, a slide, or a Q&A answer asserts something — "customers are desperate for this," "the economics work," "we have traction" — there must be a deliverable behind it (the discovery dossier, the unit-economics sheet, the GTM one-pager). If you cannot name the deliverable, you have found a hole in your evidence *before* the room finds it. That is a gift: either go get the evidence (one more interview, one more data point) or cut the claim. A pitch is evidence wearing a clear structure, not adjectives wearing a logo.

## Checklist — you have cleared the exercises when

- [ ] **Exercise 1:** all six beats outlined with a one-line script each, a timing budget summing to ≤180 seconds, and the deliverable named behind every evidence-bearing beat. Read aloud and timed under 3:00.
- [ ] **Exercise 2:** every slide (8–10) has exactly one idea, a headline written as a *claim* (not a label), and a named evidence source. The headlines, read in sequence, tell the whole story.
- [ ] **Exercise 3:** an evidence-based answer drafted for each of the four hard questions, each citing a deliverable or a concrete test plan, plus your single riskiest assumption stated in one calm sentence with the test you're running.
- [ ] All three worksheets committed to your Week 10 capstone repo.

## Rules

- Time everything strictly. People read silently faster than they speak, so a pitch that "feels" like three minutes in your head is usually four out loud. Use a timer, not a feeling.
- Cut adjectives. Run the red-pen pass on every worksheet: if "huge market" has a number behind it, replace the word with the number; if it does not, cut the word.
- Lead with the problem and the evidence, before the solution — in the outline, on the deck, and in your Q&A answers. It is the posture of the whole course.
