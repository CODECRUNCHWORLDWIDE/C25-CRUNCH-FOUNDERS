# Week 6 — Business Models & Unit Economics

You have evidence the problem is real (Weeks 3) and that people will adopt a solution (Week 5). Now the harder question: *can this ever make money?* Many startups grow enthusiastically while losing money on every customer, then scale the loss. This week you map the business model on one page and compute the unit economics that decide whether growth helps or hurts — contribution margin, customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), and payback period.

The deliverable is a lean business model canvas plus a unit-economics sheet with assumptions stated.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Fill in a lean business model canvas** — the one-page hypothesis of how the business works.
- **Describe revenue models and basic pricing** options and pick one for your idea.
- **Compute contribution margin** per unit/customer.
- **Compute CAC, LTV, and the LTV:CAC ratio**, and explain what healthy looks like directionally.
- **Compute payback period** and explain why it matters for cash.
- **State your assumptions** explicitly, so the numbers are honest rather than hopeful.

## Topics covered

- The business model canvas (nine blocks) as a hypothesis, not a plan
- Revenue models: one-time, subscription, transaction/marketplace take rate, usage, ads, freemium
- Pricing basics: value-based vs. cost-plus, the danger of underpricing
- Unit economics: contribution margin, CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC, payback
- Why a great-looking top line can hide a broken model
- Stating assumptions and sensitivity (what if CAC doubles?)

## In-class activities

- **Canvas in 20.** Fill all nine blocks for your idea in twenty minutes; the gaps you can't fill are your riskiest business-model assumptions.
- **Pricing dare.** Each founder names a price; the room asks "why not double it?" Underpricing is the most common early mistake.
- **The leaky model.** Given numbers where CAC > LTV, the group diagnoses what is broken and what would have to change.

## Worked example

The clinic founder considers $99/month per clinic. Contribution margin: $99 minus ~$9 of variable cost (SMS, payment fees) = $90/month. CAC: she expects to spend ~$300 in founder-led sales effort and tools to land a clinic. If clinics stay ~24 months, LTV (on contribution) ≈ $90 × 24 = $2,160. LTV:CAC ≈ 7:1 — healthy, directionally. Payback ≈ $300 / $90 ≈ 3.3 months — fast, good for cash. She writes every assumption down, then asks the scary question: *if CAC triples to $900, is it still viable?* (Payback ~10 months, LTV:CAC ~2.4 — thinner but alive.) That sensitivity check is the point.
