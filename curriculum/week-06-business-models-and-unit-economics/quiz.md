# Week 6 — Quiz

Ten questions. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 9/10 before you build the mini-project — the questions map directly to mistakes that sink real founder models. A few require a little arithmetic; have scratch paper. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

---

**Q1.** A business model, a revenue model, and a pricing model are:

- A) Three names for the same thing.
- B) Three distinct layers — the business model is the whole nine-box story, the revenue model is *how* you charge (subscription, take rate, usage…), and the pricing model is the actual numbers and structure.
- C) The revenue model is the broadest; the business model is just the price.
- D) Only relevant once you've raised money.

---

**Q2.** A lean canvas is best treated as:

- A) A finished plan you execute unchanged.
- B) A legal document for incorporation.
- C) A one-page *hypothesis* where each block is a guess to be tested — and the blocks you can't fill are your riskiest assumptions.
- D) A pitch deck you show investors.

---

**Q3.** You charge $49/month. Your variable costs to serve one customer are: payment processing $1.50, hosting $2.00, support $3.50. What is your **contribution margin**?

- A) $49.00
- B) $42.00
- C) $7.00
- D) $44.00

---

**Q4.** Your LTV calculation should multiply expected lifetime by:

- A) Revenue per period (the full price).
- B) **Contribution margin** per period.
- C) CAC.
- D) Gross revenue minus fixed costs.

---

**Q5.** A founder computes CAC as "ad spend ÷ signups" and gets $40, but she personally spends ~5 hours selling each customer and values her time at $50/hour. Her *honest* CAC is closer to:

- A) $40 — founder time is free.
- B) $50 — only her time counts.
- C) ~$290 — ad spend plus the ~$250 of founder selling time.
- D) Impossible to know.

---

**Q6.** Your customers churn at 5% per month. Your expected customer lifetime is approximately:

- A) 5 months
- B) 20 months
- C) 50 months
- D) 95 months

---

**Q7.** A startup has LTV:CAC of 0.8:1 and revenue growing 200% year over year. The correct read is:

- A) Great — scale aggressively; growth will fix the ratio.
- B) The growth is *making it worse* — they lose money on each customer, so faster growth burns cash faster. Fix the model before scaling.
- C) The ratio doesn't matter if revenue is growing.
- D) Raise a big round to outgrow the problem.

---

**Q8.** CAC payback period matters most because:

- A) Investors require it by law.
- B) You pay CAC up front in cash but collect contribution slowly — short payback recycles cash and lets you grow self-funded; long payback ties up cash and may force a raise.
- C) It's the same as LTV:CAC, just expressed differently.
- D) It only matters for hardware companies.

---

**Q9.** "Freemium" is best described as:

- A) A complete revenue model on its own.
- B) A pricing model that guarantees profitability.
- C) A *funnel* — a free tier that channels a slice of users toward a paid revenue model underneath; the actual revenue still comes from a subscription or usage tier.
- D) The same thing as a free trial.

---

**Q10.** Why run a sensitivity check (e.g., "what if CAC doubles, or churn halves my retention")?

- A) To make the spreadsheet look longer and more impressive.
- B) Because a model that only works under best-case assumptions is a wish, not a model — sensitivity finds the variable that breaks it and tells you what to defend.
- C) It's legally required before incorporating.
- D) Investors dislike founders who do it.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — Three layers. The business model is the whole canvas; the revenue model is one block (how you charge); the pricing model is the specific numbers. Founders constantly collapse them. (L1 §1)
2. **C** — A testable one-page hypothesis. The empty boxes are the riskiest assumptions — that's the point of "Canvas in 20." (L1 §2–3)
3. **B** — Total variable cost = $1.50 + $2.00 + $3.50 = $7.00. Contribution margin = price − variable cost = $49 − $7 = **$42**. Answer C ($7) is the *total variable cost*, a deliberate trap — margin is price *minus* that cost, not the cost itself. (L2 §1)
4. **B** — Contribution margin, never revenue. Using revenue counts money you'll spend serving the customer as if it were profit. (L2 §3)
5. **C** — Fully-loaded CAC includes founder time: $40 ad spend + 5h × $50 = $250 → ~$290. Founder-led sales is the most expensive channel, not the free one. (L2 §2)
6. **B** — Lifetime ≈ 1 ÷ churn = 1 ÷ 0.05 = **20 months**. (L2 §3)
7. **B** — This is the "scale the loss" trap. LTV:CAC below 1 means every customer loses money; growth is the accelerant, not the cure. (L2 §4, §7)
8. **B** — You pay CAC up front in cash and collect slowly. Short payback frees cash to acquire the next customer; long payback ties up cash and can force a raise. (L2 §5)
9. **C** — Freemium is a funnel, not a revenue model. Revenue comes from the paid tier underneath. (L1 §4)
10. **B** — Stress-testing the assumptions to find what breaks the model. A best-case-only model is a wish. (L2 §6)

</details>
