# Week 6 — Exercises

Short, focused drills that build directly toward the mini-project. Each one should take 30–60 minutes. **Do them in order** — Exercise 2's canvas feeds Exercise 3's numbers, and both feed the mini-project. Unlike a coding week, you write **zero code** here: these are worksheets, canvases, and a spreadsheet-shaped template you fill with *your own concept's* numbers.

## Index

1. **[Exercise 1 — Canvas and margins](exercise-01-canvas-and-margins.md)** — guided: fill the nine-block lean canvas for your idea, choose a revenue model and price, and compute contribution margin. (~50 min)
2. **[Exercise 2 — Lean canvas worksheet](exercise-02-lean-canvas-worksheet.md)** — a fill-in worksheet/template: the full nine-block lean canvas with prompts, a worked Maya example to copy from, and a "weakest block" callout. (~40 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — Unit-economics sheet](exercise-03-unit-economics-sheet.md)** — a fill-in spreadsheet template (in Markdown): CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC, payback, plus a base/worst/best sensitivity table and the one-sentence verdict. (~50 min)

## How to work the exercises

- **State every assumption next to the number it feeds.** A number with no stated assumption is not a model — it's a guess wearing a suit. Every cell in the unit-economics sheet has a "because…" attached.
- **Use contribution, not revenue, in LTV.** This is the error that sinks more founder models than any other. If you catch yourself multiplying *price* by lifetime, stop.
- **Get the founder's time into CAC.** Founder-led sales is the most expensive channel, not the free one. Price your hours.
- **Resist underpricing.** When in doubt on price, write the *higher* number and note you'll test it. The "pricing dare" applies even when you're alone: for every price, ask "why not double it?"
- **Don't fake the empty boxes.** An honest "I don't know my unfair advantage yet" beats an invented moat. The empty boxes are the point — they're your next tests.
- **Commit each file to your Week 6 repo** as you finish it, so each produces a commit you can point to in the homework and mini-project.

## Checklist

Work top to bottom; check each box only when it's genuinely true.

- [ ] **Ex 1** — All nine canvas blocks filled; weakest block flagged as riskiest assumption.
- [ ] **Ex 1** — Revenue model and starting price chosen, with value-based reasoning written out.
- [ ] **Ex 1** — Contribution margin computed with every variable cost itemized and assumed.
- [ ] **Ex 2** — Full lean canvas worksheet completed in the template, single-line answers, no "everyone" customer segment.
- [ ] **Ex 2** — Existing-alternatives line filled (what the customer does *today*), and the unfair-advantage box answered honestly.
- [ ] **Ex 3** — CAC computed fully-loaded (founder time included), with the calculation shown.
- [ ] **Ex 3** — LTV computed on **contribution**, tied to an explicit churn/retention assumption.
- [ ] **Ex 3** — LTV:CAC ratio and CAC payback period computed.
- [ ] **Ex 3** — Base/worst/best sensitivity table filled, scariest variable identified.
- [ ] **Ex 3** — The one-sentence verdict written ("makes money at scale iff `<X>`, else breaks because `<Y>`").
- [ ] All three files committed to your Week 6 repo.

There are no answer keys for these — they're about *your* concept, so the "right answer" is one that's specific, honest, and internally consistent. The mini-project rubric is how your model gets graded.
