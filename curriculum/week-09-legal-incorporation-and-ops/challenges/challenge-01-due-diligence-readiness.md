# Challenge 1 — Pre-Seed Due-Diligence Readiness

**Time estimate:** ~90 minutes.

> **This is education, not legal or tax advice.** The challenge is a *self-audit and triage*. Anything binding or jurisdiction-specific routes to a professional — that routing *is* part of the deliverable.

## The scenario

A pre-seed lead has verbally agreed to put $200k into your company on a post-money SAFE at a $4M cap. Soft-circled. Term sheet in hand. The lead's parting words: *"We'll run light diligence — just send over your cap table, your IP assignments, and your formation docs, and we'll wire by month-end."*

It's the second week of the month. You have roughly three weeks. This is the moment a clean company gets funded and a messy one watches the round quietly evaporate. The lead isn't going to *tell* you the deal died because of an unsigned contractor from 18 months ago — they'll just go quiet and stop replying.

**Your job: run the diligence on yourself first, find every problem, and decide — honestly — whether you can clear it in three weeks.**

This is the realistic version of what Week 10 only gestures at. The pitch (Week 10) sells the dream; this challenge is whether the dream survives contact with a lawyer.

---

## What to do

### Part 1 — Run the full self-audit (40 min)

Take the due-diligence checklist from Lecture 2 (§3) and run **every** item against *your actual venture* — the one you've carried since Week 1. For each, the honest answer is one of:

- ✅ **Clean** — "yes, here's the document / fact."
- ⚠️ **Gap** — "no, or not yet, or I'm not sure."

Cover all four areas. Don't skip a box because it's uncomfortable — the uncomfortable boxes are the whole point.

- **Corporate & ownership** — entity formed and in good standing; cap table accurate (your Week 8 model); founder stock issued and vesting with a cliff (Week 4); no dead equity; SAFEs/notes documented and understood.
- **Intellectual property** — every founder assigned IP (incl. pre-incorporation); every contributor assigned; no prior-employer claim; brand/name not obviously infringing; domains/repos/accounts owned by the company. (Pull this straight from your Exercise 2 audit.)
- **Operations & finance** — dedicated bank account, no commingling; clean books showing burn; tax election (83(b)-style) filed on deadline or accounted for; compliance deadlines tracked.
- **People & agreements** — founders' agreement; advisor agreements; contractor/employee agreements with IP + confidentiality.

### Part 2 — Triage every finding (25 min)

Sort each ⚠️ gap into exactly one bucket:

| Bucket | Meaning | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| **Fix now (cheap)** | Solvable this week with a signature, a transfer, or a calendar entry. | Get the favor-coding friend to sign a one-page assignment; open the company bank account. |
| **Fix before the wire** | Must be true before money lands, takes a bit of work or a pro. | Issue founder stock with a proper vesting agreement; file the tax election. |
| **`[NEEDS ADVISOR]`** | Binding, uncertain, or jurisdiction-specific — escalate. | Prior-employer IP claim; cross-border entity question. |

For every "fix now" item, assign an **owner** and a **date**. A triage with no owners is a wish list.

### Part 3 — The verdict (25 min)

Write the one-paragraph answer you'd have to give the lead if they asked *"is this a clean company we can wire into?"* It must:

1. State plainly whether you're **fundable as-is, fundable after fixes, or not yet** — and be honest.
2. Name your **single biggest exposure** and what it would take to clear it.
3. Give a **realistic timeline** — can the must-fix items actually be done in three weeks, given that some need a lawyer?
4. Not bluff. The whole skill is that you'd rather find your own problem than have the lead find it.

## Deliverable

A file `challenges/challenge-01-diligence-readiness.md` in your Week 9 repo with:

- The completed self-audit (all four areas, every item marked ✅ or ⚠️).
- The triage table (every gap bucketed; fix-now items have owner + date).
- The one-paragraph fundability verdict.

---

## Acceptance criteria

- [ ] All four diligence areas audited against your real venture; every item marked ✅ or ⚠️.
- [ ] Every ⚠️ gap is triaged into exactly one of the three buckets.
- [ ] Every "fix now" item has an owner and a date.
- [ ] At least one item is correctly escalated as `[NEEDS ADVISOR]` (or you've genuinely confirmed none apply — rare).
- [ ] A fundability verdict paragraph that names the biggest exposure and a realistic timeline, and doesn't bluff.
- [ ] Committed to your Week 9 repo.

## Stretch

- **Investor-eyes pass.** Have a cohort peer play the lead investor's lawyer and run *your* checklist against you out loud. Every "uh, I think so?" is a finding you missed. Add what they catch.
- **Cost it.** Estimate the dollar and time cost of clearing every must-fix item. Founders consistently underestimate how long "just get everyone to sign" takes when one contributor has gone quiet.
- **The kill-the-deal pre-mortem.** Write the two-sentence story of how *this specific round* dies in diligence if you do nothing. Naming it is the cheapest way to make sure it doesn't happen.

## Why this matters

This is the founder skill that separates "we got a term sheet" from "we closed." Term sheets are non-binding; the wire is real, and between them sits diligence. The founders who close are the ones who audited themselves first and walked into diligence with nothing to find. Everything you've built across nine weeks — the cap table, the vesting, the IP — gets stress-tested here. And in Week 10, "is this a clean, fundable company?" is one of the obvious hard questions you must survive. This challenge is you writing the answer before you're asked.
