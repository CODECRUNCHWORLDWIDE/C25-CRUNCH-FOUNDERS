# Week 9 — Exercises

Three focused worksheets that turn this week's concepts into artifacts about *your* venture. They are **not** code — they are founder worksheets, canvases, and templates you fill in. Each one feeds directly into the mini-project (the tailored incorporation & ops checklist) and the challenge (the due-diligence self-audit). Do them in order; later ones build on earlier ones.

> **This is education, not legal or tax advice.** Every worksheet ends by flagging the items that need a real lawyer or accountant. The point is to be a *prepared client*, not to self-execute binding decisions.

## Index

1. **[Exercise 1 — Lawyer & accountant questions](exercise-01-lawyer-questions.md)** — draft the specific, prioritized questions for a first advisor meeting, tailored to your venture. (~45 min)
2. **[Exercise 2 — IP & contribution trap audit](exercise-02-ip-trap-audit.md)** — a Markdown worksheet: map every asset the company depends on to who made it, on whose account, and whether it's assigned. Surface the three classic traps. (~50 min)
3. **[Exercise 3 — Incorporation decision canvas](exercise-03-incorporation-decision-canvas.md)** — a one-page Markdown template that captures your incorporation situation, options, and the open questions for a pro — the input a lawyer needs to give you a real answer fast. (~45 min)

## How to work the exercises

- **Make them specific to your venture.** "What entity should I form?" is a weak, generic question. "Given that I plan to raise a pre-seed within a year and I'm based in X with a co-founder in Y, what entity fits?" is a strong, answerable one. Specific beats broad every time.
- **Be honest, especially in Exercise 2.** The IP-trap audit only works if you actually admit the messy parts — the code on a personal account, the friend who never signed anything, the prototype you started while employed elsewhere. The whole value is finding these *now*, while the fix is a one-page signature instead of a dead round.
- **Flag, don't guess.** Anything jurisdiction-specific, binding, or tax-related gets marked `[NEEDS ADVISOR]`. Knowing what you *can't* answer yourself is a skill the exercises are training.
- **Keep them in your Week 9 repo.** Commit each worksheet. They are the raw material for the mini-project.

## Definition of done — the checklist

You're done with the exercises when:

- [ ] **Exercise 1** has at least **four lawyer questions** and **three accountant questions**, all tailored to your venture, plus a "known traps in my situation" section.
- [ ] **Exercise 2** has every company-critical asset listed, each mapped to a maker, an account/location, and an assignment status, with each of the three classic traps explicitly checked and a fix noted for any that apply.
- [ ] **Exercise 3** has your incorporation situation, your candidate entity options with trade-offs, and a clear list of `[NEEDS ADVISOR]` open questions.
- [ ] Every item that is binding, jurisdiction-specific, or tax-related is flagged for a professional — not answered with a guess.
- [ ] All three files are committed to your Week 9 repository.

When the three are done, you have most of the raw material for the [mini-project checklist](../mini-project/README.md) and a head start on the [due-diligence challenge](../challenges/challenge-01-due-diligence-readiness.md).

*There are no "answer keys" to these — the right answers are specific to your company. Compare notes with a cohort peer: reading someone else's honest IP audit is the fastest way to spot the trap hiding in your own.*
