# Exercise 1 — Lawyer & Accountant Questions

**Goal:** Turn this week's concepts into a specific, prioritized list of questions for a first meeting with a lawyer and an accountant — so that meeting is *cheap and useful* instead of vague and expensive. A prepared founder gets better advice in one billable hour than an unprepared one gets in three.

**Estimated time:** 45 minutes.

> **This is education, not legal or tax advice.** The output is a list of *questions to ask a professional*, not answers. That's the entire point.

---

## Why this matters

A first lawyer or accountant meeting can cost real money per hour. Two founders walk into identical meetings:

- **Founder A** says: "Hi, I have a startup, what should I do about, like, incorporating and stuff?" The professional spends the hour extracting basic facts and giving generic answers. The founder leaves with a $400 bill and "well, it depends."
- **Founder B** slides over a one-page summary of their situation and a list of eight specific questions. The professional answers all eight in forty minutes, flags two things Founder B hadn't thought of, and the founder leaves with a clear action list and change to spare.

This exercise makes you Founder B.

---

## Step 1 — Write your situation in three sentences

Before the questions, capture the context an advisor needs. Three sentences, factual:

1. **What you're building and where you are** — e.g., "A no-show-reduction tool for small clinics; we have three pilot clinics ready to pay $200/month."
2. **Your team and location** — e.g., "Two co-founders, one in [country/state A], one in [country/state B]; I'm the CEO, 55/45 split agreed."
3. **Your money/fundraising plan** — e.g., "Bootstrapping now, but planning to raise a ~$250k pre-seed on a SAFE within 12 months."

A specific situation is what turns a generic question into an answerable one.

## Step 2 — Draft the lawyer questions (at least four)

Tailor each to *your* situation. Use the lecture's themes — entity, founder stock/vesting, IP, agreements — as prompts. Strong examples (rewrite for your case):

1. **Entity:** "Given that I plan to raise a pre-seed on a SAFE within a year and my co-founder and I are in [A] and [B], what entity should we form, and does the cross-border situation complicate it?"
2. **Founder stock & vesting:** "How should our 55/45 split and our four-year/one-year-cliff vesting be papered as actual restricted stock?"
3. **IP — the honest one:** "Some of our core code was built on my co-founder's personal GitHub account before we had any agreement. How do we make sure the *company* owns it now?"
4. **Prior employer:** "I started the prototype while still employed at [bigco], which had an inventions clause. Do they have any claim, and do I need a release?"
5. **Agreements:** "Which agreements do we need *before* taking our first paying customer, and which can wait?"

## Step 3 — Draft the accountant questions (at least three)

1. **The deadline-bound election:** "Is there a time-sensitive tax election on our founder stock (like an 83(b)) that I must file, and what's the exact deadline from our stock grant?"
2. **Ongoing obligations:** "What ongoing taxes and filings will this entity owe, even at zero revenue, and when?"
3. **Bookkeeping:** "What's the minimum bookkeeping you need from us to keep things clean and make your job cheap at tax time?"
4. **Pre-seed treatment:** "When we raise on a SAFE, is there anything tax-wise we need to handle at that point?"

## Step 4 — List the known traps in your situation

A short, honest section naming any landmine you already suspect — pulled from the trap-spotting activity and Exercise 2. For example:

- Core code lives on a co-founder's personal account; no IP assignment signed yet.
- A friend wrote the first reminder script as a favor and never signed anything.
- I haven't checked my prior-employment agreement.
- We've been paying for a few tools on a personal credit card (commingling).

## Hints

<details>
<summary>How do I make a question "specific"?</summary>

Add the three pieces of context that change the answer: your **location**, your **fundraising plan**, and the **actual facts of your situation**. "What should I incorporate as?" gets "it depends." "Given that I'm in [X], plan to raise a pre-seed, and have a co-founder in [Y], what entity fits and does the cross-border part complicate it?" gets a real answer.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I genuinely don't know my traps yet</summary>

Do Exercise 2 (the IP audit) first, then come back. The traps section here is largely the output of that audit. The two exercises are designed to feed each other.

</details>

<details>
<summary>I'm a solo founder with no money — do I even need this?</summary>

Yes, a shorter version. You still have entity, IP (your own prior-employment situation especially), and the tax-election questions. You can skip the co-founder-specific ones. The discipline of writing specific questions is the transferable skill.

</details>
