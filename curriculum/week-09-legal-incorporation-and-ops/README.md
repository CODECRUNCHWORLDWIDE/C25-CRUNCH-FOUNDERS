# Week 9 — Legal, Incorporation & Ops

Boring on the surface, decisive underneath. The legal and operational scaffolding of a company — incorporating, assigning IP, papering founder vesting, opening a bank account, keeping records — is where avoidable disasters hide: the co-founder who built the core product on a personal GitHub account, the IP that legally belongs to a former employer, the company that "incorporated later" and discovered the founders never actually owned their own stock. None of this is glamorous. All of it is the kind of thing that, left unfixed, kills a fundable company during the first serious due-diligence pass — usually six weeks before the wire that would have saved it.

This week makes you a **good client** for a lawyer and an accountant: someone who walks in with a tailored checklist, asks specific questions, knows which items are jurisdiction-specific landmines, and does the cheap organizing work themselves so the expensive professional time goes to the things only a professional can do. You will not leave able to replace a lawyer. You will leave able to brief one in fifteen minutes instead of two billable hours.

> **This is education, not legal or tax advice.** Entity rules, tax elections, and securities law are jurisdiction-specific and change. Every binding decision in this week routes to a real, licensed professional in your jurisdiction. The course teaches you the *questions*, the *traps*, and the *sequence* — not the answers for your specific situation.

The week's deliverable is an **incorporation & ops checklist tailored to your concept**, with every jurisdiction- or situation-specific item flagged for a real advisor. It is the bridge between Week 8's cap-table model and Week 10's pitch: by the time you stand up to pitch, "is this a clean, fundable company?" should be answered, not hand-waved.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Explain why and when** to incorporate, and articulate the real cost of incorporating too early *and* too late.
- **Name the common entity choices** at a high level (and explain why the course refuses to prescribe one — because it is genuinely advisor-and-jurisdiction territory).
- **Connect founder stock and vesting** (your Week 4 work) to the mechanics of incorporation: stock issuance, restricted stock, vesting agreements, and the existence of early tax elections with hard deadlines.
- **Explain IP assignment** in plain terms — why the company, not the person who typed the code, must own everything the company depends on — and identify the three classic IP traps before they show up in diligence.
- **List the basic agreements** an early company needs, who signs each, and when ("now," "before money," "before hiring").
- **Set up lightweight ops** — a dedicated bank account, simple bookkeeping, organized document storage, and a compliance/deadline calendar — without over-engineering it into a dozen SaaS subscriptions.
- **Distinguish DIY from professional work**: do the organizing yourself; get the binding documents and the tax/entity choices from licensed people.
- **Run a due-diligence self-audit** — look at your own company the way an investor's lawyer will, and find the problems while they are still cheap to fix.
- **Write a checklist** tailored to your venture that flags exactly what needs professional help and what you can handle.

## Prerequisites

This week assumes you have completed **C25 Weeks 1–8**, or have equivalent founder context. Specifically:

- You have a **co-founder agreement draft** (Week 4) with an equity split and a vesting schedule with a cliff. If you are a solo founder, you still have the vesting reasoning and an idea of advisor/early-hire equity.

You do **not** need any prior legal or accounting background. We start from "what is an entity and why would I want one." If you have already incorporated something, this week is a chance to audit whether you did it cleanly.

## Topics covered

- **Why incorporate:** limited liability, ownership clarity, fundraising readiness, tax/contract cleanliness — and the honest limits of each.
- **When to incorporate:** the "around the time money or partners enter" heuristic, and the asymmetric costs of too-early vs. too-late.
- **Entity choices at a high level:** the common shapes (and why "what a US YC company does" is not automatically right for a solo founder in another country). The C-corp-for-venture-fundraising convention and its trade-offs, kept conceptual.
- **Founder stock and vesting at incorporation:** stock issuance, restricted stock with a repurchase right, vesting that mirrors your Week 4 schedule, and the existence of time-sensitive tax elections (the US "83(b)" being the canonical example — *flag it, do not self-file it*).
- **IP assignment — the quiet killer:** the company owns the work; the three classic traps (personal-account code, the unpaid "friend who helped," and prior-employer IP); how each surfaces in diligence; and how to fix them early and cheaply.
- **Core early agreements:** founders' agreement, IP/invention assignment, advisor agreement, contractor and employee agreements (with IP + confidentiality), and the sparing, deliberate use of NDAs.
- **Operational hygiene:** a dedicated business bank account (and why commingling is dangerous), simple bookkeeping from day one, one organized document store, and a compliance/deadline calendar.
- **SAFEs and the legal surface of a raise:** what a SAFE actually is as a legal instrument (a contract that converts to stock later), what gets signed, and what the cap table and post-money math from Week 8 mean once it is a real agreement.
- **The due-diligence self-audit:** reading your own company the way an investor's counsel will, before they do.
- **The boundary:** what you do yourself vs. what is lawyer/accountant work, and how being prepared makes professional help cheaper and better.

## In-class activities

- **Trap spotting.** Short scenarios — core code on a personal account, no IP assignment from a contractor, a co-founder who built a prototype while still employed at a big company. The group identifies the landmine, who it hurts, *when* it detonates (usually diligence), and the cheapest fix available today.
- **Lawyer- and accountant-question drafting.** Each founder writes the specific questions they would bring to a first professional meeting. "Given that I plan to raise a pre-seed and I live in X, what entity fits?" beats "what should I incorporate as?" every time.
- **The 30-minute diligence drill.** Pair up. You play the investor's lawyer; your partner plays the founder. Run the due-diligence checklist out loud. Every "uh, I think so?" is a finding.
- **Ops minimum.** Build the shortest possible list of operational steps that keeps the company clean without making a two-person team act like a 200-person one.

## Worked example — the clinic-scheduling founder

A founder building a no-show-reduction tool for small medical clinics is about to take her first three paying clinics at $200/month each. Before money flows she does the following, in order:

1. **Forms an entity**, consulting a local lawyer/accountant — because the right form is jurisdiction-specific and she plans to raise a pre-seed within a year, which pushes her toward a structure investors recognize.
2. **Issues founder stock** to herself and her co-founder, subject to the four-year vesting with a one-year cliff they agreed in Week 4 — so that if her co-founder leaves in month five, he leaves with nothing and she is not stuck with a 50% absent shareholder.
3. **Files the time-sensitive tax election** her accountant flagged (in the US that's the 83(b); elsewhere there is often an analogous "tax it now while it's worth almost nothing" choice) — *with the accountant*, inside the hard deadline, because missing it is expensive and irreversible.
4. **Has every founder and contributor sign IP assignment** — including the freelance designer who did the logo and the friend who wrote the first booking-reminder script over a weekend — so the code, the brand, and the content belong to the *company*, not to whoever happened to make them.
5. **Opens a dedicated business bank account** and never again pays a company expense from a personal card.
6. **Starts simple bookkeeping** — a spreadsheet now, a low-cost tool soon — tracking every dollar in and out from day one.
7. **Stores the signed agreements and the cap table in one organized place**, and **notes every filing and tax deadline** on a shared calendar.

She also writes down what she is *not* sure about and routes it to professionals: the exact entity for her situation, the tax-election deadline, and whether the weekend-script friend's contribution was properly assigned (it was not — she gets him to sign a one-page assignment before it becomes a diligence problem). She does not pretend to be a lawyer. She shows up prepared, and her first lawyer meeting costs her one hour instead of three.

That worked example is the spine of the whole week. The mini-project asks you to produce the same artifact for *your* concept.

## The "clean company" promise

Every artifact this week ends with the same test, the founder equivalent of C9's "build succeeded · 0 warnings":

> **Would this survive a pre-seed due-diligence pass?**

If a sharp investor's lawyer reading your cap table, your IP assignments, and your founder agreements would not flag anything, you are done. If they would — if there's an unsigned contributor, a commingled bank account, a missing vesting agreement, a tax-election deadline you can't account for — you are not done, and you have just found exactly the thing that would have cost you a round. We treat unanswered diligence questions the way C9 treats compiler warnings: as bugs.

## Stretch goals

If you finish the regular work early and want to push further:

- Read **YC's actual Series Seed / SAFE documents** end to end (linked in `resources.md`). You will not understand every clause; read for the *shape* of what gets signed.
- Read one real startup's published "**lessons learned the hard way**" post-mortem about a legal or co-founder mess (there are many). Identify which Week-9 control would have prevented it.
- Map your **own jurisdiction's** entity options. If you are not in the US, the US-centric defaults in most startup writing are a starting point, not an answer — find the local equivalent and the local professional who handles it.
- Write a one-paragraph note for future-you explaining, in plain language, the difference between *issuing* stock, *vesting* stock, and *a tax election on* stock — three things beginners conflate.
