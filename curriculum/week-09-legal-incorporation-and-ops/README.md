# Week 9 — Legal, Incorporation & Ops

Boring on the surface, decisive underneath. The legal and operational scaffolding of a company — incorporating, assigning IP, papering founder vesting, opening a bank account, keeping records — is where avoidable disasters hide: the co-founder who built core code on a personal account, the IP that legally belongs to a former employer, the company that "incorporated later" and created a tax mess. This week makes you a *good client* for a lawyer and an accountant: able to ask the right questions and avoid the obvious traps. **It is education, not legal or tax advice.**

The deliverable is an incorporation & ops checklist tailored to your concept, with open questions flagged for a real advisor.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Explain why and when** to incorporate, and the trade-offs of waiting.
- **Name common entity choices** at a high level and what to ask an advisor about each.
- **Connect founder stock and vesting** (Week 4) to incorporation.
- **Explain IP assignment** and why every founder and contributor must sign one.
- **List the basic agreements** an early company needs.
- **Set up lightweight ops** — banking, bookkeeping, and the records that matter.
- **Write a checklist** that flags what needs professional help.

## Topics covered

- Why incorporate: liability, ownership clarity, fundraising readiness, taxes
- When to incorporate (and the cost of "later")
- Entity choices at a high level — and why this is jurisdiction-specific and advisor territory
- Founder stock, founder vesting at incorporation, and a brief note on early tax elections (ask a pro)
- IP assignment: company owns the work; the trap of code built elsewhere
- Core early agreements: founders, advisors, contractors, employees, NDAs (used sparingly)
- Ops hygiene: separate bank account, basic bookkeeping, document storage
- Knowing the boundary: what you do yourself vs. what needs a lawyer/accountant

## In-class activities

- **Trap spotting.** A set of short scenarios (code on a personal account, no IP assignment, a friend "helped" with no agreement); the group identifies the landmine and the fix.
- **Lawyer-question drafting.** Each founder writes the five questions they would bring to a first lawyer meeting. Specific beats broad.
- **Ops minimum.** Build the shortest list of operational steps that keeps the company clean without over-engineering.

## Worked example

The clinic founder is about to take her first paying customers. Before money flows she: forms an entity (consulting a local lawyer/accountant because it is jurisdiction-specific), issues founder stock subject to the four-year/one-year-cliff vesting from Week 4, has both founders sign **IP assignment** agreements so the code and brand belong to the company (not to whoever typed it), opens a dedicated business bank account, and starts simple bookkeeping. She also flags for her lawyer: the right entity for her situation, any early tax election with a deadline, and whether her contractor's work was properly assigned. She does not pretend to be a lawyer; she shows up prepared.
