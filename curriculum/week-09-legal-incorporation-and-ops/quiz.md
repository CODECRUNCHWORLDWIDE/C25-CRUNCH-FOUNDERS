# Week 9 — Quiz

Ten core questions plus two bonus, on incorporation, founder stock, IP, agreements, and ops. Take it with your lecture notes closed. Aim for 8/10 before Week 10. Answer key at the bottom — don't peek.

> **Reminder:** this quiz tests *founder literacy*, not legal advice. The "right" answer is the one a prepared founder gives, who then confirms binding specifics with a professional.

---

**Q1.** Which of the following is the **best** description of when to incorporate?

- A) Immediately, the day you have an idea, regardless of money or partners.
- B) Around the time money or partners enter — taking money, signing real contracts, bringing on a co-founder, or hiring.
- C) Only after you've raised a priced round, since investors handle incorporation.
- D) Never, if you can avoid it — incorporation only creates tax problems.

---

**Q2.** The costs of incorporating too early versus too late are **asymmetric**. Which statement captures the asymmetry correctly?

- A) Too early can create unfixable ownership/tax messes; too late just costs a little money.
- B) Both are equally damaging, so timing doesn't matter.
- C) Too early mostly costs money and overhead; too late can create unfixable messes (murky pre-incorporation IP, missed tax-election deadlines, dead equity).
- D) Too late is harmless because you can always backdate the paperwork.

---

**Q3.** Why does the course refuse to prescribe a specific entity type (e.g., "always do a Delaware C-corp")?

- A) Because entity type doesn't actually matter for startups.
- B) Because the right entity is genuinely jurisdiction-, tax-, and fundraising-specific, and the answer for one founder can be wrong for another — it's advisor territory.
- C) Because the instructors don't know the answer.
- D) Because LLCs are always the correct choice.

---

**Q4.** Two co-founders incorporate 50/50 with **no vesting**. Six weeks later, one co-founder quits to take a job. What's the problem?

- A) Nothing — the company simply continues with one founder.
- B) The departed co-founder keeps 50% of the company forever while contributing nothing, leaving "dead equity" that makes the cap table close to un-fundable.
- C) The remaining founder automatically inherits the departed founder's shares.
- D) The company must immediately dissolve.

---

**Q5.** What is the role of a **one-year cliff** in a four-year vesting schedule?

- A) It means all stock vests immediately after one year.
- B) It's the period before *any* stock vests — a founder who leaves before the one-year mark leaves with nothing.
- C) It caps total vesting at one year.
- D) It's a tax election with a one-year deadline.

---

**Q6.** A founder is issued **restricted** founder stock and learns there's a time-sensitive tax election (like the US 83(b)) available. What is the correct founder move this week?

- A) Self-file it immediately from a template found online.
- B) Ignore it — tax elections only matter after you're profitable.
- C) Recognize it exists, note its hard deadline on the compliance calendar, and route it to the accountant to file on time — it's irreversible and easy to get wrong.
- D) Wait until the next funding round to deal with it.

---

**Q7.** By default, who owns code that a contributor writes for your company if **no IP-assignment agreement is signed**?

- A) The company automatically owns it because they asked for it.
- B) The contributor who created it owns it — which is exactly why IP assignment matters.
- C) Nobody owns it; it's public domain.
- D) The first investor to fund the company owns it.

---

**Q8.** Which classic IP trap can be genuinely **unfixable**, and is therefore the one most worth surfacing early?

- A) A co-founder built core code on a personal GitHub account before incorporation.
- B) A friend wrote a small script as a favor and never signed anything.
- C) A founder built the prototype (or even the core idea) while employed at a company with a broad "inventions belong to us" clause.
- D) The company hasn't opened a dedicated bank account yet.

---

**Q9.** A first-time founder asks an investor to sign an **NDA before hearing the pitch**. What does an experienced observer conclude?

- A) Smart move — it protects the founder's idea.
- B) It signals inexperience; most investors won't sign one to hear a pitch, and demanding it can cost you the meeting. NDAs are for genuinely sensitive, specific situations.
- C) It's legally required before any pitch.
- D) It guarantees the investor will take the company more seriously.

---

**Q10.** Why is **commingling** (paying company expenses from a personal account, or vice versa) dangerous?

- A) It's only a minor bookkeeping inconvenience with no real consequences.
- B) It can pierce limited liability (erasing the protection you incorporated for) *and* makes books and taxes a mess that reads as risk in diligence.
- C) It's actually recommended early on to save on bank fees.
- D) It automatically dissolves the company.

---

**Q11.** (Bonus) A pre-seed lead says "send your cap table, IP assignments, and formation docs and we'll wire by month-end." What is the smartest thing a founder can do *first*?

- A) Wait for the lead's lawyer to find any problems and react to them.
- B) Run the diligence checklist on themselves first, find and triage their own gaps, and fix the cheap ones before the lead's lawyer looks.
- C) Send everything immediately without reviewing it.
- D) Ask the lead to skip diligence to save time.

---

**Q12.** (Bonus) Which item below is correctly a **`[DIY]`** founder task rather than something to route to a lawyer/accountant?

- A) Choosing the entity type in a cross-border, venture-track situation.
- B) Filing the time-sensitive founder-stock tax election.
- C) Opening a dedicated business bank account and starting a bookkeeping spreadsheet.
- D) Drafting the binding restricted-stock and vesting agreements.

---

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answers</summary>

1. **B** — Incorporate around the time money or partners enter. Day-one is premature; after a priced round is too late. (L1 §2)
2. **C** — Too-early wastes money/overhead (recoverable); too-late risks unfixable messes: murky pre-incorporation IP, missed irreversible tax-election deadlines, dead equity. (L1 §2)
3. **B** — The right entity depends on jurisdiction, tax, and fundraising plans. "Always a Delaware C-corp" is folklore that's wrong for many founders. It's an advisor decision. (L1 §3)
4. **B** — No vesting means the departed co-founder keeps 50% forever for six weeks of work — "dead equity" that makes the company nearly un-fundable. Vesting with a cliff prevents it. (L1 §5)
5. **B** — The cliff is the period (commonly one year) before *any* stock vests. Leave before it and you get nothing. (L1 §5)
6. **C** — Recognize it, calendar the hard deadline, route to the accountant. It's irreversible (US 83(b): 30 days) and a classic avoidable, expensive mistake. Do *not* self-file. (L1 §6)
7. **B** — By default the *creator* owns their work. Without an assignment, the company doesn't own what it depends on — the diligence killer IP assignment fixes. (L1 §7)
8. **C** — The prior-employer claim can be both true and unfixable, so it's most worth surfacing early; the others are cheap one-page-signature fixes. D isn't an IP trap. (L1 §7)
9. **B** — Demanding an NDA to hear a pitch signals inexperience; investors won't take on liability for one deck. Reserve NDAs for genuinely sensitive situations. (L2 §1.5)
10. **B** — Commingling can pierce limited liability *and* corrupts your books. A dedicated business account is the first ops rule. (L2 §2.1)
11. **B** — Run diligence on yourself first. Term sheets are non-binding; the wire is real, and it dies quietly over gaps you didn't find. (L2 §3 / Challenge 1)
12. **C** — Bank account and bookkeeping are `[DIY]` organizing work. Entity choice, the tax election, and binding stock/vesting agreements are all professional work. (L1 §9 / L2 §4)

</details>
