# Week 9 Homework

Six practice problems that revisit the week's topics and feed the mini-project. The full set should take about **5.5 hours**. Work in your Week 9 venture repo so each problem produces a committed file you can point to later — and so the mini-project becomes mostly assembly.

> **This is education, not legal or tax advice.** Where a problem touches something binding or jurisdiction-specific, the deliverable is to *flag it for a professional*, not to decide it yourself.

Each problem includes a **statement**, **acceptance criteria**, a **hint**, and an **estimated time**.

---

## Problem 1 — IP & contribution trap audit

**Problem statement.** Complete Exercise 2's worksheet for your venture at `notes/trap-audit.md` (or finalize the `exercises/exercise-02-ip-trap-audit.md` you started). Inventory every asset the company depends on — code, brand/name, logo, domain, content, data — and for each, record the maker, the account/location, and the assignment status. Then explicitly check all three classic traps (personal-account work, the unassigned helper, the prior-employer claim), and triage every gap into "fix now" vs. `[NEEDS ADVISOR]`.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present with an asset inventory (maker / account / assignment status for each).
- All three traps explicitly checked (YES/NO) with a fix for each that applies.
- Triage separates cheap fixes (with owner + date) from `[NEEDS ADVISOR]` items.
- An honest one-line overall risk rating.
- Committed.

**Hint.** "We have no contributors yet" is a valid answer — write it down, so future-you remembers to get IP assignment signed *before* the first one starts. And don't forget the brand and the domain; they're assets too.

**Estimated time.** 50 minutes.

## Problem 2 — Core agreements register

**Problem statement.** At `notes/agreements-register.md`, list every agreement your venture needs now or soon: founders' agreement (stock + vesting), IP/invention assignment, advisor agreement(s), contractor/employee agreements (with IP + confidentiality), and an NDA only if genuinely warranted. For each, record **who signs**, **when** (now / before money / before hiring / later), and **status** (have / need / later).

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present with at least the four core agreements (plus NDA, justified or set aside).
- Each has signer(s), timing, and have/need/later status.
- NDA use is either justified for a specific situation or explicitly set aside as unnecessary.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Most early companies have *zero* of these signed and need most of them. Be honest about "need" vs. "have" — that honesty is the point. Demanding an NDA to pitch is a tell; note where one is *actually* warranted (e.g., sharing detailed financials with a potential acquirer).

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

---

## Problem 3 — Ops minimum plan

**Problem statement.** At `notes/ops-minimum.md`, write your operational minimum: a dedicated bank account, a bookkeeping approach, one document store, and a compliance/deadline note (including the time-sensitive tax election from Lecture 1). Keep it deliberately short and un-over-engineered.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File present with all four ops items.
- The time-sensitive tax-election deadline is explicitly on the compliance note.
- Genuinely minimal — no pile of premature SaaS tools.
- Committed.

**Hint.** If your plan needs ten tools, it's too much for this stage. A free spreadsheet and one drive folder is a complete answer for a pre-revenue two-person company. The discipline is restraint.

**Estimated time.** 40 minutes.

---

## Problem 4 — Incorporation decision canvas

**Problem statement.** Finalize Exercise 3's canvas at `notes/incorporation-canvas.md` (or `exercises/exercise-03-incorporation-decision-canvas.md`). Capture your venture, founders/ownership, fundraising intent, revenue/risk, two-to-three candidate entity options with trade-offs, your current lean (labeled as a hypothesis), timing and trigger, the founder-stock/tax-election flag, and at least three `[NEEDS ADVISOR]` open questions.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- Every section filled with your real facts (no leftover placeholders).
- At least two entity options compared with upside and downside.
- A current lean stated *and* labeled a hypothesis, not a decision.
- At least three `[NEEDS ADVISOR]` open questions.
- A concrete next action with a date.
- Committed.

**Hint.** Fundraising intent is the single biggest driver of the entity decision — be honest about whether you'll actually raise venture money or not, because the answer changes everything downstream.

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

---

## Problem 5 — Advisor questions

**Problem statement.** Finalize Exercise 1 at `notes/advisor-questions.md` (or its exercise file): a 3-sentence situation summary, at least four tailored lawyer questions, at least three accountant questions (including the time-sensitive-election question), and a "known traps in my situation" section pulling from Problem 1.

**Acceptance criteria.**
- Situation summary present (3 sentences, specific).
- ≥4 lawyer questions, each tailored (location + fundraising plan + real facts).
- ≥3 accountant questions, including the deadline-bound tax election.
- Known-traps section with at least two honest entries.
- Committed.

**Hint.** The difference between a $400 "it depends" meeting and a useful one is specificity. "Given that I'm in X, plan to raise a pre-seed, and have a co-founder in Y, what entity fits?" beats "what should I incorporate as?"

**Estimated time.** 45 minutes.

## Problem 6 — Reflection essay

**Problem statement.** Write a 300–400 word reflection at `notes/week-09-reflection.md` answering:

1. Before this week, what did you *assume* about incorporating and "the legal stuff"? What turned out to be different?
2. Which of the three IP traps is most live in your venture right now, and what's your honest plan to close it?
3. Where exactly is the line, for your company, between what you'll do yourself and what you'll route to a lawyer/accountant?
4. If a pre-seed lead asked tomorrow "is this a clean company?", what's the one-sentence answer — and what would it take to make that sentence "yes"?

**Acceptance criteria.**
- File exists, 300–400 words.
- Each numbered question addressed in its own paragraph.
- Committed.

**Hint.** This is for *you*, not a grade. Honesty here is what makes the mini-project real instead of a paperwork exercise. Future-you, holding a term sheet, will be grateful you wrote down the trap you knew about in week 9.

**Estimated time.** 30 minutes.

## Grading rubric

Each problem is scored against the same bar. Total 100 points.

| Problem | Points | Full marks require |
|---------|------:|--------------------|
| **1 — Trap audit** | 20 | Complete asset inventory; all three traps checked honestly; gaps triaged with owners/dates; honest risk rating |
| **2 — Agreements register** | 15 | All core agreements listed with signers, timing, status; NDA judgment shown |
| **3 — Ops minimum** | 15 | Four ops items; tax-election deadline noted; genuinely minimal, no over-engineering |
| **4 — Incorporation canvas** | 20 | All sections with real facts; ≥2 entity options compared; lean labeled a hypothesis; ≥3 advisor questions; dated next action |
| **5 — Advisor questions** | 15 | Specific situation; ≥4 tailored lawyer Qs; ≥3 accountant Qs incl. the election; known-traps section |
| **6 — Reflection** | 10 | 300–400 words; all four questions addressed honestly |
| **Consistency** | 5 | Founder stock/vesting matches Week 4; ownership matches Week 8 cap table; every binding item flagged for a pro |

**Grade bands:** 90–100 = ready to brief a real advisor and survive light diligence · 75–89 = solid, a couple of gaps to close · 60–74 = the shape is right but the honesty or specificity is thin · below 60 = redo; you're describing a generic company, not yours.

> **The meta-rubric:** across all six, the single thing being graded is whether you could hand this to a lawyer and a lead investor and have them take you seriously. Generic, dishonest, or hand-wavy fails even if every box is technically filled.
