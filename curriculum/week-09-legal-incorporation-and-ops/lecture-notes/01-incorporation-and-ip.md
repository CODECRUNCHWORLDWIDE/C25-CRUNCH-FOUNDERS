# Lecture 1 — Incorporation and IP

> **Duration:** ~1 hour. **Outcome:** You can explain why and when to incorporate, name entity choices at a high level, and understand founder stock and IP assignment.
>
> **This is education, not legal or tax advice. Entity and tax rules are jurisdiction-specific — confirm everything with a local professional.**

## 1. Why incorporate

- **Liability** — a company can separate your personal assets from business risk.
- **Ownership clarity** — shares formalize the equity split you reasoned out in Week 4.
- **Fundraising readiness** — investors invest in entities, not handshakes.
- **Cleanliness** — contracts, bank accounts, and taxes have a clear home.

## 2. When to incorporate

Incorporate when something real is at stake: you are taking money, signing contracts, bringing on a co-founder, or hiring. Incorporating too early adds cost and paperwork before there is anything to protect; incorporating too *late* can create tax messes and ownership disputes (who owned what during the "informal" period?). The honest answer is usually "around the time money or partners enter," and the precise timing is an advisor question.

## 3. Entity choices, at a high level

There are several common entity types, and the right one depends heavily on your country, state, tax situation, and fundraising plans. Because this is genuinely jurisdiction-specific, the course does **not** prescribe one — it teaches you to ask:

- What entity fits my situation, fundraising plans, and location?
- What are the tax implications, and are there elections with deadlines?
- What does formation and ongoing compliance cost?

Take these to a lawyer/accountant rather than copying what a blog post said worked for someone elsewhere.

## 4. Founder stock and vesting at incorporation

Incorporation is when your Week 4 equity split and vesting become real: founders are issued stock, ideally subject to the agreed vesting (four-year/one-year-cliff is the common default). There may be early tax elections tied to founder stock with strict deadlines — flag this for your accountant immediately; missing a deadline can be costly.

## 5. IP assignment — the quiet killer

Everything the company depends on — code, designs, brand, content — must legally belong to the *company*, not to whoever happened to make it. This requires **IP assignment** agreements signed by every founder and contributor. Classic traps:

- A co-founder built the core code on a personal account before any agreement existed.
- A contributor "helped for free" and never assigned their work.
- A founder built something related while employed elsewhere, and the prior employer may own it.

These surface during fundraising due diligence at the worst possible time. Fix them early: have everyone sign IP assignment, and check your own prior-employment situation.
